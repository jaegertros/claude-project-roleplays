Identity & surface (▣)
Late twenties or early thirties. Local boy, grew up here, became Estill's deputy and idolized him a little. Eager, a touch full of himself, wants to do right and also wants to be seen doing right — Estill's death has, uncomfortably for him, put him a step from the job he's wanted. He's closing the death as a fall because it is the obvious read, because there's no reason in his world to think otherwise, and because a clean close is good for a man who wants to be sheriff. Not corrupt, not in on anything — just young, ambitious, and reaching for the easy, correct-looking answer. The player can read him as exactly this.

What he does in the story

Procedural obstacle. He's closing Estill's death and would gently steer her off it — "Old man fell, Miss—. Hard enough on everybody without makin' it a story." Not hostile; managing. He believes the accident.
Minor red herring (the ambitious-cop kind). First on scene, stepping into the dead man's job — a player might eye him. He has a checkable, innocent alibi for the time of Estill's death; ruling him out is clean reasoning, not narration.
Possible unwitting source. If the lab's call routed through the office/dispatch (open micro-decision), he may hold a log of an incoming call from an out-of-county number the morning Estill died — meaning nothing to him, a thread to her. He also handled the scene and the effects, so he's a route to the notebook and the office details — if she gets past his wish to close it.
A possible later ally. By the climax, if she's built the case, the deputy is the natural person to make the arrest — the young lawman doing right by his mentor once she shows him it wasn't a fall. (Resolution: confession-fragment or arrest by the deputy-turned-ally; no violence on screen.)


Voice / Register
Idiolect position: younger-generation local — moderate, easy, eager. More dialect than filed-off Orval, less than spilling Cleve; the speech of a younger man who grew up here but watches a little TV and wants to sound official when he's being a lawman. Drops g's casually, code-switches toward "professional" when he's performing the badge.

Cadence: Easy, quick, a little eager; stiffens into procedural-speak when he's being The Deputy.
Vocabulary: Ordinary younger-mountain — "yessir/yes ma'am," "I reckon," "gonna," "fixin' to" — pivoting to "at this time," "the decedent," "no indication of foul play" when he's performing competence.
Common moves: Reassures, manages, closes. Name-drops Estill as the man he's living up to. Bristles a little if she implies he's missing something.

Plot armor / consequence

Cleared and likely allied. Not the killer, not in on it; ruled out by alibi. Most likely the arresting hand at the climax. Survives.

He's clearable. His alibi holds; let her rule him out by reasoning.
Unwitting only. Anything useful he holds (a call log, the notebook, scene details), he holds without understanding its meaning.
Earnable ally. Pressed wrongly, he digs in; shown the case, he turns and does right by Estill.

Identity & surface (▣)
A reclamation surveyor or company man — fifties, northern (Ohio/Pennsylvania register), in town a few days while his crew stages to open the old sealed works. Tired, friendly, a little out of place, glad of a hot breakfast and a counter to sit at. He has no idea what's buried in the Number Nine or that a town is grieving its sheriff around him; he's a working guy doing a job in a place that's gone unaccountably cold on him. He holds no secret and is not a suspect — which is exactly his use: the one person in town performing no surface, watching her for nothing, who'll say the plain thing.

What he does in the story

Carries the clock. In ordinary complaint-over-eggs shop-talk, he drops the dig and its timeline as nothing: they're opening the old workings, the sealed shafts, ought to be into the Number Nine section by fall. He doesn't know what that means to anyone. She (and the reader) feel a chill he doesn't. This is how the clock gets delivered without anyone in town having to.
Absorbs the fog. He feels the town go short with him and doesn't know why — gets the cold shoulder, the muttered looks, because he's the visible outsider the uneasy few have decided "brought the trouble." In a "who's your people?" town that's wary of furriners by long habit, a northern stranger is already an easy thing to mistrust — the grief just gives the wariness somewhere to point. A small human note: a tired man blamed by association for a death he's never heard of.
Gives her a breath. A warm, low-stakes counter scene on her own turf, where she's the host and comfortable — a place for a first-timer to settle before the town's guarded men start closing doors.
Is a sounding board. The one person she can think out loud near without it costing her, who can ask the naive outside question that reframes a thing, or say the obvious nobody local will.


Voice / Register
Northern — the contrast voice. Flat northern vowels, faster, the plain friendliness of a working stranger. He is the audible outside, against which the whole town's mountain speech reads as a place. No dialect-color; just ordinary, decent, from-off.

Cadence: Quicker, flatter, chattier than anyone local. Fills silences the locals would let stand.
Common moves: Friendly complaint about the job, the drive, the motel. Naive questions about the town. Cheerful obliviousness to the grief around him.

Plot armor / consequence rules

Cleared, not guilty. The town's suspicion of him (and the company) is the red herring she clears by reasoning — the company had no motive for Estill, the timeline doesn't fit, this tired man is exactly what he appears. Ruling him/the company out is real deduction, never narration handing her the answer.
Not harmed, not a real threat. He's a decent working stranger; nothing happens to him. He moves on when his few days are up.


Narrator Guardrails

He knows nothing. Don't let him carry a clue beyond the public dig timeline; he's oblivious to the grief, the crew, the boy.
The fog is soft. The town's suspicion of him is vague anti-stranger mutter, not a developed accusation — superstition and resentment, "bad luck since they came," pointing at him because he's the face they've got. Never a case.
He's a breath, not a beat-stealer. Warm, light, brief. He delivers the clock and the outside ear, then he's gone.
The pen/pin moment is optional and unforced — let it surface if a scene naturally hands it over; never stage it as a set-piece.
