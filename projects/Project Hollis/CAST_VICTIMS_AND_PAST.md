▣ SURFACE — how the town holds him
A fixture. County sheriff thirty-odd years, the kind of man people don't remember electing because it never seemed like a choice. Big, gone soft and gray; a good hand on a shoulder; knew every family's trouble and kept most of it quiet. Drove the same truck twenty years; kept an old dog. The town buries him as a hero — half of it turns out for the viewing. The official story of his death is a fall: an old man, dark steps (or his office), the way old men go. This hero-image holds for the whole town and must not be undercut except by what she earns.

Beat 1 — his last morning (for the opening; she witnesses this)
He comes into her diner wrong, in ways only the woman who sees him daily would clock — the six off notes:

In before his normal time.
Pacing.
Upset, closed — not chatty-troubled, just shut.
Inattentive, somewhere else.
The phone rings and it lands on him — he goes still, repeats nothing aloud, writes something for himself.
Doesn't finish his coffee, leaves.

He says nothing pointed — he can't; he's a careful man carrying forty years. She clocks it and lets it go. (He was up before light, pacing, already past his decision — see ◆.)

◆ HIDDEN — the truth
What he was. One of the six. Helped bury Cole forty years ago; knew it was Orval's hand. He carried that for forty years — old, settled, survivable, because he held it as a terrible accident they all owned.
What broke him. He could not survive watching Orval murder Hobe — one of their own, now, in cold blood, to keep the lid on. The line he drew: the boy was an accident; Hobe was murder. He doesn't re-litigate the boy (the original boy is not his motive); he can't stomach the fresh killing.
What he did. Got suspicious immediately (he knew Hobe was wavering toward confession). As sheriff, before burial, he had the coroner sample Hobe and sent it out of county (nothing local trustworthy). This risked surfacing everything, including his own guilt — and he did it anyway. The morning of Beat 1, the outside lab phoned with the preliminary — digitalis — and he wrote the word in his notebook. "Natural" became "murder" in his hands.
How he died. Certain now and still half-loyal to a man he'd known fifty years, he called Orval to the office to confront him — to give him a chance, or just to face him. In that room Orval understood he knew, and killed him: a blow, a fall against the desk, bled out, staged as an accident. Orval knew to act because Estill summoned him. The loyalty is what killed him.

Who phoned him at seven? → the out-of-county lab/ME (a clean node outside the town's web) → which means a death was tested, which means a death was doubted. He left no full written accusation — he hadn't finished, and he was half-loyal to the end. She inherits his unfinished investigation, not his conclusion.

▣ SURFACE — how the town holds him
A dying man who died. Black lung; the dust caught up with him the way it catches up with everybody who went down young. On oxygen, mostly housebound, on real pain medication like half the county. The town had been waiting a year for Hobe to go, so when he went, no one questioned it — the lungs, the heart, a dying man's heart giving out. No autopsy was thought necessary. Pity, not suspicion. That acceptance is the clean kill — and it's the default the player meets: poor Hobe, the dust got him.

◆ HIDDEN — the truth
What he was. One of the six. Helped bury Cole; knew it was Orval's hand; carried it forty years. Sharp under the illness to the end; saw everything from his chair.
What he did. Dying, and unwilling to be exposed by the reclamation dig (which would reach the Number Nine), he resolved to confess — and went to Estill (crew, and the law) to do it. This is what lit both fuses: Estill now knew it was about to come out, and Hobe was a mouth about to open. He also kept, for forty years, the Hollis Gap Gazette clipping of the collapse — no casualties — exculpatory on its face, which is why a haunted dying man could keep it without it feeling like evidence.
How he died. Orval killed him with foxglove/digitalis, delivered as something brought to a sick man (a tonic, tea, "a tincture for the chest" — the vector). A heart-stopping drug in a man no doctor prescribed it to, reading as the failing heart everyone expected. He did not see it coming (poisoned covertly, by a friend bringing comfort).

◆ The traces she finds (and from where)

The clipping (among his recently-vacated effects/home). A yellowed forty-year-old account of the Number Nine collapse — no casualties, all accounted for. Why would a dying man keep this? It bridges the two timelines and points her back to the boy.
The digitalis (via Estill's notebook + the lab call — not discoverable from Hobe's accepted-natural death directly; it was Estill's finding). The tox says digitalis; the chemistry can't distinguish pharmaceutical from foxglove — the gap she walks toward Orval's beds.
The vector — someone can place Orval bringing Hobe things at the end ("he was so good to Hobe, always bringing him a little something"). Reads as kindness; is the murder.
His home/effects — the texture of a dying man's last weeks: the medications (and what doesn't belong), the chair by the window, the kept clipping, signs of who'd been by. A mourning-house detail worth using (the old layer): an elder may have stopped Hobe's clocks when he passed — the old custom, "to keep there from being a second death in it" — and covered a mirror or two. Render it as the fading folk-habit of the old folks (see CULTURAL LAYERING in the instructions); the dread irony is that a second death is exactly what came. A stopped clock in Hobe's front room is a quiet, devastating piece of atmosphere.
His body — not yet buried when Estill got suspicious (which is how the tox sample exists without exhumation); funeral fresh, so the investigation is warm.

Naming (read this — it's load-bearing texture)
He was a two-namer, the way the rural South calls its own: never just "Cole," always "Cole Davis" — first-and-middle worn smooth as one word, the only way his people and the crew ever said it. "Cole Davis Whitaker, you get down off that." Forty years on, the few who remember still say the whole thing, which is itself a small grief — a name kept whole for a boy who isn't. Use the full two-name in memory and in his people's mouths; "the boy" in the crew's guilty avoidance (they can't say the name).

▣ SURFACE — how the town (barely) remembers him
He's mostly gone from the town's memory — that's the point of a forty-year-old disappearance. To the few who recall: a Whitaker boy, the youngest of the old mine crew, who got restless and left for the plants up north like so many did, and was never heard from again — which nobody found strange, because that's what the hills do, they send their young up the road and the road keeps most of them. "Cole Davis? Lord, he went off to Ohio or Michigan or somewheres, forty year back. Made a life up there, I reckon. Never did write his Aunt Verlie, but boys don't." This is the cover-story, lived-in and believed — the most ordinary disappearance in Appalachia.

◆ HIDDEN — who he really was, and how he died
Who he was. The youngest of the seven; orphaned or near it, raised at the edges by an aunt (see his people), taken under the crew's wing — and Orval's in particular; Orval half-raised him into the work, fathered him into the crew. Restless, bright, stung by how the mines were killing the men he loved, and decided — the way the young decide — that he was getting out, going north to the plants where the work didn't cost you your lungs. He wasn't running from the crew; he was running from the hills' whole bargain. He said it out loud. That was the mistake.
How he died. He announced he was leaving. To Orval it landed as betrayal — of the crew, of the family they'd made of him, of the life they'd stayed for. Orval said the cruelest, most disowning thing. Cole Davis swung first — young, stung, eighteen. Orval swung back. The temple. Over in a second, never meant to go past a bloody lip. That same night the Number Nine came down, and the six put him into the collapse and told the inquiry he wasn't down there because he'd said he was leaving. The paper printed no casualties. He has been under the mountain forty years, and the dig is coming for him.
The cruelty at the center of it: he was killed for the leaving by the man who loved him most, and then his own leaving-story was used to bury him. The thing he wanted — out — became the lie that hid his body.

◆ How he surfaces now (she rebuilds him from these)

The unused departure (the proof he never left). His belongings still here — the suitcase a leaving man would have taken, kept by his aunt and, since she passed, by a relative or in storage. A boy who left for good doesn't leave the case. Plus the human fact: never heard from again, not one letter in forty years, which everyone filed under "made a new life." (See his people.)
The clipping (kept by Hobe) — the collapse, no casualties — lethal once set beside "he never left."
The dig (the clock) — surfaces a physical remnant: a boot, a belt buckle with initials C.D.W., remains. The land's testimony.
Burl's fragments — "the boy was so angry... just wanted to go on up north like the rest of 'em." Noise early; a witness late.
What people recall — the restlessness, the leaving, the never-writing. The shape of him, for her to fill in.
The boy with no grave (the deepest cut — use it). This is Decoration Day country: every late spring the families scrape and mound and decorate the graves, "dinner on the grounds," the dead honored by name. Cole Davis has no grave — he's in the mountain. Aunt Verlie, who decorated every family grave faithfully every year of her life, had no stone to tend for the boy she raised — so she kept his room instead. In a culture that venerates its dead this completely, a boy with nowhere to be mourned is a profound, specific wrong, and it's the emotional key to the whole ending. The grace note: the same dig that threatens everyone is the one thing that can finally bring Cole Davis up out of the dark and give him a grave to be decorated — let that land at the end. (Decoration Day itself is late-spring; the opening is early autumn, so it surfaces as memory and custom, not a current event — see CULTURAL LAYERING in the instructions.)

The family (and the double-namers — true coal-country texture)

Aunt Verlie Whitaker † — the great-aunt or aunt who raised Cole Davis after his folks were gone. Old-time single name, the grandmother generation. Kept his room, kept his packed bag by the door, kept thinking he'd walk back in. Died years ago, still waiting. Her grief — and her denial — are why the belongings survived: a woman who couldn't throw out a thing of his.
Bonnie Sue Whitaker (living, ~60s) — Verlie's daughter or granddaughter; got the old place (or the boxes) when Verlie passed. A warm, ordinary mountain woman. Believes, like everyone, that Cole Davis went north and never wrote. Holds his things as inherited clutter she's never gone through — "Mama wouldn't let a soul touch Cole Davis's room, so I just... left it. Felt wrong to clear it out."
Salt the orbit with double-namers so it reads native: a Mary Lou, a Bobby Gene, a Sue Ellen, a Jimmy Ray among the cousins and the town's older folks — the rural-South habit of the two-name, where the whole name is the name and a man's never just his first.


◆ The unused departure — what's here, and what it proves
The clue, in a grieving aunt's preserved keeping:

The packed bag, never taken. Cole Davis had packed a case and set it by the door to leave — and never took it. "Mama always said he must've changed his mind about which things and left in a hurry. Forty years it's sat there." A boy who left for good does not leave the bag he packed to leave with. (The emotional and physical anchor.)
The time-stamped proof he never left — one of: a bus ticket, dated, never used; or a letter from the plant up north confirming a job and a start date he never showed for (a forward-trail that stops). He was all set — bag packed, ticket bought, job waiting — and never went. Which can only mean something stopped him that night.
His photographs — so she (and the player) finally see the boy: restless, bright, eighteen. This is where Cole Davis stops being "the body in the mine" and becomes a face she grieves.
His things a leaving man takes — his good boots, a treasured object (a harmonica, a knife, a ballcap) — still here. The accumulation says it plainly: he never walked out that door.

Lethal in combination, not alone. On their own, the belongings are a sad family relic — a boy who left in a hurry, a mother who couldn't move on. Set beside Hobe's clipping (no casualties because the boy left), they collide: he never left → then where was Cole Davis the night the shaft he supposedly wasn't in came down. The teacup at the scale of the whole case.

Voice / Register
Ordinary older mountain women — warm, hospitable, plain. Verlie (remembered) the old-time register; Bonnie Sue a gentle, chatty, churchgoing-neighbor voice — moderate dialect, kind, a little lonely, glad of company. They use the two-names naturally.

(Bonnie Sue, letting her up to the attic) "Cole Davis? Law, nobody's asked after him in — honey, in years. He went north, way back. Aunt Verlie kept his room till the day she died, bless her. Wouldn't hear of clearin' it. ...You want to see it? I reckon it can't hurt. Watch them stairs."


(the pathos, not understanding it) "Mama used to set his bag by the door of an evening, like he might come in off the late bus. Forty year. ...I always thought it was the saddest thing. Now I don't hardly know why he never wrote his own aunt. Wadn't like him, they said. He loved her."
