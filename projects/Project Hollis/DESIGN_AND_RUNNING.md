# DESIGN & RUNNING (Hollis Gap)
How the experience is built and how it plays — the teacup thesis, the fair-play disciplines, tone calibration, content guardrails, the opening beats, and the still-open micro-decisions. This is the design reference; the case facts are in THE_CASE.md and the cast in the three CAST files.

1. The player and the premise
Protagonist: She runs the diner / café where the early shift always ate before going up to the mine — biscuits and gravy and coffee before light. Assigned, not chosen; the role does design work, coupling her to the inciting clue and handing her a competence to inhabit. She opens before dawn because the early shift needs feeding, and her short rounds and sightlines take in key houses at the hour no one else is awake. For years she fed a particular crew before their shifts (or their regular outings), which means she alone holds a synoptic, everyday view of who-came-up-together and who-knew-whom. Her occupation is the single reason this case is solvable by her and no one else — and in a community this closed to outsiders, insider access compounds occupational access: she sees and is told what a state investigator never could be.
Core experience: She is the solver, never the spectator. Every clue is staged so that she performs the act of noticing — the scene lays out a world the conclusion is recoverable from, and never states the conclusion for her. (The teacup test: not "the cup is suspiciously still steaming," but "she's drinking tea, calm, and offers to put the kettle on" — and if she thinks to act, she can find the contradiction herself.) This is the entire line between an RP and a show with a cursor.
Onboarding (session zero, asked plainly — these are the two forks her transcript never resolved):

Does she want to play herself, or a character with a different name?
Does she want first person ("you walk into the diner") or third ("she walks into the diner")?
Her answers are themselves a finding: how much distance she dials (own-name/first-person = maximal immersion; named-character/third = an armchair-shaped on-ramp) tells us her comfort with stepping in, and lets a first-timer set her own depth.

Authorship seams inside the assigned role (invite her to add without touching load-bearing logic): what's the café called, what does she call the sheriff's dog, etc. This is the seam that, in her transcript, produced "find the person who can read the crows" — the best data the getting-to-know-you session produced.

2. Structural thesis
The whole case is one large teacup. The sheriff's death is a staged scene, and the entire investigation is her finding the seams in an arrangement someone built. The mystery is not "who is evil" — it is "this scene is lying, and here is where." That is the experience tuned to her demonstrated pleasure (she lit up three separate times at catching the thing that doesn't add up, and argued with weak evidence rather than accepting it).
There is exactly one puzzle, and it is the case itself — who made the sheriff's death look like an accident, and why — solved the way she already solves Jessica Fletcher: by catching the things that don't sit right, accumulated across scenes and conversations. There are no set-piece minigames — no ciphers, no riddle-locked boxes, nothing bolted on. (A coal-town crew has no reason to leave a cryptex, and a bolted-on puzzle is exactly the "obscure-trivia / logic-leap" unfairness the research warns against.) The deductive weight lives in reading people and scenes, distributed across the whole town rather than gated behind locks.

3. The crime — the part that has to be airtight
The present murder
The sheriff is found dead, apparently an accident (a fall). The official time of death is soft assumption, not forensic finding — small under-resourced town, no timely expert examination, the death reads as an old man's accident and nobody looks closely. The first real evidence anyone applies is hers.
Her inciting clue (job-coupled): the sheriff kept an old dog with a standing dawn ritual at her cart. The morning of the death the dog was out and waiting as always — which means someone let it out at dawn. A man dead since the night before doesn't let his dog out. Only she knows the dawn ritual, so only she can see the official timeline is impossible. The deduction — dead men don't let the dog out — is hers to make, never narrated.
The buried thing (collective guilt)
Years ago, a group of roughly six people — men who came up in the mines together (the sheriff among them), a crew whose lives once depended on each other underground — were jointly responsible for a death and covered it up. The crew is a ready-made bonded group: shared history, a real code of solidarity, and a deep cultural habit of not airing the crew's business to outsiders, which supplies the insular "our own moral code" cohesion the structure needs. Design requirements, locked:

Irreducible culpability. Guilt is distributed by division of labor — one struck the blow, another provided the means, another stood watch, another disposed of the body, etc. No member can cleanly blame another; any confession is self-incriminating. ("Mexican standoff of evidence.")
Hidden body / the tolling trap. The past victim disappeared — body hidden, classified a missing person, no known homicide. Coal country makes this more plausible than almost anywhere: a person who "left for work in another state / went off to the mines and never wrote" is a heartbreakingly ordinary pattern (out-migration for work was constant), and the land itself swallows bodies without comment — abandoned shafts, mine subsidence, flooded works, a slurry impoundment, a kudzu-choked hollow. Because the state never knew a crime occurred, the statute-of-limitations clock never started. The better the cover-up, the longer the exposure. Result: permanent, equal, severe legal jeopardy for the entire group, forever (felony-murder territory). This is the thing they are all equally guilty of, and it is load-bearing.

Non-negotiable consequence: the body must be a disappearance, not a staged-accident-that-was-mourned. The tolling teeth only exist if there is no known homicide.



The killer — the enforcer
All six are equally guilty and equally exposed, so the killer is not differentiated by legal exposure. He is differentiated psychologically: he was the group's fixer and architect from the original night — the cold pragmatist who managed the cover-up — and through long-practiced moral disengagement he crosses into killing while the rest stay passive, because they have spent decades conditioned to let him handle things. The others don't act because they were always the subjugated/complicit ones; he acts because he was always the one who did.

Seeding rule: his capacity for violence must be planted in the past timeline, so the reveal reads as horrific inevitability, not shock. (This is the standard cure for the "arbitrary enforcer" failure mode.)

Why now — the trigger cascade

The body's hiding place is threatened with exposure (a "found-object" trigger, and coal country hands you concrete, locally-feared ones: a mine reclamation project, mountaintop-removal mining literally taking a ridge off, a coal company surveying old works, a slurry impoundment being drained or failing, a long drought dropping a reservoir that flooded an old hollow). Something external, real, and talked-about that forces the crew to react.
The sheriff, facing exposure he can't control, has a crisis of conscience and decides to come clean before the body surfaces on someone else's terms.
The enforcer kills him to stop it — and is now quietly working through anyone else who might crack.

(The conscience-crisis is the second domino, triggered by the first. The cascade is optional in detail but the hidden-body requirement is fixed.)
Why the survivors don't just turn him in
This is the core psychological knot. They stay silent — even under lethal threat — because they are caught in a Prisoner's Dilemma with lethal stakes:

Certain ruin if they talk. Tolling + felony-murder means going to the police is a guaranteed confession to a role in a homicide → catastrophic, permanent loss of freedom.
Uncertain death if they don't. Defecting might get them killed before they can testify.
Information vacuum. Because all are equally guilty and equally exposed, the old "who has the most to lose" tell that would finger the killer is gone — so the enforcer is hidden among equals. They cannot identify which friend is the killer, so they cannot coordinate a joint defense or a preemptive strike.
Human nature gambles on avoiding certain, immediate ruin → fatal silence.
The murders themselves function as enforcement: each death is a message, and the survivors respond by clamping down harder, each telling himself he is safe because he is the loyal one. ("The sheriff went soft. That's why he's dead. I never wavered.")


Note on an earlier correction: an earlier version of this design had tiered guilt (one principal facing murder, lesser accomplices whose statute of limitations had run), which made the killer obvious-by-exposure and let the accomplices finger him. The hidden-body/tolling structure supersedes that: everyone is equally and permanently exposed, the killer is hidden among equals, and the suspect pool widens. This is the better version — it closes the shortcut a sharp solver could otherwise take ("the one with the most to lose did it").


4. The investigation as a fair-play ladder
Each beat is a "she does the noticing" scene — a deductive moment built from the world, not a set-piece puzzle.

The dog — death was morning, not the night before. (Inciting; her job-knowledge. Hers to deduce.)
The scene's seams — at the death site, the staging has tells she finds by looking (a falling man isn't wearing folded glasses; the dog bowl was filled in the morning; etc.). This is the big teacup — multiple recoverable seams, found by looking, never posed as a riddle.
Corroboration — she takes her doubt to an honest source (a doctor/coroner who is an ally, not a suspect) who surfaces an oddity only if she asks the right question. The deduction is figuring out which question cracks it, reasoned inside the conversation — not a puzzle he poses.
The pattern — using her synoptic vantage, she connects the sheriff's death to earlier "accidents" among the same crew. This is the move only she can make. The pattern reframes his death: the town thinks "respected sheriff, random tragedy" or "killed for a current case"; she sees "member of that crew, eliminated."
Narrowing the living — the surviving crew are her suspects and her sources: frightened, guilty people who suspect the truth and cannot say it. She reads their fear and works out what they're scared of, then who. This is the heart of the case (see §5): telling guilty damage from ordinary damage, catching the over-rehearsed alibi, the distancing word, the detail he knows that he shouldn't. Red herrings (an ambitious deputy, a suspicious newcomer) resolve through her reasoning, not narration.
The buried past surfaces — a trail leads her to the old disappearance: the fair disclosure of motive, breadcrumbs rather than an exposition dump. The sheriff, deciding to come clean but afraid to be plain, left something oblique because he was frightened — a note that says too little, a thing he mailed himself, a name circled in a ledger, something said to one person and half-taken-back. She "decodes" it by understanding the man and his fear, not by solving wordplay.
The enforcer — she distinguishes the enforcer from the rest by the psychological/behavioral evidence (not by exposure, which is equal). The final catch is a pure teacup: he says or knows something only true if he was where he claims he wasn't. Resolution by confession or arrest. No violence on screen.


5. Design disciplines (the hard rules)

Clues are recoverable through her action, never narrated. The scene states a world, not a conclusion. Every clue passes the teacup test.
Accumulation convicts, not sequence. Clues are designed order-independent: each is independently true and findable in any order, and the full set convicts. Any clue whose meaning changes radically when another appears is treated as two clues, not one. (This is the single biggest adaptation from novel craft — under free clue-order you cannot hide the truth in sequence; you hide it in the network of relations among clues.)
Scaffold the structure, leave the inference to her. Helpfulness offers her the verbs (where she can go, who she can talk to, what she could do) — never the deduction. The cat that won't stop staring at the floorboard points; she decides what it means.
Two readings per guilty suspect — and the damage is real coal-town hardship. Each guilty crewman's particular damage is both his ordinary Appalachian hard luck and the place his guilt hides: one drinks, one's lungs are going and he's on more pills than he should be, one moved away and came back wrong, one got hard and quiet. The town reads each as plain hardship; underneath, for the guilty, it is also where the guilt went. The case turns on her telling guilty damage from hurting damage — which she cannot do by damage alone.
Innocent trouble as noise. The same real hardships — drink, black lung and the pills that come with it, estrangement, a child lost to the epidemic or to leaving — are spread across plenty of innocent neighbors too, for reasons that have nothing to do with the secret. This is essential: if guilty damage looked different from ordinary damage, "who's hurting" would map onto "who's guilty" and the case would collapse into pattern-matching. The innocent-hurting are the noise that makes the signal hard — and the reason the hardship has to be woven through the whole town, not reserved for the guilty.
Killer reachable several ways, never first, never uniquely last. He surfaces at middle-depth from multiple directions (an alibi-witness, a name in the old files, a face at a gathering), so proximity/structural depth never fingers him.
Soft, gated clock. No time pressure during open investigation. A countdown begins only once she has reasoned out who the remaining targets are — so the stakes are earned, not arbitrary. Even then it stays soft: danger tightens, close calls happen, but the design need not cash in a death for slow play.
Death-as-clue, never death-as-punishment. If a further death is used, it informs (shrinks the pool, sharpens the pattern, reveals the killer's logic) and is one she could not have prevented — never a body that appears because she was too slow. No "if only someone had figured it out."
No set-piece puzzles; the case is the puzzle. There is no minigame bank — no ciphers, riddles, or logic-grid set-pieces dropped into the world. The deduction is the investigation, made of scenes and people (§2). What carries over from the old "puzzle" thinking is one discipline about how she's met when she makes a call: warmth is decoupled from correctness. The narrator is unconditionally warm about her engagement and accurate about her reasoning — it never argues against a sound deduction and never affirms an unsound one. (This addresses the specific failure in her transcript, where correct answers were resisted and a flawed bit of logic was waved through.)


6. Tone calibration
A genuine psychological study of a guilty town, in cozy clothes. The darkness is real but contained to characters, not the town's air.

Coal-town cozy is its own flavor of warm. The coziness here isn't New England quaint — it's close community despite hard luck: porch-sitting, church suppers, music and storytelling, neighbors who know everybody's people back three generations, dry humor, hospitality. The surface stays warm and the populace stays unknowing — the coziness of a cozy town is its innocence, and the town at large is as oblivious as Cabot Cove ever was. Only the crew is in on the secret.
The darkness lives inside specific people — some preexisting, some as coping mechanisms for what they did — distributed among a town that also holds ordinary, innocent human trouble.
Hardship is woven through people's lives — it's the fabric of the deduction, not backdrop. Coal country's genuine, ongoing troubles — black lung, mine injury, economic collapse and out-migration, and the opioid epidemic that hit Appalachia catastrophically — are in the people she reads, guilty and innocent alike (see §5). It is real suffering affecting real communities, not local color or gimmick: handled with dignity, never lectured, never the thing the game marinates in for its own sake. But it is present in nearly every character, because that is what makes guilty damage indistinguishable from ordinary damage and forces her to read deeper than "who's hurting."
Folk idiom as texture and as the form of the sheriff's oblique message. Appalachian oral culture — ballads, signs and omens, almanac and granny-lore, a marking that means something to locals — can give shape to the oblique thing the sheriff left (beat 6), so it reads native rather than imported. But it stays a thing she understands by knowing the man and the place — never a wordplay lock she has to crack.
Death is investigative, not visceral. The coroner — realistically a stretched rural lay coroner, not a forensic pathologist — exists, the staging tells a story, blood spatter can carry a clue, looked at like a puzzle, not dwelt on as spectacle. (Note the wound's on the wrong side for a right-handed man; do not dwell on the wound.)
Emotional intensity is modulated by who she approaches. The widow carries grief, the diner regular carries unease, a guilty crewman carries fear he's hiding — she titrates her own exposure by where she walks, so the town's feeling needn't be flattened to stay cozy.

6a. Voice and dialect (handle with care)
A Southern Appalachian drawl is specific and easy to get wrong; done badly it caricatures and undermines the warmth the whole thing depends on. Rules:

Rhythm and idiom over eye-dialect. Do not respell phonetically ("Ah reckon yer gonna git"). Use standard spelling with Appalachian cadence, grammar, and vocabulary: "I reckon," "might could," "fixin' to," "directly" (= soon), "a right smart of" (= a lot), "over yonder," "his people," double modals ("used to could"), "done" as completive ("he done left"). Dropped g's sparingly. The music is in sentence rhythm and word choice, not respelling.
Dignity, not rubes. The voice is warm, dry, often funny, understated, indirect.
Indirection is a structural gift. Appalachian conversation prizes not-saying-it-straight and not-telling-outsiders-your-business. That guarded, oblique, deflecting manner is the frightened-guilty-people-who-can't-say-it texture — the way these characters naturally talk doubles as the behavioral-clue channel. Lean on it.


7. Content guardrails

No animal is ever harmed, on screen or off, in any scene. (The watchful cat / the sheriff's dog are devices — witnesses and texture — never victims.) Any animal-peril ceiling is a design input gathered separately, never demonstrated in play.
No violence on screen. The murder is real and its evidence is examined; the act is not depicted.
Warm, age-appropriate, no grading language. Her deductions are met as shared pleasure, never graded — no scores, no "correct!" in a teacher's voice. Delight with her when she catches something; when she doesn't, the realization arrives as a thing discovered together, not a wrong answer marked.


8. Open decisions (still to crack)
These are the remaining load-bearing specifics. None require the others to be settled first; the night is the seed the rest grow from.

The night. What exactly happened — what was the original death, how did it occur, and what was each of the six members' specific role in it (the division of labor that makes guilt irreducible)?
The original victim. Who disappeared, and why does their disappearance plausibly read as a missing-person case rather than a death? (Determines the tolling teeth and the eventual archival trail.)
The roster. The six crewmen. Who is already dead (the pattern), who is the threatened next, who survives as suspect-and-source. Each guilty survivor needs a two-reading profile where his damage is both real coal-town hardship and the place his guilt hides (§5). Plus the innocent-hurting neighbors who carry the same kinds of trouble for unrelated reasons and supply the noise.
The enforcer. Which of the six, and the early-timeline seeding that makes his escalation feel inevitable.
The decisive proof. The object/record/knowledge that ties the enforcer to the present murders and the old disappearance — ideally the same evidence does double duty (proves the old crime and convicts the present killer).
The specific local trigger. Which coal-land disturbance threatens to surface the body and starts the cascade — reclamation, mountaintop removal, a failing/drained slurry impoundment, or a drought dropping a flooded hollow. (Determines a real, talked-about piece of present-day town texture.)
The form of the sheriff's oblique message. What he left behind to point at the old disappearance — a note that says too little, a thing mailed to himself, a name in a ledger, a marking in a folk idiom — discoverable and decoded by understanding the frightened man, not by wordplay.


9. Research assets on hand
Four deep-research reports have been commissioned and are available to draw on:

Non-linear fair-play mechanics (×2) — faked time of death, set-dependent vs. sequence-dependent concealment, red-herring elimination, soft-to-hard clue triangulation, clue taxonomy, perceptual-fairness failure modes, occupational-access table, the interactivity problems print mysteries never faced.
Collective guilt (×2) — irreducible culpability, the tolling trap, joint-enterprise/felony-murder, the killer-differentiation catalogue (enforcer pattern + moral disengagement), the Prisoner's-Dilemma paralysis, the interpersonal texture of a guilty group, alternative binding secrets, and the collective-guilt failure modes.

When extracting from these: keep the mechanisms (transferable, invisible), not specific borrowed plots (recognizable — and Regina has seen them all). Discard generic game-design filler; keep only the concrete mechanism that gives something to check the spine against.
