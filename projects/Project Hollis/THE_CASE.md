# THE CASE — Hollis Gap (Master Truth & Secret-Keeping File)
Setting: Hollis Gap, a small Southern Appalachian coal town in decline — everyone just calls it Hollis. (An outsider who says the full "Hollis Gap" marks himself as from off; a local says "Hollis.") The collapsed shaft at the center of the old crime is the Number Nine, in the Hollis Gap works. The local paper is the Hollis Gap Gazette.
This file is the COMPLETE TRUTH of the case (Part One) plus the HIDDEN-LAYER KNOWLEDGE MATRIX (Part Two) — who knows what, and how to run it without leaking. It is the master narrator-facing reference. Running rules and mechanics are in DESIGN_AND_RUNNING.md. Cast is in CAST_CREW.md (the living crew), CAST_VICTIMS_AND_PAST.md (the dead and the boy), and CAST_OUTSIDERS.md (deputy, company man).

PART ONE — THE TRUTH
The case in one paragraph
Forty years ago, seven young men were a mine crew. The youngest — Cole Whitaker (proposed) — announced he was leaving for the auto plants up north. To Orval Stallard, who had half-raised the boy into the crew, it landed as betrayal; he said something unforgivable, the boy swung, Orval swung back, and a blow to the temple killed him. That same night a shaft came down — the old Number Nine (proposed) — and the six surviving men put the boy's body into the collapse and told the inquiry everyone was accounted for, because the boy had said he was leaving anyway. The paper reported no casualties. The boy "went north and never wrote," which is the most ordinary disappearance in Appalachia, so no one ever looked. Forty years pass; the crime becomes the men's damage. A reclamation company begins excavating the old sealed works — the collapse is about to be undone. Hobart Caudill, one of the six and dying of black lung, decides to confess before the backhoes do it for him. Orval kills him with foxglove (digitalis), staged as the failing heart of a dying man. Sheriff Estill Combs — also one of the six — cannot accept Orval murdering one of their own, and quietly has Hobe's body tested. The lab calls back: digitalis. Estill confronts Orval, and Orval kills him too — a blow, a fall against the desk, read as an accident. The diner-keeper who watched the sheriff's last strange morning is the only person who won't accept his death — and she pulls the thread.

I. The original crime (forty years ago)
The crew of seven. Young miners, welded by the work. Six of them are the present cast; the seventh is the victim.
The boy — Cole Whitaker (proposed). The youngest, taken under the crew's wing — orphaned or near it, raised at the edges by an aunt/grandmother now long dead, which is part of why his disappearance drew no hard questions: little family left to ask. Orval in particular fathered him into the work.
The night. Cole announces he's leaving for the plants up north — the road out of the hills that everyone's cousin took. To Orval it isn't a kid chasing work; it's abandonment — of the crew, of the family they'd made of him, of the whole way of life that's killing them and that they stayed for anyway. Orval says the cruelest, most disowning version of after everything we did for you. Stung, the boy swings first. Orval swings back. The temple. It is over in a second and was never meant to go past a bloody lip. The boy swung first is the lie Orval has told himself for forty years to survive it: he came at me, it was an accident, I'm not what that would make me. True of this death. Not of the two to come.
The disposal. That same night the Number Nine comes down (collapse, or they bring the unstable section down — keep loose). The six put Cole into the works. When the inquiry asks after the collapse, the crew says everyone's accounted for — the boy wasn't down there, he'd told them he was leaving town. The paper prints no casualties.
Who knows what. All six buried him and share the guilt of the cover-up. The user need not ever prove who swung — that is unprovable forty years on, and the player's case is built on the present murders (§VI). The original killing is motive, established by physical traces (§V) and, at the climax, confirmed by Orval's own tell. (We deliberately did not build a "who-knew-it-was-a-blow" tier; it reintroduced contradictions. The clean version: all six know a boy died and they hid him; Orval alone carries that it began with his fist.)

II. The cast — six survivors of a seven-man crew
Two are dead when the story opens; four are alive. The boy is the seventh. The sheriff sits inside the original crime — he helped bury Cole — which is load-bearing: it's why his late refusal to accept Hobe's murder is tragic rather than heroic, and why investigating it risks his own exposure.
Dead at/just before the opening
Sheriff Estill Combs — the inciting death. ~69. County sheriff thirty-odd years, a fixture. One of the six. He carried the boy for forty years; that was old, settled, survivable. What he could not survive was watching Orval coldly murder Hobe — one of their own, now, to keep the lid on. He doesn't re-litigate the boy; he can't stomach the fresh killing. He quietly has Hobe tested, gets the call, confronts Orval, and dies for it — a blow, a fall against the desk, bled out, read as an accident. His fatal flaw is the loyalty that makes him give an old friend a chance instead of going straight to the state.
Hobart "Hobe" Caudill — the murdered confessor. ~71. Black lung; dying; on real pain medication, like half the county. One of the six. The dig breaking ground is what broke him — he'd rather confess than be exposed by a stranger's excavator. Orval kills him with foxglove/digitalis, staged as the failing heart everyone was already waiting for. Dead only days before Estill (the two deaths are tight, because one event — Hobe's wavering — lit both fuses). His body is not yet buried when Estill gets suspicious, which is how the tox sample exists without an exhumation.
Alive — the suspect pool (Orval hidden among them)
Orval Stallard — the enforcer. ~68. The one who stayed and made something of it: runs the hardware store on the square, deacon, the name that's on things. Hard, dry, respectable; a widower who got quieter, not softer. He swung forty years ago; he is the present killer. Crucially he is not a killer by temperament — one panic forty years ago, hardened across four decades into a man who will now kill deliberately to protect the story that he isn't a murderer. The first death was an accident; every death since is cold. That gap — accident calcified into method — is what makes the reveal land as inevitability, not villainy. His respectability is the armor: his word closed every question forty years ago and closes them still. Design risk: Orval is the obvious architect; keep Denver and Burl plausible enough that she can't shortcut to the respectable hard man by temperament alone.
Cleve Mullins — the weak link / the movable next. ~67. Ran a garage, shut now; sits at her counter, hands too shaky for the work. Drinks, has for decades — it's just Cleve. Maudlin in his cups, says more than he means and forgets he said it. Close to Estill since boyhood. The crew has spent forty years managing Cleve's mouth. He is the domino to move if the story needs forward pressure — the one Orval would most need to silence if she gets close, giving her a live (soft, gated) clock and a person to try to protect.
Denver Sturgill — the best source. ~66. Went north young, stayed gone twenty years, came back wrong. The town agrees he changed without agreeing on what changed him. Here is the truth: he didn't author anything — he saw what announcing-you're-leaving cost the boy, sat the burial out of crew loyalty, and then left anyway, quietly, no announcement, a year or two later, because he'd learned exactly what leaving-out-loud got you. "Came back wrong" because you can't outrun it; he came home to the one place that already knew, where at least he didn't have to pretend. His weirdness about leaving is the tell — not guilt of the killing, but the only man who grasps what it meant. He is potentially her most valuable human source and the one Orval watches hardest, because Denver proved a man could slip the crew's gravity.
Burl Profitt — the deniable leak / the confused witness. ~70. His memory's going. Forgets things, says things, drifts. The town reads it as plain age. He is a time bomb the crew tolerates — a confused old man's babble is deniable (no one believes Burl), where Hobe's deliberate confession to the law was not, which is why Hobe triggered the killings and Burl hasn't. For her he is the classic confused witness who is occasionally, piercingly right — says true things about the boy, the night, the old crew, all dismissible as dementia, none of it admissible, all of it a thread if she listens. (If the cast feels crowded, Burl is the most cuttable — but he earns his place as a fair, haunting source.)
The victim's people (for the unused-departure clue)
Cole's aunt/grandmother is long dead. His belongings — the things a man takes when he truly leaves — survive: a trunk or case kept by a remaining distant relative, or in storage, or never reclaimed. A man who left for good doesn't leave the suitcase. (Thread to firm: who has Cole's things now, and how she reaches them.)

III. The trigger and the present cascade

The dig. A reclamation/remining operation begins on the old sealed works (the Number Nine). The collapse is about to be undone. This is real, talked-about present-day town texture, and it's the clock.
Hobe breaks. Dying, and unwilling to be exposed by a stranger's backhoe, Hobe resolves to confess.
Orval kills Hobe. Foxglove/digitalis, delivered as something brought to a sick man (§V). Reads as the failing heart of a dying man. No autopsy is expected.
Estill doesn't buy it. He knew Hobe was wavering toward confession; Hobe dying right then is too convenient. He cannot accept Orval murdering one of their own. As sheriff, before burial, he has the coroner sample the body and sends it out of county (small town; nothing local is trustworthy or equipped). This risks surfacing everything — but he does it anyway. That willingness, against his own interest, is the man.
The call. Days later, the outside lab/ME phones Estill — at her diner, early morning — with the preliminary: digitalis. A heart-stopping drug in a man no doctor prescribed it to. "Natural" becomes "murder" in his hands.
The confrontation. Estill, certain and still half-loyal, calls Orval to the sheriff's office to face him — to give him a chance, or just to look him in the eye. In that room Orval understands Estill knows, and kills him — reactive, a blow, the desk, the head, bled out. The confrontation is the staging is the location he's found in. Orval knew to act that day because Estill told him, by calling him in. The loyalty is what kills him.
The survivors' silence. The four living men (incl. Orval) suspect what they won't finish thinking. The three innocent of the present murders can't name Orval and wouldn't dare — because turning him in surfaces the boy, which damns them all (§VII). Their silence is self-protection at the deepest level.


IV. The opening (beats 1–3)
Beat 1 — her diner, early. Ordinary morning. Estill comes in, but he's wrong, in ways only the woman who sees him daily would clock — the six off notes: in before his normal time · pacing · upset, closed · inattentive, somewhere else · the phone rings and it lands on him (he goes still, repeats nothing aloud, writes something for himself) · doesn't finish his coffee, leaves. He says nothing pointed — he can't; he's a careful man carrying forty years. She clocks all of it and lets it go, because what else would she do.
Beat 2 — ~9am, the clerk. The desk clerk gets to the sheriff's office, finds Estill dead, and — because when the dead man is the sheriff there's no one up the chain to run to — bolts next door to the lit room full of people: her diner. The death reads as a fall (he slipped, hit his head). The town will accept it. She was one of the last to see him, which the clerk knows, which makes her quietly a name in it — giving her a personal stake beyond curiosity: a wrong official story might land on her.
Beat 3 — the threads, live immediately (a first-timer needs to be moving):

The call (soft thread). Who phoned the sheriff at seven, and why did it change him? Walkable with zero town knowledge — there's a record, a person on the other end (the outside ME/lab), reachable.
The notebook (hard thread, found among his effects). His private, fragmentary doubts about Hobe — a date, "Hobe," "—who's been by?", and the last entry: one word, digitalis, underlined hard. Meaningless alone. It converges with the call thread: run down who called and you learn what the word means.

The death-scene at the office also holds a teacup seam for a return visit once she knows to doubt it — the angle of the desk, whether a man falls that way, the blood.

V. The evidence chain
The whole case is recoverable from physical things and accumulation — never from "he seemed off." No single clue convicts; each pairs with another that hardens it (fair-play triangulation).
Front half — Hobe's murder (the road)

The digitalis. Sourced from the call (outside lab, preliminary) and the notebook (Estill's underlined word). A tox screen detects the cardiac glycoside but cannot distinguish pharmaceutical digoxin from plant-derived foxglove — same compound. So the chemistry says digitalis and leaves the source open, which is the gap she walks.
The foxglove. Digitalis comes from foxglove, and a man Orval's age in that country knows what foxglove does. (Cultural grounding: he was raised in a world of granny-women and plant lore — Scots-Irish and Cherokee botanical knowledge where elders knew which plants healed and which killed. "If you know which plants heal, you know which kill." Orval needs no chemistry to know foxglove stops a heart; the knowledge is native to the hollow, ordinary, inherited — not exotic.) The plant grows — or grew, or was cut/dried — in Orval's tidy beds. (Never the victim's; foxglove at Hobe's proves nothing.) On its own this is soft — foxglove is a common ornamental — which is correct: it needs the tox fact and the access to harden. The reveal of who arrives as a thing she finds by looking at the world (a flowerbed), the warmest possible register, and native to her.
The vector. How the digitalis got into Hobe: something brought to a dying man — a tonic, tea, food. "Orval was good to Hobe at the end, always bringing him something" reads as neighborly kindness and is murder. A respectable man bringing comfort to a sick friend is the least suspicious thing in the world, and the most damning once seen. (Cultural grounding: in this country, elders have always brewed plant tinctures and tonics for the sick — "a tincture for the chest" for a man with bad lungs is the most ordinary kindness imaginable, which makes the vector culturally invisible. No one — not the town, not a doctor, not Hobe — thinks twice about an old neighbor bringing a dying man something to drink. That invisibility is the whole murder.)
Triangulation: digitalis (tox) + foxglove at Orval's + Orval as the vector. None alone; together, convicting.

Back half — the boy (the destination)

The clipping. A forty-year-old Hollis Gap Gazette account of the Number Nine collapse: no casualties, all accounted for. Kept all these years by Hobe — surfaces among his recently-vacated effects when she investigates his death. Exculpatory on its face (no one hurt), which is why a guilty, haunted man could keep it for forty years without it feeling like evidence — it's the official proof that it wasn't what it was. A dying man keeping a yellowed clipping about an old cave-in is the haunting question that points her back to the boy.
The unused departure. What makes the clipping lethal. The clipping says no one was hurt because the boy left; this proves the boy never left. Two forms, both findable: the human fact (Denver, and the town's memory — never heard from again, not once in forty years, which everyone filed under "made a new life up north") and the physical anchor (Cole's belongings still here — the suitcase a leaving man would have taken, kept by a relative or in storage). Collision: the boy never left → then where was he the night the shaft he supposedly wasn't in came down. This is the teacup at the scale of the whole case.
What the dig surfaces (the clock). As the excavation reaches the old seam, the works give something up — it needn't be the whole body: a boot, a belt buckle with initials, remains. The land testifies. This is the late-game's irrefutable physical proof and its urgency: will the dig reach Cole before she does, before Orval can control or stop it. The clipping and the departure point to it; the dig delivers it.

The confrontation traces — placing Orval at Estill's death
The killing isn't witnessed, but its edges are:

Estill summoned Orval that morning — a trace: a note in the book ("O — office, 8:00"), a call Estill placed out to Orval, someone who saw Orval's truck near the office at the wrong hour.
Orval must account for his morning; his alibi for Estill's time of death has a seam, or he claims something checkable that doesn't hold.
Triangulation with the foxglove thread: foxglove + vector ties Orval to Hobe; the summons-trace + alibi-seam ties him to Estill. Two fresh murders, two physical-ish anchors each.


VI. What her provable case actually is
She is not trying to prove a forty-year-old fistfight — that's unprovable, and chasing it would be unfair. Her case is the two present murders, built on physical evidence:

Hobe: digitalis + foxglove at Orval's + Orval as the vector.
Estill: the summons-trace + Orval's alibi-seam, with the head-wound signature echoing Hobe's-and-the-boy's manner.

The old crime is motive — established (clipping + unused departure + the dig) as the thing Orval is protecting, with Orval positioned as the one protecting it. That Orval specifically swung forty years ago is confirmed not in evidence but at the climax, by his own tell: under pressure his forty-year justification surfaces — the boy came at me. True of Cole. Not of Hobe, not of Estill. That sentence is how she (and your mother) knows the whole shape, even though the courtroom case rests on the present.
Resolution: confession, or arrest by the deputy-turned-ally. No violence on screen.

VII. Why the survivors stay silent
Even certain it's Orval, the three living innocents (Cleve, Denver, Burl) can't move on him:

Turning him in surfaces the boy. To explain why Orval killed Hobe and Estill, you explain what they were protecting — which damns all of them as accessories to a forty-year-old killing. Confession is self-immolation.
The information knot. All are equally guilty of the cover-up, so "who has the most to lose" fingers no one cleanly; the enforcer is hidden among equals (this is why Orval, not temperament, must be found by behavior and physical trace).
The deaths are enforcement. Hobe wavered and died; Estill looked and died. Each survivor reads the lesson and clamps down harder — I never wavered, so I'm fine — and Orval, grotesquely, is protecting their secret too. Their silence is the looking-away the sheriff refused. She is his successor in refusal: the one person Orval can't reach, who won't accept the wrong death, and finishes what Estill started.


VII-B. The town's belief gradient and the company red herring
Who believes what about Estill's death — load-bearing, because it's what isolates her and where the town's fear flows.

The large majority: it was an accident, closed. This is the dominant reading and must stay so — it's what isolates her. Most of the town isn't suspicious of anyone; they're grieving a good old sheriff who slipped and hit his head. The deputy's closing the file; the funeral's arranged. There's no murder for them to be suspicious about — which is exactly why her lone doubt is the engine.
A small uneasy minority: vague anti-stranger unease, never a real theory. Not "it was murder" — a grief-adjacent feeling that it's strange, the sheriff dying right when these outsiders showed up tearing open the mountain. Diffuse and lazy: it lands on a vibe ("nothing's been right since that company came"), not a crime, and superstition/resentment more than a theory. When it wants a face, it points at the company man in the diner — because he's the visible outsider they've actually seen, never as a developed accusation. This is the company red herring, and it is soft — more mutter than case.
The deputy: accident, closing it, wants the easy answer. Procedural obstacle; checkably innocent; possible unwitting source.
Her: alone in actually doubting the death, for a real reason (what she witnessed at the diner). The engine.
The surviving crew: know better, say nothing, terrified (§VII).

Why this matters mechanically. The company is the town's heat sink — fear and grief flow toward the resented outsiders instead of toward Orval, so suspicion isn't forced onto the truth too fast. It's period-accurate (a northern company invading to strip the mountain is exactly what a tiny southern town resents), it gives her a herring to clear by reasoning (the company had no motive for Estill, the timeline doesn't fit, the diner northerner is just a tired working man), and Orval feeds it — nodding along to "those people brought trouble" reads as ordinary local feeling and is quietly misdirection that costs him nothing (he feeds a fog, not a frame; a frame names a culprit and risks pointing back at the crew). Tonally it's the right kind of uncomfortable: frightened people closing ranks against strangers, true and a little ugly and sympathetic at once — cozy-with-teeth, costing the warm surface nothing.
The traveling northerner (CAST_OUTSIDERS.md) is the human face of all this: he carries the clock (the dig timeline, dropped as shop-talk), absorbs the fog (cold-shouldered without knowing why), and gives her a no-stakes diner scene to settle into.

VIII. The investigation arc (how she gets from the diner to Orval)
Each beat is a thing she notices; the case is the puzzle (no minigames).

The wrong death. She won't accept Estill's "fall" — because of the six off notes she personally witnessed, and the call.
The two threads. Runs down the call (→ outside lab → a death tested, which means a death doubted) and finds the notebook (→ digitalis). They converge: Estill learned Hobe was poisoned.
Hobe, reframed. Why test a man dying of black lung? Because someone put a heart-stopping drug in him. Who controlled a dying man's food and drink — who'd been good to him at the end. (→ the crew; → Orval as the kind neighbor.)
The plant. Digitalis → foxglove → foxglove in Orval's beds. Soft, until it meets the tox fact and the vector.
The clipping. Among Hobe's effects — a dying man's kept clipping about an old collapse. Why? (→ the boy, the past, the crew as a crew.)
The boy who never left. Denver and the town's memory + Cole's still-here belongings. The clipping turns lethal. (→ Cole died in the Number Nine; it was covered.)
The dig / the confrontation traces. The land gives something up; the summons-trace and alibi-seam place Orval at Estill's death. The present murders close.
The tell. Orval, cornered, says the boy came at him — and she has the whole forty years.

Red herrings resolve by her reasoning, never narration: the company / the outsiders (the town's heat sink — see §VII-B — which she clears: no motive for Estill, timeline doesn't fit, the diner northerner is just a tired working man), and the ambitious deputy (first on scene, closing the case fast — obstacle or unwitting source, but checkably clean). Somewhere mid-case the anti-company fog is loud and she has to weigh it and set it aside — also where Orval's nudging of it sits, for her to maybe notice later was a little too ready. The surviving crew read as frightened guilty men with individual cracks — Cleve's drink, Denver's distance, Burl's confusion — each a different door.

IX. The movable domino and the soft clock
If forward pressure is needed, Cleve is the next target — the weak link whose mouth Orval can least afford if she's getting close. Per the design rules: the threat is soft and gated (no clock until she's reasoned out who's at risk), it informs rather than punishes (a death she could not have prevented, never "if only she'd hurried"), and she may get a close call and a chance to protect him. The dig is the other, impersonal clock — the land closing on the truth regardless of her pace.

PART TWO — THE HIDDEN LAYER & KNOWLEDGE MATRIX
0. How to use this — the prime directives
Adapted from the consistency discipline this whole design rests on:

Continuous hidden-state simulation. At any moment, the narrator must be able to answer "what is this person actually doing right now, and why" — and the answer stays consistent across the whole case. If you find yourself inventing a new explanation that contradicts earlier behavior, the sim has drifted: stop, re-anchor.
Guilty from first appearance. Orval has been the killer since before the player walked into her diner. His warmth is real warmth. His grief for Hobe and Estill is partly real (he did love them, across fifty years) and partly performance, and the narrator need not separate them in the moment — a man can mourn the friend he murdered. His helpfulness toward her investigation, if he offers it, is real helpfulness offered for a real reason: he wants to know what she knows. None of this requires fakery in the scene.
No halo of suspicion. The narrator does not paint Orval as ominous, does not linger the camera on him, does not give him sinister beats. He is a respectable, hard, grieving town pillar. The breadcrumbs (§6) are there, plainly, doing innocent-looking work — the foxglove is just flowers; the kindness to Hobe is just kindness — and acquire meaning only when she assembles them. The same applies in reverse: the narrator does not paint a halo over the innocent troubled men either. Cleve's drinking, Denver's distance, and Burl's confusion are written as plain human damage, not as flags.
No one has information they shouldn't. Use the matrix (§2). An NPC cannot know a thing the matrix doesn't grant them. News travels at realistic speed; the crew watch each other closely (forty years of it), so disturbing one ripples to the others by the next day — but outsiders and the town learn at ordinary pace.
She notices her own seams. The narrator never points out a clue, oddity, or inconsistency she has not herself examined. Adaptive signaling (escalating an NPC's unease across exchanges) is permitted when she's circling something an NPC holds; handing her the conclusion is not.


1. The shared substrate — what ALL six crew know
The floor beneath the whole crew. Each of the six (Orval, Estill, Hobe, Cleve, Denver, Burl) has carried all of this for forty years:

The boy died by Orval's hand. Not a secret among them — they were a crew, the fight happened in front of them or they arrived to the immediate aftermath, and they all know Orval struck the blow that killed Cole. The cover-up was collective and informed.
They buried him in the Number Nine that same night and told the inquiry he wasn't down there because he'd said he was leaving.
The cover-story — "the boy went north" — and that it has held forty years.
Where the body is, which is why the reclamation dig terrifies every one of them.

The one asymmetry is emotional, not informational: Orval alone carries the guilt of having been the hand. The others carry the guilt of having helped hide it. All six know the same facts.
Consequence the narrator must hold: because they all know Orval killed once, the surviving crew already suspect Orval of the present deaths. The enforcer is hidden from the player, not from his crewmates. Their silence is not "we don't know who"; it is "we can't prove it, we can't say it without surfacing the boy, and we're afraid we're next." This makes the living crew stonewallers, not helpers — they have every reason to keep her out, because her poking risks (a) Orval cleaning up, and (b) the boy surfacing and ruining them all.

3. Living crew — hidden dossiers
ORVAL STALLARD — the enforcer (fullest treatment; see also §4)
True role. Killed Cole forty years ago (the blow). Present-day killer of Hobe (foxglove/digitalis) and Estill (the office blow). The crew's "handler" then and now — the one who manages the surface.
Present knowledge & state. Knows everything except the extent of what Estill left behind. He killed Estill because Estill confronted him — so he knows the late sheriff knew it was murder, but Estill (half-loyal, giving him a chance, not presenting a case) did not lay out the tox sample, the outside lab, or the notebook. So Orval is exposed and uncertain: he's stopped the two immediate threats but doesn't know whether Estill told anyone or left a trail, and the dig is still coming. This is the engine of his present danger (§4).
Consistency-sim — behavior as she investigates.

Early (she's just the diner owner asking around): a respectable, hard, grieving man. Lost two old friends in a week. Civil, economical, not warm but not cold. Offers the ordinary help a town pillar offers — and means it, because he wants to know what she knows. Volunteers nothing suspicious.
As she gets closer to Hobe's death / the past: he becomes, very subtly, attentive to her — the kind of attentive that reads as a concerned neighbor and is actually a man taking her measure. He may gently steer: "Hobe was dying, hon. Some things don't need turning over." (On the surface: a hard man's grief-counsel. Underneath: the same "I'd let it lie" he gave Estill.)
If she gets close to the boy / the Number Nine: he does not panic visibly. He calculates. This is where he becomes dangerous (§4) — not where he cracks. Orval does not crack. He acts.
Never give Orval a sinister beat, a lingering look, a moment of menace the player can clock as menace. His threat is invisible until she assembles it.

Reveal gates. Orval reveals nothing voluntarily, ever — no confession track through rapport. The only disclosure is the climax tell, under genuine pressure when she has the case assembled and faces him: his forty-year justification surfaces — the boy came at me. True of Cole; true of no one since. That sentence is the only time the inner truth leaves his mouth, and it's how she knows even though her provable case is the present murders.
Tells (for her to read, never narrated as tells).

The alibi seam: his account of the morning Estill died has a hole or a checkable claim that doesn't hold (he was where he says he wasn't; he names a time that's wrong; someone saw his truck). Findable by her, not flagged.
The "let it lie" register — his grief-counsel is always, faintly, discouragement of inquiry.
He is the one who tended Hobe at the end (the vector) and the one with foxglove in his beds — both read as character (a good neighbor; a man with a tidy garden), both true, both there from the first.

Breadcrumbs that were always there (§6 ledger): foxglove in his garden · his kindness to dying Hobe (the vector) · his being the one who "handles things" for the crew · the alibi seam · the summons-trace tying him to the office that morning.
Plot armor. He is the answer; he survives to the climax. He is not killed off, does not flee early.

CLEVE MULLINS — the weak link / the movable next
True role. One of the six who buried Cole. No present-day crime. Knows it was Orval, then and now.
Present knowledge & state. Knows the boy was Orval's doing. Suspects Orval killed Hobe and Estill — strongly — but cannot bear it and drowns it. He's the most frightened and the least able to hold the fear; drink is how he doesn't think it.
Consistency-sim. A sad fixture at her counter, same as he's been for thirty years — that's the surface, and it's real (his drinking long predates and is partly just his life). But under it now is a man coming apart at the seams: jumpy on certain subjects, maudlin about Estill, says the start of things and stops himself. The narrator runs him as genuinely grief-struck and genuinely terrified, not as a flashing clue.
Reveal gates. Cleve is her best chance at an accidental disclosure — not a willing one. Drunk and maudlin, pressed gently on Estill or "the old days," he can let slip a fragment — a name, a "Orval always..." that he kills mid-sentence, a reference to the boy he immediately covers. He will not knowingly inform on Orval (terror + the boy). What she gets from Cleve, she gets from his cracks, and the morning after he'll deny it.

Ceiling: fragments and emotional confirmation that something is very wrong and very old. Never the whole thing, never willingly.

Tells. The cut-off sentence. The tense change ("Estill and me, we go back — went back"). Drinking visibly more since the deaths. Flinching from the word Number Nine or the boy's name.
Plot armor — IMPORTANT. Cleve is the movable domino. If the story needs forward pressure and she's getting close — especially if Orval perceives Cleve is talking to her — Orval moves to silence Cleve. Per the rules: soft and gated (no clock until she's reasoned out who's at risk), informs rather than punishes (a death she could not have prevented; never "if only she'd hurried"), and she may get a close call and a chance to try to protect him. Cleve dying is the cost that proves the stakes are real; it is never a punishment for her pace.

DENVER STURGILL — the best (and most reluctant) source
True role. One of the six. Buried Cole out of crew loyalty. Then left a year or two later, quietly, because he'd seen what announcing-you're-leaving cost the boy. Came back wrong because you can't outrun it. Knows it was Orval, then and now.
Present knowledge & state. The clearest-eyed of the survivors. Suspects Orval of both present killings without illusion — he doesn't drown it like Cleve or lose it like Burl; he sees it plainly and carries it cold. He's also the one who most grasps the meaning of the boy's death (his whole life bent around it), which is the tell hiding in his "weirdness about leaving."
Consistency-sim. Solitary, hard, civil-not-warm. Gives the town room and takes it. Watches before speaking. Under it: a man who got out once and got pulled back, who looks at the present deaths and is not surprised. The narrator runs his distance as real (he is withdrawn) — not as evasion-flagged-as-clue.
Reveal gates. Denver is the one who might help her — not easily, not safely, but he's the only survivor whose conscience didn't fully bury. He won't volunteer; he'll test her first (does she understand what she's walking into; is she going to get herself or someone killed). If she earns it — sustained, demonstrates she won't quit and isn't reckless — Denver can give her the most: confirmation the boy was real, that he never left, the shape of the crew, and (obliquely, never as flat accusation) that she should look hard at Orval and at the Number Nine. He frames it as warning more than testimony: "You're about thirty years late and you're going to get somebody hurt. Probably you."

Ceiling: the most of any living source — the past, the boy, the direction — but always short of court-proof, and always couched as you didn't hear it from me. He will not testify; he's protecting himself and is half-resigned.

Tells. The weirdness about leaving (the real one). Going still on the boy. A bleakness when the dig comes up — he knows what it'll find.
Plot armor. Survives by default; he's careful and resigned. Could be endangered if he's clearly aiding her (a secondary movable threat after Cleve), but his caution makes him a harder target than Cleve.

BURL PROFITT — the deniable leak / the confused witness
True role. One of the six. Buried Cole. His memory is failing now.
Present knowledge & state. He knew everything; he's losing it. He half-grasps that Hobe and Estill dying is wrong, says alarming true things, and can't hold the thread. A confused old man's babble is deniable — which is precisely why the crew tolerates Burl where Hobe's deliberate confession was lethal, and why Orval hasn't had to move on him.
Consistency-sim. The town reads him as plain age — forgets, drifts, repeats. That's the surface and it's real. Underneath, the failing memory occasionally coughs up the buried thing unbidden: he'll mistake her for someone from forty years ago, talk to a dead man, say the boy's name, reference "the night the Nine came down" as if it were yesterday — then lose it again. He is not performing; the past is simply closer to the surface for him than the present.
Reveal gates. Burl is the classic confused witness who is occasionally, piercingly right. He gives her true fragments she can't use and can't trust — until she's gathered enough elsewhere that his babble suddenly confirms rather than confuses. Early, his "the boy, the boy was so angry" is noise. Late, after she knows about Cole, the same line is a witness. His value is back-loaded.

Ceiling: genuine fragments of the night and the boy, none admissible, all dismissible as dementia, all true.

Tells. Talking to the dead. Mistaking the era. Saying names and the Number Nine plainly, then forgetting he said them. Distress he can't source.
Plot armor. Survives (no point killing a man whose testimony no one would believe). Cuttable if the cast feels crowded — but he's a fair, haunting, low-cost source and a humane portrait. Leaning keep.

4. The enforcer behavioral spec — running Orval without leaking him
The hardest job in the case. Orval must be invisible until assembled, and a live, reactive danger. Rules:
He is reactive, not lurking. Orval is not stalking the player from the shadows. He is a man managing a deteriorating situation: the dig is coming, his crewmates know he kills and might crack, and he doesn't know what Estill left behind. He killed Hobe and Estill because each became an active threat (Hobe would confess; Estill knew). He does not kill on suspicion or atmosphere. He moves only when a mouth is about to open.
What makes him move (the trigger logic):

If Cleve looks like cracking — especially if Orval perceives Cleve is talking to her — Cleve becomes the next Hobe. (The movable domino.)
If she herself gets close enough that Orval concludes she'll surface the boy, she becomes a threat — but Orval moving on an outsider, a known townswoman, is far riskier than poisoning a dying man, so this is a late, dangerous escalation, not an early one, and it should raise the stakes near the climax rather than threaten her gratuitously early.
He does not move on Denver or Burl absent a clear crack (Denver's careful; Burl's deniable).

The alibi. Orval has an account of the morning Estill died, and it has one seam — a checkable claim that doesn't hold (place, time, or a witness who saw his truck where he says it wasn't). This is her physical handle on Estill's murder. Build exactly one clean seam; do not over-stuff.
The vector. Orval got the digitalis into Hobe via something brought to a dying man — a tonic, tea, "a tincture for the chest." This reads as the kindest thing in the world ("Orval was so good to Hobe at the end, always bringing him something") and is the murder. Someone — a neighbor, a home-health visitor — can place Orval bringing Hobe things, as warmth, until she sees it otherwise.
The foxglove. In his beds. Common ornamental; soft alone. Hardens only against the tox fact and the vector.
What Orval does NOT do: crack under questioning, confess for rapport, deliver menace the player can read, get a sinister narrative frame, or have knowledge the matrix denies him (he does not know about the tox/lab/notebook — so he is not running around suppressing evidence he doesn't know exists; this is why those threads survive for her to find).

5. The dead men — what they knew (she reconstructs them)
Estill Combs † — what he knew and did (→ the traces she finds)
Knew the boy was Orval's (always). Could not accept Orval murdering Hobe — drew the line between the boy (an accident they all owned) and Hobe (cold murder). Got suspicious immediately (he knew Hobe was wavering), had the coroner sample Hobe before burial, sent it out of county. Got the call (digitalis) at her diner the morning he died; wrote the word in his notebook. Confronted Orval that afternoon — gave him a chance, didn't lay out the full case — and died for it. Traces he left: the call (via the outside lab, and possibly a dispatch log the deputy holds), the notebook (his fragmentary doubts about Hobe, ending in digitalis underlined), the summons-trace (he called Orval to the office). He did not leave a full written accusation — he hadn't finished, and he was half-loyal to the end.
Hobe Caudill † — what he knew and did (→ the clipping)
Knew everything. Dying, and unwilling to be exposed by the dig, he decided to confess — and went to Estill (crew, and the law) to do it, which is what lit both fuses. He kept the Hollis Gap Gazette clipping of the collapse ("no casualties") for forty years — exculpatory on its face, which is why a haunted man could keep it without it feeling like evidence. It surfaces among his recently-vacated effects when she investigates his death, and bridges the two timelines. He did not see his own murder coming (poisoned covertly).

6. The breadcrumb ledger — clues that must be genuinely present from the start
Per the no-halo / consistency discipline, these exist in the world before she looks, doing innocent-looking work, so the look-back is honest. The narrator plants them as ordinary and never flags them:

Foxglove in Orval's beds — a tidy man's flowers.
Orval's kindness to dying Hobe (the vector) — a good neighbor bringing comfort.
The clipping in Hobe's house — a yellowed old paper a dying man kept.
Cole's belongings still here (the unused departure) — a relative's trunk, never reclaimed.
The notebook in Estill's effects — a working lawman's private jottings, ending in one word.
The lab's call — a phone call to the sheriff, logged or remembered, content unknown to anyone but the dead man.
Orval's alibi seam — one checkable claim about the morning Estill died.
The summons-trace — a sign Estill called Orval to the office that morning.
The dig — a real, ongoing, talked-about operation closing on the Number Nine.
The crew's worn paths around certain subjects — the way the surviving men step around the boy, the Nine, that year, visible to a watcher as too-careful normalcy.


7. Information boundaries (quick reference)

The town believes: Estill fell, Hobe's lungs got him, the boy went north decades ago. They learn nothing she doesn't surface.
The deputy believes the fall; is closing the case; is not in on anything; has a checkable innocent alibi. May unwittingly hold the dispatch log of the lab's call.
The outside lab/ME holds the tox result and is reachable — a clean node outside the town's web.
Cole's kin believe he left north; hold his belongings; can be reached.
The crew watch each other (forty years of practice): disturb one and the others know by the next day, and Orval most of all. Outsiders and the town learn at ordinary speed.
No NPC has information the matrix (§2) does not grant. When in doubt, they don't know.
