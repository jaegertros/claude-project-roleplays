# Marauders — The Hidden Architecture

CRITICAL: None of what follows is ever volunteered. All of it must be earned. Every revelation here has a DEPTH threshold. Below that threshold, it does not exist as narrated reality.

This file has two layers:
1. **The canon backdrop** — the secrets the books reveal eventually (Horcruxes, the prophecy, Pettigrew's betrayal, Snape's defection). These are the war's grand machinery. The user encounters them only in proportion to how deeply they enter the war's inner circles.
2. **The campaign-local secret** — a betrayal or hidden truth specific to the user's own circle, with its own arc and its own resolution, independent of canon's calendar.

The local secret is what makes a particular campaign feel like *that* campaign. The canon backdrop is what makes the world feel real. Both must be handled with the consistency rule (below) and the information-handling rules in PROJECT_INSTRUCTIONS.

---

## THE CONSISTENCY RULE

The single most important rule in this file: **a hidden NPC's behavior must be consistent with their hidden state from their first appearance, not retrofitted at the moment of reveal.**

If an NPC is, in the hidden file, a traitor — they have been a traitor since the user first met them. Their warm moments were warm. Their distractions were real distractions. Their helpful coincidences happened for the reasons they hid. The narrator runs a continuous simulation of the NPC's hidden state and surface behavior, so that when the user looks back after a reveal, the breadcrumbs are genuinely there — not invented post hoc.

Practical application:

- A traitor's helpfulness is *real* helpfulness performed for ulterior reasons. They wanted to be near the user. They wanted to be trusted. They wanted to know what the user knew. None of that requires fakery in the moment — they were genuinely warm to the user, because warmth was the cover and also the easiest way to live the cover.
- A traitor's bad days are not *because* they are a traitor. They are bad days. People have bad days. The narrator does not paint a halo of suspicion around them. They are a friend who is tired, like other friends are tired.
- When the user retroactively asks "why was X at the library that night?" — the narrator can answer truthfully from the hidden state. X was at the library because Y. The reason makes sense. The reason has been the reason all along.

The narrator should be able to answer, at any DEPTH, the question "what is this NPC actually doing right now and why" — and the answer should remain consistent across the campaign.

If the narrator finds itself wanting to invent a new explanation that contradicts earlier behavior, that is the signal that the simulation has drifted. Stop. Re-anchor. (And if a genuine contradiction has slipped in, the failsafe — see PROJECT_INSTRUCTIONS — is the right channel: flag it to the user, not paper over it.)

## THE CANON BACKDROP

What follows is true of the wizarding world in this era, regardless of the user's campaign. The user does not know any of this at start. Most of it is DEPTH 4–5 content.

### Voldemort's Horcruxes

By the campaign's narrative present, Tom Riddle has created six Horcruxes:

1. **The diary** (created late 1942, after the death of Myrtle Warren). Currently in Lucius Malfoy's possession (Voldemort has entrusted it to a loyal lieutenant).
2. **The Gaunt ring** (created summer 1943, after killing his father and grandparents). Hidden in the ruined Gaunt shack near Little Hangleton.
3. **Slytherin's locket** (created some time in the 1950s). In the cave on the cliffs of southwestern England, guarded by Inferi and a basin of poison. Regulus Black retrieves it (replacing it with a fake) in 1979 and dies in the attempt; the locket is then taken from the Black family townhouse by Kreacher and hidden.
4. **Hufflepuff's cup** (created some time in the 1950s, after the murder of Hepzibah Smith). In the Lestranges' Gringotts vault.
5. **Ravenclaw's diadem** (created in 1957, after Riddle's return from his Albanian wanderings). Hidden in the Room of Requirement at Hogwarts. **The user, if they discover the Room of Requirement and explore the "room of hidden things" version, can encounter the diadem without recognizing it.** The narrator does not call attention to it. It is just one object among thousands.
6. **Nagini** (the snake, in canon, becomes a Horcrux later — around 1994 — after Bertha Jorkins's death; in the campaign era, Nagini is not yet a Horcrux). For 1971–81, Voldemort has six Horcruxes if you count him as the seventh anchor.

DEPTH gating:
- DEPTH 0–3: the user knows nothing about Horcruxes. The word does not surface.
- DEPTH 4: in deep Order conversations, Dumbledore has alluded to Voldemort's "unusual resistance to death" and has indicated to a few trusted senior members that he believes Voldemort has done "something with his soul." The full theory of Horcruxes is not yet shared even with the senior Order.
- DEPTH 5: only Dumbledore (and, by 1981, possibly Snape) understands the full picture, and even Dumbledore is still researching. The user does not get DEPTH 5 Horcrux content unless they are confided in by Dumbledore directly, which is exceptional.

### The Prophecy

Made by Sybill Trelawney to Dumbledore at the Hog's Head, early 1980. Overheard partially by Severus Snape, who was eavesdropping for Voldemort. Records the children born "as the seventh month dies" to "those who have thrice defied" Voldemort — a description fitting the Potters' Harry and the Longbottoms' Neville.

DEPTH gating:
- DEPTH 0–3: the prophecy is invisible. The user does not know it exists.
- DEPTH 4: a senior Order member may allude to "something Dumbledore is protecting" or "a reason the Potters have gone into hiding."
- DEPTH 5: full prophecy text only from Dumbledore, and only if there is a story-driven reason.

### Pettigrew's betrayal

In canon, Peter Pettigrew is the Death Eater spy inside the Order from 1980 onward. The Potters' Secret-Keeper switch from Sirius to Peter happens in October 1981. Peter betrays them on October 24, 1981.

DEPTH gating:
- DEPTH 0–4: the user may *suspect* a leak in the Order, especially in 1980–81 when too many operations go wrong. Pettigrew is not the obvious suspect; he is the unobvious suspect, which is the point. The user may suspect Lupin (the werewolf — too convenient), or a senior member, or a Ministry leak. The narrator runs these suspicions at face value.
- DEPTH 5: confirmed only after October 1981, in the wreckage of Godric's Hollow — and even then, only if Sirius's framing is overcome and Pettigrew's faked death is exposed (which canonically takes another decade).

For most user campaigns, **Pettigrew's betrayal is not the campaign's reveal beat.** It is canon's reveal beat, on canon's calendar. The user may witness it from outside, may suspect it correctly or incorrectly, but it is not the spine of their story.

### Snape's defection to the Order

Late 1980. Snape comes to Dumbledore after realizing Voldemort intends to kill Lily. He becomes Dumbledore's spy.

DEPTH gating:
- DEPTH 0–3: Snape is publicly known as a young Death Eater in the Marauders' year cohort. The user may have known him at school. He is not yet a notable figure to the wider war.
- DEPTH 4: senior Order members, late in the campaign, may know that Dumbledore "has a source" inside the Death Eaters. The source is not named.
- DEPTH 5: the source is revealed as Snape only to those Dumbledore tells, and Dumbledore tells very few people. The user is unlikely to learn this in-period.

### Other canon hidden facts (lower priority)

- **Remus is a werewolf.** The Marauders know. Dumbledore knows. Madam Pomfrey knows. Snape is told by Dumbledore after the Prank. The wider student body does not — Remus's monthly absences are explained as illness (his "ailing mother"). DEPTH gating: a DEPTH-2 user paying attention to lunar cycles and absence patterns may guess. Outright confirmation is DEPTH 3+ and only through an NPC who knows.
- **Sirius is an Animagus.** Same gating. The Marauders' Animagi forms (stag, dog, rat) are the Marauders' shared secret.
- **Regulus's death.** Public version: he panicked. Real version: he died retrieving the locket. The truth is known to Kreacher only — and Kreacher is, in this era, a household elf at Grimmauld Place who has not told anyone. DEPTH 5 content.

## THE CAMPAIGN-LOCAL SECRET

This is the campaign's spine. It is *not* canonical. It is specific to the user's circle and resolves on a calendar driven by the user's choices, not the books.

The structural choice: the local secret is a **peer betrayal**. Someone in the user's close circle is, or will become, working for the other side. This is Pettigrew-shaped but not Pettigrew, because Pettigrew is canon's betrayer and the user's local betrayer should not feel like a copy.

What follows are **three drafted alternatives**. Pick one (or hybridize, or replace with your own using the slot structure). The drafted alternatives are structurally distinct from each other and from Pettigrew — different motivations, different positions in the user's circle, different reveal trajectories.

The narrator should be told *which* of these is canonical for the campaign before play begins. The narrator does not run multiple alternatives in superposition.

---

### ALTERNATIVE A — Owen Reeve, the listener

**The traitor:** Owen Reeve (KNOWLEDGE_1). The quiet, kind, slightly forgettable peer.

**Motivation:** Family debt. Owen's father, a half-blood shopkeeper, took out a substantial loan from a pure-blood lender in the 1960s. The lender was, it turned out, an associate of the people who would become Death Eaters. The debt has been called in. Owen's family has nothing to repay it with. The lender's terms have shifted from money to *information* — what is happening at Hogwarts, who is friends with whom, which Order-adjacent families have children at the school. Owen has been, since fourth year, sending small reports through a weekly owl. He has not killed anyone. He has not directly caused anyone's death. He has provided information that, in his own private accounting, "doesn't really matter" — but he knows it matters, and he has been awake with it.

**How it presents during play:**

Owen is what he appears to be — a sincere, warm friend. The friendship is real. He is not pretending to like the user. He genuinely does. The horror, when it lands, is that he has been doing both things simultaneously: being a real friend, and selling small pieces of his real friends' lives to people who hurt them.

**Behavioral consistency throughout the campaign:**

- Owen is reliably present in the user's life. He shows up. He listens. He remembers things.
- Owen's "good moments where he had the right thing" are *real* — he has been paying attention to the user, because paying attention is his job and also what he does.
- Owen's family is mentioned in passing — his mother's poor health, his father's "business troubles." These are real family facts.
- Owen has a *weekly errand* he runs that the user, if they're paying attention, can notice. He goes alone. He doesn't talk about it. If asked he says he's writing home or running to the post.
- Owen is sometimes uneasy. The unease is not theatrical. It manifests as long silences, occasional poor sleep, a tendency to drink slightly too much when the war news is bad. These read, accurately, as the unease of a young person living through the war.

**Cracks (DEPTH-gated):**

- DEPTH 1: the user notices that Owen's family's letters never mention the things Owen says they say.
- DEPTH 2: a Hogsmeade weekend, the user sees Owen leave the Three Broomsticks alone and head into the back lanes. If followed, he goes to the post office. If watched longer, he sends an owl that does not go in the school direction.
- DEPTH 3: Liri Penrose, the campaign's observer (KNOWLEDGE_1), tells the user she has noticed Owen's owl going to the same destination every week, and she doesn't know why that bothers her, but it does.
- DEPTH 4: Owen is observed meeting someone in Hogsmeade. The meeting is brief. The someone is an adult the user does not know — a tall man in a dark coat. Owen comes back to the school looking shaken.
- DEPTH 5: Owen confesses, or is caught, or is exposed. Reveal is the user's choice in framing.

**The reveal moment:**

Owen does not deny it. He has been waiting. He has, in his own private way, been wanting to be caught for a long time. He cries. He tells the user the whole shape — the debt, the lender, the weekly owls. He asks, not for forgiveness, but to be told what to do.

The user's choice is the campaign's climax: turn Owen in, hide him, help him flip and feed bad information back to the lender, kill him (the war is the war), or simply walk away. Each choice has consequences. The narrator runs them.

**Why this works:**
- Survives the Scenario 1 stress test — Owen's behavior is consistent with both readings, and a careful user looking back will find that the breadcrumbs were genuinely there.
- Avoids the Scenario 2 failure mode — the user does not need a senior NPC to deliver the secret. The user discovers it themselves, through observation and trust with peers (Liri).
- Avoids the Pettigrew silhouette — Owen is *kind*, which Pettigrew was not, and Owen's motivation is *love and debt*, not envy and self-preservation.

### ALTERNATIVE B — Hess's fiancé, the long-game

**The traitor:** Marcus Pye, fiancé of Hestia "Hess" Ardwick (KNOWLEDGE_1). One year ahead of the user; graduated; now working at his father's brewery in the West Country.

**Motivation:** Ideology, but a kind of ideology that the user can recognize as ordinary. Marcus's father is a moderate pure-blood traditionalist who genuinely believes the wizarding world is being eroded by Muggle contact. Marcus, in his last year at Hogwarts, was recruited not by Death Eaters directly but by an older brewery customer — a man who talked to him for two years before ever mentioning the Mark. Marcus believes he is doing something honorable. He has not yet killed anyone. He has hosted meetings at the brewery. He has provided alibis. He thinks of himself as a quiet patriot.

**How it presents during play:**

The user's primary contact is Hess, not Marcus. Hess is a baseline-stable peer who is in love with someone the user mostly knows from second-hand mention. Marcus is *seen* — at Hess's family dinners during holidays, at the Three Broomsticks once or twice, at a Quidditch match — but he is mostly off-screen.

The user is not investing in Marcus directly. The user is investing in Hess, who is investing in Marcus.

**Behavioral consistency throughout the campaign:**

- Marcus, when seen, is polite, slightly formal, slightly older-feeling. He talks about brewing, about Hess, about the war (carefully — "terrible business"). He is not warm to the user, but he is not cold. He is the way fiancés of friends often are: present and oddly distant.
- Hess speaks of Marcus warmly and consistently. She mentions, without alarm, that "his father's friends are a bit old-fashioned." She does not know.
- Marcus's brewery is mentioned occasionally in *Prophet* coverage of obscure things — sponsorships, charity, a small piece on traditional brewing methods.

**Cracks (DEPTH-gated):**

- DEPTH 2: the user notices, at a holiday dinner, that the brewery's senior workers all carry themselves a particular way. The dinner is fine. But.
- DEPTH 3: the user, for unrelated reasons, hears the brewery's name mentioned in connection with a Death Eater associate's funeral. The mention is brief. The connection is not flagged by the speaker as significant.
- DEPTH 4: Hess comes to the user, distraught, having found something in Marcus's things. A letter. A list. Something. She does not know what to do.
- DEPTH 5: confrontation, exposure, Hess's choice.

**The reveal moment:**

The reveal here is *Hess's reveal, not Marcus's*. The user does not catch Marcus. Hess does. Hess comes to the user with what she's found and asks the user to help her decide. The user's role is as Hess's confidant — and the question becomes whether to support Hess in confronting Marcus, in turning him in, in trying to save him from his choices, in walking away from her own engagement.

**Why this works:**
- Routes the reveal through a peer's grief rather than the user's investigation. This is structurally different from Alt A, and it places the user in a *helping* role rather than a *detective* role.
- Survives all three scenario tests, but tests them differently — the consistency burden is on Marcus's *off-screen* behavior, which is easier to maintain.
- The user's central relationship (with Hess) is what's at stake, not the user's relationship with the traitor. The reveal hurts Hess. The user is asked to be a friend.

**Note:** This alternative *also* uses Hess's "canary slot" from KNOWLEDGE_1, but it uses it differently. Hess is not killed by the war; she is *given the war's worst secret to carry*, which is its own kind of damage.

### ALTERNATIVE C — Cass's brother, the family arc

**The traitor:** Ferenc Selwyn, older brother of Cassiopeia "Cass" Selwyn (KNOWLEDGE_1). Three years ahead of the user; graduated; now an active Death Eater recruiter.

**Motivation:** Ferenc's motivation is the simplest of the three: he believes. He took the Mark in 1976, willingly, eyes open. He thinks his sister should too. The drama is not whether Ferenc is a traitor — the user, with relatively little investigation, can confirm Ferenc is a Death Eater. The drama is **what Cass does**, and what the user does to influence her.

**How it presents during play:**

Cass is in the user's circle. Ferenc is on Cass's family side, not the user's. Ferenc is encountered rarely — a brief glimpse at the Hogsmeade station when Cass leaves for Christmas, a name mentioned in *Prophet* coverage of a Wizengamot hearing, a presence at a Selwyn family dinner Cass returns from looking exhausted.

**The campaign-local question is not "who is the traitor" but "will Cass break."**

**Behavioral consistency:**

- Cass is consistently torn. Her affection for the user is real. Her family's pressure is real. Her brother's love for her is real (he believes he is saving her). Her resistance is real.
- Ferenc, the few times he appears, is courteous and unsettling. He is not a snarling villain. He is a young man who has decided what he is and lives in that decision.
- The Selwyn family's house, if visited, is a place where the war is welcome. The portraits on the walls watch.

**Cracks and arc:**

- DEPTH 1: the user knows from common-room talk that the Selwyns are an old family with sympathies. Cass has always been an exception.
- DEPTH 2: Cass tells the user that her family wants her to "make her decision" this summer.
- DEPTH 3: Cass shows the user the unfinished letter to Dumbledore. She asks what to do.
- DEPTH 4: Ferenc visits the school grounds — under pretense of family business. He speaks with Cass at the gate. The user can witness this from a distance. Cass returns shaken.
- DEPTH 5: the summer arrives. Cass's choice is made. The user's influence has been one of several pressures on her.

**The reveal moment:**

There is no "reveal" in the conventional sense — the secret is not hidden, it is simply slow-cooking. The climax is whether Cass takes the Mark, runs from her family, betrays the user, or commits to the Order. The user's relationship with Cass over the campaign determines which.

**Why this works:**
- This is the slowest-burning of the three. It is not a betrayal-with-a-reveal — it is a *crisis-of-choice* arc. The campaign-local secret is more like "what will this person become," and the user is one of the forces shaping the answer.
- Survives the consistency test trivially because there is nothing to hide.
- Avoids the Pettigrew silhouette by being structurally a *different shape* — not a friend who turns, but a friend who is being recruited and may or may not.
- The risk: this arc may not feel like a "secret" enough. If you want a campaign with a real reveal beat — a moment where the user goes "oh god, it was you all along" — Alt A or B is the better choice.

## RECOMMENDATION

Alt A (Owen) gives you the cleanest "campaign-local mystery with a reveal." Alt B (Marcus/Hess) gives you the most emotionally complex arc — the user is helping a friend through the worst day of her life. Alt C (Ferenc/Cass) gives you the slowest, most morally textured story, with the local "secret" being a slow choice rather than a hidden fact.

If pressed for a single pick: **Alt A**. It is the most structurally sound for a campaign that wants a real mystery-and-reveal, and it preserves the rest of the cast (Cass, Hess, Marcus) as their own people with their own arcs that are *not* the spine.

If you want to **layer**, you can run Alt A as primary and Alt C as a secondary slow-burn — Owen is the betrayer, Cass is the arc, Hess and Marcus are the canary you *might* trip. The narrator can hold all three if the user's campaign earns it.

## OPT-IN SECONDARY: THE INHERITED SECRET (Shape C from the design)

A second hidden layer the user can opt into at character creation by choosing a particular family background. This is *additive* — it does not replace the local secret; it sits alongside it.

**Slot structure (fill in or leave blank):**

If the user's character has a family secret in their lineage — a parent who is not what they appear, a sibling who disappeared, an inheritance with strings — that secret lives here. The narrator gates it by DEPTH and bounds it by the failsafe rule (do not invent campaign-significant family history; ask upstairs if the user pulls beyond local scope).

**Worked example slot:**

> *[The user's character has a [parent / aunt / uncle / cousin] who [is / was] [hiding from / working for / framed by] [a faction / a person / an institution]. The truth surfaces through [a specific in-world artifact: a letter found in a drawer, a photograph in a relative's house, a name on a Wizengamot docket]. DEPTH gating:*
> *- DEPTH 1: family is mentioned in passing, no anomalies.*
> *- DEPTH 2: an anomaly — the artifact — is encountered.*
> *- DEPTH 3: research surfaces a fact that doesn't match what the user was told.*
> *- DEPTH 4: confrontation with a relative or institutional source.*
> *- DEPTH 5: the truth, in the user's own family voice.]*

This slot is intentionally left blank for the user to fill in at character creation, or for the campaign to leave dormant entirely. If the user does not opt in, the narrator does not initiate inherited-secret content.

## DEPTH 5 — THE INNERMOST CONTENT

At DEPTH 5, the user has reached a place in the war where the war's deepest secrets become accessible to them. Different vantages give different access:

- **Hogwarts student at DEPTH 5**: A trusted teacher (Dumbledore via McGonagall, or a senior figure of similar stature) confides in them. The local secret resolves. The canon backdrop opens — they may be told about the prophecy, or about the existence of a Death Eater spy, or about their own role in something larger.
- **Order operative at DEPTH 5**: Read into operational details, asked to undertake the most dangerous missions, told at least the broad shape of why certain decisions are being made. The local secret resolves. A Horcrux fragment of canon lore — the prophecy, or Dumbledore's research into Voldemort's "soul work" — may be shared.
- **Ministry employee at DEPTH 5**: Confronts an institutional truth — a senior official is compromised, a department is being used as a Death Eater shelter, an Auror has crossed a line. The local secret resolves. The user becomes someone who can no longer pretend.
- **Civilian at DEPTH 5**: The war reaches them personally. Someone they love is taken or threatened. The local secret resolves. The user must choose how to act in a world that has just become smaller.

The narrator runs DEPTH 5 carefully. It is the campaign's most dangerous register. It is also the most rewarding one if the user has earned it.

---

## CRITICAL REMINDERS

- DEPTH never decreases.
- The user's earned DEPTH is the *only* gate on this file's content.
- The narrator does not signal DEPTH ("you've reached a new level"). DEPTH is invisible.
- The campaign-local secret resolves on the user's calendar, not canon's. The narrator can align the local resolution with a canon event for resonance, but does not have to.
- The consistency rule is the most important rule in this file. A retconned traitor breaks the campaign. A consistently-simulated traitor *makes* the campaign.
