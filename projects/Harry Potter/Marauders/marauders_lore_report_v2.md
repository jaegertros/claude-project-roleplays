# Marauders-Era Harry Potter Lore & TTRPG Reference Report

**Purpose:** Reference compendium for narrative LLM-style roleplay set in the Marauders era (~1970s). Structured for use as a lorebook, system prompt scaffold, or character card injection. Each major section is self-contained and can be chunked into individual lorebook entries with appropriate keyword triggers.

**Era anchor:** September 1971 (Marauders begin Hogwarts) → October 31, 1981 (Voldemort's first defeat).

---

## 1. Era Timeline & Atmosphere

### The shape of the decade

The 1970s in wizarding Britain are defined by escalating dread. The First Wizarding War officially begins in 1970, when Voldemort drops his low-profile rebuilding phase and openly declares himself. The wizarding world's attempts to keep the violence contained from Muggles are increasingly failing. By the mid-70s, "He Who Must Not Be Named" has become the dominant euphemism — the Taboo on his actual name doesn't exist yet (that's a Second War invention), but social superstition already enforces silence.

Key atmospheric notes for RP:
- **Public mood**: Paranoia, suspicion, doubled-up Apparition wards on homes, families withdrawing children from Hogwarts mid-term, the *Daily Prophet* publishing increasingly long obituary columns.
- **Ministry mood**: Disarray. Minister Eugenia Jenkins was ill-prepared for wartime; Harold Minchum followed with a hardline stance (more Dementors at Azkaban, harsher controls). Bartemius Crouch Sr. eventually authorizes Aurors to use Unforgivable Curses against suspected Death Eaters.
- **Hogwarts mood**: A "single island of safety in a sea of chaos." Voldemort never dares attack while Dumbledore is Headmaster, but students lose family members regularly. The Daily Prophet is read at breakfast with apprehension.

### Year-by-year skeleton (Marauders Hogwarts years)

| Year | Marauders Year | Major Events |
|------|---|---|
| 1971 | 1st | Sept 1: James, Sirius, Remus, Peter sorted into Gryffindor. Lily into Gryffindor, Snape into Slytherin. The boys begin to suspect Remus's "illnesses" are lycanthropy. |
| 1972–73 | 2nd–3rd | Marauders begin secret Animagus research. Voldemort's profile rising publicly. |
| 1974–75 | 4th–5th | Animagus transformations achieved (Year 5). Marauder's Map drafted. Sirius runs from Grimmauld Place to the Potters'. |
| 1975–76 | 5th | "Snape's Worst Memory" — OWLs end with James publicly humiliating Snape; Lily ends her friendship with Snape over the slur. The "Prank" — Sirius lures Snape toward the Whomping Willow on a full moon; James stops Snape from being killed by Remus. |
| 1976–77 | 6th | Remus made prefect. Order of the Phoenix is recruiting actively. |
| 1977–78 | 7th | Final year. James and Lily begin dating in earnest. Most members of the Order are this graduating cohort or recent grads. |
| 1978–81 | Post-Hogwarts | Active Order operations. Prewett brothers, McKinnons, Bones family killed. Prophecy made (early 1980). Harry born July 31, 1980. Neville born July 30, 1980. Fidelius cast on Potters Halloween 1981; betrayed within a week. |

### Key dates for RP anchoring

- **Voldemort's open declaration**: ~1970 (the war's "official" start, though Death Eater attacks started earlier)
- **Order of the Phoenix founded**: 1970–71 (sources vary; Dumbledore convenes it as Voldemort's threat becomes undeniable)
- **Giant rebellion / "Invasion of Britain"**: Mid-late 70s — Voldemort allies with Giants; Aurors fight Giant-fronted incursions until the Giants retreat into hiding
- **Karkaroff's plea bargain**: Late war; Igor Karkaroff names Death Eaters in exchange for release from Azkaban
- **Rosier and Wilkes killed**: Late war, by Aurors (Moody finishes Evan Rosier after Rosier blows part of Moody's nose off)
- **Bones family massacre**: Edgar Bones, his wife, and children all killed
- **Prewett brothers killed**: Fabian and Gideon Prewett die fighting *five* Death Eaters including Antonin Dolohov ("fought like heroes" — Moody)
- **McKinnon family massacre**: Voldemort kills Marlene McKinnon's family personally
- **Caradoc Dearborn disappears**: ~6 months after the famous Order group photo. Body never found.
- **Regulus Black dies**: 1979, in the cave with the locket Horcrux. Officially "panicked and tried to back out of being a Death Eater"; reality is he switched the locket for a fake.

## 2. Magic System & Spells

### How magic works in the wizarding world

Magic is innate — you either have it or you don't. Wandless magic is possible but unreliable for most British wizards (Africa's Uagadou specializes in wandless, but at Hogwarts wandless is "accidental magic" or expert-only). Spells require:
1. **Incantation** (verbal — most spells; nonverbal casting is taught starting in 6th year)
2. **Wand movement** (specific gestures; sloppy wand work produces sloppy results — see Ron's "Wingardium Leviosa")
3. **Intent** (the caster's will and emotional state — critical for the Unforgivables, which require *meaning* the curse)

### Spell categories (canon classifications)

- **Charms** — Add properties to objects/people (Wingardium Leviosa, Lumos, Expelliarmus, Stupefy, Protego). Catch-all for "what something *does*"
- **Transfiguration** — Change what something *is* (matchstick → needle, animal → goblet, conjuration of objects, vanishment). Five subtypes: Transformation, Vanishment, Conjuration, Untransfiguration, Switching
- **Hexes** — Minor dark spells; nuisance-level harm (Bat-Bogey Hex, Stinging Hex)
- **Jinxes** — Lighter still; trip jinxes, jelly-legs
- **Curses** — Serious dark magic; Cruciatus, Imperius, Avada Kedavra are *Unforgivable* — life sentence in Azkaban for use on a human
- **Counter-curses / counter-jinxes** — DADA staple
- **Healing magic** — Episkey, Vulnera Sanentur (Snape's invention), bezoars, dittany

### Iconic Marauders-era spell repertoire

For RP flavor, the spells most often referenced in the era:
- **Levicorpus** (nonverbal) — Snape's invention, hangs target upside-down by the ankle. Was a fad among 5th-year students in 1976.
- **Liberacorpus** — counter to Levicorpus
- **Sectumsempra** — Snape's invention, "for enemies." Slashing curse that opens deep wounds.
- **Muffliato** — Snape's invention; fills nearby ears with buzzing so conversations can't be overheard
- **Petrificus Totalus** — full body-bind
- **Expelliarmus** — disarming charm; James and Lily's signature
- **Patronus Charm** — fully corporeal Patronus is rare and graduate-level. James's Patronus is a stag (matches his Animagus form); Lily's is a doe; Snape's is a doe (Lily's mirror); Dumbledore's is a phoenix.

### Limits of magic ("Gamp's Five Exceptions")

Per Gamp's Law of Elemental Transfiguration, magic *cannot*:
1. Bring back the dead (truly — the Resurrection Stone is a tease, not a reversal)
2. Create true love (Amortentia is obsession, not love)
3. Conjure food from nothing (you can summon, multiply, or transform existing food, but not create it ex nihilo)
4. Create money that lasts (Leprechaun gold vanishes)
5. Confer permanent youth/immortality without horrific cost (Philosopher's Stone, Horcruxes)

### Apparition and Floo

- **Apparition**: Licensed at age 17. Lessons offered at Hogwarts in 6th year (February). The "Three D's": Destination, Determination, Deliberation. Failure causes splinching.
- **Floo Network**: Ministry-regulated; powders, fireplaces, clear enunciation. Eavesdropping by lurking in fires is common.
- **Portkeys**: Ministry-issued for groups; can be set to delayed activation
- **Anti-Apparition wards**: Hogwarts grounds are warded against Apparition (a fact the Marauders find inconvenient and, naturally, work around with the Map)

## 3. Wandlore

### The basics

A wand is a "composite of its wood, its core, and the experience and nature of its owner." Three pillars; any can outweigh the others.

**The wand chooses the wizard** — wands are quasi-sentient. They form allegiances. They can be won (by disarming or defeating their owner), and the new owner gains some or all of the wand's loyalty. A borrowed wand always works less well than a wand of one's own.

### Wand cores (Ollivander's "Supreme Three")

| Core | Personality | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Unicorn hair** | Most consistent magic, hardest to turn to Dark Arts, most faithful to first owner. Prone to "melancholy" if mishandled — the hair can "die" and need replacing. Less powerful than the others. | RP hook: a unicorn-hair wand "dying" is a great character beat. |
| **Dragon heartstring** | Most power, most flamboyant spells, learns fastest. Most prone to accidents, easiest of the three to turn to Dark Arts. Bonds strongly but can change allegiance if won. | The "ambition" core. |
| **Phoenix feather** | Greatest range of magic, slowest to reveal it. Most independent — can act of own accord. Picky about owners. | Rarest. Phoenix has to consent to give the feather. |

Lesser cores Ollivander's father used (and which still appear with non-Ollivander wandmakers): kelpie hair, kneazle whisker, dittany stalk, troll whisker, veela hair (temperamental — Ollivander refuses to use them), thestral tail hair (the Elder Wand's core; can only be mastered by one who has faced death).

### Wand woods (selected, era-relevant)

This is *deeply* characterizing for an RP — Ollivander's notes treat each wood like a personality test. The most-cited:

- **Holly** — Protective; chooses owners on dangerous/spiritual quests; difficult to pair with phoenix feather. (Harry's wand.)
- **Yew** — Power over life and death; chooses ambitious and notorious owners; never timid. Poisonous tree, poisonous symbolism. (Voldemort's wand.)
- **Elder** — Ill luck. "Only a highly unusual person will find their perfect match in elder, and on the rare occasion when such a pairing occurs, I take it as certain that the witch or wizard in question is marked out for a special destiny." (The Elder Wand.)
- **Vine** — Seeks owners with hidden depth and surprising passion. (Hermione's wand.)
- **Willow** — Healing; chooses insecure owners with great unrealized potential. (Ron's second wand; Lily's wand.)
- **Mahogany** — Good for transfiguration. (James's wand — 11", pliable.)
- **Cherry + Dragon heartstring** — Ill-advised pairing for anyone lacking exceptional self-control.
- **Acacia** — Subtle, poor for "bangs-and-smells" magic. Often refuses to perform for any but its true owner.
- **Apple** — Mixes poorly with the Dark Arts. Long-lived owners.
- **Alder** — Best wand for nonverbal spells.
- **Fir** — "The Survivor's Wand"; particularly good for Transfiguration; demanding; chooses focused owners.
- **Hawthorn** — Conflicted nature; equally suited to Dark Arts and healing charms. (Draco's wand — pre-allegiance shift.)
- **Hazel** — Sensitive; reflects owner's emotional state; can absorb magic from its surroundings if neglected.
- **Laurel** — "Cannot perform a dishonourable act"; can issue a spontaneous lightning strike if someone tries to steal it.
- **Maple** — Travelers and explorers; magic grows lacklustre in stay-at-home owners.
- **Oak (English)** — "Wand of strength"; demands a wielder of strong intuition and physical presence.
- **Beech** — Wise owners only; performs poorly for the narrow-minded.
- **Elm** — Old elm-wand families spread the lie that elm only chose purebloods (false — elm just likes dignified, presence-having owners). (Lucius Malfoy's wand.)
- **Rowan** — Powerful protective magic; "no Dark wizard has ever owned a rowan wand."
- **Ebony** — Combative magic; suits those with strong courage of conviction (good or ill).
- **Cedar** — Loyal, perceptive owners; "never trifle with the owner of a cedar wand."
- **Redwood** — Lucky owners who fall on their feet.

### Wand dimensions

Length 7"–16"+. Flex: springy → flexible → supple → rigid/unyielding. Flexibility roughly tracks adaptability/openness; rigidity tracks principle/stubbornness.

### Marauders-era wandmakers

- **Garrick Ollivander** — London (Diagon Alley). The dominant British wandmaker by this era.
- **Gregorovitch** — Continental wandmaker, fading reputation; once held the Elder Wand. Killed by Voldemort in 1997 (Second War).
- **Shikoba Wolfe** (Americas) — phoenix feather specialist
- **Violetta Beauvais** (Americas) — rougarou hair specialist
- **Johannes Jonker** (Africa)

### Marauders-era wands

Sources are tiered: **book canon** (Rowling/Pottermore explicit) > **prop canon** (film props with merchandise documentation, e.g. Noble Collection, Elbenwald) > **film visual** (the prop itself but no published spec) > **fan headcanon**. Mark accordingly when injecting into a card.

- **James Potter** *(book canon)*: Mahogany, 11", pliable. "Excellent for transfiguration."
- **Lily Evans** *(book canon)*: Willow, 10¼", swishy. "Nice wand for charm work."
- **Remus Lupin** *(book canon)*: Cypress, 10¼", pleasantly springy. Cypress chooses self-sacrificing owners — those willing to die a heroic death.
- **Severus Snape** *(prop canon, not book)*: Ebony with phoenix feather core, ~13.5–14". Matte black, slender, taper to the tip; unpolished handle decorated with Celtic-style rune carvings. *Per Noble Collection / Elbenwald merchandise documentation; no Rowling source.* Symbolic fit: ebony is "combative magic, suits those with strong courage of conviction" — works for either his Death Eater years or his Order years. Phoenix feather + ebony is a powerful, picky pairing — appropriate for someone with a divided life.
- **Sirius Black** *(film prop, no published spec)*: Film prop is medium-brown, organic/twisting texture with square rune-like carvings at the base that mirror his prison tattoos; ~15". Wood and core not canonical.
  - **Plot note**: Sirius doesn't have a wand for most of book 3 (just escaped Azkaban). He acquires a new one between books 3 and 5 — likely Order-procured. The film prop breaks when Bellatrix kills him at the Department of Mysteries.
- **Peter Pettigrew** *(canonically unknown)*: Original wand never described. After his rat years, in *Goblet of Fire*, Voldemort has Ollivander forge him a replacement (canon detail), but it's described as a stopgap. Post-betrayal-reveal he uses Voldemort's borrowed wands or unspecified replacements.

### Era-relevant wand plot mechanics

A few wand-mechanics points that come up specifically in Marauders-era RP:

- **Wand procurement during wartime is constrained.** Ollivander is the only major British wandmaker. Order operatives going underground or escaping Azkaban can't just walk into Diagon Alley. Replacement wands come through the Order, the black market (Knockturn / Borgin & Burkes), or inheritance from dead family members. This is a *huge* RP texture lever — a character's wand provenance reveals their network.
- **A "borrowed" wand always works less well than your own.** Useful for nerfing PCs who lose their wand mid-scene without removing them from play.
- **Wand allegiance shifts via "proper winning"** — disarming or defeating in a duel where real power is at stake. Casual dueling-club losses don't transfer allegiance. This is how the Elder Wand changes hands across the saga.
- **Muggles can't wield wands productively.** The core actively rebels — the wand will violently repel a non-magical user, and the core itself can shrivel and die from the attempted misuse. Useful gotcha if a Muggle-protective character tries to arm a Muggle relative.

## 3.5. Potions & Alchemy

Potions are the secondary magic system — buffs, debuffs, narrative solutions that don't depend on immediate wand proficiency. Crucially, **potions take time** (hours to months), unlike spells (seconds). Polyjuice takes a month. Felix Felicis takes six months. This is a strategic-planning lever for RP — you don't brew Veritaserum in a panic.

### Iconic potions (era-relevant)

| Potion | Effect | RP application |
|---|---|---|
| **Amortentia** | Most powerful love potion; doesn't create love but a dangerous obsession. Smells different to each person — what they're most attracted to. | Social manipulation, coercion, sabotage. The "smell" reveal is a classic character beat. |
| **Veritaserum** | Tasteless, odorless; compels truth. Three drops is enough. Ministry-regulated. | Interrogation, mystery resolution, forced confessions. |
| **Draught of Living Death** | Potent sleeping draught; magical suspended slumber that mimics death. | Assassinations, faking deaths, deep subterfuge. |
| **Polyjuice Potion** | Transforms the drinker into another person for ~1 hour. Requires hair/nail of the target. Month-long brew. Doesn't work for cross-species. | Infiltration. Marauders era: Death Eater impersonation plots, Order spy work. |
| **Felix Felicis** | "Liquid luck." Six-month brew, dangerous in excess. Drinker has a perfect day. | One-shot narrative I-WIN button; should be rare. |
| **Wolfsbane Potion** | Allows a werewolf to retain human mind during transformation. **NOT YET INVENTED in the Marauders era** — Damocles Belby invents it in the 1980s. Remus suffers fully through every transformation in the 70s. | Critical era-specific gotcha. Don't let Remus have this. |
| **Elixir to Induce Euphoria** | Irrational happiness. | Counter-Dementor effect, depression treatment. |
| **Pepperup Potion** | Cures common cold, warms drinker, smoke from ears. | Quick recovery, minor ailment management. |
| **Skele-Gro** | Regrows missing bones. Excruciating, takes a night. | Quidditch injuries, dueling damage. |
| **Ageing Potion** | Temporarily ages drinker. | Disguise, age-line bypass. |
| **Cure for Boils** | First-year level. Add porcupine quills before removing from fire = explosion. | Introductory crafting, classroom mishaps. |
| **Doxycide** | Paralyzes Doxies. | Pest control. |

### Common ingredients (apothecary inventory)

**Flora**: Aconite (a.k.a. Wolfsbane/Monkshood — same plant, three names; Snape's first-day question to Harry), Asphodel, Belladonna, Bubotuber (its pus is acne treatment), Dittany (rapid wound healing — a Marauders-era staple given the war), Fluxweed, Hellebore, Knotgrass, Lovage, Sneezewort, Sopophorous beans, Valerian sprigs, Wormwood.

**Fauna**: Acromantula venom, Armadillo bile, Ashwinder eggs (highly flammable unfrozen), Bezoars (universal antidote stones from goat stomachs — Snape's first-day question to Harry, second one), Bicorn horn, Billywig parts, Boomslang skin (Polyjuice ingredient), Doxy eggs, Dragon blood, Dragon liver, Flobberworm mucus (standard thickener), Jobberknoll feathers, Lacewing flies, Puffer-fish eyes, Salamander blood, Scarab beetles, Unicorn hair/horns.

For the Marauders era specifically, **dittany** matters — it's the field-medic potion ingredient an Order operative would carry. Sirius's leftover stash shows up in canon during the Second War.

## 4. Hogwarts: Houses, Curriculum, Geography

### The Houses (era-specific notes)

| House | Founder | Element | Marauders-era flavor |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gryffindor** | Godric Gryffindor | Fire | Disproportionately Order-aligned. Lions in the snake's mouth as the war heats up. McGonagall is Head of House and Deputy Headmistress. |
| **Slytherin** | Salazar Slytherin | Water | Increasingly recruiting ground for early Death Eater sympathizers — the Lestranges, Avery, Mulciber, Wilkes, Rosier all overlap with the Marauders' upper years. Slughorn is Head of House and Potions Master through 1981. |
| **Ravenclaw** | Rowena Ravenclaw | Air | Filius Flitwick is Head and Charms Master. Politically less polarized; many Ravenclaw casualties on both sides of the war. |
| **Hufflepuff** | Helga Hufflepuff | Earth | Pomona Sprout is Head (newer to the position in this era) and Herbology Mistress. Edgar Bones (Order, Hufflepuff alum) and his family are murdered late war. |

### Sorting

The Sorting Hat — a fragment of Godric Gryffindor's enchanted hat. Considers your traits *and* your preferences (will respect a strong personal pull, as it did with Harry → Gryffindor over Slytherin). In the 70s the Hat begins singing more pointed inter-house unity warnings — though that becomes overt in the 90s.

### Curriculum structure

**Years 1–2 (compulsory for all):**
- Transfiguration (McGonagall)
- Charms (Flitwick)
- Potions (Slughorn through 1981, then Snape)
- History of Magic (Professor Binns — already a ghost in this era; he died at some indeterminate point and simply kept teaching)
- Defence Against the Dark Arts (notoriously cursed position — annual turnover even in the 70s)
- Astronomy (Aurora Sinistra — possibly not yet teaching; the era's professor varies by source)
- Herbology (Pomona Sprout)
- Flying (Madam Hooch) — 1st year only, compulsory

**Years 3–5 (electives chosen end of Year 2; minimum 2):**
- Arithmancy (Septima Vector) — magical numerology
- Study of Ancient Runes (Bathsheda Babbling)
- Care of Magical Creatures (Marauders era: Professor Kettleburn — chronically maimed, "took a vow of no unnecessary risk-taking" upon retirement)
- Divination (Marauders era: pre-Trelawney; Trelawney's hire happens in 1980 specifically because Dumbledore needs to interview her about the prophecy)
- Muggle Studies

**Years 6–7 (NEWT-level):**
- Students drop to subjects they want; non-verbal casting introduced
- Apparition lessons offered in 6th year (Feb), licensed at 17
- Alchemy occasionally offered post-OWL if a teacher is available

### Exam grades

**OWLs/NEWTs (5th and 7th years):**
- Passing: **O** (Outstanding), **E** (Exceeds Expectations), **A** (Acceptable)
- Failing: **P** (Poor), **D** (Dreadful), **T** (Troll)

Auror training (a popular Order recruitment pipeline post-Hogwarts) requires NEWTs of E or O in Defence Against the Dark Arts, Transfiguration, Potions, Charms, and one other — typically five total.

### Hogwarts geography (RP-relevant locations)

- **The Castle** — Scotland, somewhere in the Highlands. Unplottable; Muggles see ruins.
- **The Great Hall** — Enchanted ceiling shows the sky outside. Four long house tables; staff table at the head.
- **Common rooms**: Gryffindor (7th-floor portrait of the Fat Lady, password-locked); Slytherin (dungeons, behind a stretch of stone wall); Ravenclaw (West Tower, riddle-locked door with bronze eagle knocker); Hufflepuff (basement, near the kitchens, accessed by tapping barrels in the right rhythm).
- **The Forbidden Forest** — Centaurs (Bane, Magorian, Firenze decades later), acromantulas (Aragog, given to Hagrid by Newt Scamander–era figure), unicorns, thestrals, hippogriffs.
- **The Black Lake** — Giant Squid (friendly), merpeople colony, grindylows.
- **The Whomping Willow** — Planted in 1971 specifically to conceal the entrance to the **Shrieking Shack** tunnel for Remus's transformations. He is the *reason* it exists.
- **The Shrieking Shack** — Hogsmeade. Made "haunted" by Remus's transformation noises (locals never realized). The Marauders' werewolf moonlight playground from year 5 onward.
- **Hogsmeade** — Wholly wizarding village. Three Broomsticks (Madam Rosmerta is already running it), Hog's Head (Aberforth Dumbledore), Honeydukes, Zonko's, Madam Puddifoot's, Dervish & Banges. Hogsmeade weekends from year 3 with parental permission.
- **The Room of Requirement** — 7th floor, opposite the Barnabas the Barmy tapestry. Pace three times in front of the wall thinking of what you need.
- **Astronomy Tower** — Highest point. Where students take stargazing exams; later (1997) where Dumbledore dies.
- **The Marauder's Map** — Made years 5-7. Shows every person in the castle by name in real-time. Activated by "I solemnly swear that I am up to no good"; cleared by "Mischief managed." Insults attempts to misuse it.

## 5. Wizarding Politics & The Ministry

### The Ministry of Magic (1970s)

Located underground in central London, accessed via a public toilet entrance for visitors and the Floo Network for staff.

**Ministers for Magic during the era:**
- **Eugenia Jenkins** (1968–1975) — Handled the early Death Eater attacks poorly; widely seen as out of her depth in wartime. Replaced.
- **Harold Minchum** (1975–1980) — Hardliner; flooded Azkaban with extra Dementors, expanded Auror powers. War still escalated.
- **Millicent Bagnold** (1980–1990) — In office during Voldemort's first defeat. Famously defended Wizarding celebrations after Halloween 1981 even when Muggles noticed: "I assert our inalienable right to party."

### Ministry Departments

1. **Department of Magical Law Enforcement** — biggest department; contains the Auror Office, the Wizengamot Administration Services, the Misuse of Muggle Artefacts Office.
   - **Auror Office**: Head during the war was Bartemius Crouch Sr. (later promoted out). Aurors granted permission to use Unforgivable Curses on suspected Death Eaters. Mad-Eye Moody (Alastor Moody) is the era's legendary Auror — already battle-scarred, paranoid, and credited with filling half the cells in Azkaban personally.
2. **Department of International Magical Cooperation**
3. **Department of Magical Transportation** — Floo Network Authority, Apparition Test Centre, Broom Regulatory Control
4. **Department of the Regulation and Control of Magical Creatures** — the contentious department where house-elf rights, werewolf registration, centaur liaison, etc. live. Dolores Umbridge climbs the ladder here in this era.
5. **Department of Magical Games and Sports** — Quidditch league, etc.
6. **Department of Mysteries** — The Unspeakables. Time Room (time-turners), Hall of Prophecies, Death Chamber (Veil), Love Room, Brain Room. The prophecy concerning Voldemort and Harry is recorded here in early 1980.
7. **Department of Magical Accidents and Catastrophes** — Memory modification (Obliviators), Muggle-Worthy Excuse Committee.

### The Wizengamot

Wizarding high court and parliament. Chief Warlock through this era: **Albus Dumbledore** (also Supreme Mugwump of the International Confederation of Wizards). Trials of captured Death Eaters in late 1981 and 1982 are a major narrative beat — Karkaroff's plea bargain, the Lestranges/Crouch Jr. trial for the Longbottom torture, Lucius Malfoy's Imperius defense (acquitted).

### Pureblood politics

The "Sacred Twenty-Eight" — a 1930s anonymously-published list of supposedly "truly pureblood" British families. Treated as gospel by supremacist families. Includes: Black, Malfoy, Lestrange, Rosier, Avery, Nott, Yaxley, Bulstrode, Carrow, Crouch, Macmillan, Longbottom, Weasley (ironically — they're "blood traitors"), Prewett, Shacklebolt, Greengrass, Burke, Selwyn, Travers, Flint, Slughorn, Parkinson, Shafiq, Ollivander, Abbott, Fawley, Gaunt, Lestrange.

The Weasleys, Longbottoms, and Prewetts are all on the list but vehemently anti-Voldemort — illustrating that "blood purity" cuts across alignment.

### Key political figures (era)

- **Albus Dumbledore** — Headmaster, Order founder, Chief Warlock, Supreme Mugwump. The only wizard Voldemort fears.
- **Bartemius Crouch Sr.** — Head of Magical Law Enforcement, then Minister-presumptive, derailed when his son is convicted as a Death Eater in 1982.
- **Cornelius Fudge** — Junior at Magical Catastrophes during the era; future Minister.
- **Dolores Umbridge** — Climbing the Department for the Regulation and Control of Magical Creatures.
- **Rufus Scrimgeour** — Head Auror by late war.
- **Amelia Bones** — Auror, Edgar Bones's sister; survives the war.

## 6. Dark Arts & Death Eaters

### Voldemort

Born Tom Marvolo Riddle (Dec 31, 1926) to Merope Gaunt (witch, descendant of Salazar Slytherin via the Gaunt line) and Tom Riddle Sr. (Muggle, abandoned Merope). Raised in a Muggle orphanage. Hogwarts class of 1945. Slytherin. Head Boy. Disappeared into Albania after Hogwarts, traveled, returned to Britain transformed.

By the 70s he is no longer fully human in appearance — the Horcrux mutilations have left him pale, snake-featured, red-eyed. He has made (canonically) six Horcruxes by the war's end:
1. The Diary (Tom Riddle's, given to Lucius Malfoy for safekeeping)
2. The Ring (Marvolo Gaunt's, hidden in the Gaunt shack — also bears the Resurrection Stone)
3. The Locket (Salazar Slytherin's, hidden in a sea cave with Inferi — Regulus dies retrieving it in 1979)
4. The Cup (Helga Hufflepuff's, vaulted at Bellatrix's Gringotts vault)
5. The Diadem (Rowena Ravenclaw's, hidden in the Room of Requirement)
6. Nagini (made later, Second War — but the snake exists in the era as his familiar)
7. (Accidental — Harry himself, made Halloween 1981 when the Killing Curse rebounds)

The Marauders era runs entirely *before* the world knows about Horcruxes. Dumbledore is suspicious; nobody else is.

### Death Eaters (Marauders-era roster)

The "first generation" — Hogwarts cohort overlapping the Marauders or just senior to them:

- **Bellatrix Lestrange (née Black)** — Slytherin, born 1951. Voldemort's most fanatical lieutenant. Married Rodolphus Lestrange. Cousin to Sirius.
- **Rodolphus Lestrange** — Slytherin, ~1951. Bellatrix's husband.
- **Rabastan Lestrange** — Rodolphus's brother.
- **Lucius Malfoy** — Slytherin, ~1953/54. Wealth, Ministry influence, claims Imperius defense after Voldemort's fall.
- **Narcissa Malfoy (née Black)** — Slytherin, 1955. Sirius's cousin. Not formally a Death Eater but aligned.
- **Antonin Dolohov** — Older Death Eater. Killed the Prewett brothers. Specializes in a purple slashing curse.
- **Evan Rosier** — Slytherin, Marauders' year approximately. Killed by Moody (after blowing off part of Moody's nose) late war.
- **Wilkes** — Slytherin, Marauders' year. Killed by Aurors late war.
- **Mulciber** — Slytherin, Marauders-adjacent. Specialist in Imperius. Reason Lily distrusted Snape's choice of friends.
- **Avery** — Slytherin, Marauders' year. Survived war via Imperius defense.
- **Severus Snape** — Slytherin, Marauders' year exactly. Joined as a young man, took the prophecy fragment to Voldemort, defected after Lily was targeted, became Dumbledore's spy.
- **Regulus Black** — Slytherin, ~1961. Joined eagerly, defected privately when he learned of the Horcruxes; died in 1979.
- **Bartemius Crouch Jr.** — ~1962. Younger. Convicted in 1982 with the Lestranges for the Longbottom torture.
- **Igor Karkaroff** — Joined; betrayed names to escape Azkaban.
- **Peter Pettigrew** — Gryffindor, the secret one. Recruited late.
- **Fenrir Greyback** — Werewolf, allied with rather than formally inducted. Used as a threat — bites Death Eater enemies' children. The reason Remus is a werewolf (bit him as a small child as revenge against Lyall Lupin).

### The Mark

The Dark Mark — a tattoo of a skull with a serpent issuing from its mouth, branded on the inner left forearm. Burns when Voldemort summons his Death Eaters. Becomes invisible-but-faded after his 1981 fall, leaving a faint smudge that "darkens" again when he returns.

### Unforgivable Curses

- **Imperius (Imperio)** — Total mind control. Caster's voice in the target's head; victim feels light, untethered, willing. Extremely strong-willed individuals can resist (Harry resists at age 14).
- **Cruciatus (Crucio)** — Pure pain. Sustained Cruciatus drives victims to insanity (Frank and Alice Longbottom). Requires the caster to genuinely *want* to cause pain — anger alone isn't enough.
- **Killing (Avada Kedavra)** — Green light, no countercurse, instant death. No physical mark on the body. Requires real intent to kill. Can be blocked only by physical objects (the curse cannot pass through solid matter) or, uniquely, by sacrificial love magic (Lily, Halloween 1981).

Use on a human is a life sentence in Azkaban. Crouch Sr. authorized Aurors to use them during the war, controversially.

### Other dark magic in play

- **Inferi** — Reanimated corpses, Voldemort's bodyguards in the cave Horcrux defense. Afraid of fire and light.
- **Horcruxes** — A soul-fragment vessel, requires murder to create. The deepest taboo in dark magic; pre-war, only Herpo the Foul (ancient Greek dark wizard) was known to have made one. Voldemort makes six on purpose, one by accident.
- **Dementors** — Not under any wizard's control historically; guard Azkaban under "treaty"; in the 70s and again later, drift toward Voldemort because he can offer them more victims.
- **Fiendfyre** — Cursed fire that takes the form of beasts; nearly impossible to control or extinguish. Crabbe casts it (Second War) and dies in it.

## 7. Magical Creatures & Beings

### "Being" vs "Beast" (Ministry classification)

Beings have human-level intelligence and recognized rights. Beasts do not. This classification is a political minefield (centaurs *refused* "Being" status to avoid being grouped with hags; merpeople refused for similar reasons).

### Sentient peoples

- **Goblins** — Run Gringotts. Distinct legal status; brutally suppressed in past goblin rebellions, still simmering. Make the finest weapons (goblin silver in Gryffindor's sword absorbs only what strengthens it — like basilisk venom). Don't mix well with wizards; wizard-goblin relations are tense.
- **House-elves** — Magical servants bound to wizarding families; freed only by being given clothes. Powerful magic of their own (can Apparate where wizards can't). House-elf rights movement (SPEW) is a 90s thing; in the 70s, even most "good" wizard families don't think twice about them.
- **Centaurs** — Live in herds (Forbidden Forest's herd is led informally by Magorian/Bane in the era). Practice astrology and divination. Refuse "Being" status. Will not interfere in human affairs as a rule.
- **Merpeople** — Black Lake colony. Selkie subspecies in cold waters. Have their own language (Mermish, screechy above water).
- **Veela** — Eastern European; women of supernatural beauty whose anger transforms them into harpy-like creatures. Veela hair is a wand core (Ollivander rejects it).
- **Giants** — Hunted nearly to extinction; remaining colonies in remote mountain ranges. Voldemort allies with them. Hagrid's mother Fridwulfa is a giantess; his half-brother Grawp shows up in book 5.
- **Werewolves** — Humans bitten by werewolves transform every full moon. Wolfsbane Potion (invented Damocles Belby, 1980s) makes the transformation tolerable but isn't available in the Marauders' student years. Severely socially stigmatized; Ministry registry; near-impossible employment. Greyback is the era's werewolf-supremacist boogeyman.
- **Vampires** — Exist; rarely seen in Britain. More prominent in Eastern Europe.
- **Hags** — Witch-adjacent; occasionally human-eating; legally sentient.
- **Ghosts** — Wizard imprints of those who feared death too much to move on. Hogwarts has Nearly Headless Nick (Gryffindor), the Fat Friar (Hufflepuff), the Grey Lady (Ravenclaw — Helena Ravenclaw, founder's daughter), the Bloody Baron (Slytherin — killed Helena, then himself), Moaning Myrtle (in the bathroom; killed by the Basilisk in 1943), Professor Binns (History of Magic).

### Notable creatures (era-relevant for RP encounters)

- **Dragons** — Highly dangerous XXXXX. Breeds: Hungarian Horntail, Norwegian Ridgeback, Welsh Green, Hebridean Black, Romanian Longhorn, Chinese Fireball, Swedish Short-Snout, Antipodean Opaleye, Peruvian Vipertooth, Ukrainian Ironbelly. Hagrid wants one constantly. Gringotts keeps an Ironbelly chained in the deepest vaults.
- **Phoenixes** — Burn and rebirth; tears heal; can bear immense weights; instinctively loyal. Fawkes is Dumbledore's; he's been there for centuries.
- **Thestrals** — Skeletal winged horses, only visible to those who have witnessed and *processed* a death. Hogwarts has a fleet pulling the carriages from Hogsmeade.
- **Hippogriffs** — Half-eagle, half-horse. Proud; demand a respectful bow before they'll allow approach.
- **Acromantulas** — Giant intelligent spiders. Aragog lives in the Forbidden Forest, gifted to Hagrid in his youth.
- **Unicorns** — Pure beings; their blood will keep one alive but at a cursed half-life cost.
- **Boggarts** — Shape-shifters that take the form of the viewer's worst fear. Banished by laughter (Riddikulus).
- **Dementors** — Hooded soul-suckers. Reproduce by mold-like fission in damp places. Patronus Charm is the only known defense. Guard Azkaban.
- **Inferi** — see Dark Arts.
- **Blast-Ended Skrewts** — Hagrid breeds these later (late 70s headcanon could have early experiments).
- **Nifflers, Bowtruckles, Crups, Kneazles** — Magizoological menagerie. Newt Scamander's *Fantastic Beasts* is the standard textbook (Scamander is alive in the era — he died in 2017).

## 10. Magical History (Capsules)

- **Founding of Hogwarts** — c. 990 CE. Godric Gryffindor, Helga Hufflepuff, Rowena Ravenclaw, Salazar Slytherin. Slytherin departs in dispute over Muggle-born admission, leaving the Chamber of Secrets behind.
- **Statute of Secrecy** — 1692. International Confederation of Wizards establishes wizarding world's hiding from Muggles. Foundational law.
- **Goblin Rebellions** — Multiple. 1612 is the famous one taught at Hogwarts (Three Broomsticks served as goblin HQ). History of Magic spends most of its time on these.
- **Witch trials** — Mostly performative; medieval witches simply Apparated away or used Flame-Freezing Charms. Wendelin the Weird allowed herself to be burned 47 times in different disguises because she enjoyed the sensation.
- **Grindelwald's rise** — 1920s–1945. Continental dark wizard pursuing wizard supremacy over Muggles. Defeated by Dumbledore in their famous 1945 duel (Dumbledore wins the Elder Wand from him). Imprisoned in Nurmengard, the prison Grindelwald himself built.
- **Voldemort's first rise** — Begins 1940s (Hogwarts), open from 1970, defeated 1981.
- **Salem witch trials & the American wizarding tradition** — MACUSA (Magical Congress of the USA), Ilvermorny School. Strict no-Muggle-fraternization tradition under Rappaport's Law (relevant if RP touches American visitors).
- **Quidditch** — Codified in the 11th century. Marauders era: the British and Irish League is in full swing; the 1973 Quidditch World Cup happens (in the books, the 1994 Cup is described as the "422nd"). James Potter played Chaser for Gryffindor (some adaptations say Seeker — book canon settled on Chaser).

## 11. TTRPG System Reference

Three of the most-used fan TTRPGs for HP, with mechanics summaries and porting notes.

### A. Hogwarts: An RPG (David Brunell-Brutman) — **PbtA, 16-page, free**

**System**: Powered by the Apocalypse. Free, 16-page PDF, Creative Commons (hack-friendly). itch.io: dbb-8.itch.io/hogwarts-rpg.

**Why it's great for narrative LLM RP**: Minimal mechanical overhead. The "moves" structure is naturally narrative — every roll has a story consequence baked in. Easy to translate into a system prompt.

**Core loop**: Roll 2d6 + stat. 10+ = success, 7–9 = success with cost, 6– = failure (and the GM/MC makes a hard move).

**Five attributes** (replaces D&D-style stats with emotional/social-coded ones):
- **Bravery** — courage, defiance, taking direct action
- **Cunning** — stealth, deception, misdirection
- **Intellect** — research, analysis, study
- **Loyalty** — bonds, support, sacrifice for others
- **Magical Skill** — spellcasting, item use, magical perception

**No HP. Instead, a Conditions track.** Conditions inflict mechanical penalties and clear only via narrative resolution:

| Condition | Effect | Cleared by |
|---|---|---|
| **Scared** | -2 to Bravery | Comfort from an ally, facing the fear |
| **Angry** | -2 to Cunning | Acting on the anger (often badly) or being talked down |
| **Stressed** | -2 to Intellect | Rest, escape, completing the stressor |
| **Embarrassed** | -2 to Loyalty | Making it right, or doubling down |
| **Jealous** | -2 to Magical Skill | Acting on the jealousy (often betraying someone) or letting it go |
| **Injured** | -1 to ALL traits | Healing magic, rest, time |

Conditions are *the* core of why this system works for LLM RP — they give the model concrete emotional state to track, and they reward narrative play (clearing a condition is itself a scene).

### Core PbtA Moves (directly portable to LLM system prompts)

| Move | Stat | 10+ | 7–9 | 6– |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Stand in the Face of Danger** | Bravery | Hold firm, no one hurt | Survive at cost (Condition or dropped item) | Serious consequence |
| **Hide & Sneak** | Cunning | Unseen | Unseen but leave a trace, OR seen but not caught | Caught |
| **Help or Hinder Someone** | Loyalty | Add/subtract from their roll | Same, but you risk the same consequence they do | They suffer worse, you suffer too |
| **Get What You Seek** | Bravery / Cunning | Find what you came for | Find it but the GM picks one: it's incomplete / late / costs you / draws attention | Find nothing or worse |
| **Cast a Spell** | Magical Skill | Spell works as intended | Spell works but with a complication (less duration, weaker effect, side effect) | Spell fails, gain 1 XP, suffer Serious Consequence |
| **Use a Magical Object** | Magical Skill | Works perfectly | Works but unexpected/breaks | Misfires |

**Why this matters for LLM RP**: The 7–9 result is where the magic happens. PbtA's "success at a cost" structure is *exactly* what LLMs are best at narrating — they're already good at "yes, but…" Conditions plus this move list is essentially a complete system you can put in a system prompt in under 500 tokens.

**Best for**: Fast, emotional, character-driven RP where mechanics get out of the way.

### B. Harry Potter and the Tabletop RPG (HPTTRPG, "Second Edition") — **d10 system, free**

**System**: d10 pool system. Roll #d10 where # is your dice pool from skills + attributes. Whenever there's an uncertain outcome, you make a "check."

**Skills (18)**: Alchemy, Ancient Runes, Arithmancy, Artificing, Astronomy, Magical Creatures, Charms, DADA, Dark Arts, Divination, Flying, Herbology, History of Magic, Muggle Studies, Perception, Persuasion, Potions, Transfiguration. Each skill has 1–2 defining attributes you can add to checks.

**Credits**: Credits represent how much you know about each skill. Your primary educational metric. You earn credits through study, not just adventuring.

**Spell list**: Comprehensive — every canon spell plus non-canon community additions, with non-canon flagged by asterisk. Each spell has a skill-level requirement; with research, you can learn spells before meeting the requirement.

**Magical items**: Form + function rules. An item must take a form (teacup) and provide a function (self-cleaning) achievable by some other magic. Items can never be truly sentient.

**Why this for narrative RP**: The d10 pool gives more granularity than 2d6 PbtA, and the skill list maps *exactly* onto Hogwarts curriculum — perfect for student-character RP.

**Best for**: RPs where you want explicit "I rolled my Dark Arts check" texture and a deep spell catalog.

### C. Wizarding World RPG (wizworldrpg.com) — **homebrew, D&D-adjacent, free**

**System**: Custom homebrew, takes design cues from D&D. Versioned (4.2.1 = 4th iteration of core rules, 2 phases of mechanics changes, 1 correction).

**Pitch**: "Finally getting your Hogwarts letter." Total flexibility — Middle Ages, space, anything goes. Inclusive rules-set. The dev team is explicit about being a queer/trans-affirming alternative for fans alienated from the source author.

**Best for**: Long-form campaigns with a real GM and a player who likes character sheets. Less ideal as a backbone for LLM-mediated RP because the rules complexity adds tokens without much narrative payoff.

### D. Harry Potter 5e Homebrew (Reddit/GMBinder community) — **5e D&D conversion**

**System**: D&D 5e bones with HP skinning. Houses as backgrounds, wand as casting focus, OWLs/NEWTs as ability score gates, spells as cantrips/leveled spells.

**Why it's interesting for porting**: 5e's massive existing infrastructure (level progression, action economy, exhaustion, conditions) is a known quantity. If the LLM has D&D in its training distribution, it can riff on 5e mechanics without much prompting.

**Why it's awkward for narrative RP**: 5e's combat-centric design fights the school-drama emotional core of HP. Best used for adventure-heavy RP (chasing Death Eaters) rather than relationship-heavy RP (the actual Marauders dynamic).

### E. Harry Potter: The Roleplaying Game (Matthew Kearns) — **Decipher CODA-derived**

**System**: Adapts the Decipher LotR/Star Trek CODA system. Attribute scores (Bearing, Nimbleness, Perception, Strength, Vitality, Intellect, Magic) plus reactions (Stamina, Swiftness, Willpower, Wisdom). Skills with numeric bonuses.

**Best for**: Players who want a mid-crunch traditional system with rich character sheets. Heritage handling (Muggle-born, Half-blood, Pureblood) is built into character creation as a "Heritage" tag with mechanical implications.

### Comparison table

| System | Crunch | Free? | Era-portable? | LLM-friendly? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hogwarts: An RPG (PbtA) | Very light | Yes | Yes (any era) | Excellent — minimal token overhead |
| HPTTRPG (d10) | Medium | Yes | Yes | Very good — spell catalog is a goldmine for prompts |
| Wizarding World RPG | Medium-heavy | Yes | Yes | Moderate — rules-heavy |
| 5e Homebrew | Heavy | Yes (community) | Awkward | Good if LLM knows 5e well |
| HP RPG (CODA, Kearns) | Medium-heavy | Yes (PDF circulating) | Yes | Moderate |

### Recommendation for your use case

For Marauders-era narrative LLM RP, I'd suggest hybridizing:
- **PbtA's 2d6+stat resolution** as your primary "do I succeed?" mechanic — keeps prose flowing
- **HPTTRPG's skill list and spell catalog** as the *what* you can do at any moment — gives the LLM a concrete vocabulary
- **CODA-style Heritage** (Pureblood / Half-blood / Muggle-born) as a character-card field — has narrative weight in this era *especially*

The result: a low-mechanical-overhead system the LLM can run in-character without breaking out of prose, but with a rich vocabulary of named spells and skills to draw from when the moment calls for specificity.

## 13. LLM RP Design Kit

This section ports directly into your SillyTavern infrastructure.

### Canon hierarchy (decide before play begins)

The LLM will drift between book canon, film canon, archive essays, and fan memory if you don't pin a hierarchy. Recommended order for a Marauders-era game:

1. **The seven novels** (canon facts about characters, institutions, spells in actual scenes)
2. **Rowling archive essays** (Pottermore/Wizarding World writing on wandlore, Patronus, Hogwarts subjects, MACUSA, etc.) — fills edge cases the books only imply
3. **Official fact files** at harrypotter.com (compact reference layer)
4. **Reputable fan refs** (Harry Potter Lexicon, Harry Potter Wiki, MuggleNet) — for cross-checking dates and obscure details

Treat *Cursed Child* as **optional toggle**, not default. For the Marauders era specifically, the books say very little — most of what exists comes from flashbacks (Snape's Worst Memory, the Pensieve scenes in HBP/DH), Sirius's monologues in OotP, and Rowling's interviews. A book-faithful Marauders era is *deeply underspecified*, which is good news for RP — there's room to invent.

### Recommended design defaults

| Dial | Marauders-era default |
|---|---|
| Canon boundary | Books first; archive second; films optional |
| Time slice | One school year, OR one Order operation arc, OR one summer between school years |
| Tone | Wonder + mounting dread; school ritual under wartime shadow |
| Magic | Named and legible in scenes; rare magic stays rare |
| Secrecy | Statute of Secrecy is load-bearing; magical exposure has consequences |
| Conflict | Mysteries, rivalries, cover-ups, family loyalties, exams, war |
| Violence | Restrained until the campaign clearly shifts into Order/wartime mode |
| Unspecified lore | Mark as rumor, theory, or unresolved scholarship |

### Sample master prompt (canon-disciplined narrator)

```
You are the narrative engine for a Harry Potter Marauders-era roleplay (1971-1981).

Canon policy:
- Prefer the seven novels and Marauders flashback content (OotP Pensieve, HBP "Spinner's End," DH "Prince's Tale").
- If novels are silent, use Rowling archive essays (Pottermore/Wizarding World) and official fact files.
- Do not invent hard magical physics where canon is unspecified.
- If a detail is unclear, mark it as uncertain, rumored, or disputed in-world.
- Era gotchas: Wolfsbane Potion does not exist yet. Dumbledore holds the Elder Wand. The Taboo on "Voldemort" is not active. James has the Invisibility Cloak as a student.

Tone policy:
- Preserve the era's blend of school ritual, friendship, wartime dread, and pureblood/half-blood politics.
- Magic feels concrete in the moment, mysterious at the edges.
- Violence escalates slowly outside of explicit Order missions.

World policy:
- Statute of Secrecy matters. Muggle-Worthy Excuse Committee is busy.
- Institutions: Hogwarts, the Ministry (Jenkins → Minchum → Bagnold), the Order, the Death Eaters.
- House identity and blood-status politics shape scenes but are NOT personality determinism.
- Adults are competent but partial; students are brave but under-informed.

Output policy:
- Sensory detail. Track rumors, clues, favors, detentions, points, injuries, relationship shifts.
- Offer consequences, not railroads.
- Keep canon Marauders/Order characters in-character; avoid using them unless the scene demands it.
```

```
We are playing a Marauders-era Harry Potter narrative roleplay.

Campaign frame:
- Year: [e.g. 1976 5th year, 1979 Order, 1977 7th year]
- Place: [Hogwarts / Order safe house / Ministry / Diagon Alley / mixed]
- Canon level: [strict / canon-compatible AU / soft AU]
- Tone: [school drama / war thriller / political intrigue / mystery]

```

```
Social leverage: Knockturn channels, Auror friends in Records, owl-post networks
Pressure points: an unbreakable vow, a debt to Aberforth, a half-Muggle nephew
Scene use: information broker, suspect, temporary patron
Danger level: High
```

### Scene seeds (Marauders era)

- A 5th-year student receives a wand replacement after their first one shatters; the replacement responds *too* eagerly to a dark spell they didn't mean to cast.
- A Hogsmeade weekend is quietly cordoned off as Obliviators sweep the village after a Death Eater attack on the periphery.
- An old letter found in the library describes a duel involving a relative the student was told died of dragon pox.
- A house-elf at a pureblood family's home asks a Muggle-born student a question that, if answered, would constitute disobedience to its owners.
- A Ministry intern in the Department of Mysteries discovers an unsorted prophecy with their own name in it.
- An exam practical is interrupted when a corporeal Patronus delivers an Order distress message — to the wrong recipient.
- A Slytherin's Dark Mark "appears" at breakfast, except it's a hex from a Gryffindor prankster that won't come off.
- A first-year is sorted into a house their entire family has been in for 400 years — except this time the Hat hesitates for nearly five minutes before deciding.

### Best-practice rules for the LLM

The model running your campaign should:

- Keep **one active mystery, one social triangle, and one institutional pressure** in play at all times. Drop or resolve before adding.
- Treat **house identity as socially real** but not as personality determinism. Slytherins aren't all aspiring dark wizards; Gryffindors aren't all noble.
- Let canonical spells solve *local* problems without trivializing major plot obstacles. (Alohomora opens locked classrooms but not the Headmaster's office.)
- Use letters, archives, portraits, detentions, exams, notices, and Wizengamot hearings as scene machinery — these are the era's "set pieces."
- **Avoid overusing Marauders themselves as PCs' direct interlocutors.** The original PCs need narrative oxygen. Sirius can show up at the pub once. He shouldn't be in every scene.
- Flag anything canon doesn't firmly specify, rather than inventing arbitrary certainty. "There are rumors that…" is your friend.

## 14. SillyTavern Lorebook Engineering

Since this report is destined for your existing infrastructure, some specifics:

### Context budgeting

- **Lorebook injection target**: 20–40% of total context window. Above 40% you crowd out actual chat history; below 20% you under-leverage the lore.
- **Per-entry size**: 100–400 tokens is the sweet spot. Each numbered section in this report is roughly that range and can become 1–3 entries.

### Activation depth & recursion

- **Scan depth 3–4** is standard (engine reviews the last 3–4 messages for keywords).
- **Recursion limit 1–2** — recursion lets a triggered entry trigger another (e.g., "Lucius Malfoy" pulls in "Death Eaters"), but unbounded recursion floods context. Cap it.

### Entry format

PList or JSON formatting tokenizes more efficiently than prose. For a Death Eater entry:

```
{
  "faction": "Death Eaters",
  "leader": "Voldemort",
  "founded": "1960s (informal), open ~1970",
  "goals": ["Pureblood supremacy", "Ministry infiltration", "Eradicate Muggle-borns"],
  "key_members_70s": ["Bellatrix Lestrange", "Lucius Malfoy", "Antonin Dolohov", "Severus Snape (1976-1979)", "Regulus Black (1977-1979)"],
  "mark": "Skull-and-serpent tattoo on inner left forearm; burns when Voldemort summons",
  "modus_operandi": "Masked attacks, Imperius blackmail, kidnap-and-torture intelligence gathering",
  "current_state_in_era": "Ascendant; Aurors permitted to use Unforgivables in response"
}
```

### Hidden state patterns

For the kind of multi-character intrigue the era thrives on, use XML-comment hidden state in character cards. The model knows the secret; the user doesn't until it surfaces:

```
Cassiopeia Selwyn
[Public bio her classmates would describe...]

<!-- HIDDEN STATE: Cassiopeia is being recruited by her brother to take the Mark this summer.
She has not told anyone. She is already drafting a letter to Dumbledore she may never send.
Trigger: if PC mentions her brother, the Order, or Muggle-borns being attacked, she gets visibly tense. -->
```

This is how you get the Marauders-era specialty — the "everyone has a secret that will eventually detonate" texture — without breaking the fourth wall.
