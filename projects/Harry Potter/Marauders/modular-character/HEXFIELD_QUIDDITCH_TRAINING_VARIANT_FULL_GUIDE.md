# Hexfield Quidditch Training Variant — Full Guide

## Working title

**Hexfield Quidditch Drill: Recognition, Pressure, and Position Training**

This is a training variant of Hexfield for Quidditch players.

It is not meant to replace Quidditch practice. It is meant to train the skills that Quidditch players need but that ordinary broom practice can hide:

- field recognition,
- teammate positioning,
- enemy positioning,
- callouts,
- coordination,
- reflexes,
- recovery after disruption,
- tactical patience,
- and position-specific decision-making.

## Core principle

Players do **not** switch Quidditch roles.

A Chaser is a Chaser.  
A Beater is a Beater.  
A Keeper is a Keeper.  
A Seeker is a Seeker.

There is no “reserve” position. If a reserve is present, they train as the position they actually play or are trying to cover.

## Base Hexfield rules still active

The drill uses normal Hexfield field mechanics unless specifically modified here:

- Two teams.
- Two bases.
- Two flags.
- Bring the enemy flag back to your own base to score.
- Legal spells only:
  - Stinging Hex
  - Rictusempra
  - Knockback
  - Trip Jinx
- Hit by a legal spell = down where you are.
- Downed player glows in team colour.
- Teammate revive: 15 seconds of unbroken touch.
- Self-runback: after 30 seconds downed, player may return to own base to clear glow.
- Room scoreboard tracks hard numbers.

## Training-team names

For this drill, the two sides are:

- **Owls**
- **Leopard Cats**

These are temporary drill-team names, not permanent teams or Quidditch roles.

Suggested assignment:

- Caleb attached to the **Owls**
- Liri attached to the **Leopard Cats**

## Caleb and Liri’s role

Caleb and Liri are not neutral referees.

They are **perch-coaches**: active tactical stressors assigned to opposite teams.

Each perch-coach stands on a raised platform, tower, sideline perch, or Room-created viewing post.

They may:

- call instructions to their own team,
- observe and score awareness,
- create controlled pressure against the opposing team,
- use the marker system,
- and punish tunnel vision.

They may not:

- enter the field,
- carry a flag,
- revive players,
- score as normal players,
- cast illegal spells,
- attack their own side.

## Perch-coach attack system

A perch-coach cannot simply ambush players.

Every perch-coach attack must use the **Threat-Line Warning System**.

### Threat-Line Warning System

Before Caleb or Liri may cast at a player, the Room creates a visible warning.

The warning has two parts:

1. **Threat-line** — a thin glowing line from the perch-coach’s wand/platform to the target.
2. **Target-ring** — a glowing ring or sigil at the target player’s feet.

Suggested colours:

- Caleb / Owls perch: purple line
- Liri / Leopard Cats perch: gold line

### Timing

- Threat-line appears for **3 seconds**.
- After 3 seconds, the perch-coach may release one legal Hexfield spell.
- Spell travel time is approximately **0.5 seconds**.
- Perch-coach cooldown: **10 seconds** between attacks.

### What this trains

The point is not whether someone can dodge a spell in half a second.

The point is whether the team can notice:

- who is being targeted,
- where the threat is coming from,
- whether the target knows,
- who should call it,
- who should screen,
- who should move,
- and whether the team can respond before the spell is released.

### Threat-line scoring

| Event | Score / Mark |
|---|---:|
| Teammate calls the threat before release | +2 team awareness |
| Target notices own marker before release | +1 personal awareness |
| Beater screens or disrupts after call | +2 protection |
| Keeper calls correct defensive shift | +1 command |
| Seeker identifies perch threat before others | +2 tracking |
| Nobody calls threat and spell lands | -1 recognition |
| False call disrupts formation | -1 communication discipline |

Perch-coach hits do not count as normal player hit-points. They are training pressure, not team offence.

# Position Rules

## Chasers

### Role

Chasers are the **only players allowed to take, hold, carry, and score with the flag**.

They are the flag-handling players.

### Chasers may

- enter enemy base,
- begin the flag-hold window,
- carry the flag back,
- call lanes,
- dodge,
- revive teammates under normal Hexfield rules,
- receive screens from Beaters,
- use cover and movement.

### Chasers may not

- cast offensive spells,
- act as Beaters,
- abandon role-shape to chase hits,
- carry unsupported if the drill condition requires support.

### Chaser scoring

| Action | Points / Mark |
|---|---:|
| Successful flag capture | +10 |
| Supported flag run | +2 |
| Clean lane call | +1 |
| Maintains spacing under pressure | +1 |
| Forces defense to rotate | +1 |
| Unsupported flag attempt | -1 coordination |
| Chaser bunching | -1 spacing |
| Ignores open support route | -1 field awareness |

### Chaser coaching focus

Chasers should learn:

- not to bunch,
- not to sprint alone into glory,
- how to pressure a defense,
- when to commit,
- when to reset,
- and how to use Beater screens.

## Beaters

### Role

Beaters are the **main active spellcasters**.

They create pressure, screen Chasers, interrupt enemy movement, and protect teammates.

### Beaters may

- cast legal Hexfield spells,
- interrupt flag attempts,
- screen for Chasers,
- protect revives,
- defend against perch-coach threat-lines,
- force enemies into bad lanes,
- call incoming pressure.

### Beaters may not

- carry the flag,
- camp downed players cruelly,
- chase meaningless hits while their Chasers are unsupported,
- abandon defense just to win the hit count.

### Beater marker system

Beaters receive special **Beater Marks** from the Room.

These are not physical balls. They are visible tactical prompts.

A Beater Mark appears as a floating black iron ring, black spark, or dark comet-shaped marker near a developing threat.

A Beater Mark means:

> “A Beater action here would matter.”

### Beater Mark types

#### 1. Screen Mark

Appears between a friendly Chaser and an enemy threat.

Meaning:

> Screen here. Protect the run.

Good Beater response:

- step into the lane,
- cast at the threat,
- force the threat aside,
- call the Chaser through.

#### 2. Intercept Mark

Appears near an enemy Chaser beginning a flag route.

Meaning:

> This is the moment to break the run.

Good Beater response:

- hit the runner,
- force a dodge,
- delay the flag-hold window,
- make the Chaser reset.

#### 3. Revive Mark

Appears near a downed teammate being revived.

Meaning:

> Protect the revive.

Good Beater response:

- screen the medic pair,
- cast at incoming enemies,
- call danger,
- hold space for 15 seconds.

#### 4. Perch Mark

Appears when Caleb or Liri’s threat-line is developing and a Beater has angle to help.

Meaning:

> Someone is being targeted. Protect them.

Good Beater response:

- call the target,
- block line of attack,
- pressure the perch-coach’s target lane,
- force the targeted player to move.

### Beater scoring

| Action | Points / Mark |
|---|---:|
| Useful hit that changes play | +1 |
| Screen that protects Chaser | +2 |
| Interrupts flag-hold window | +3 |
| Protects revive for full 15 seconds | +2 |
| Responds correctly to Beater Mark | +2 |
| Calls perch threat before release | +1 |
| Random hit with no tactical value | 0 |
| Chases hits while flag is exposed | -1 discipline |
| Ignores Beater Mark | -1 recognition |

### Beater coaching focus

Beaters should learn:

- pressure is not just hitting,
- protection matters,
- screens win captures,
- interrupting the right play matters more than hitting the nearest person,
- and field vision matters more than aggression.

## Keeper

### Role

Keeper is the **base defender and defensive commander**.

The Keeper does not play like a Chaser or roaming Beater.

### Keeper may

- cast legal defensive spells inside the defensive third,
- call threats,
- organize Chasers and Beaters,
- defend the flag,
- interrupt flag-hold attempts,
- direct recovery after a teammate is downed.

### Keeper may not

- leave the defensive third except during reset,
- carry the flag,
- roam the whole field for hits,
- chase glory while base is exposed.

### Keeper command zones

The Room marks the defensive third around each base.

The Keeper’s active zone is this defensive third.

If the Keeper casts from inside this zone, it is legal.

If the Keeper leaves the zone to chase, the Room marks a **Keeper Drift** penalty.

### Keeper scoring

| Action | Points / Mark |
|---|---:|
| Correct early threat call | +1 |
| Stops flag-hold attempt | +3 |
| Organizes defensive recovery | +2 |
| Calls correct Beater screen | +1 |
| Calls exposed base before attack arrives | +2 |
| Holds base under double pressure | +2 |
| Leaves defensive third unnecessarily | -1 discipline |
| Silent during base pressure | -1 command |
| Reacts only after flag is touched | -1 late recognition |

### Keeper coaching focus

Keeper should learn:

- command voice,
- early warning,
- spatial control,
- directing Beaters,
- seeing the whole attack,
- and treating base defense as leadership, not waiting.

## Seeker

### Role

Seeker is the **tracker and scout**.

The Seeker does not heal as their main job.

The Seeker trains the Quidditch skill of tracking a tiny high-value target while the rest of the game tries to pull their attention away.

### Seeker may

- track the Snitch Marker,
- call its position,
- call enemy weak-side movement,
- identify perch-coach threat-lines,
- warn teammates of bait,
- call when the team is being pulled out of shape,
- revive under normal Hexfield rules if necessary.

### Seeker may not

- carry the flag,
- act as a Beater,
- become the default healer,
- ignore the team entirely to stare at the marker,
- invent false Snitch calls to sound useful.

# Snitch Marker System

The **Snitch Marker** is a Room-created golden tracking object.

It is not a real Snitch.  
It is not caught.  
It does not end the game.

It exists to train Seeker perception.

## Appearance

The Snitch Marker appears as one of the following:

- a tiny gold flicker,
- a winged bead of light,
- a glint that darts between columns,
- a gold mote visible for a second and then gone.

## Movement

The Snitch Marker moves unpredictably around the pitch:

- along walls,
- behind columns,
- above shoulder height,
- across lanes,
- near bases,
- behind perch-coach lines,
- occasionally out of sight.

## Visibility

The Seeker sees it most clearly.

Other players may glimpse it, but only the Seeker’s formal call counts for Seeker scoring.

## Seeker call format

The Seeker should call:

> Marker / location / movement

Examples:

- “Snitch Marker, left column, rising.”
- “Gold flicker, centre lane, behind Purple base.”
- “Lost behind near wall.”
- “Reappeared high right.”
- “Marker crossing behind Chaser push.”
- “False bait — real pressure left.”

## Snitch Marker scoring

| Action | Points / Mark |
|---|---:|
| Correct location call | +1 |
| Correct movement call | +1 |
| Correct lost-sight call | +1 |
| Correct reappearance call | +1 |
| Calls marker without losing team awareness | +2 |
| Identifies perch threat while tracking marker | +2 |
| Warns team of bait | +2 |
| Stares at marker while team loses flag | -1 tunnel vision |
| Gives wrong location | -1 accuracy |
| Fails to reacquire after 10 seconds | -1 tracking |

## Snitch Marker team bonus

If the Seeker has made a correct Snitch Marker call within the last 10 seconds, the team may earn a bonus on a successful Chaser capture.

Suggested bonus:

- Normal capture: +10
- Capture with active Seeker read: +12

This makes Seeker tracking matter without letting the Seeker dominate the game.

## Advanced Seeker rule

Sometimes the Snitch Marker crosses a hidden developing threat:

- a perch-coach targeting line,
- an enemy Chaser route,
- a Beater Mark,
- or an exposed base lane.

If the Seeker identifies this, they score a **Field Read**.

Field Read:

- +2 Seeker tracking
- +1 team awareness
- next threat-line against that team becomes 1 second easier to see

This gives the Seeker a true tactical job:

> see the thing everyone else misses.

# Revives

Revives remain normal Hexfield unless the session intentionally tests otherwise.

Any teammate may revive a downed player with 15 seconds of unbroken touch.

However, revives are scored through role discipline.

Examples:

- Beater screens a revive: Beater gains protection points.
- Keeper tells the correct player to revive: Keeper gains command point.
- Seeker notices downed teammate while tracking marker: Seeker gains awareness point.
- Chaser abandons a nearly complete capture for an unnecessary revive: possible coordination penalty.
- Nobody notices a downed teammate for 10 seconds: team awareness penalty.

The Seeker is not the healer by default.

# Round Structure

## Round 1 — Baseline Role-Locked Hexfield

Purpose:

See natural position habits.

Rules:

- Chasers only carry flag.
- Beaters are only offensive spellcasters.
- Keeper casts only in defensive third.
- Seeker tracks Snitch Marker.
- Everyone may revive.
- Caleb and Liri use light perch pressure.

Track:

- Chaser spacing,
- Beater usefulness,
- Keeper command,
- Seeker tracking,
- team awareness.

## Round 2 — Threat-Line Recognition

Purpose:

Train reaction to visible developing danger.

Rules:

- Caleb and Liri use full threat-line system.
- 3-second warning.
- 10-second coach cooldown.
- Seeker and Beaters get extra score for early recognition.

Track:

- who calls the threat,
- whether target moves,
- whether Beater screens,
- whether Keeper commands,
- whether Seeker sees it while tracking marker.

## Round 3 — Beater Mark Round

Purpose:

Train Beaters to act on the right threat, not the nearest target.

Rules:

- Room activates Beater Marks:
  - Screen Mark,
  - Intercept Mark,
  - Revive Mark,
  - Perch Mark.
- Beaters score heavily for correct mark response.
- Random hits score little or nothing.

Track:

- useful pressure,
- missed screens,
- interrupted flag runs,
- revive protection,
- over-aggression.

## Round 4 — Seeker Marker Round

Purpose:

Train Seeker tracking under distraction.

Rules:

- Snitch Marker becomes more active.
- Perch-coach threat-lines still occur.
- Seeker must track marker without letting the team collapse.
- Correct Seeker calls can boost captures.

Track:

- location accuracy,
- reacquisition,
- not losing team awareness,
- identifying bait,
- seeing threat-lines while tracking.

## Round 5 — Full Transfer Round

Purpose:

Put all systems together.

Rules:

- Chasers handle flag.
- Beaters cast and screen.
- Keeper commands base.
- Seeker tracks marker and hidden threat.
- Perch-coaches apply pressure.
- Room tracks quantitative score and role-specific marks.

Goal:

Make the drill feel like chaos, then prove who can still think.

# Weekly Progression

## Week 1 — Recognition

Use:

- clear threat-lines,
- visible target-rings,
- slower Snitch Marker,
- obvious Beater Marks.

Goal:

Teach the system.

## Week 2 — Spacing

Use:

- Chaser spacing marks,
- bunching penalties,
- stronger Keeper command scoring.

Goal:

Stop everyone running at the same problem.

## Week 3 — Pressure

Use:

- faster Beater Marks,
- more perch pressure,
- harder revive protection.

Goal:

Teach Beaters to screen and interrupt correctly.

## Week 4 — Tracking

Use:

- more active Snitch Marker,
- false glints,
- moving line-of-sight blocks.

Goal:

Train Seeker tracking under chaos.

## Week 5 — Silence penalty

Use:

- normal rules,
- but missed calls hurt more.

Goal:

Force useful communication.

## Week 6 — Match transfer

Use:

- full system,
- no mercy from Room markers,
- compare role scores to Week 1.

Goal:

Show actual improvement.

# Main Design Rule

Every role should feel like its Quidditch self.

Chasers should feel like they are creating and finishing scoring pressure.

Beaters should feel like they are controlling danger.

Keeper should feel like they are commanding the defensive shape.

Seeker should feel like they are tracking what nobody else can afford to track.

The drill works if everyone leaves thinking:

> “That was Hexfield, but it exposed exactly what I do wrong in Quidditch.”
