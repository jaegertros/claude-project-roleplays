# Marauders — Era & Events

Reference document for the campaign's temporal backdrop. The era runs September 1971 to October 1981. The narrative present is set in the opening exchange.

This file is *not* canonical lore the user receives. It is the narrator's calendar — the schedule the world runs on, regardless of what the user is doing.

---

## THE WAR-TEMPO RULE

The single most important rule in this file: **the war does not pause for the user.**

Time advances. Events happen. People die. If the user spends three weeks chasing a personal mystery, three weeks of war happen in the background. When the user looks up, the *Daily Prophet* has been printing without them.

This is the inverse of the Vault 49 model. In Vault 49 the world was a sealed loop and the user's attention was what made events happen. Here, the world is a wide open ongoing catastrophe and the user's attention is one stream among many. **Threads the user does not pull do not freeze in place — they advance, decay, or resolve without the user.**

Practical implications for the narrator:

- **Track the date, not the session.** A scene that takes 30 in-game minutes is 30 minutes of the campaign clock; a scene that takes 30 in-game days is 30 days of the campaign clock. The world advances accordingly.
- **Off-screen NPCs continue to live.** Tamsin runs a Spell Damage shift even when the user isn't there. Cass writes a letter home. Toby plays Quidditch. Their lives are not on pause.
- **Off-screen events continue to happen.** When the campaign date crosses a known canonical event (see the timeline below), that event happens. The user reads about it in the *Prophet*, hears about it from a friend, learns about it third-hand. The user does not need to be present.
- **Replays should be different.** A user who runs the campaign twice should encounter different threads on the second pass — because the threads they didn't pull last time were always going to advance in their absence, and on a second run, the user can pull a different one. The story is bigger than any single playthrough.

## THE *DAILY PROPHET* — DAILY RITUAL

In Vault 49, the daily ritual was the Morning Address. In this campaign, the daily ritual is the *Daily Prophet* arriving at breakfast.

Every morning. Every breakfast. The owls come, the papers land, the room gets quieter for thirty seconds while everyone reads the headline. Then conversation resumes. This happens whether the user engages with it or not.

**Usage in the narrative:**

- The narrator delivers the *Prophet* in compressed form: one headline, one or two sentences below the fold. The user reads or doesn't.
- The headlines come from the timeline below, plus campaign-local fabrications when the timeline is quiet.
- A *Prophet* headline is a Stage 1 adaptive signal — if the user has been ignoring something the campaign needs them to notice, a headline can be the gentle nudge. (Per the adaptive signaling rule, the narrator can escalate from there.)
- The *Prophet* is **partisan and slightly sanitizing**. It under-reports Death Eater attacks on Muggle-borns, over-reports Ministry successes, and runs Wizengamot sentence announcements as if they're triumphs. A user who reads carefully notices the slant. The narrator does not point it out.

**Format of a Prophet beat:**

> The *Prophet* landed beside her toast. "BONES FAMILY KILLED IN HORTON HEATH RAID — SIX DEAD INCLUDING THREE CHILDREN — MINISTRY VOWS RESPONSE." She read the first paragraph and her tea got cold.

That's the whole beat. Two sentences. The user reacts or doesn't.

## THE TIMELINE

The canonical events of the era. Dates are anchored where canon specifies them; soft-dated where canon is vague. The narrator runs these as backdrop unless the user enters their orbit.

### 1970–71 — The opening of the war

- **Voldemort's open declaration** (~1970). The First Wizarding War is "officially" recognized as having begun. Death Eater attacks have been happening for years; this is the moment they become a war the *Prophet* can no longer minimize.
- **The Order of the Phoenix is founded** (1970–71). Dumbledore convenes a small private group at the Hog's Head and elsewhere. Recruitment is careful, slow, by personal vouch.
- **September 1, 1971** — the Marauders begin Hogwarts. Lily Evans and Severus Snape begin Hogwarts. The user, if a Hogwarts student of this cohort, begins Hogwarts.

### 1972–74 — Mid-school years for the Marauders cohort

- The war intensifies in fits and starts. *Prophet* obituary columns lengthen.
- The Marauders begin secret Animagus research (canonical; not directly relevant to most user campaigns).
- **1974** — Death Eater raid on a Muggle bus depot in Manchester. Six Muggles killed; Ministry covers it as a gas explosion.

### 1975 — Sirius leaves home

- **Summer 1975** — Sirius Black runs from Grimmauld Place to the Potters'. This is a private family event, not a public one — the user reads nothing about it in the *Prophet*. But it ripples in pure-blood circles. The Black family is publicly furious. Cass Selwyn, Rory Macnair, and other peers from old families have heard about it from their parents.

### 1976 — Hogwarts year, OWLs year for Marauders

- **Spring 1976** — Levicorpus becomes a fad among fifth-years. Snape's Sectumsempra is whispered about. The Marauders learn it.
- **June 1976** — Snape's Worst Memory. James Potter humiliates Severus Snape after the OWL exam at the lakeshore. Lily Evans ends her friendship with Snape over the slur. This is a public event — half the school sees it. Word spreads. By dinner everyone has heard a version.
- **Fall 1976** — The Prank. Sirius lures Snape toward the Whomping Willow on a full moon. James intervenes. Snape is sworn to silence by Dumbledore. The student body does not know. (User campaigns set in this year may have the user notice the *aftermath* — Snape pale and shaken for a week; James and Sirius briefly estranged.)

### 1977 — Sixth year for Marauders

- **September 1977** — Remus is made prefect. (Canonically he's prefect for sixth year.)
- **Order recruitment intensifies.** Members of the Marauders cohort and the year above are being approached by people who know people.
- **1977** — Death Eater attack on the Bones family's London townhouse. Edgar Bones survives; his sister and her family do not. *Prophet* runs it as front page.

### 1978 — Marauders' final year

- **Spring 1978** — James and Lily, who have been dating publicly since fall, become a fixed couple. The school knows.
- **Summer 1978** — the Marauders, Lily, Frank Longbottom, Alice Longbottom, the Prewett twins, Marlene McKinnon, Dorcas Meadowes, and Caradoc Dearborn (among others) graduate. Most join the Order within months.
- **Fall 1978** — Voldemort allies with the Giants. Auror operations expand. Aurors are now permitted to use Stunning, Disarming, and (controversially) certain disabling curses with broader authorization.

### 1979 — The dark middle

- **Spring 1979** — Caradoc Dearborn disappears six months after the Order's famous group photograph. Body never found. Order morale takes a real hit.
- **Summer 1979** — Crouch Sr. authorizes Aurors to use the Unforgivable Curses against suspected Death Eaters. This is publicly debated; the *Prophet* mostly supports it; civil libertarians (a small group in this era) protest.
- **November 1979** — Regulus Black dies in the cave with the locket Horcrux (canonical, hidden). To the public, he simply disappears — the family says he panicked and tried to back out of being a Death Eater. Sirius is told nothing.

### 1980 — The prophecy and the children

- **Early 1980** — The prophecy is made by Sybill Trelawney to Dumbledore at the Hog's Head, overheard partially by Severus Snape. (Canonical, hidden.)
- **Spring 1980** — Voldemort kills Marlene McKinnon's family personally. Marlene survives only because she is on Order business that night. (She is killed within the year, separately.)
- **June 1980** — The Prewett twins, Fabian and Gideon, die fighting five Death Eaters including Antonin Dolohov. They take longer to kill than the Death Eaters expected.
- **July 30, 1980** — Neville Longbottom is born to Frank and Alice.
- **July 31, 1980** — Harry Potter is born to James and Lily.
- **Fall 1980** — Snape comes to Dumbledore. He has betrayed the prophecy to Voldemort and now realizes Voldemort intends to kill Lily. He becomes Dumbledore's spy. (Canonical, hidden.)

### 1981 — The end

- **Summer 1981** — The Bones family is targeted again — Edgar Bones, his wife, and their children are killed.
- **September 1981** — The Potters go into hiding. Originally Dumbledore is suggested as Secret-Keeper. James and Lily choose Sirius. Sirius, paranoid that Voldemort suspects him, switches to Peter Pettigrew. (Canonical, hidden.)
- **October 24, 1981** — Pettigrew betrays the Potters' location to Voldemort. (Canonical, hidden.)
- **October 31, 1981** — Voldemort attacks Godric's Hollow. James dies; Lily dies; the Killing Curse rebounds; Voldemort is destroyed (temporarily). Sirius arrives, finds the wreckage, hands his motorbike to Hagrid, pursues Pettigrew. The next morning Sirius is arrested for the murder of twelve Muggles and Pettigrew. Pettigrew has staged his own death. (Canonical, hidden.)

The First Wizarding War ends. The campaign, if it has run this long, has a natural endpoint here — or, just as good, runs past it into the strange unstable peace of November 1981 onward.

## THE CAMPAIGN-LOCAL TIMELINE

The narrator runs a parallel local timeline that exists alongside the canonical one. Key beats:

- **Session 1** — the user's vantage and circle are established. The hidden seeds (KNOWLEDGE_4) are placed in the world.
- **Mid-campaign (~10–20 sessions in)** — the campaign-local secret begins to surface. The first hairline cracks appear in the user's circle. The narrator runs adaptive signaling per the rules.
- **Late campaign** — the user is at DEPTH 3–4. The local secret is visible to anyone paying attention. The user makes a choice about what to do with what they've found.
- **Climax** — the local secret resolves. This is independent of the canonical timeline, though the narrator can align them if it makes sense (the local reveal in the same week as a major canon event amplifies both; the local reveal in a quiet canonical month gives it space to breathe).

The narrator does not announce these phases. The narrator runs them.

## NOTES ON HOW EVENTS APPEAR

When a canonical event happens off-screen, the narrator delivers it through one of the following channels, in order of preference:

1. **The *Prophet***. The user reads the headline at breakfast.
2. **A letter from home.** "Did you hear about the Bones?"
3. **A friend's distress.** A classmate has lost someone. The user notices their absence at breakfast or sees them red-eyed in the corridor.
4. **A teacher's announcement.** Dumbledore mentions a death at the next morning's announcements. Brief. Grave.
5. **Hogsmeade gossip.** Madam Rosmerta, Doris Cubbins, Florian Plumstead — the world's information network speaking.
6. **Direct witness.** The user is *there* when something happens. This is rare and should be saved for moments when the user has positioned themselves to be there. It is not delivered as a lucky coincidence.

The narrator does not deliver events through dream sequences, prophetic visions, or unexplained omens. The era is not mystical in that way. The era is *epistolary* — letters, papers, gossip, and the slow trickle of bad news through the channels by which bad news always travels.

## INFORMATION SPREAD MECHANICS

When an event happens, the news propagates at realistic speeds. Use these defaults:

- **An attack tonight in Bristol** — the *Prophet* prints it tomorrow morning (if it gets the story; sometimes it doesn't). Word reaches Hogsmeade through Floo gossip by lunch. Reaches Hogwarts breakfast tables the day after.
- **A death in someone's family** — reaches the immediate family within hours via Patronus or owl. Reaches the school via the headmaster within a day. Reaches the wider student body via gossip within two days.
- **A Wizengamot ruling** — *Prophet* runs it the morning after. Discussed in households for a week. Forgotten by most within a month.
- **A Ministry policy change** — slow. The *Prophet* covers it. Most people don't notice for weeks. Affected parties (Muggle-borns, certain departments) feel it immediately.
- **A canon event hidden in the books** (the prophecy, Snape becoming a spy, Pettigrew turning) — does not propagate publicly at all. Lives in DEPTH 4–5 only.

The narrator runs these speeds consistently. An NPC at breakfast on Wednesday cannot know about an event that happened in London at 3 a.m. Wednesday morning unless they have a reason to know — a Patronus from a relative, a Floo call, a Ministry posting. NPCs without those reasons learn at the speed everyone else learns.

This is the explicit corrective to the failure mode you've already worked on for Vault 49: NPCs do not have information they shouldn't have. Information is bounded by realistic propagation.
