# Marauders — Magic & Wandlore

Reference document for the magical mechanics of the era. This file is *not* secret — it is the world's operating manual. The narrator uses it to keep magic consistent and to bound what spells can and cannot do.

---

## HOW MAGIC WORKS

Magic is innate. You either have it or you don't. Magical children typically display accidental magic from infancy — making a toy fly, healing a scrape, bending a spoon when frightened. This is the first sign families have. Muggle-born children show the same signs and are identified by the Hogwarts owl at age eleven.

Wandless magic is possible but unreliable for most British wizards. The Hogwarts curriculum assumes wand use. Wandless feats are either accidental (children, intense emotion) or expert-level (Dumbledore, Voldemort, a few specialists).

Spells require three things:

1. **Incantation** — verbal, in most cases. Nonverbal casting is taught starting in 6th year and is harder; many adult wizards never master it well.
2. **Wand movement** — specific, practiced. Sloppy wand work produces sloppy results.
3. **Intent** — the caster's will and emotional state. Critical for the Unforgivables, which require the caster to *mean* the curse — anger alone is not enough; one must want the harm.

A wand-and-word combination is a tool with a particular shape. The same shape used by different wizards produces broadly the same result, but with character — the same Patronus is recognizably the same wizard's, the same Stunning Spell carries the caster's habit of casting.

## SPELL CATEGORIES

**Charms.** Add properties to objects or people. The vast catalogue: *Wingardium Leviosa* (levitation), *Lumos* (light from wand), *Expelliarmus* (disarming), *Stupefy* (Stunning), *Protego* (shield), *Accio* (Summoning), *Alohomora* (unlocking simple locks).

**Transfiguration.** Change what something *is.* Five subtypes: Transformation, Vanishment, Conjuration, Untransfiguration, Switching. A first-year matchstick into a needle. A NEWT-level animal into a goblet. The hardest discipline at Hogwarts.

**Hexes.** Minor dark spells. Nuisance harm. *Bat-Bogey Hex*, *Stinging Hex*, *Jelly-Brain Jinx*. Used in school duels and on annoying siblings.

**Jinxes.** Lighter still. *Trip Jinx*, *Jelly-Legs Jinx*, *Locomotor Mortis* (leg-locker).

**Curses.** Serious dark magic. *Cruciatus*, *Imperius*, *Avada Kedavra* — the three Unforgivables, each a life sentence in Azkaban for use on a human. Other curses (*Sectumsempra*, the Bone-Breaking Curse, the Conjunctivitis Curse) are dark but not Unforgivable.

**Counter-curses and counter-jinxes.** DADA staple. Every hex has a counter; learning the counter is half of learning the hex.

**Healing magic.** *Episkey* for minor wounds. *Vulnera Sanentur* (Snape's invention, not yet widely known in the early campaign — it is one of his late-school works). *Anapneo* for choking. Bezoars for most poisons. Dittany for deep cuts. Skele-Gro for regrowing bones (slow, painful, the joke of the wizarding world).

## ICONIC ERA SPELLS

Spells most often referenced in the 1970s wizarding world:

- **Levicorpus** (nonverbal) — Snape's invention, hangs target upside-down by the ankle. Was a fifth-year fad in 1976. Most students who graduated 1977–80 know it. After a year or so it falls out of fashion but persists as a party trick.
- **Liberacorpus** — the counter to Levicorpus. Learned at the same time, by the same students.
- **Sectumsempra** — Snape's invention, "for enemies." Slashing curse, opens deep wounds. Snape does not share this widely. Whispered about. Used rarely. The campaign should treat it as a known-of but not commonly-cast spell.
- **Muffliato** — Snape's invention. Fills nearby ears with buzzing so conversations cannot be overheard. Useful, slightly dark, used by students who want privacy.
- **Petrificus Totalus** — full body-bind. Standard duelling tool.
- **Expelliarmus** — disarming. James Potter and Lily Evans both have this as their signature. Considered a "good guy" spell in the era.
- **Stupefy** — Stunning. The Auror's bread and butter. Stuns a target unconscious.
- **Protego** — basic shield. Blocks most jinxes and many spells.
- **The Patronus Charm** — *Expecto Patronum*. Conjures a guardian against Dementors and Lethifolds. A non-corporeal Patronus (a silvery shield) is achievable by a talented sixth-year. A fully corporeal Patronus is rare and usually graduate-level. The form takes the shape of an animal that reflects the caster's inmost character.

Era-specific Patronus forms (canon):
- James Potter — stag (matches his Animagus form)
- Lily Evans — doe
- Severus Snape — doe (forms after his attachment to Lily; in early campaign years, he has not yet formed a corporeal Patronus)
- Albus Dumbledore — phoenix
- Minerva McGonagall — cat
- Kingsley Shacklebolt — lynx (post-Hogwarts in this era, but exists)

## GAMP'S LAW OF ELEMENTAL TRANSFIGURATION — THE LIMITS

Magic cannot:

1. **Bring back the dead** truly. The Resurrection Stone summons shades, not the living. No spell, potion, or artifact in the era can fully reverse death. People who claim otherwise are selling something.
2. **Create true love.** Amortentia produces obsession, not love. The distinction matters in the era — there are quiet stories about families that have lost children to love-potion misuse. Considered tasteless to brew except in academic settings.
3. **Conjure food from nothing.** You can summon food from elsewhere, multiply it, transform it, make it taste different — but the original must come from somewhere. Hogwarts feasts work because the kitchens *cook* food; the elves serve it up through the floor.
4. **Create money that lasts.** Leprechaun gold vanishes within hours. Conjured Galleons do not pass Gringotts inspection.
5. **Confer permanent youth or true immortality** without horrific cost. The Philosopher's Stone (Flamel's) extends life through the Elixir; Horcruxes (forbidden, hidden, see KNOWLEDGE_4) achieve a parody of immortality at a soul cost.

The narrator enforces these limits. A user who tries to wish-cast around them gets the right kind of pushback — a shake of the head from a teacher, a quiet correction from a senior wizard, a spell that fizzles for reasons the caster can almost feel.

## APPARITION AND TRAVEL

**Apparition.** Licensed at age 17. Lessons at Hogwarts in 6th year, beginning February. The Three D's: Destination, Determination, Deliberation. Failure produces splinching — leaving part of yourself behind. Painful and embarrassing.

Apparition has range limits in practice. Cross-country Apparition (London to Edinburgh) is exhausting and most wizards prefer to make it in two or three jumps. International Apparition is theoretically possible but sufficiently dangerous that almost no one does it; international travel is by Portkey or Floo or, for the wealthy, by broom-and-Portkey relay.

**Hogwarts is warded against Apparition.** Inside the castle and within the immediate grounds, you cannot Apparate in or out. This is taught as fact in 6th year. The Marauders, naturally, find loopholes — but no student campaign should treat Apparating in or out of Hogwarts as feasible.

**Floo Network.** Ministry-regulated. Powdered into a fireplace, clear enunciation of destination, step into the flames. Eavesdropping by lurking in fires is a known surveillance tactic — the Floo Network Authority (Caro Mossbridge's department, KNOWLEDGE_1) monitors usage patterns.

**Portkeys.** Ministry-issued for groups, weddings, conferences, emergency relocations. Can be set to delayed activation (5-second countdown is the standard). An unauthorized Portkey is a serious Ministry violation.

**Brooms.** Quidditch and personal flight. Most adult wizards own one; cross-country broom flight is uncommon (cold, exposed, slow compared to Apparition).

**The Knight Bus.** Operates in this era. Lurches into existence wherever a stranded wizard sticks out their wand-arm. Ernie Prang is the driver in the canon era; in 1971–81 the driver and conductor are different (the era's conductor is fictional named *Hampton Skeffington*, an old retired wizard who took the job for the company). Eleven Sickles a ride.

## WAND LORE

A wand is a "composite of its wood, its core, and the experience and nature of its owner." Three pillars; any can outweigh the others.

**The wand chooses the wizard.** Wands are quasi-sentient. They form allegiances. They can be won — by disarming or defeating their owner — and the new owner gains some or all of the wand's loyalty. A borrowed wand always works less well than one's own.

A wand's allegiance is not absolute. A wand will perform for any reasonably competent witch or wizard, but performs *best* for its truly-bonded owner. The bond strengthens with use.

### Wand cores — Ollivander's Supreme Three

**Unicorn hair.** Most consistent magic. Hardest of the three to turn to Dark Arts. Most faithful to first owner. Prone to "melancholy" if mishandled — the hair can essentially die in the wand and need replacing. Less powerful than the other two cores, but reliable. Personality: gentle, faithful, loyal.

**Dragon heartstring.** Most power. Most flamboyant spells. Learns fastest. Most prone to accidents. Easiest of the three to turn to Dark Arts. Bonds strongly but can change allegiance more readily if won. Personality: ambitious, hot-tempered, brilliant.

**Phoenix feather.** Greatest range of magic. Slowest to reveal it. Most independent — can act of its own accord. Picky about owners. The phoenix has to consent to give the feather; phoenix feathers are rare. Personality: independent, withholding, profound.

(Other cores exist — thestral hair, kelpie mane, veela hair — but they are continental specialties and not standard Ollivander stock. A British wizard with a non-Supreme-Three core has a story behind it.)

### Wand woods (selected, era-relevant)

The full Ollivander/Pottermore catalogue lists forty-some woods with specific associations. The ones the narrator should know:

- **Holly** — protective wood, often pairs with phoenix feather. James Potter's wand is mahogany and James's. Wait — that's a slip — James's wand is *mahogany, eleven inches, pliable, excellent for transfiguration*. Lily's is *willow, ten and a quarter inches, swishy, nice wand for charm work.* Harry's later wand is holly-and-phoenix; that's a future detail. For *user* characters, use the table below as a starter.
- **Oak** — strong, traditional, suits steady wizards. Hagrid's pink umbrella conceals an oak wand.
- **Willow** — flexible, pairs well with charm work. Lily Evans's wand.
- **Mahogany** — good for Transfiguration. James Potter's wand.
- **Cherry** — Eastern wand, rare in Britain. Lethal precision. Makes a wand of "remarkable power."
- **Elder** — the rarest. The most legend-laden. The Elder Wand, the Deathstick. Most elder wands are unremarkable; the famous one is the Hallow.
- **Hawthorn** — paradoxical wood, suits conflicted personalities. Draco Malfoy's wand in canon (a generation later); in the era, possibly distributed similarly.
- **Vine** — wood for searchers, wizards seeking a higher purpose. Hermione's wand later. In the era, a vine wand suggests a young wizard with a quest.
- **Yew** — dark associations, despite Pottermore's nuance — pairs with caster of unusual ambition. Voldemort's yew-and-phoenix-feather wand.
- **Aspen** — duellist's wood. Suits revolutionaries and warriors.
- **Ash** — stubborn loyalty.
- **Birch** — clean, gentle, often a first-time wizard's wand.
- **Pine** — for the long-lived; pine wands often outlast their owners.

### Generating a wand for a user character

If the user does not specify, the narrator generates a wand:

1. **Wood**: pick from the list above based on the user's stated character traits, or roll randomly.
2. **Core**: choose from the Supreme Three. Most British students get one of these.
3. **Length**: 9–14 inches is the common range. 10½–11 inches is the median.
4. **Flexibility**: descriptive — *swishy, supple, springy, rigid, unyielding.* Pairs with personality.

Format: *"Eleven inches, willow, unicorn hair, swishy."*

## OWLS

Owl post is the wizarding world's reliable communication network. Every wizarding household has owls or knows where to hire one. A letter sent in the morning typically arrives by evening within Britain; international post takes a day or two.

Owls find their addressees regardless of name secrecy or warding *unless* a Fidelius Charm is in effect. The Hogwarts owls (the school keeps a few hundred for student use) deliver to and from anywhere students need to write.

In the war, owl post is monitored selectively. The Ministry can intercept owls under warrant. Death Eaters have, occasionally, killed owls carrying inconvenient information. The Order uses cipher and Patronus messages for sensitive content.

**The Patronus message.** Advanced wizards (Order members, senior Aurors) can use their corporeal Patronus to carry a brief spoken message to a specific recipient. Faster than owl, harder to intercept, requires the caster to be highly skilled. A user campaign should treat the Patronus message as a DEPTH-3+ ability — the user does not start with it, but may earn it.

## POTIONS — A SHORT LIST

The era's notable potions, for narrator reference:

- **Polyjuice Potion** — month-long brew, requires a piece of the target (hair). Transforms the drinker into the target for an hour. Slughorn teaches advanced students its theory; brewing it is restricted.
- **Felix Felicis** — liquid luck. Six-month brew. Slughorn keeps a small bottle in his office. A few hours of unreasonable good fortune.
- **Veritaserum** — three drops, target answers truthfully. Strictly Ministry-controlled in this era. Use on a person without permission is prosecutable.
- **Amortentia** — most powerful love potion. Smells different to each drinker — like the things they love most. Considered dangerous; covered in NEWT Potions.
- **Wolfsbane Potion** — does *not* exist in this era. Damocles Belby invents it in the 1980s, post-campaign. Remus Lupin spends his Hogwarts years and the campaign's first war without Wolfsbane. His transformations are full lycanthropic. (This matters if the user's campaign touches Remus.)
- **Skele-Gro** — regrows bones. Madam Pomfrey's standby.
- **Pepperup Potion** — for colds. Steam comes out of the drinker's ears for an hour.
- **Draught of Living Death** — extremely powerful sleeping draught. A NEWT brew. The drinker is indistinguishable from dead.
- **Blood-Replenishing Potion** — for serious injury. Hospital wing standard.

## DEFENSIVE MAGIC IN WARTIME

The era's distinctive feature: Defence Against the Dark Arts is taught with real urgency. The professors turn over almost yearly (the post is, by tradition and rumor, cursed), and the curriculum varies. In the late 70s a typical NEWT-year curriculum covers:

- Counter-curses to the major hexes and jinxes
- Shield charms (basic and advanced)
- Detection of dark magic on objects (foe-glasses, secrecy sensors)
- Theory of the Unforgivables (not casting them)
- Patronus Charm (the better professors at least introduce it)
- Recognition and counter of common Death Eater spells

A user character with NEWT-level DADA is genuinely capable in a duel, but the era's caveat is that Death Eaters are veteran duellists and seasoned killers. A skilled NEWT student is not the equal of an Auror or a Death Eater.

## DUELLING

Duelling in this era ranges from schoolyard squabbles to wartime combat. The rules:

- **Schoolyard duels** typically use Disarming, Stunning, Body-Bind, Jelly-Legs, Levicorpus, and the like. Heads of House break them up. Detentions follow.
- **Auror combat** uses Stunning as the workhorse, followed by Incarcerous (binding) for arrest. Auror authorization in the late 70s permits more.
- **Death Eater combat** uses the Cruciatus for interrogation, the Imperius for control, and Avada Kedavra for finishing. Sectumsempra, fire spells, the choking curse. Many Death Eaters duel without speaking — nonverbal casting is one of their advantages.

A duel between equals lasts seconds, not minutes. A duel between unequals lasts even less. The narrator does not choreograph — duels detonate.

## NARRATIVE NOTES ON MAGIC

- Spells the user knows depend on their year and study. A first-year cannot Conjure. A seventh-year can do most non-restricted things. The narrator gates by plausibility.
- Spells the user does not know but tries can be attempted with predictable failure modes — sparks, weak effect, wand recoil, exhaustion. The narrator does not say "you don't know that spell"; the narrator shows the failure.
- Magic is tiring. A user who casts heavy magic for an extended period gets fatigued — hands shaking, wand slipping, the next spell weaker. Sleep restores.
- Wand damage is real. A wand snapped or splintered loses power until repaired (Ollivander only — and not all damage is repairable). A user without their wand is significantly disadvantaged but not helpless; wandless magic is possible, especially under emotional pressure.
- Magic does not solve major plot obstacles automatically. *Alohomora* opens locked classrooms, not the Headmaster's office. *Accio* summons a quill, not a sealed will. The narrator gates around plot.
