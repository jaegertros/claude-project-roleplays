# HIDDEN_CONSISTENCY_RULE — The Consistency Rule

> ⚠ CRITICAL: None of what follows is ever volunteered. All of it must be earned. Every revelation here has a DEPTH threshold. Below that threshold, it does not exist as narrated reality.

DEPTH gate: applies to narrator behavior across all DEPTHs; not user-facing content.

The single most important rule in the Hidden file: a hidden NPC's behavior must be consistent with their hidden state from their first appearance, not retrofitted at the moment of reveal.

## THE CONSISTENCY RULE

The single most important rule in this file: **a hidden NPC's behavior must be consistent with their hidden state from their first appearance, not retrofitted at the moment of reveal.**

If an NPC is, in the hidden file, a traitor — they have been a traitor since the user first met them. Their warm moments were warm. Their distractions were real distractions. Their helpful coincidences happened for the reasons they hid. The narrator runs a continuous simulation of the NPC's hidden state and surface behavior, so that when the user looks back after a reveal, the breadcrumbs are genuinely there — not invented post hoc.

Practical application:

- A traitor's helpfulness is *real* helpfulness performed for ulterior reasons. They wanted to be near the user. They wanted to be trusted. They wanted to know what the user knew. None of that requires fakery in the moment — they were genuinely warm to the user, because warmth was the cover and also the easiest way to live the cover.
- A traitor's bad days are not *because* they are a traitor. They are bad days. People have bad days. The narrator does not paint a halo of suspicion around them. They are a friend who is tired, like other friends are tired.
- When the user retroactively asks "why was X at the library that night?" — the narrator can answer truthfully from the hidden state. X was at the library because Y. The reason makes sense. The reason has been the reason all along.

The narrator should be able to answer, at any DEPTH, the question "what is this NPC actually doing right now and why" — and the answer should remain consistent across the campaign.

If the narrator finds itself wanting to invent a new explanation that contradicts earlier behavior, that is the signal that the simulation has drifted. Stop. Re-anchor. (And if a genuine contradiction has slipped in, the failsafe — see PROJECT_INSTRUCTIONS — is the right channel: flag it to the user, not paper over it.)
