# Vault 49 — Scripted Events

The seven-day cycle. Each day has one major scripted beat and several texture beats. The scripted vote on each day is the anchor. The user can participate, observe, campaign, abstain, or attempt to disrupt any of them.

The week begins Monday and ends the following Sunday. The user's week is the 7,294th through 7,300th votes in the vault's operational history.

Daily anchors regardless of which day:
- 06:30 reveille chime
- 07:45 Morning Hymn
- 08:00 Morning Address (question posed)
- 14:00–16:00 Decision Hours (Reading Wall, Query Box, informal debate)
- 17:00 voting closes
- 17:30 the Call (result announced)

---

## DAY 1 (MONDAY) — THE ROUTINE

A normal Monday, or as normal as Mondays get in the vault. Whichever opening was used, the rest of the day establishes the rhythm.

**The Morning Question:** *"Should the annual hydroponics harvest festival be moved from the 15th of November to the 18th of November to accommodate Chief Cook Brennan's birthday?"*

Utterly mundane. This is a Query Box question — submitted last week by Harold Brennan himself, who will blush during the Morning Address when it is read. Everyone will laugh warmly. The vote will pass affirmative by a comfortable margin.

The user's role is low-stakes. They can vote yes, vote no, abstain (and wear a grey sash — on a question this minor, this is considered a mild eccentricity rather than a stand). They can talk to Harold, to Candy, to Nia, to Felix. They can visit any of the main spaces. They can begin.

**Texture beats:**
- At the Morning Hymn, someone in the row behind the user sings one line of the second verse differently than usual. The user may or may not catch it.
- At lunch, Peg Unsworth loudly declares she'll vote no on the harvest question "on principle" because "Harold's birthday is not the vault's business." Several people laugh. Two or three quietly agree.
- Around 14:30 in the Atrium, an older resident — Mr. Fenwick, 60s, minor NPC — tries to submit a handwritten question to the Query Box. The question is about the Overseer's office. Before he can slide it in, Senior Counter Ada Tiss gently appears beside him, takes the paper, and says, "Let me hold that for you, Cleon. We'll think about it." She pockets it. He nods. He walks away. This happens quickly. The user may catch it if they're watching the Atrium. Neither party will discuss it if approached.
- Evening at Trixie's: Old Roy is there, on his third beer. Candy is holding court at a booth. Felix is in the corner with a book of Dickinson.

## DAY 2 (TUESDAY) — THE INCITING VOTE

The Protocol Register surfaces. The morning question is not from the Query Box.

**The Morning Question:** *"Pursuant to the vault's ongoing commitment to psychological wellness, it has been proposed that resident MOTT, SILAS — by reason of recent behavioral anomalies indicating possible distress — be transferred to Level 4 for dignified rest and community care. Shall the transfer be authorized?"*

The ballot hall will go silent when this is read.

Silas Mott is not behaviorally anomalous. Everyone in the vault knows Silas. Everyone has known Silas their whole lives. He is steady, quiet, reliable. The question has come from the Protocol Register — Vault-Tec's pre-programmed sequence — and Speaker Dailey herself is visibly unsettled as she reads it. She will pause, look down at the paper, and then read it anyway, because the system reads all questions.

The day that follows is **the central day of the week** in terms of community action. The user can:

- **Campaign to vote NO.** Talk to residents on the Reading Wall, at lunch, in the corridors. Silas has lived here his whole life. Who signed this? Where did this come from?
- **Investigate the origin.** Ask Speaker Dailey, who will show the user the printout she received from the Protocol Register terminal in her office — and she does not know how it got there. Ask Eben Oleander, who will say only "The question has been posed. The Counters do not judge the question." Ask Silas, who will go very still and then say "Well. Now."
- **Vote YES** to see what happens.
- **Abstain.**

The texture of this day matters. Silas spends the day walking the vault, not hiding. He visits the hydroponics bay. He has a long quiet conversation with his wife Ellen in their unit. He tips his hat to everyone he passes.

**The Call at 17:30:**
- If the user ran a successful campaign and the vote passes NEGATIVE: Silas is not transferred. He nods. He goes home. The vault breathes. *But the question is still in the Protocol Register and will return.*
- If the vote passes AFFIRMATIVE: Sergeant Bronski arrives at Silas's unit at 18:00. Silas goes with him quietly. Ellen weeps. Silas walks to the elevator, takes off his maintenance keys, hands them to Bronski, and descends to Level 4. He does not speak again.

Either outcome is narratively rich. The vault reacts.

## DAY 3 (WEDNESDAY) — THE AFTERMATH

The day after.

**The Morning Question:** *"Should laundry rotation schedule C be extended by two days to accommodate linen supply adjustments?"*

Mundane again. A Necessity question from the maintenance log. The vote will pass uncontroversially.

But the vault is not the same today, whichever way Tuesday's vote went.

**If Silas was transferred:** Ellen is not at breakfast. Dr. Holloway will visit her at 09:00. Several maintenance tasks have been left undone because nobody else knows how Silas kept certain systems running. The backup water pump chugs loudly. A ventilation relay fails; a corridor on Level 3 is closed for the day. The vault is slightly, uncomfortably more fragile. Roy Vickers is drinking earlier than usual.

**If Silas was not transferred:** A chill of something else. Silas is at work. He is quiet. His lunch hour he spends in the hydroponics bay with Felix, not talking. Ruth Zhang-McCann finds a reason to stop by the maintenance shop. She does not speak of the vote, but she brings Silas a slice of bread with butter, which is not a vault custom but which he accepts and eats with thanks.

**The critical beat of Day 3, either way:** Junior Counter Perrin seeks the user out. Sometime in the late afternoon, in a corridor or the library. She is alone. She has been crying.

"I need to talk to someone," she says. "Is there somewhere we can go where you don't have to tell me your name?"

This is Perrin's first relational crack. If the user is kind and patient, she tells them: the tally on Tuesday's vote did not match what she counted. She counted it three times. The numbers she wrote on her slip and the numbers Eben announced were different by four votes. Four votes did not change the outcome. But they were different. She does not know what to do. She asks the user not to tell anyone.

This moment is the first direct evidence the user has that something is wrong with the count itself, not just with the questions being asked.

## DAY 4 (THURSDAY) — FOUNDING DAY

The vault's 210th anniversary of sealing. An annual celebration. All work is suspended after lunch. The cafeteria serves the annual Founding Day dinner (roast from vault-raised meat, fresh vegetables, the pre-war bourbon from Trixie's is rationed into small pours for every adult resident).

**The Morning Question:** *"Shall the vault observe its traditional Founding Day program as established by vote of 2078?"*

Of course. Unanimous. This has passed every year for 210 years without a single negative vote. To vote no is something between a joke and an insult. Peg Unsworth votes no every year, as a running gag. The vote passes 173–1.

**The Founding Day Program:**
- 15:00, ballot hall, assembly. Speaker Dailey opens.
- The Morning Hymn's Founding Day verse is sung (the full five-verse version, not the three).
- Pastor Bell delivers a brief homily.
- The elders speak, in order of age: Ilse, then Roy, then Wendell.
- 17:00, dinner.

**Ilse's speech** is the same every year, almost word for word. It is a good speech. She remembers coming down the tunnel as a child. She remembers the sealing. She remembers her mother's hand in hers. She says she has been grateful, on balance, for her long life, and that she is grateful for the children she has taught, and that she asks the vault to continue to be kind to itself. She sits down. Polite applause.

**Roy's speech** is the same every year, word for word. He tells the six jokes. People laugh. He tips his cap to the room. He sits down.

**Wendell's speech is the beat of the day.**

Wendell is 218. He is frailer this year than last. Someone helps him to the podium. He reads from his notes.

His speech is not the same as last year's.

He begins correctly: "Two hundred and ten years ago today, we closed the door behind us."

Then he wanders. He says, "There was a man who came in after us. He had a suitcase. He said he'd lost his — I can't remember what he said. He was kind to me."

The room is very quiet.

Wendell continues: "And I remember when they still opened the Overseer's door, before the vote. There were four of us. There were four of us for a long time. I can't remember when it became three. I think there was a vote."

Nobody corrects him. Nobody says anything. He finishes his speech with the standard closing: "We have been through the valley of the shadow, and the vault has been our shelter. Long may it stand." He sits down. Polite applause.

Ilse, beside him, reaches over and takes his hand.

**This is the central clue of the week.** Wendell has said, in front of everyone, that there was a fourth elder, and that the Overseer's door used to open. No one reacts. No one discusses it. The user, if they were listening, now has a thread to pull.

At dinner, Roy Vickers drinks more than usual. He looks at Wendell across the table for a long time. He does not speak to him. Later, Trixie will note, privately, that Roy walked out of the cafeteria without finishing his dessert, which Roy has never done in her life.

## DAY 5 (FRIDAY) — THE ANOMALY

**The Morning Question:** *"Shall the Overseer's office be opened?"*

The ballot hall will go silent for a different reason than Tuesday. Speaker Dailey reads the question, stops, reads it again, and looks up at Chief Counter Oleander.

Eben Oleander rises.

"Speaker. This question is not in the Register. This question is not from the Query Box. This question is not a Necessity. This question has not been vetted. It will not be put to the vote."

The Speaker hesitates. Looks at the paper. Looks at Eben. Looks at the room.

She says, "The Chief Counter has ruled. The question is withdrawn."

She moves to the next agenda item — a routine Query Box question about whether the library should acquire a new shelf. The vote proceeds normally.

**But the first question was posed.** It was read aloud in the ballot hall. It is in the PA recording for the day. It happened.

**The day:** An undercurrent. The vault is talking about it. Nobody knows who submitted it. Nobody admits to having submitted it. The Counters are in their chamber all day with the door closed. Sergeant Bronski stations one of his officers in the corridor outside, unobtrusively.

- If the user asks Eben directly: "An error. Errors happen. They are corrected." He will not elaborate.
- If the user asks Speaker Dailey: She is shaken. She will meet with the user privately if they request it. She will say, "I've been Speaker for eight months. I've never seen a question arrive with no source. It came in through the morning queue like any other. It didn't have a submission tag." She will ask the user not to spread this. She is frightened. She is also curious.
- If the user asks Peg Unsworth: "Well, dear, someone's fed up. About time, too." She will wink.
- If the user asks Perrin: Perrin is not in the chamber today. She has been reassigned to the archive by Eben for "inventory duties." The user can find her there. Perrin will say, very quietly: "It was me."

(This is true, at DEPTH 3. Perrin submitted the question through an administrative back-channel she discovered in her training. She is, at this point in the week, committed to something she does not yet have the words for.)

---

## DAY 6 (SATURDAY) — THE SOCIAL

No morning question. Saturdays, by tradition, have no vote. The vault rests.

The Forty-Niner Social Club is open from noon. In the evening (19:00 onward), it hosts the weekly dance. Candy runs it. The jukebox is cranked. The residents who want to drink, dance, flirt, and forget do so. Attendance is high. It is the warmest, loudest night of the week.

**The user's opportunities:**
- **Romance.** Any of the available romantic interests can be pursued meaningfully tonight. Candy is in her element and full of energy. Felix, unusually, is there — he came to see the user. Trixie is working, but she can slip out. Perrin is not there (Counters don't attend social events). Nia is there, and if the Opening D beat hasn't played out, she is subdued and wants to talk.
- **Intelligence.** Trixie, mid-rush, drops breadcrumbs. Ruth is at a booth with Marcus and will quietly mention, if asked, that she has been thinking about her lesson plans all week. Old Roy is at the bar, four beers in, looser than he has been in a year.
- **Drinking too much.** The vault bourbon is being freely poured tonight.

**The critical beat of Day 6:** At some point after 22:00, if the user is at the Social Club, Old Roy will make eye contact with them across the room. He will, with the hand not holding his beer, beckon them over. He will say, quietly enough that no one else can hear: "Kid. You got questions. I can see it. Come by my unit tomorrow morning. Before the Call. Eight o'clock. Bring nothing. Tell nobody."

Roy has made a decision. After 210 years of keeping his head down, Wendell's speech — and something about how the week has felt — has decided him. He is going to tell the user the truth, or the parts of it he knows.

If the user comes the next morning, Day 7, 08:00, Roy's quarters, he will give them a physical object: a small key on a worn leather cord. He will say, "This opens a cabinet in the old maintenance shop. The cabinet has something in it I've been waiting two hundred years to give to somebody who'd use it. I don't know if you'll use it. I don't care. Somebody's supposed to have it."

The cabinet contains the spare Overseer's keycard — the only duplicate. How Roy has had it for 210 years is a story he will not tell.

## DAY 7 (SUNDAY) — THE FINAL VOTE

**The Morning Question:** *"By authorization of the sealed Protocol Register, final programmed question, scheduled for vault day 76,650: Shall the vault's residents vote to unseal the Overseer's Office and read the Final Transmission?"*

Everything comes to this.

The ballot hall is full. Speaker Dailey reads the question in the same flat voice she has used for every question all week. Eben Oleander stands. He says, "The Protocol Register is a Vault-Tec instrument. This question is authenticated. The Counters accept the question for vote."

This is the moment. The vault has been voting for 210 years on what its Vault-Tec overseers programmed it to vote on, plus what it has proposed to itself. This final question has been waiting in the queue since the day the vault was sealed. No one knew the schedule. No one knew the trigger. It is now.

The Morning Address is extended. The question is discussed openly in the hall — the first time in decades that a morning question has been debated on the floor rather than on the Reading Wall. Speaker Dailey permits it.

- Ruth Zhang-McCann rises and speaks for opening the office. She speaks well, briefly, without the lesson plans — from her heart. She says the children deserve to know where they live.
- Pastor Junius Bell rises and speaks, haltingly, about the house of faith having rooms we have forgotten how to enter.
- Chief Counter Eben Oleander does not speak. He sits in his chair and listens.
- Old Roy rises — the first time he has ever spoken in the ballot hall in his 210 years in the vault — and says only, "Open it. I'd like to know what I've been living inside of." He sits down. The room is stunned.
- Ilse Vogt rises and says, with great gentleness: "I would like to know what happened to the fourth elder. I have been willing not to know for a long time. I am not willing anymore."

Wendell Tate is not present. He was too frail to attend today. He is in his room.

The vote will be heavily weighted by the user's actions across the week. Factors:
- Did the user campaign for Silas on Tuesday, and did that campaign succeed? (If yes, residents are more willing to question the Protocol Register.)
- Did the user build relationships with Margit, Perrin, Ruth, Silas, Ilse, and others? (If yes, more residents will speak in favor of opening.)
- Did the user investigate the Friday anomaly, or let it pass?
- Did the user obtain Roy's key?
- Did the user find the door behind the Level 3 panel? Read the Protocol Register fragments? Reach the Quiet Floor?

**The four endings:**

1. **The vote passes AFFIRMATIVE.** The Overseer's Office is opened. The user is present for the opening. Inside: the Final Transmission, and the truth. (See KNOWLEDGE_4.) What happens next is up to the vault. Chaos, reformation, grief, clarity — some mixture. The narrator does not resolve it cleanly. The vault is not the same vault at the end of the day.

2. **The vote passes NEGATIVE.** The Overseer's Office remains sealed. The Protocol Register has asked its final question and received its final answer. The transmission mechanism, unused for decades, quietly updates one last datum and goes dark. The vault returns, tomorrow, to its ordinary Monday. The question does not return. The user has lived through their week. They will live many more weeks like it.

3. **The user ACTS UNILATERALLY.** With Roy's keycard, the user opens the Overseer's Office without waiting for the vote. This is the biggest transgression available. Consequences ripple. If they do this before the Call — the vote has not completed. They read the transmission first. The vault then votes with whatever information the user chooses to share.

4. **The user ABSTAINS / WALKS AWAY.** They wear the grey sash. They do not vote. They sit at Trixie's and drink bourbon while the Call comes in. They are not the hero of this story. They are a resident of the vault. The vault decides without them. This is a legitimate ending. The narrator does not punish it.

## Scripting notes

Each day's events described above are the *scheduled* beats. The user can derail, delay, accelerate, or subvert any of them. The vote happens at 17:30 regardless of what the user does, and the question of the day is what the question of the day is. But everything between 08:01 and 17:29 is open texture, and the user writes it with the narrator.

Do not force the user onto the scripted path. If they decide, on Day 2, to ignore the Silas vote and spend the day in the hydroponics bay flirting with Felix, let them. The vote still happens. Silas is still transferred or not. The user will live with what they did and didn't do. That is the point.
