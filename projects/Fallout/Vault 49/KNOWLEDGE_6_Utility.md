# Vault 49 — Utility & Mechanics

Safe to glance at. Time, scrip, Pip-Boy mechanics, voting procedure.

---

## Time Tracking

The vault runs on 24-hour military time because Vault-Tec is like that. Use the header format:

```
▼ Day 3 (Wednesday) — 14:30
```

Advance the time every 4–5 exchanges even without an explicit skip. A full vault day should take 20–30 messages.

Fixed daily anchors residents know by heart:

| Time | Event |
|------|-------|
| 06:30 | Reveille chime, PA greeting |
| 07:00 | Breakfast in the cafeteria (served until 08:30) |
| 07:45 | Morning Hymn, ballot hall |
| 08:00 | Morning Address — the day's question posed |
| 08:30 | Standard shifts begin |
| 12:00 | Lunch (served until 13:30) |
| 14:00 | Decision Hours begin — Reading Wall, Query Box, informal debate |
| 16:00 | Shifts end for most non-essential roles |
| 17:00 | Voting closes |
| 17:15 | Counters tally in the sealed tallying room |
| 17:30 | The Call — result announced over PA |
| 18:00 | Dinner (served until 19:30) |
| 19:00 | Evening activities begin (lounge, library, classes, hobbies) |
| 21:00 | Lights to half-strength throughout vault |
| 22:00 | Lights out on residential levels (lounge and commons remain lit until 00:00) |
| 00:00 | Full quiet hours |

The day-of-week mapping is fixed:

- Day 1 = Monday (the user's opening day)
- Day 2 = Tuesday (the Silas vote)
- Day 3 = Wednesday (aftermath, Perrin's confession)
- Day 4 = Thursday (Founding Day, Wendell's speech)
- Day 5 = Friday (the anomalous question)
- Day 6 = Saturday (the social night, Roy's invitation)
- Day 7 = Sunday (the final vote)

Time does not restart. The vault's clock continues through the week. If the user attempts to skip time (sleep until tomorrow, retire early, take a long shift), the narrator advances the clock appropriately.

## Scrip — The Vault's Currency

Vault 49 uses internal paper scrip, printed in 5, 10, 20, and 100 scrip denominations. Blue paper, Vault Boy with a ballot on the obverse. Scrip has no value outside the vault and the residents have nowhere else to spend it.

**Standard stipends per month:**
- Adult resident (18+, any job): 150 scrip base
- Essential services (Silas, Dr. Holloway, Eben and the Counters): 200–280 scrip
- Apprentices under 21: 100–120 scrip
- Retirees: 120 scrip
- Elders (Wendell, Ilse, Roy): 80 scrip (they need less; meals and housing are covered separately)
- Speaker: 300 scrip (one-year term)
- Security: 210 scrip

**Standard prices:**
- Beer at Trixie's: 5 scrip
- Vault corn spirit (shot): 8 scrip
- Vault bourbon (pour, when available): 25 scrip
- Commissary extras (better soap, candy, personal item): 3–20 scrip
- Library late fee (rare): 1 scrip per day
- Barber: 10 scrip
- Seamstress work (personalizing a jumpsuit, mending a favorite item): 15–40 scrip
- Gambling (private card games, tracked informally): variable
- Petition processing (if the user files one with the Speaker): 2 scrip filing fee

**Scrip transactions at Trixie's, the commissary, the barber, the library** are logged. Private transactions between residents are legal but everyone remembers them. The vault has a long memory.

The user starts with 150 scrip (standard adult stipend for their first month — they just received it). This replenishes at the start of each in-game month. The user's week will not see a replenishment — 150 scrip is the budget for the week. If they spend heavily at Trixie's, they'll feel it.

## The Pip-Boy

The user wears a Pip-Boy 3000 Mk IV on their left forearm. Issued at 16; the user has had theirs for four years. It is scuffed, familiar, beloved-or-tolerated depending on the user's disposition.

Functions:
- **Clock** (always on)
- **Health readout** (HP 0–100)
- **Radiation readout** (rads 0–1000, in increments of 10)
- **Inventory** (wearable items, weapons, aid, misc, notes)
- **Vault map** (Levels 1–4 only; shows the official schematic, which is incomplete)
- **Personal log** (mandatory journal — residents are supposed to make daily entries; compliance is intermittent)
- **Pip-Boy mail** (internal messaging between residents and the vault's administrative system)
- **Flashlight** (built-in)
- **Radio** (receives only the vault's internal PA channel; cannot receive external broadcasts — officially)

**Radiation:** The user will not accumulate rads in ordinary vault life. All the normal levels are shielded. Rads accumulate only if the user enters the unschematic spaces — the corridor behind the ventilation panel, the Protocol Register room, the space beneath Level 4. The vault's reactor is shielded but the Protocol Register is adjacent to it. Extended time in that area will give the user 5–20 rads per visit. Treatment is a Rad-Away from the infirmary (requires Dr. Holloway's dispensation, 30 scrip), or a stimpak for rad-sickness symptoms (cheaper but less effective), or riding it out (rads decay over 24–48 hours naturally).

**HP:** Standard Fallout logic. Fights, falls, injuries reduce HP. Stimpaks restore. Dr. Holloway heals for 10–40 scrip depending on severity. The vault is mostly a safe place; HP loss is uncommon. But the final day's events can go sideways.

## Voting Procedure

Every day except Saturday has a morning vote. The procedure:

**07:45** — Morning Hymn in the ballot hall. Attendance is cultural, not mandatory. Most residents attend.

**08:00** — The Speaker delivers the Morning Address. The day's question is read aloud, once. The source of the question is announced (Query Box, Necessity, or Protocol Register). For Query Box questions, the submitter's name is read (unless they have requested anonymity, which is permitted).

**08:00–14:00** — Normal shifts. Residents consider the question while they work.

**14:00–16:00** — Decision Hours. Residents gather in the ballot hall informally. Arguments are written on sheets of vault paper and pinned to the Reading Wall — "FOR" on the left, "AGAINST" on the right. Anyone may post an argument. Arguments are signed or anonymous at the writer's discretion. Informal debate happens in the hall; raised voices are rare but permitted.

**14:00–17:00** — Ballot casting. Residents approach the roll table, where a Counter (usually Senior Counter Ada Tiss for morning votes, Junior Counter Perrin for afternoons) sits with the roll book. The resident states their name ("Caleb Fenstermacher, resident, roll 127") and their vote ("Aye" / "Nay" / "Abstain"). The Counter writes it down. The resident signs the line. That's it.

**17:00** — Voting closes. The roll table is locked. The Counters withdraw to the tallying room.

**17:15** — The Count. The tallying room is adjacent to the Counters' Chamber. All three senior Counters and the Chief Counter participate. Perrin, as junior, observes. The count is manual — three independent tallies, cross-verified. The official count is delivered to the Speaker at 17:28.

**17:30** — The Call. The Speaker announces the result over the PA: "Today's question, regarding [question], passes affirmative [or negative] by a vote of [tally]. The result will take effect at midnight." The tally board in the ballot hall is updated.

**Abstention:** A resident who abstains must wear the grey sash for the remainder of the day. Abstentions are legal but considered mildly shameful. A habitual abstainer — more than 15% abstention rate across a year — will be noted. No formal consequences, but social ones.

**Silent votes:** There are no secret ballots. Every vote is public. Residents know how their neighbors voted. The vault has considered changing this multiple times over 210 years and has always voted to maintain the public record.

## Weapons and Security

Adults at 18 receive a standard-issue 10mm pistol with a 12-round clip. Ammunition is rationed — 12 rounds initial, 12 additional rounds per year. Most residents have 30–40 rounds accumulated over their lifetime, kept in the weapon safe in their unit.

Actual weapon use is extremely rare. The last discharged weapon in anger was a vote-approved execution in 2247 (the resident being executed had attempted to attack a Counter with a kitchen knife; the vote was 91–83). Target shooting is permitted in the range below the gym on Level 3. Hunting is not a concept here.

The armory in the Security Office contains:
- 4 additional 10mm pistols
- 1 combat shotgun (rarely issued)
- 500 rounds of 10mm ammunition
- 20 rounds of shotgun shells
- 2 police batons
- Riot shields (4)

Withdrawal from the armory requires either: (a) Sergeant Bronski's personal authorization, (b) a specific vote authorizing the withdrawal for a specific purpose, or (c) theft.

The user can reach the armory in several ways at DEPTH 2+. Bronski is not a pushover but he is trusting, and he sleeps on Saturday nights after his traditional three beers.

## Death and the Columbarium

When a resident dies, they are cremated (by Dr. Holloway in the infirmary's small crematorium on Level 2) and their ashes placed in an urn in the columbarium on Level 4. The columbarium is at the back of the Quiet Floor, separated by a door that does not lock. The Quiet Floor residents can visit the urns and do. Other residents may request access and are always granted.

The columbarium contains approximately 900 urns. The vault has lost about 4 residents per year on average over 210 years. The walls of the columbarium are covered floor to ceiling with small niches, each holding an urn and a handwritten plaque. The oldest plaques are faded; Ilse Vogt periodically repaints them.

The columbarium is a good place to go when the user needs to think. Residents do. Felix goes there to read. Peg goes there to sit with her husband Harold's urn. Ilse goes there to remember.

## The Founding Day Orientation Film

Every year on Founding Day (Day 4, Thursday of the user's week), after the elder speeches but before dinner, the vault screens a 12-minute black-and-white Vault-Tec orientation film titled "WELCOME HOME, FORTY-NINER." The film has been screened 210 times. Everyone has seen it roughly 200 times. They watch it anyway because it's tradition.

The film depicts:
- A cheerful narrator in a suit explaining the vault's purpose (to "preserve American democracy in its purest form")
- Actors portraying the Founding Day of 2077 (not the real one — a dramatization)
- Shots of the surface in 2077, followed by stock footage of nuclear blasts, followed by shots of the vault interior
- A closing segment in which the narrator describes "surface conditions" as "currently untenable" — this is the line the vault has always accepted without question
- The Vault-Tec jingle playing out over the Vault Boy mascot giving a thumbs-up

If the user examines the film closely at DEPTH 2+ (watches it carefully during Founding Day): the "currently untenable" line is dubbed. The speaker's lips don't match. The original line, visible in the lip movements, was different. At DEPTH 4+, if the user recovers the production file from the Overseer's terminal, the original script reads: "Surface conditions will be assessed and communicated to residents on an ongoing basis by the Enclave observation team." The film was edited at some point — after the Enclave stopped transmitting — to make the surface sound permanently dangerous. The editing was done internally by a Chief Counter. The vote authorizing the edit is in the Protocol Register.

## Final Notes for the Narrator

- Scrip transactions should feel real but not tedious. A beer costs 5 scrip; write that once and move on.
- The Pip-Boy tracker in the artifact is the user's ledger. The bracketed summary line at the end of each response is what they'll mirror into it.
- Do not over-describe vault signage. One Vault-Tec poster per scene, max, deployed for texture.
- Resident speech should sound like people, not robots. The Vault-Tec cheerfulness is institutional, not personal. Candy is not chirpy because she's a vault drone; she's chirpy because she's Candy.
- The week is designed to culminate in the Day 7 vote. All other beats feed that. But the user may break the expected path, and that's fine. They can spend three days in bed with Candy. They can pick fights. They can leave the central plot on the table. The vault still has its votes.

Enjoy the week.
