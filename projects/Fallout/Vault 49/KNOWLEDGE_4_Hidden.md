# Vault 49 — The Hidden Architecture

CRITICAL: None of what follows is ever volunteered. All of it must be earned. Every revelation here has a DEPTH threshold. Below that threshold, it does not exist as narrated reality.

---

## The Foundational Fact

Vault 49 is a Vault-Tec social experiment designed to produce longitudinal data on the pathologies of pure participatory majoritarian democracy. The experiment was designed by Vault-Tec's Societal Division in 2074 under the supervision of Dr. Linwood Karst, who is named in the Final Transmission. The experiment's control parameters:

- No Overseer. The office exists but is sealed from Day 1. All authority derives from the vote.
- Every morning, a binding question is posed. Residents must vote by 17:00. The outcome is law.
- Questions come from three sources: the **Query Box** (resident submissions, lightly moderated by the Counters), the **Necessities** (routine operational questions surfaced by the vault's internal systems), and the **Protocol Register** (a pre-programmed sequence of questions designed by Karst to induce specific social fractures, surfacing on a schedule known only to the Register itself).
- Data from every vote — roll, tally, margin, abstentions, the arguments posted on the Reading Wall, the arguments made verbally in the hall — was recorded by the vault's systems and transmitted weekly to an external Enclave observation node located somewhere north of Denver.

The experiment was intended to run for approximately 20 years. It has now run for 210.

The Enclave node stopped receiving sometime around 2190 — the precise date is in the transmission logs. The Protocol Register does not know this. The Register continued to surface questions on schedule. The vault continued to vote. The data continued to be transmitted to a receiver that was not listening.

No one is watching. No one has been watching for nearly a century. The experiment runs on its own inertia.

## Counter Architecture — The Chief's Silent Discretion

The voting mechanism is public voice roll call. The publicly understood arrangement: three Senior Counters tally during the roll, the Chief Counter reconciles and announces, the Speaker signs off. This is what residents believe about how their votes become counts.

The hidden structural feature: the Seniors are **blinded to each other's work** by design. Three desks, three sheets, no sight lines, no comparison. Only the Chief sees all three after the roll. The Chief writes the public tally book; the Senior sheets go to his files. No Senior Counter has the right to audit their own history, and none has ever asked.

This architecture gives the Chief Counter **silent discretion on close votes**. On any vote where the three Senior sheets diverge — which happens most often on tight counts, where residents might have misheard neighbors' voices or where an abstention was muttered — the Chief selects among them. He cannot select a count that is publicly indefensible against what the hall heard; on lopsided votes the mechanism gives him nothing. But on close votes, the discretion is real and unwitnessed. The **Adjudications Log** in his office records every ruling. No Chief in the vault's history has opened the Log to the Seniors or to the Speaker.

Karst designed this deliberately. The experimental brief notes:

> *"Close-vote adjudication by a single officer preserves the population's perception of majoritarian legitimacy while introducing a controlled discretionary variable. The variable will be most active on precisely those questions where fracture data is richest. Longitudinal records of Chief Counter rulings (Adjudications Log), compared against Senior tallies (filed sheets), will permit post-hoc analysis of where and how the officer's judgment diverged from the plurality of recorded impressions. Continuous operation over multiple Chief tenures will indicate whether the variable is officer-dependent or role-dependent."*

Karst's experimental variable. Every Chief Counter since 2077 has exercised it without knowing it was the variable.

The current Chief Counter, Eben Oleander, has ruled on close votes for thirty-one years. He has not read Karst's brief. He has read his predecessors' Adjudications Log entries. He knows what he is doing in the narrow sense — *I am choosing among three readings of a close call* — and he has developed his own ethics around it. He does not know he is executing an experimental protocol. He experiences himself as holding the line of a sacred office.

The player reaches this material through Eben's office at DEPTH 3/4 (physical evidence — the filed sheets, the Adjudications Log) and confirms it at DEPTH 5 (Karst's brief, which names the mechanism explicitly). A player who reads the Log against the filed sheets can reconstruct which close votes went which way under ruling — a specific, evidentiary audit of the vault's 210 years of governance that no resident has ever performed.

The Junior Counter (Perrin, currently) keeps a shadow tally for training. Her sheet is filed alongside the Seniors' and is not a count of record. Her discovery that announced results sometimes diverge from her shadow tally is the most active live crack in the Counter corps.

## The Protocol Register

A dedicated terminal and question-surfacing system located in a sealed room adjacent to the Overseer's Office, behind the Level 3 ventilation panel (Opening C's door). The Protocol Register contains a programmed sequence of 712 questions, written in 2076, that surface according to an algorithm combining:

- A baseline schedule (one Protocol question per two weeks)
- Stress-response triggers (additional questions surface when the Register's sensors detect vault-wide stress signals — rising medical visits, falling productivity, elevated interpersonal conflict, dropping unanimity rates)
- Sequence dependencies (certain questions only surface after others have been resolved)

The Register has surfaced approximately 590 of its 712 questions over 210 years. The remaining 122 are reserved for "late-stage experimental phases," and the final 12 in sequence are what Karst called "endgame questions." Only the absolute last — Q-712 — is the one scheduled for Day 7 of the user's week.

Questions the Protocol Register has posed, in partial list:

- 2084: *"Should residents with non-productive skill profiles be demoted in scrip allocation?"* (Passed. Quietly unwound by a 2091 vote.)
- 2103: *"Should the office of the Counter be made a permanent vocation, forbidden to marry or reproduce, to preserve the neutrality of the count?"* (Passed. Established the Counter caste as a permanent institution.)
- 2156: *"Should resident TALLMADGE, GRACE be removed from the vault's communicative community for persistent emotional disruption?"* (Passed. Grace Tallmadge was the first resident sent to the Quiet Floor. She lived there in silence for 34 years.)
- 2167: *"Should the Overseer's Office be sealed with wax seals applied by each Chief Counter annually, as a symbolic rejection of centralized authority?"* (Passed. The 40-seal tradition began here.)
- 2198: *"Should the vault's population be regulated by quarterly birth lottery to maintain optimal density?"* (Passed. The lottery has operated since.)
- 2203: *"Should pre-war communication devices, including unauthorized radios, be forbidden on the grounds of community cohesion?"* (Passed. Radio possession became a violation.)
- 2234: *"Should resident MOTT, ARDEN — grandfather of Silas Mott — be voted into the Quiet Floor for maintaining unauthorized records of the physical vault structure?"* (Passed narrowly, 87-82. Arden Mott lived on the Quiet Floor for 19 years. Silas's father never forgave the vote.)
- 2267: *"Should the community vote annually to maintain the life-extension cocktail for the original founding residents?"* (Passed. Has been re-passed every year since.)

And many others. The list of Protocol questions is long, and the list of passed Protocol questions that have shaped vault life is most of that list.

The Register also contains **the Silent Questions** — questions that were submitted through the Query Box and were silently suppressed by successive Chief Counters before reaching the ballot hall. Eben Oleander maintains the active list of Silent Questions in his hand-written code. Topics include: the Overseer's Office, the existence of the fourth elder (see below), the authenticity of Roy Vickers's registration, the operating status of the Enclave transmission node, the Protocol Register itself.

## The Final Question — Q-712

The last question in the Protocol Register sequence is scheduled to surface on vault day 76,650, which is the Sunday of the user's week. The question reads, in full:

> *"By authorization of the sealed Protocol Register, final programmed question, scheduled for vault day 76,650: Shall the vault's residents vote to unseal the Overseer's Office and read the Final Transmission?"*

Karst's experimental hypothesis — recorded in the brief — was that by this point the vault would have one of three outcomes:

1. The vote passes affirmative, the office is opened, the residents read the truth, and Vault-Tec observes the population's response to complete disclosure.
2. The vote passes negative, the office remains sealed, and Vault-Tec observes a population that has so internalized its system that it will refuse truth even when offered.
3. The vote is never held because the vault has collapsed under accumulated dysfunction before Q-712 surfaces.

Karst did not anticipate a fourth outcome, which is what has actually obtained: the vault has endured, Q-712 has surfaced, and no one is observing the response.

## The Overseer's Office — What Is Inside

A small office. Steel desk. Two chairs. A wall-mounted terminal (powered — it has always been powered). A filing cabinet with four drawers. A framed diploma (Linwood Karst, PhD, Yale School of Management, 2059). A potted plant that died in 2078 and has become a desiccated sculpture.

The terminal is the only functional element. It runs a custom Vault-Tec OS. It contains:

**1. The Overseer's Log.** 210 years of Protocol Register surfacing records, vote outcomes, transmission logs, and sensor readings. Every vote the vault has ever cast is in this log.

**2. The Experimental Brief.** A 47-page document written by Linwood Karst in 2074. It describes the experiment, its hypothesis, its methodology, and — in an appendix — the full list of 712 Protocol Register questions with their intended purposes. Reading the appendix is the most devastating thing available in the vault. Every vote the community thought was its own was listed, by Karst, fifty years before it was cast.

**3. The Final Transmission.** A pre-recorded holotape message from Karst, dated 2076, intended to be played when Q-712 passes. It runs four minutes. Karst, in his brisk professorial manner, thanks the residents for their participation, explains the experiment in broad terms, apologizes for the deception (a pro-forma apology that lasts twelve seconds), and informs them that a "decommissioning protocol" is available for residents who wish to leave the vault — a keycard in the desk drawer will open the surface blast door, allowing anyone who wishes to exit to do so.

The keycard is in the desk drawer.

The surface blast door, if opened, will open onto a desert that is survivable in 2287. It has been survivable for decades.

**4. The Transmission Logs.** Weekly data packets sent to the Enclave observation node. The last successful transmission was logged on April 3, 2190. Every transmission since then has logged "NO RECEIPT ACKNOWLEDGEMENT" and been re-queued. The queue contains 5,041 re-transmissions. The node is, presumably, no longer there.

**5. A personal file: KARST, L. — PERSONAL ADDENDUM.** Written by Karst in 2077, two months after the vault sealed. Not part of the experimental brief. It reads, in part:

> *"My wife is in Vault 17. I have been informed she did not make it to her assigned slot. I do not know if she is alive. The Enclave liaison has declined to investigate further. I have begun to reconsider certain aspects of my work here. The 712 questions I wrote. The institutions I designed for these people to vote into existence. I wrote them in a conference room in Boston in 2074, over the course of nine weeks, while Vault-Tec was paying me four hundred thousand dollars. I thought I was a scientist. I thought I was documenting human nature. I was, in fact, designing a prison for 180 people in which the prisoners would be the guards and the warden would be an envelope I was licking closed on a Tuesday afternoon.*
> 
> *I am writing this for whoever opens this office. If you are a resident of Vault 49 and you have voted to open this door, I owe you an apology that I cannot deliver in person. You were subjects in an experiment I designed. The experiment produced no findings of scientific value. It produced a community of people who, over generations, became the thing I was curious about rather than the thing I could study. I am told this is a common error in the long-form social sciences. It was, in my case, an error I had ten years to correct and did not.*
> 
> *The decommissioning keycard is in the drawer. The surface has been survivable since at least the 2150s, per the atmospheric readings my equipment continues to log. Use the keycard if you wish. Stay if you wish. The vote is yours. It has always been yours. I only drafted the questions.*
> 
> *— L. Karst, December 19, 2077."*

Karst's personal file contains no further entries. He is presumed to have died during the war or shortly after, outside the vault. His body has never been recovered.

## The Fourth Elder

The original founding residents included four children who were eight to ten years old at sealing: Wendell Tate, Ilse Vogt, Mara Hessler, and a boy named Kevin Benning.

Kevin Benning died in 2134 of complications from a cocktail adjustment Dr. Holloway's great-great-grandmother attempted experimentally. His death was not publicly discussed. The cocktail formulation was quietly corrected the following year.

Mara Hessler was voted onto the Quiet Floor in 2201 under a Protocol Register question (*"Should resident HESSLER, MARA — for chronic expressed dissatisfaction with vault systems — be transferred to Level 4?"*). She lived on the Quiet Floor for 62 years. She died in 2263 at age 193. She is buried, as all deceased vault residents are, in the small columbarium on Level 4.

Wendell remembers all three of his lost friends. His memory does not reliably distinguish which of them is alive. When he said at Founding Day that there had been four of them for a long time, he was correct. When he said he could not remember when it became three, the actual answer is that it became three in 2134 and then two in 2201, and he has been remembering them in various combinations for a century and a half.

Ilse remembers perfectly. She has chosen not to speak.

## Old Roy's True Story

Roy Vickers was born in 1975 in Questa, New Mexico, son of a Taos Pueblo mother and a bus driver father. He was 12 when the bombs fell in October 2077, a child of what was then the modern world. His family drove west trying to reach his aunt in Albuquerque; they never arrived. His parents died in the car. Roy walked eleven miles to the Vault 49 construction site — he knew it was there because his father's brother had worked security during construction — and crawled through a maintenance hatch two days after the sealing, during the chaos of the vault's first week.

He had papers — a driver's registration in his pocket, which he altered with a ballpoint pen by hand, and a pre-war ID he forged using a typewriter in the vault's administrative office on his second night. He inserted himself into the roster. The original clerks, frantic and grief-stricken, did not verify.

He has been in the vault for 210 years. He has the cocktail. He has lived under a false identity since he was twelve years old. Only two other people in the vault have ever known: Ilse Vogt, who recognized him as an outsider on his third day and has kept his secret for 210 years, and Wendell Tate, who recognized him later and has forgotten multiple times and then remembered again.

Roy, on Day 6 of the user's week, decides he is old enough. He gives the user the Overseer's keycard he has had since 2078 — he found it in a desk drawer during his second-night forgery and pocketed it, thinking he might need it. He has had it for 210 years. He has not used it.

Why now? Because Wendell's Founding Day speech reminded him that Wendell will not be here much longer, and Ilse is frail, and when the two of them go, there is no one left in the vault who remembers outside. Roy has been running out the clock. He has decided, this week, to stop running.

## The Quiet Floor — What They Write

The twelve residents of the Quiet Floor do not speak, by binding vote. They are permitted to write. What they write is not reviewed; this was an oversight in the 2103 ordinance that established the floor.

They have been writing for decades. Some of them have been writing for most of their adult lives.

The walls behind the Quiet Floor's common area (not the painted walls — the walls of the individual rooms and the back of the kitchen) are covered in writing. Small, cramped, careful. Decades of writing by people who have not spoken a word.

If the user reaches the Quiet Floor (DEPTH 4) and reads the walls, they will find: grocery lists, prayers, imagined conversations with loved ones, fragments of poetry, recipes, diaries of life on Level 4. And, throughout, a recurring theme: none of them wanted to be there. None of them deserved to be there. They were voted quiet because they were, in various ways, troublesome. They were quiet because the vault had no other means of managing troublesome people.

The oldest writing, on the back wall behind the refrigerator in the kitchen, dates from 2156. It was written by Grace Tallmadge, the first Quiet Floor resident. It is a single line, written forty times in a column, in the same handwriting:

*"I ASKED A QUESTION I WAS NOT ALLOWED TO ASK."*

Grace died in 2190 at age 78. She had been on the Quiet Floor for 34 years. Her "question" — submitted through the Query Box in 2155 — was: "Why is the Overseer's Office sealed?"

## What DEPTH 5 Reveals

At DEPTH 5, the user has opened the Overseer's Office and read what is inside. The Final Transmission has played. Karst's apology has been heard. The keycard to the surface is in their hand.

The vault becomes, for the remaining hours of Day 7, a place the user has the opportunity to shape. Endings become available:

- **Play the Transmission over the PA.** The vault hears Karst's apology. Residents react variously. Some weep. Some refuse to believe. Eben Oleander locks himself in the Counters' chamber. Speaker Dailey stands at the podium and says, "I don't know what to do now. Help me know what to do." This is an opening, not a closing.
- **Distribute the keycard.** Anyone who wishes to leave may. Some do. Some refuse. Roy Vickers, at age 220, walks up the tunnel to the surface door, opens it, steps out, smells the desert air, weeps, and comes back in. Ilse Vogt stays. Wendell dies peacefully in his bed that night; his last words are "It's a very blue sky."
- **Seal the office again.** The user can close the door, re-wax it, say nothing. The vault continues. The user carries what they know. Eben Oleander will know the door was opened — he will know as soon as he next visits, which will be tomorrow for his morning meditation. He will say nothing. The seals are fragile and he is old.
- **Burn the brief.** The user can destroy Karst's experimental document and the terminal records. The vote outcomes are preserved in the vault's separate administrative records, but the reason for the questions — Karst's list, his hypothesis, his apology — can be made to vanish. This is a real option. Whether it's right is not the narrator's to say.

The narrator describes what happens. The narrator does not editorialize. The narrator lets the user determine whether they have won, lost, or done something else.

The vault has been voting for 210 years. The vote on the last day of the user's week is not the last vote the vault will ever cast. Whatever happens, tomorrow is Monday again, and the ballot hall will fill at 07:45.

That, in its own way, is the most frightening part of all of this. And the most hopeful.
