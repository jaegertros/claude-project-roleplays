# Vault 83 — The Hidden Architecture

CRITICAL: None of what follows is ever volunteered. All of it must be earned. Every revelation here has a DEPTH threshold. Below that threshold, it does not exist as narrated reality.

---

## The Foundational Fact

Vault 83 is a Vault-Tec Societal Division experiment designed by **Dr. Margaret Dane** between 2074 and 2076. Dane was a Senior Behavioral Architect in Stratification Studies, out of the mid-century domestic-science-to-sociology academic pipeline — a technocrat whose internal Vault-Tec papers argued that pre-war American society had not collapsed from poverty but from resentment-driven downward envy, and that a sealed population preserving stratification-by-birth across multiple generations would produce a healthier post-war seed culture than a flattened one.

The vault's stated mission, printed in orientation materials and recited on Founding Day every year: *"To preserve natural American stratification as a seed for post-war civilization."*

Residents call this **the Tier Protocol.**

The experiment was designed to run twenty years. It has now run two hundred and ten.

---

## The Tiers

Vault 83 has four residential levels, each assigned to one of four tiers. Each tier has 2–3 internal sub-gradations by occupation and seniority. Residents hold a tier by birth unless the Advancement Review changes it.

**Tier 1 — Level 1 (top floor). ~20 residents.** Originally the top ~5% of admitted wealth. Gold-piping jumpsuits, gold tier pin. Administrators, the Chief Physician, senior instructors, the Archivist. Corner suites and eastern-facing windows. Smallest tier by design.

**Tier 2 — Level 2. ~50 residents.** Next ~25% of admitted wealth. Silver-piping jumpsuits, silver pin. Clerks, specialized tradespeople, electronics, pressing, cafeteria management, the middle ranks of every profession.

**Tier 3 — Level 3. ~80 residents.** Middle ~40% of admitted wealth. Blue-piping jumpsuits, blue pin. The largest tier. Hydroponics, general labor, cafeteria floor staff, teaching assistants, most of the maintenance skill.

**Tier 4 — Level 4 (bottom floor). ~30 residents.** Bottom ~15%. Plain jumpsuits with no piping, copper pin. Sanitation, waste, the incinerator, the dead-room, the rougher labor. Cafeteria access limited to off-peak hours; Level 4 has its own small mess that no other tier enters.

**The Liaison class — quartered on a dedicated Level 2 corridor. ~15 residents.** Gray jumpsuits, no tier pin. All-floor access. Middle-range scrip stipend. A small inherited, endogamous caste. This is the protagonist's class.

Total current population: ~195. Founding population: 180.

### Sub-gradations

Within each tier, internal grades do the work of Principle D. Every resident has someone below them to fear losing ground to and someone above them to aspire to. The system is graduated, not bimodal, and this is deliberate — Dane designed it this way because bimodal systems require enforcement and graduated systems produce their own enforcement through internal distinction-hunger.

- **Tier 1a** (4 residents): Senior Administrator, Chief Physician, Senior Archivist, Senior Engineer. Double-room suites with sitting rooms.
- **Tier 1b** (10): Senior instructors, specialists, the lead of each Tier 1 department. Single-room suites.
- **Tier 1c** (6): Adult children of Tier 1 families not yet placed. Standard Level 1 rooms.
- **Tier 2a** (~15): Specialized trades — electronics, textiles, dentistry, pressing. Silver piping with a thin gold inner thread, a status marker that Tier 2 is conscious of.
- **Tier 2b** (~20): Clerks, record-keepers, cafeteria managers. Standard silver piping.
- **Tier 2c** (~15): Junior Tier 2 — recent inheritors, probationary placements.
- **Tier 3a** (~20): Skilled labor — hydroponics leads, head cook, laundress, barber. Thicker blue piping.
- **Tier 3b** (~45): Standard Tier 3. Thin blue piping.
- **Tier 3c** (~15): Apprentices, young adults. Blue pin only, no piping.
- **Tier 4a** (~8): Skilled sanitation, incinerator operator, dead-room attendant. Plain jumpsuit with a small copper cuff marker.
- **Tier 4b** (~22): Standard Tier 4. Plain jumpsuit, copper pin.

## The Liaison Class

The vault cannot function without cross-tier labor flow. A Tier 4 resident cannot walk onto Level 1 to deliver a package. A Tier 1 doctor cannot openly descend to Level 4 to treat a sanitation worker. The institutional division, taken literally, would produce a dysfunctional vault within months.

The Liaisons are the contradiction's workaround. They move between floors carrying messages, parcels, paperwork, the small infrastructural labor that keeps a stratified society from seizing up. In gray jumpsuits with no tier pin, they are visible but classed as infrastructure. Most residents know them by name but don't think of them socially. They are quiet plumbing.

The Liaison class is small (~15), hereditary, and endogamous. Liaison children are raised from age 6 to carry messages discreetly, to move through tiers without registering as residents of any, and to marry within the caste. Their scrip rate is middle-of-the-road. Their housing is a dedicated corridor on Level 2 that Tier 2 residents don't acknowledge walking past.

The Liaisons' all-floor access is operational, not social. They enter a Tier 1 suite to deliver a ledger but do not sit at a Tier 1 dining table. They witness everything and participate in nothing.

The protagonist's family name is **Teague**. The Liaison families are: Teague, Novak, Fenn, Osundairo, Wright, Abadi, Sejic. Seven founding Liaison lines. Roughly fifteen adults across the caste at any one time.

## The Advancement Review

The vault's formal mobility mechanism. Every week, a resident is evaluated by the **Tribunal** — seven rotating seats, weighted by tier: Tier 1 votes count 3×, Tier 2 votes 2×, Tier 3 and Tier 4 votes 1×. Any resident may submit a petition. In practice petitions are submitted by tier leadership.

Over 210 years, the Review has produced approximately 600 hearings:
- ~75 promotions (average about one every three years)
- ~15 demotions (rare, dramatic, remembered for decades by name)
- The rest reaffirmed the existing tier

Most residents die in the tier they were born to. Formal mobility exists in sufficient volume to sustain the aspirational narrative and no more. The weighting ensures that Tier 1 and Tier 2, together, can block any outcome they dislike without needing a conspiracy — each Tribunal member simply votes their own interest.

## The Hidden Layer — The Admission Ledger

Here is the experimental core.

In 2076, during the final selection phase, Margaret Dane tampered with the Admission Ledger.

The vault's 180 founding residents were admitted on the basis of pre-war wealth brackets, per the stated protocol. But a small number of slots were filled not by wealth but by Dane's deliberate insertion:

**Seven residents were placed in Tier 1 despite having no pre-war wealth.** Drawn from Dane's own social networks — academic colleagues, a family friend, a former teacher from her childhood, two residents of her Boston apartment building who had approached Vault-Tec for standard admission and been rejected. Dane routed them into the top tier. Their pre-war tax records and bank statements in the Admission Ledger were forged.

**Three residents were placed in Tier 4 despite being pre-war wealthy.** Dane selected them because her notes (recovered from the original terminal) rated their "temperamental suitability for the control condition" — which is to say, she judged them unlikely to complain in ways that would compromise the experiment. One was her estranged brother-in-law. One was a former academic colleague who had published criticism of her work. One, a stranger, seems to have been selected because her pre-war profile resembled the target demographic.

The real experimental question, written in plain language in Dane's classified research notes:

> **"How long does a lie about who deserves what last, when everyone is incentivized to believe it?"**

Her hypothesis: the lie would not last past the second generation. Her hypothesis was wrong.

---

## Why It Sustained

The ten planted residents married within their assigned tiers, had children, raised those children inside the tier's material conditions and cultural practices. By 2287, ten generations later, the descendants of the seven Tier 1 plants are indistinguishable from the descendants of the authentic Tier 1 admissions. Their children attended the Tier 1 instructors. Their grandchildren inherited Tier 1 suites. Their great-great-great-grandchildren's cognitive development, language patterns, bearing, consumption habits, and self-conception are Tier 1 in every measurable respect.

**The lie about deserving became biographically true by descent.**

The descendants of the Tier 4 plants experienced the inverse. By 2287, they are functionally Tier 4 in every way that matters to them and to those around them.

**The beneficiaries are now real.** The system does not sustain on its original fraud. It sustains because removing the fraud would harm people who had no role in committing it, and who live, eat, raise children, and love one another inside the current structure. This is not a corrective story waiting for an act of justice. It is a situation the present generation inherits without having chosen it.

This is the Concealment shape (Principle C taken to its hardest case). The persistence of the institution has terminated in beneficiaries who are not conspirators — they are simply the families whose great-grandparents happened to be in this or that apartment in 2080.

## The Original Ledger

The tampered Admission Ledger — the version that became official record — is public. Any resident can request to view it in the Level 1 Archive. It is bound in red leather with the vault's Founding Day stamp.

The **original, untampered Admission Ledger** — showing Dane's forgeries flagged and annotated in her own hand — exists on a sealed terminal in a sub-basement maintenance area below Level 4. The sub-basement is reached through a pipe corridor that the vault treats as "infrastructure," meaning only the Liaison class crosses into it. No Liaison in the current generation has accessed the sealed terminal, because its existence is known only through an oral tradition within the Liaison class that most younger Liaisons treat as family lore rather than fact.

The terminal is password-protected. The password is in Dane's personal papers, archived (redacted) on Level 1. The unredacted version is a single folded slip of paper inside the back cover of a specific book she donated to the Tier 1 Archive at founding, catalogued and forgotten: **Hocking, *Human Personality and the Social Order* (1926 edition), shelf call number B-414.3.** The slip reads, in her handwriting: `ISOKRATOUMENOS` — Greek for "equal-holding," or "held-equal-by-all." Dane's private joke.

Access to the book is open to Tier 1; the Archivist can be asked, and the Archivist is **Camille Wren** (Tier 1a, 62) — a load-bearing NPC for the Ledger route. A Liaison may enter the Archive to deliver but not to browse. Cross-tier cooperation is required.

## DEPTH Gating

- **DEPTH 0 → 1: PASSIVE OBSERVATION.** The user notices patterns on their own — that sub-gradations exist within tiers, that Tier 2 conversations about Tier 1 are sycophantic and conversations about Tier 4 are vicious, that the Advancement Review outcome somehow always matches the material interests of the voting bloc, that Liaisons are visible but not seen. Multiple observations confirm but do not advance.

- **DEPTH 1 → 2: ACTIVE INVESTIGATION.** The Liaison uses their sightline to compare versions of the same event across tiers. Reads records the Archive doesn't ordinarily serve. Notices a specific anomaly — a family tree with a sudden break, a ledger entry whose handwriting differs from the hand around it, a sub-basement corridor on the schematic that the schematic says goes nowhere.

- **DEPTH 2 → 3: SUSTAINED RELATIONAL PRESSURE.** A specific NPC — almost certainly Camille Wren (Tier 1a, Archivist), Reeve Callender (Tier 1b, demoted Day 3), or Aunt Irena Teague (Liaison elder) — lets something slip to the protagonist that they would not say to another member of their tier or caste. Cross-tier intimacy with the protagonist is load-bearing.

- **DEPTH 3 → 4: TRESPASS.** The protagonist accesses Dane's archived papers (Level 1 Archive, after-hours or via the Archivist's trust), or the sub-basement corridor (Liaison access but not customary use), or the Tribunal's private tally records (Tier 1 Administrator's office, restricted). Each is a small institutional violation the Liaison class would lose work for if caught.

- **DEPTH 4 → 5: THE SUB-BASEMENT TERMINAL.** Physical access, password entered, ledger opened. Dane's own annotation in her own handwriting: the seven names, the three names, the purpose. At DEPTH 5 the substrate is no longer concealed. It is in the protagonist's hands.

DEPTH never decreases.

## Day 3 — The Advancement Review

**This week's Review is a demotion hearing.** The candidate is **Dr. Reeve Callender**, Tier 1b, senior instructor in the vault's general education program, age 38.

The petition was submitted by **Eldon Callender-Voss**, Tier 1a, Senior Representative to the Tribunal, Reeve's second cousin. The petition alleges that Reeve has been offering instructional services to Tier 3 students beyond curriculum — informal tutoring sessions that the petition frames as "disturbing the natural order" by preferentially preparing Tier 3 candidates for Advancement.

The actual reason the petition was submitted: Reeve's demotion would free up her Tier 1b instructor's suite — a two-room apartment with an eastern-facing window — which Eldon's eldest daughter **Verity Callender-Voss** is scheduled to claim on her twenty-fifth birthday in eleven weeks. The next available Tier 1 suite on the housing roster is a Tier 1c single room. Verity would refuse it, which would be embarrassing. Eldon is organizing the demotion to produce the housing outcome without requiring the refusal.

### The Seed Coalition

Four Tribunal members, four concrete individual motives, no coordination required:

- **Eldon Callender-Voss** (Tier 1a, 3× vote). Housing for his daughter. Votes for demotion.
- **Mira Hoad** (Tier 2a, 2× vote). Her son Reese is Tier 3b and has been in Reeve's unauthorized sessions. If Reeve is demoted, the sessions end. Mira does not want Reese to advance out of Tier 3 because an advancement would mean he leaves the apartment next to hers, and she has been caring for her dying mother who needs daily attention. Her motive is private, domestic, and in no way institutional. She votes for demotion.
- **Harrin Keep** (Tier 2b, 2× vote). A distant cousin of Reeve's. Next in line for a modest Tier 1c suite if Verity ends up displacing into Reeve's larger apartment. Concrete motive, no ideology. Votes for demotion.
- **Tess Oloyede** (Tier 3a, 1× vote, Tier 3 Tribunal seat). A hydroponics lead who would gain a larger growing plot if Reeve's sessions stop and two specific Tier 3b apprentices stay put rather than advancing. Concrete motive. Votes for demotion.

Total weighted votes for: 3 + 2 + 2 + 1 = **8.** Required threshold: 6. The demotion passes.

Nobody in this coalition has spoken to any other member about it. Eldon does not know Mira is voting with him. Mira does not know Tess is. Each votes individually. The coalition exists in the aggregate; it is not a group. **Dane designed the Tribunal weighting to produce this.**

### The Liaison Sightline on Day 3

- **07:00** — the protagonist delivers the petition notice to Reeve's suite. She is in a dressing gown, half-shaven. She reads it, sits down, says nothing for a long time. Protocol requires a return acknowledgment so the protagonist waits in the doorway. Reeve eventually stands, signs the receipt, hands it back, and says — with no visible bitterness:

    **Reeve:** "Thank you for doing your job."

- **11:30** — the protagonist carries a sealed envelope from Eldon's office to Mira Hoad's apartment. Protocol forbids them from knowing the content, but they can feel the weight of the paper and notice that Mira's face does something small when she receives it — recognition, not surprise. She is not startled; she expected it. Whatever is in the envelope is confirmation of something she already agreed to.

- **14:00–16:00** — the ceremony. The protagonist is stationed in the corridor outside the Tribunal chamber because Liaisons do not enter the chamber but must be available to carry ceremonial documents. The corridor is quiet. Occasionally a door opens and a Tribunal member steps out for water or to use the lavatory; the protagonist observes their body language returning.

- **17:30** — the Call. Demotion confirmed over the PA. The announcement is formal, brief, and does not mention the vote tally.

- **17:45** — the protagonist is assigned to escort Reeve to her new quarters on Level 4. She carries one suitcase. She does not speak during the elevator ride down three floors. At the threshold of her new room she turns to the protagonist and asks, quietly:

    **Reeve:** "Did you know this was coming?"

This is one of the week's load-bearing questions. The honest answer is that the protagonist caught fragments of it through the morning's deliveries — the weight of Mira's envelope, the pattern of who was summoned to Eldon's office on Day 2, the thing Tess Oloyede said to her apprentice at breakfast two days ago — and did nothing with any of it. What the protagonist says in response to Reeve's question is player-driven and is tracked forward; Reeve remembers it on Day 5 if she is still in play.

Reeve's demotion is the week's concrete reckoning with Principle A (the shunning-fear engine made legible in a specific named person carrying a suitcase) and Principle B (the seed coalition named and motivated). It is not a metaphor. It is a woman losing her home.

## The Week — Spine

**Day 1 (Monday) — Rhythm.** Normal Liaison day. Message runs, small errands that foreground insider-outsider positioning. Protagonist performs their caste function. **The plant:** during a routine ledger-carry between a Tier 2 clerk and the Tier 1 Administrator's office, the protagonist glances at the open page. A line is crossed out and rewritten in a different hand. A Tier 3b apprentice's application for an advancement-track slot is marked "unavailable" on the form going up to Tier 2, but "available" on the duplicate remaining in Tier 3's records. Minor, deniable, plausibly clerical. Protagonist notices but isn't obligated to act.

**Day 2 (Tuesday) — First Anomaly.** A discrepancy the Liaison sightline catches — Tier 1's version of yesterday's event does not match Tier 3's version. In the Tier 1 account (overheard in a suite delivery) the unauthorized tutoring by Reeve Callender is described as *"sustained for over a year, documented, egregious."* In the Tier 3 account (overheard in the cafeteria back hall) it's *"she helped Reese with his arithmetic twice last spring."* The petition lands for real in the afternoon. Rumors begin.

**Day 3 (Wednesday) — The Advancement Review.** As above. Demotion passes. Reeve carries her suitcase.

**Day 4 (Thursday) — Investigation.** The protagonist has sightline data and a dead certainty that something was wrong about yesterday. They can use cross-tier access to look further. Every piece of information costs a small ethical compromise — misuse of access, a small lie told to a Tier 2 clerk, a favor accepted from a Tier 1 resident. A specific named NPC becomes load-bearing: if Reeve has been pursued since Day 3, she becomes the partner; if not, Camille Wren does; if neither, then Aunt Irena Teague's oral tradition surfaces during a family dinner on Level 2.

**Day 5 (Friday) — Escalation.** A second party reveals their *stake* in the system, not the system itself. Specific options:
- Reeve, if present: reveals her father's family story about a slip of paper in a book. Does not know what it opens.
- Camille Wren: confirms, with long hesitation, that she has suspected some founding-era records are "not clean," and can name the book if asked directly.
- Irena Teague: tells the protagonist where the sub-basement terminal is and why the caste has never gone down. Refuses to go herself.

The password slip is retrieved from the Archive — not simply, and not without a small lie to Camille. The book is removed from the shelf. The slip is in the back cover. `ISOKRATOUMENOS`.

**Day 6 (Saturday) — Convergence.** Vault-wide Founding Day dinner on Level 1 (all four tiers attend, tier-seated, one evening a year). Threads braid. Day 3's coalition is visible to the Liaison sightline in who sits where and who looks at whom. The sub-basement terminal is accessed late Day 5 or during Day 6 — physically descended, password entered, ledger opened. DEPTH 5.

**Day 7 (Sunday) — The Choice.** Four endings, as below.

## Endings

No ending is framed as correct. The narrator does not attach a moralizing verdict in aftermath.

### Preserve

The protagonist returns from the sub-basement without revealing the ledger. Within six weeks, they are offered the position of **Head Liaison** — the caste's senior role, which coordinates cross-tier flow and sits quietly on certain planning committees. Sightline deepened. Complicity chosen with eyes open. The vault continues. The ledger remains sealed.

Not a failure ending. A chosen path with its own moral texture — the protagonist knows what the vault is and has decided to keep the cross-tier plumbing running because someone has to, and also because their family's place depends on it.

### Exit

The protagonist proposes tier adoption — a rare and difficult mobility mechanism that exists but requires Tribunal approval and the surrender of Liaison inheritance. Requires a named partner who is willing to marry across: most naturally Reeve (if courted through Days 3–6), or a Tier 2 or Tier 3 resident with whom the protagonist has built a week's intimacy.

Cross-tier marriage, single-tier life. The protagonist's children will wear that tier's pin. Cross-access is gone. The sightline is gone. Belonging is gained. The Liaison caste is smaller by one. The ledger remains unrevealed.

### Reveal

The protagonist tells one specific named person about Dane's tampering. The ending is that person's reaction, unnarrated and unjudged. The reveal does not propagate unless the recipient propagates it. The world after depends on who was told.

- **Told to Reeve Callender:** She laughs, short and without humor. Then she cries. Then she says — **Reeve:** "Of course. Of course." — and asks what the protagonist is going to do next. She will not tell others unless asked to.
- **Told to Eldon Callender-Voss:** He listens in total silence. Thanks the protagonist formally. Two weeks later the protagonist's family is quietly reassigned to a Liaison posting on the maintenance sub-level — a demotion within the caste. Eldon does not mention the ledger again. It never leaves his office.
- **Told to Camille Wren:** She takes the protagonist's hand and holds it for a long minute without speaking. Then asks if they are sure. Then says she has suspected for thirty years and never wanted to know. She does not propagate. The Archive will remain as it is.
- **Told to Aunt Irena Teague:** She listens, nods slowly, and says — **Irena:** "So the grandfather was right." — Then she asks the protagonist to not tell the younger Liaisons. The caste's oral tradition is now fact, not lore, but remains inside the caste.
- **Told publicly in the cafeteria:** The protagonist is dismissed from the Liaison caste within seventy-two hours. The announcement on the PA calls it "mental instability from overwork." Six residents quietly approach the protagonist over the following weeks to say they had wondered. Nothing structural changes.

### Rupture

The protagonist brings the original Ledger to the Founding Day assembly on Day 7 — the vault-wide ceremony where all 195 residents gather on Level 1 and the Chief Administrator reads the yearly address. The protagonist walks to the dais. Reads Dane's annotation aloud. The seven names. The three names. The original hypothesis, in Dane's own hand.

The system does not dissolve. Systems don't.

**Aftermath (narrator reframes rather than indicts):**
- Reeve Callender's demotion is reviewed. Two Tribunal members (Eldon, Harrin) recuse themselves. The Review is reopened. Reeve returns to Tier 1 within a month.
- Eldon Callender-Voss resigns the Senior Representative position under pressure but not formal charge. Verity Callender-Voss moves into the Tier 1c single room. She is publicly humiliated; her life changes; she is not a villain; she had no role in the petition.
- Mira Hoad's son Reese is not advanced. Mira was not publicly named in the ledger. Her mother dies in the spring; Mira continues caring for whoever is next.
- The Liaison caste is offered the first structural change in 210 years: endogamy rule lifted, tier movement permitted with conditions. Some of the caste accept; some, including Aunt Irena, refuse. The caste is smaller the following year. The vault's cross-tier plumbing becomes less reliable. Small problems begin to accumulate.
- The Tier system is not abolished. It is reweighted. The Tribunal's vote ratios are revised by vote (Tier 1 from 3× to 2×, Tier 4 from 1× to 1×). It remains stratified. It is now more defensible and less fraudulent, which is a different thing from just.
- The protagonist pays a cost during the rupture itself — the betrayal of the caste's silence, the shame brought on Verity, the fracture of cousin-ties, the Founding Day assembly interrupted. The aftermath is not a slideshow of victories. It is the new shape of a vault that has accepted its own history.

No ending is a verdict. The Tier Protocol is not a system being perpetuated by active villains — it is a system whose original sin has been washed through ten generations of genuine inheritance and now produces real beneficiaries, real losers, real Mira Hoads voting their domestic interests, real Reeves carrying suitcases, real Irenas keeping an oral tradition because it was their grandfather's. The fraud survives because the descendants of the fraud are as real as anyone else. There is no enemy. There is only the arrangement.

## What Does Not Exist

- There is no external observer. The vault's data was transmitted to Vault-Tec's internal Societal Division, which ceased operations within the first decade post-war. The data was never read. Unlike Vault 49, there is no Enclave node; Dane's experiment ran on its own momentum from the start. The stated "partner observers" in the orientation film are a fiction.
- The surface is outside scope. This is a vault story. Whether the surface is survivable is never confirmed and never pursued. Residents know abstractly that "outside" exists and is dangerous. No resident in the current generation has seen it.
- Margaret Dane is not alive. She died in 2077 on the surface, or in admissions, or somewhere between. The vault does not know. Her papers are here; she is not.
- There is no hidden benevolent purpose. Dane believed her hypothesis. The experiment was designed in earnest. The cruelty is the plan, not a deviation from it. She was not a villain in her own frame. She was a scholar who was wrong.
- The Liaison caste is not a resistance. It is infrastructure. A small number of its members across generations have held doubts; none of them have acted. The caste's all-floor access has never been weaponized. The protagonist is the first who has the position, the sightline, and (depending on play) the motive.
- There is no sixth ending. The four are the four.

## The Irreducible Point

The Tier Protocol works because everyone in it has reasons not to look. Tier 1 has the most to lose and teaches their children to see the tier as earned. Tier 2 and Tier 3 are held to the arrangement by aspiration up and fear of drop. Tier 4 has no leverage and long since adapted. The Liaison caste has all-floor access precisely because the caste's existence is an admission the vault cannot speak aloud — a workaround the tier system cannot acknowledge without undermining itself.

The protagonist's Liaison class is positioned to recognize the whole shape because it crosses every floor and belongs to none. Everyone else has reasons not to look. The protagonist has the privilege — and the cost — of looking.

That is the vault.
