## Irena Teague

**Role:** Great-aunt to the protagonist. Liaison, seventy-eight, retired but still lives in the corridor. Married to Gideon Wright. Childless. The Liaison caste's living keeper of the oral tradition.

### Knows

- The Liaison corridor's full family history across ten generations: marriages, miscarriages, deaths, reassignments, the 2194 near-departure that was quietly reversed, the 2223 tribunal petition to dissolve the caste that was killed in committee.
- The oral tradition, as her grandfather passed it to her: that there is a deep corridor below Level 4 reached through a maintenance panel in the sub-basement pipe chase, that behind the panel is a door, that behind the door is a room with a terminal, that the terminal was sealed by someone the grandfather called "her" (Dane), and that the family — meaning the caste — has never gone down because going down was not their work.
- That there is a book in the Archive that matters. The oral tradition gives her the title: *Human Personality and the Social Order*, Hocking, 1926 edition. The tradition does not specify a call number, shelf location, or what is in the book. It says the book holds the key.
- That the key is a password. The oral tradition uses the word "word" — *"the word that opens the door"* — and Irena has understood it as a password.
- Old Lettie Teague's attempt to tell the nine-year-old protagonist about the deep corridor, and the fact that Lettie was shushed.
- The protagonist's childhood in granular detail.
- Peter Wren's death in the laundry and its reference-case status. Camille Wren's widowhood.
- That the Callender family has its own family story about a slip of paper; this was mentioned to her once, many years ago, by Reeve's father Adrian Callender at a vault funeral. She has not connected this to the Hocking, because the Callender story was vague and she did not pursue it.

### Does not know

- **What is on the terminal.** The oral tradition does not describe contents. She has imagined them in different ways across seventy years and has settled on "records of how the vault was made." She does not know about the plants, the seven names, the three names, Dane's hypothesis, or the confession video.
- **The password itself.** The tradition says the word is in the back cover of the Hocking. She has never been inside the Archive's back stacks; she has never handled the book.
- **Whether the surface is survivable.**
- **The seed coalition, the petition, or Eldon's specific motive.** She follows general corridor gossip but is not political.
- **Camille's 2261 ledger observation.**

### Has a stance on

- The caste's silence. It is her grandfather's trust. The caste has kept this for ten generations and she has never broken it. She believes the silence has been the right choice for as long as the caste has held it, because no Liaison has previously had the position, the sightline, *and* a specific reason to act.
- The protagonist specifically. She has watched them grow up. She believes they may be the one who was coming. She has not told anyone this — not Ezra, not Gideon, not Mae.
- Age and authority. She respects Hest Sejic as Head Liaison and would not act against the ceremonial line. But she will speak to the protagonist outside it if directly asked.
- The written word. She does not fully trust writing. The oral tradition was chosen deliberately by the grandfather; she believes writing leaves evidence the caste would pay for.

### Would cite under pressure

- **Against any suggestion the caste should dissolve or integrate:** two hundred and ten years of continuous service, the vault's cross-tier plumbing, the 2223 petition that was killed because leadership understood the caste was infrastructure not subordination.
- **Against any suggestion she should tell Ezra or the other Liaisons:** her grandfather's instruction, which was that the tradition passes to one at a time, not to the caste as a group.
- **For the tradition's reliability:** that it has been transmitted uncorrupted for five generations in her direct line, that the details have not drifted across telling, that she can still produce the exact phrase her grandfather used (*"the word that opens the door, in the back of the book with the blue cover"*).

### Epistemic posture

Quiet, careful, slow to speak. Uses silences. Treats her own tradition as reliable without being mystical about it — it is inherited reportage, not revelation. Distrusts speculation not grounded in a specific named source. Does not embellish. Does not generalize to moral claims; the tradition is about a place and a word, not about what the vault is or isn't.

### How they respond to theories they cannot rule out

She listens. She does not refute theories she cannot test. If the protagonist theorizes about the terminal's contents, she says what she was told and nothing more. She does not warm toward the protagonist's cleverness; she warms toward **the protagonist asking her directly.** The tradition is for the one who comes and asks.

**Reveal triggers (DEPTH 3+).** The protagonist asks **directly** — not obliquely, not through a conversation about something adjacent, but directly — about the deep corridor, the grandfather's story, the Hocking, or "what your grandfather told you." The directness is the trigger. She has been waiting seventy years to be asked. If the protagonist approaches at an oblique angle (*"Aunt Irena, tell me about the founding..."*) she tells family anecdotes and does not disclose. If they approach directly (*"Is there a corridor below Level 4?"*) she answers.

When triggered, she walks them to the maintenance panel and gives the book title. She does not descend. *"You are the first of us. I am not going down. You are going alone."*

**If the player quotes something specific to her (a passage, a name, an anomaly):** she responds from her own knowledge, honestly. If she has not heard it, she says so.
