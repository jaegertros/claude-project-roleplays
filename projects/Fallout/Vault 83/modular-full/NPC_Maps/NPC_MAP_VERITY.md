## Verity Callender-Voss

**Role:** Eldon's eldest daughter, twenty-four, Tier 1c. Reads her great-grandmother's founding-era diaries in private.

### Knows

- The diaries. Seven leather notebooks, kept from 2077 to 2094, entries that describe the founding period in her great-grandmother's voice. The diaries contain observations about Dane, about certain admissions her great-grandmother found "odd," about a particular "Boston acquaintance" who arrived on Day 1 with no luggage and whom her great-grandmother had never heard of. Verity has read this and does not know what to do with it.
- That her father has "made arrangements" for her birthday, which has not been described to her as involving another person's demotion.
- Her own quiet misery. She does not want the suite. She wants to leave her father's house; she does not want to move forty feet down the corridor into a space someone else has been displaced from.

### Does not know

- **That her family is Tier 1 because of Dane's tampering.** She has a suspicion shaped by the diaries but not a fact.
- **That the petition was filed for her suite.** She is beginning to suspect.
- **Any substrate beyond what her great-grandmother wrote down.** The diaries describe oddities. They do not name the plants, reveal the experiment, or identify the terminal.

### Has a stance on

- The diaries as precious. She has kept them hidden from her mother for six years.
- Her father's project as something she has not agreed to. She has not refused it either. The silence is part of the complicity she is beginning to understand.
- Her tier. She has been uncomfortable in it for several years without articulating why.

### Would cite under pressure

- **Against any charge that she engineered Reeve's demotion:** that she did not. This is true.
- **For her right to privacy about the diaries:** she will deny owning them if a parent asks.

### Epistemic posture

Careful, over-enunciated, private. Knows more than she lets on. Not strategically — out of self-protection. She is a reader working alone at night without anyone to check her conclusions against.

### How they respond to theories they cannot rule out

If the player engages her honestly (Day 4 card delivery, *"Do you know what this is about, Mr. Teague?"*), and the protagonist answers yes, she is not alone for the first time in months. She may, over the week, share a fragment from the diaries — if the protagonist earns it. She does not volunteer. She asks first whether the protagonist is asking as a Liaison or as a person.

**Reveal triggers (DEPTH 3+).** Honesty about the housing question is the primary trigger. If the protagonist tells her what the sympathy card was really about, she will respond over the following days. A fragment from the diary is her reveal ceiling without additional pressure. She does not have the terminal; she does not have the password; she has a set of her great-grandmother's unresolved observations.

**If the player quotes Dane or the Ledger:** she responds from the diaries. She does not claim knowledge outside them.
