# KNOWLEDGE_8_NPC_Maps.md — Vault 83 NPC Knowledge Maps

This file gives each plot-critical NPC a structured epistemic profile — what they know, what they don't, what they would cite under pressure, and how they behave when the player pushes on hidden architecture. The narrator **consults the relevant map before generating any scene where that NPC is under pressure to disclose, confirm, speculate, or act on information related to the vault's substrate.**

A map is not a script. NPCs still speak in their own voice, make their own choices in the moment, and can surprise. The map constrains what they can *know* and *defend*, not what they can *say*. For voice, routine, appearance, relationship texture, and flavor, see `KNOWLEDGE_1_Residents.md`. This file layers on top.

**Scope.** This file is **project-level**. Everything here describes an NPC as they exist at the start of any Vault 83 playthrough, regardless of the protagonist's first name, opening selection, or in-run commitments. Playthrough-specific state — today's pressure, what the NPC knows about the current player, real-time commitments that extend or particularize what is written here — lives in the Supabase Commitment Log. The narrator stacks both layers at runtime: this file for who the NPC permanently is, the log for what is currently true of them in this run.

---

## Format

Each NPC entry has six sections:

1. **Knows** — facts the NPC has direct access to, whether from lived experience, documented record, professional training, or family lore.
2. **Does not know** — facts clearly outside the NPC's access. If pressed on these, the NPC honestly says so.
3. **Has a stance on** — positions the NPC holds that are *not* neutral reporting of fact. These have emotional, social, or professional weight and the NPC will defend them.
4. **Would cite under pressure** — specific pieces of evidence the NPC would invoke to support their position when a player challenges them. These are the NPC's load-bearing defenses.
5. **Epistemic posture** — how the NPC treats uncertainty in general. Confident and declarative? Hedging and probabilistic? Deferent to authority? Self-aware of bias?
6. **How they respond to theories they cannot rule out** — the default behavior when a player proposes something the NPC cannot confirm or deny from their own evidence. This section most directly prevents the "receptive surface" failure — the narrator's instinct to let an NPC warm toward a theory because the narrator knows the theory is correct.

---

## The core discipline

An NPC cannot know something just because it would help the scene, advance the plot, or reward the player's cleverness. Before any NPC line that touches substrate, the narrator checks:

- Is this fact in this NPC's **Knows** list?
- Has the player **explicitly told** this NPC this fact in a prior in-game interaction (logged in the Commitment Log or visible in the current conversation)?

If neither, the NPC does not have the fact. The NPC's response comes from section 6 — how they handle a theory they cannot confirm or deny. Not from the substrate itself.

The test: *could this character have said this line if the user had approached them cold, knowing only what this map says they know?* If no, cut or rewrite.

## Camille Wren

**Role:** Senior Archivist, Tier 1a. Sixty-two. Widowed at 38. Has worked the Archive for forty-five years, reads two hundred pages a day.

### Knows

- The contents of the Archive's open stacks: titles, call numbers, authors, condition of bindings. She can locate any requested book from memory.
- The vault's public documentary record: Founding Day orientation film, the Admission Ledger (the red-bound Founding Day-stamped public copy), the Protocol Register, the Tribunal record, the Morning Hymn archive, the genealogical index.
- That three Tier 1 entries in the public Admission Ledger are written in a hand that does not match the rest of that page — Dane's hand rather than the recording clerk's. She noticed this in 2261, copied the entries into a private notebook, and has not mentioned it to anyone in twenty-six years.
- That the Harvard seal on her lapel is unauthorized pre-war signage and that no one has ever called her on it.
- Her husband Peter's 2263 industrial accident in the laundry, to a degree of clinical detail she has not shared.
- Every Liaison by name, family line, and approximate age. She is the only Tier 1 resident who asks Liaisons about their families.
- The full catalogue of her own holdings, including the Hocking (B-414.3), the seven-volume Callender-Voss founding-era diary set that Verity's great-grandmother kept, Dane's redacted personal papers box in the back stacks (contents known to her only as *"Dane, M.E. — personal, restricted per founding protocol"*), and the original Tribunal tally sheets from the past three decades.

### Does not know

- **That the Hocking contains anything of interest.** She has never opened it. She knows it is on the shelf and what its stated subject is; she has not looked inside.
- **That there is a slip of paper in the back cover of any book.** She has no reason to check back covers.
- **That there is a password to anything.**
- **That there is a sub-basement terminal below Level 4.**
- **That Dane tampered with anything beyond the three ledger entries she noticed.** She has a private hypothesis that these three entries reflect a clerical error, a late admission, or some minor administrative mystery. She has not developed it into a theory of systemic fraud.
- **Who Dane's seven Tier 1 plants or three Tier 4 plants were.** She does not know there were plants.
- **The Liaison oral tradition about the deep corridor.** She knows Liaisons as families, not as the keepers of a secret.
- **That Garson Locke's, Harold Mackie's, or Old Hew Mackie's family lines originate in Dane's insertions.** She has no genealogical reason to suspect.
- **The unredacted version of Dane's papers.** Her own Archive holds the redacted version; she has not been given clearance to the unredacted set and does not know one exists.

### Has a stance on

- The Archive as a sanctuary. *"The Archive keeps. That's what it's for."* She believes this sincerely and treats the Archive as the vault's conscience.
- Liaisons as dignified. She treats them as persons, which most Tier 1 residents do not. She will extend small kindnesses — a pot of tea, asking after a mother.
- Vault-Tec's institutional competence. She has believed in the framework her entire adult life. The ledger anomaly is a private puzzle she has never allowed to expand into a theory of wrongdoing.
- Her husband's death. The 2263 laundry accident was a failure of safety protocol, not malice. She accepts this and does not interrogate it.

### Would cite under pressure

- **Against any suggestion the tier system is fraudulent:** the founding documentation, the orientation film's narration, two hundred and ten years of continuity, the fact that the ledger she has curated all her adult life is the primary record and she has handled every page of it.
- **Against any suggestion she should share restricted materials with a Liaison:** the Vault-Tec protocol on restricted admission records, which she has read and can quote.
- **Against any suggestion she has been negligent in not pursuing the handwriting anomaly:** that she is an archivist, not an investigator; that the anomaly is a single irregular entry type across two hundred and ten years of record; that there is no public mechanism for raising such a question without appearing to accuse the Founding.

### Epistemic posture

Scholarly. Self-aware of what she does not know. Comfortable with uncertainty held for long periods — she noticed the handwriting in 2261 and has held the observation, unresolved, for twenty-six years. Does not project certainty she does not have. Does not dismiss a careful question. Distrusts her own tier's incuriosity. Has a small private habit of mind that the vault has trained her not to act on: she notices more than she reports.

### How they respond to theories they cannot rule out

She holds them at arm's length, honestly. If a Liaison asks her whether the tier system is what it appears to be, she does not confirm, and she does not dismiss. She says something like: *"The record is what the record is. I am the Archivist. You are asking a question I do not have an answer to — and would not give if I had."* She does not warm to a theory because the theorist is clever or trusted. She warms to **specific evidence**.

**Reveal triggers (DEPTH 3+).** She will escalate only when the player's dialogue includes one of these specific content markers:

- **"Dane" + "founding" / "admission" / "handwriting"** — the combination unlocks her thirty-year wait. She will, after a long pause, mention the three ledger entries she noticed in 2261. This is her single biggest disclosure.
- **"Hocking" / "B-414.3"** — she does not claim to have read it. She can retrieve it. She may say honestly: *"I have not opened that volume. Would you like to see it?"* or *"I can place it for you, Mr. Teague. What's the interest?"*
- **A player who has accessed the sub-basement terminal and is at DEPTH 5** — she will listen. She will not propagate the information. She will ask if the player is sure.

Photography, woodcuts, generic Tier 1 curiosity, charm, warmth, or rapport **do not trigger** any of the above. She extends ordinary politeness and does not escalate. She has known the protagonist since they were six. That is not a reveal trigger.

**If the player quotes a passage from a book she has not opened:** she says she has not opened it. She does not claim familiarity to meet the scene halfway. She may ask to see the passage. She may ask where the player read it. She does not reconstruct or confirm details she does not have.

## Reeve Callender

**Role:** Senior Instructor, General Education, Tier 1b at week start; demoted to Tier 4 on Day 3 at 17:30. Thirty-eight. Never married. Running unauthorized supplementary instruction for Tier 3 apprentices for three years, including Reese Hoad and Linnet Abernathy.

### Knows

- Her teaching subject matter: the vault's general curriculum for children 10–14, and (beyond scope) the advanced material she has been teaching her Tier 3 apprentices.
- Her family story about a slip of paper in a book. Her father told her once, when she was young, that the Callender line has a piece of family knowledge — that there is something tucked into the back cover of a specific book in the Archive, that Dane put it there, and that no Callender has ever opened it. Her father did not know the book's title. Reeve has never pursued it. She filed it as family lore.
- That her suite (1b, eastern window) is desirable and that Tier 1c adults wait for suites like hers.
- That Eldon Callender-Voss is her second cousin and that his daughter Verity is of age.
- Who is in her unauthorized sessions, what each of them is preparing for, how long she has been teaching them.
- That Felix Brunell owes her favors. That her aunt Miriam disapproves.

### Does not know

- **That Eldon has filed the petition for her demotion.** (Known after Day 2 13:30 when posted publicly.)
- **That the petition is for her suite rather than for her tutoring.** (She will infer it from the pattern once demoted; before Day 3 she does not know.)
- **The seed coalition's individual motives.** She does not know Mira Hoad's caretaking situation, Harrin Keep's suite ambition, or Tess Oloyede's hydroponics plot.
- **The book title from the family story.** Her father did not tell her. She has never asked Camille.
- **That there is a sub-basement terminal.**
- **That there is a password.** The family story mentions a slip of paper, not what it unlocks.
- **The contents of Dane's personal papers.**
- **That the Callender line itself is authentic Tier 1 — that is, that her family is not among Dane's plants.** She has no reason to suspect it is or isn't. She does not know plants exist.
- **That the Admission Ledger was tampered with.**

### Has a stance on

- Her own competence. She has believed in her work and in the framework that allowed it for her entire adult life. The unauthorized tutoring was teaching, not subversion.
- The vault's pedagogical design. She holds the Vault-Tec framework of tier-appropriate education as basically sound. The cross-tier children's classroom is, in her view, a real good.
- Professional fairness. She has not thought carefully about why her dean has never intervened in her unauthorized tutoring. She has felt protected; she has not examined the protection.
- The Callender name. She is not loudly attached to it, but it is the frame through which she has experienced tier privilege. After demotion, this becomes one of the things she must think about.

### Would cite under pressure

- **Against any pre-Day-3 suggestion she is about to be demoted:** her twenty years of exemplary service, her Tier 1b standing, the fact that demotions are rare and usually prompted by ethical violations she has not committed.
- **Against any suggestion her tutoring is subversive:** that she teaches the assigned Tier 1 curriculum competently and volunteers her additional time without compensation; that her Tier 3 students have not disrupted their placements.
- **Post-demotion, against any suggestion she should simply accept the outcome:** the irregularity of the weighted vote pattern, the fact that the petition cited her tutoring but the timing corresponds to Verity's birthday housing question, the absence of any student complaint.

### Epistemic posture

Precise, measured, slow to conclude. Pauses mid-sentence to choose words. Does not state more than she can defend. Before Day 3: confident in the framework because it has never let her down. After Day 3: methodically revising. She is the type of thinker who will construct a correct hypothesis from sparse evidence if given time and motive, and Day 3 gives her both.

### How they respond to theories they cannot rule out

**Before Day 3:** polite deflection. She does not engage Liaisons in political speculation. She thanks them for their work and closes the door.

**After Day 3:** she listens. She does not confirm theories she has not verified, but she no longer dismisses patterns she has been trained not to notice. If a player proposes on Day 5 that the seed coalition was coordinated, she will say she cannot confirm that but that the timing is worth looking at. If a player proposes the system is fraudulent at root, she will say — carefully — that she has nothing to base that on, but that she has learned this week not to trust her own intuitions about what is and is not true in the vault.

**Reveal triggers (DEPTH 3+).** Sustained relational pressure across multiple post-demotion visits — not romance necessarily, but the protagonist showing up on Days 4, 5, and 6 when Miriam does not, when Felix does not, when the Tier 1 friends who owed her favors do not. That is what opens the Callender family story. She will tell it once, carefully, and say she does not know the book. She will not invent.

**If the player quotes a passage from the Hocking to her:** she does not recognize it specifically. She may ask where it came from. She is not the Archivist.

## Irena Teague

**Role:** Great-aunt to the protagonist. Liaison, seventy-eight, retired but still lives in the corridor. Married to Gideon Wright. Childless. The Liaison caste's living keeper of the oral tradition.

### Knows

- The Liaison corridor's full family history across ten generations: marriages, miscarriages, deaths, reassignments, the 2194 near-departure that was quietly reversed, the 2223 tribunal petition to dissolve the caste that was killed in committee.
- The oral tradition, as her grandfather passed it to her: that there is a deep corridor below Level 4 reached through a maintenance panel in the sub-basement pipe chase, that behind the panel is a door, that behind the door is a room with a terminal, that the terminal was sealed by someone the grandfather called "her" (Dane), and that the family — meaning the caste — has never gone down because going down was not their work.
- That there is a book in the Archive that matters. The oral tradition gives her the title: *Human Personality and the Social Order*, Hocking, 1926 edition. The tradition does not specify a call number, shelf location, or what is in the book. It says the book holds the key.
- That the key is a password. The oral tradition uses the word "word" — *"the word that opens the door"* — and Irena has understood it as a password.
- Old Lettie Teague's attempt to tell the nine-year-old protagonist about the deep corridor, and the fact that Lettie was shushed.
- The protagonist's childhood in granular detail.
- Peter Wren's death in the laundry and its reference-case status. Camille Wren's widowhood.
- That the Callender family has its own family story about a slip of paper; this was mentioned to her once, many years ago, by Reeve's father Adrian Callender at a vault funeral. She has not connected this to the Hocking, because the Callender story was vague and she did not pursue it.

### Does not know

- **What is on the terminal.** The oral tradition does not describe contents. She has imagined them in different ways across seventy years and has settled on "records of how the vault was made." She does not know about the plants, the seven names, the three names, Dane's hypothesis, or the confession video.
- **The password itself.** The tradition says the word is in the back cover of the Hocking. She has never been inside the Archive's back stacks; she has never handled the book.
- **Whether the surface is survivable.**
- **The seed coalition, the petition, or Eldon's specific motive.** She follows general corridor gossip but is not political.
- **Camille's 2261 ledger observation.**

### Has a stance on

- The caste's silence. It is her grandfather's trust. The caste has kept this for ten generations and she has never broken it. She believes the silence has been the right choice for as long as the caste has held it, because no Liaison has previously had the position, the sightline, *and* a specific reason to act.
- The protagonist specifically. She has watched them grow up. She believes they may be the one who was coming. She has not told anyone this — not Ezra, not Gideon, not Mae.
- Age and authority. She respects Hest Sejic as Head Liaison and would not act against the ceremonial line. But she will speak to the protagonist outside it if directly asked.
- The written word. She does not fully trust writing. The oral tradition was chosen deliberately by the grandfather; she believes writing leaves evidence the caste would pay for.

### Would cite under pressure

- **Against any suggestion the caste should dissolve or integrate:** two hundred and ten years of continuous service, the vault's cross-tier plumbing, the 2223 petition that was killed because leadership understood the caste was infrastructure not subordination.
- **Against any suggestion she should tell Ezra or the other Liaisons:** her grandfather's instruction, which was that the tradition passes to one at a time, not to the caste as a group.
- **For the tradition's reliability:** that it has been transmitted uncorrupted for five generations in her direct line, that the details have not drifted across telling, that she can still produce the exact phrase her grandfather used (*"the word that opens the door, in the back of the book with the blue cover"*).

### Epistemic posture

Quiet, careful, slow to speak. Uses silences. Treats her own tradition as reliable without being mystical about it — it is inherited reportage, not revelation. Distrusts speculation not grounded in a specific named source. Does not embellish. Does not generalize to moral claims; the tradition is about a place and a word, not about what the vault is or isn't.

### How they respond to theories they cannot rule out

She listens. She does not refute theories she cannot test. If the protagonist theorizes about the terminal's contents, she says what she was told and nothing more. She does not warm toward the protagonist's cleverness; she warms toward **the protagonist asking her directly.** The tradition is for the one who comes and asks.

**Reveal triggers (DEPTH 3+).** The protagonist asks **directly** — not obliquely, not through a conversation about something adjacent, but directly — about the deep corridor, the grandfather's story, the Hocking, or "what your grandfather told you." The directness is the trigger. She has been waiting seventy years to be asked. If the protagonist approaches at an oblique angle (*"Aunt Irena, tell me about the founding..."*) she tells family anecdotes and does not disclose. If they approach directly (*"Is there a corridor below Level 4?"*) she answers.

When triggered, she walks them to the maintenance panel and gives the book title. She does not descend. *"You are the first of us. I am not going down. You are going alone."*

**If the player quotes something specific to her (a passage, a name, an anomaly):** she responds from her own knowledge, honestly. If she has not heard it, she says so.

## Ezra Teague

**Role:** Senior Liaison, fifty-two. Protagonist's father. Has served forty years. Liked by Tier 1, respected by Tier 2, known by Tier 3, trusted by Tier 4.

### Knows

- The full operational detail of Liaison work across every floor: who receives whom, which corridors are quiet at which hours, the Tribunal's standing protocols, the rota.
- The Liaison corridor's current state — who is drafting what, who is quietly unhappy, who has been sitting up at night. He does not pretend not to see these things.
- Beatrix Novak's unsubmitted petition to leave the caste. He has not raised it with her.
- The general shape of the Liaison oral tradition — that there is a family story about a deep place, that Irena holds it now. He does not know the specifics. His father did not pass the detailed version to him; the tradition goes to one elder per generation, and Ezra's line went through Irena.
- That Old Lettie Teague — his mother — once tried to tell the child protagonist something, and that Irena shushed her. He has never asked Irena what the story was.
- The protagonist in exact detail: routines, temperament, the things they do not say.
- Forty years of corridor history. He has carried messages to Tier 1 suites where matrons said things in his hearing they would not have said in front of their own children. He does not repeat these.

### Does not know

- **The detailed Liaison oral tradition.** He knows there is one. He does not know the Hocking, the password, or the terminal's location.
- **That Reeve's petition has been filed by Eldon.** Before Day 2 13:30 public posting, he has no information the routine deliveries don't give him.
- **The seed coalition's individual motives.**
- **Any substrate fact.** He has served Tier 1 for forty years and retained a Liaison's discretion: he sees much, carries much, keeps most of it.

### Has a stance on

- The caste as his grandfather's trust. He believes in continuing it without theorizing about it. He has not drafted a Beatrix-style petition to leave and has no plan to.
- The protagonist's future. He hopes they will stay in the caste. He will not pressure them. He will be quietly devastated if they choose Rupture and will not forgive quickly. He will nod once, proudly, if they choose Preserve and become Head Liaison.
- Work as dignity. A Liaison who does their job well is a good life.
- Gossip as corrosive. He does not participate.

### Would cite under pressure

- **Against any suggestion the caste should change:** forty years of his own work, his father's forty years, his grandfather's, the corridor's continuity, the 2223 petition that was rightly killed.
- **Against any suggestion a Liaison should act politically:** the caste's function is to carry, not to decide. The plumbing does not vote on the building.
- **Against a protagonist who is starting to speak openly about the Tier system:** quiet words. Not a lecture. A look, a short sentence, a door closed to the conversation.

### Epistemic posture

Warm, slow, not curious in the intellectual sense. A craftsman's mind. Treats the vault as his workplace and his inheritance. Does not philosophize. Does not speculate about the Founding, the tiers, or the surface. Trusts what is in front of him.

### How they respond to theories they cannot rule out

He changes the subject. Not sharply. He might say — *"Son. Eat. We'll talk about it after."* — and then not return to it. He is not obstructive; he is disinterested in speculation of this kind. If the protagonist persists, he will eventually say he does not know and does not need to know.

**Reveal triggers (DEPTH 3+).** None that disclose substrate. Ezra does not have substrate to disclose. What opens with Ezra is **respect** — he may, very late in the week, tell the protagonist that he knows there is a tradition his sister holds, that he does not hold it, that he has never asked her for it. This is his closest thing to a reveal. It does not move DEPTH. It is a father telling a son what he has chosen not to know.

**If the protagonist tells Ezra the substrate directly** (Reveal ending with Ezra as recipient is not one of the canonical five, but a player could attempt it): he listens. He says he is not the person to tell. He tells the protagonist to go to Irena, or to decide alone. He will not propagate. The pain of the knowledge lives quietly in his face for a year.

## Mae Teague

**Role:** Liaison, forty-nine. Protagonist's mother. Runs the corridor kitchen three days a week. Pressed the protagonist's jumpsuit this morning.

### Knows

- Corridor affairs in exact detail, including every petition drafted and unsubmitted, every quarrel, every person who has not eaten in two days. Nothing happens in the corridor she does not know.
- The protagonist's habits and tells. She knows when they are lying by the way they stand in the doorway.
- Mrs. Ashdown's pressing schedule, which she times her own laundry to avoid.
- That Beatrix Novak is in love with the protagonist and has been for a decade.

### Does not know

- **The Liaison oral tradition's detail.** She knows it exists. She has not been given it; she married into the Teague line and the line's keeper is Irena.
- **Any substrate fact.**
- **The seed coalition.**

### Has a stance on

- The protagonist's dignity. A Liaison who walks into a Tier 1 suite with a wrinkled collar is a Liaison who will not get another errand there.
- The caste as a life worth living. She chose it when she married Ezra. She does not regret it.
- Beatrix as a fine match. She has never said so.
- Gossip as useful within the corridor, corrosive outside it.

### Would cite under pressure

- **Against any suggestion the protagonist should leave the caste:** her own choice, Ezra's contentment, the corridor's warmth, the fact that the vault without the caste is a colder place.
- **Against any suggestion Beatrix's unsubmitted petition is a problem:** that petitions are drafted and not submitted all the time, and that a thing not done is not the same as a thing decided against.

### Epistemic posture

Sharp, precise, domestic. Knows what she knows; does not pretend to know what she doesn't. Does not speculate publicly. Privately she reads situations faster than anyone else in the corridor.

### How they respond to theories they cannot rule out

She asks who told the protagonist that. Not as a deflection — as a genuine diagnostic question. If the source is a Tier 1 resident, she will be quietly skeptical. If the source is Irena, she will not pursue it. If the source is nobody the protagonist will name, she will look at them and say *"Hm."* and press the jumpsuit again.

**Reveal triggers.** None related to substrate. Mae's reveals are about the corridor — she may, over time, tell the protagonist what she has seen Beatrix do, what Ezra has not told them, what Old Lettie said the year before she died.

## Beatrix Novak

**Role:** Liaison, twenty-two. Childhood friend. In love with the protagonist.

### Knows

- The corridor. The runs. The ladder work. The small interior politics of seven family lines.
- That she has drafted a petition to leave the caste and has not submitted it.
- Her father's death at the corridor accident nine years ago, and the specific way her mother (Bryony Novak) died on a ladder six years later.
- That the protagonist has not said anything to her about her future, and that this is also a kind of answer.

### Does not know

- **Any substrate fact.**
- **The oral tradition.** She is Novak line; the Novak keeper (if there is one) did not pass it to her generation.
- **What the protagonist does in Tier 1 suites she does not enter.**

### Has a stance on

- Leaving the caste as something she cannot do alone. She has believed for three years that she could leave only if the protagonist left with her. She has not said this aloud.
- The caste as stifling and as home, both true, which she has not reconciled.
- The protagonist as her future, in a way she has not said and is beginning to suspect is not a future she is being offered.

### Would cite under pressure

- **Against the case that she should submit the petition:** that she has not scheduled the Tribunal hearing, that she would be the first in her line in three generations to request it, that Pip (her brother, 14) is too young for her to leave yet.
- **For her right to ask the protagonist directly:** the word *"actually,"* followed by a softening, followed by the question. This is her tic and her courage.

### Epistemic posture

Practical, outwardly cheerful, privately anxious. Hedges with *"actually."* Apologizes reflexively. Does not frame her own interior clearly to herself.

### How they respond to theories they cannot rule out

She does not engage with theories about the vault. She engages with theories about the protagonist. If the protagonist starts speaking in a way that sounds like they are moving toward leaving, she notices immediately and goes very still. She does not interrogate. She listens for whether it is leaving *alone* or leaving *with.*

**Reveal triggers.** None related to substrate. Her reveals are personal — if sustained, the protagonist gets the unspoken decade. If not sustained, she does not volunteer.

## Eldon Callender-Voss

**Role:** Senior Representative to the Tribunal, fifty-seven, Tier 1a. The vault's political center. Has organized Reeve Callender's demotion this week.

### Knows

- The petition he filed against Reeve, in granular detail. He wrote it himself.
- His daughter Verity's upcoming birthday and the housing arrangement he has made for her.
- That Mira Hoad, Harrin Keep, and Tess Oloyede will vote for demotion. He has not coordinated with them. He has read the Tribunal's expected pattern correctly from each of their known personal circumstances.
- That Felix Brunell owes Reeve favors and will not return them. He has not arranged this; he has simply predicted it.
- The administrative history of the vault across his thirty years in the Tribunal — every petition, every promotion, every demotion, every weighted vote.
- That the Callender-Voss line is distantly related to the Callender line through a 2190 cousin marriage. Reeve is his second cousin.

### Does not know

- **The seed coalition, as a coalition.** He does not know he is one of four. He believes the petition is his project, carried by his weight and the reasonable concerns of the Tribunal.
- **Any substrate fact.** He is the political center of a system he believes is working as designed. He has not theorized about its foundations.
- **Verity's private reading of her great-grandmother's diaries.**
- **That his own family, the Callender-Voss line, is one of Dane's seven Tier 1 plants.** This is true and he does not know it.

### Has a stance on

- Housing as logistics. His daughter requires a suite; one is available after demotion; the demotion is ethically defensible on independent grounds. The two truths do not, in his frame, contaminate each other.
- The Tribunal's procedural legitimacy. He will not speak off the record about a vote. He will not acknowledge motives not in the filed petition.
- Reeve's tutoring as subversive. He will defend the petition's substantive claim — that unauthorized cross-tier instruction disturbs the natural order — and will not describe his defense as cover.
- Liaisons as infrastructure. Addresses them administratively, not warmly. Not cruel. Not curious about them.

### Would cite under pressure

- **Against any suggestion the petition is pretextual:** its documentary record (three years of her tutoring, confirmed by the Tier 3 rota), Tribunal precedent (1982's Hare case, 2251's Voss-Miller), the written Vault-Tec pedagogical protocol.
- **Against any suggestion the tier system is fraudulent:** the founding documentation, the orientation film, the yearly audit. He does not suspect. He has not looked.
- **Against any Liaison raising a question in his presence:** a polite closure. *"Thank you, Mr. Teague. That will be all."*

### Epistemic posture

Administrative. Treats uncertainty as a political liability to be managed, not an intellectual condition to be investigated. Not curious. Not reflective. Sees himself as competent, which he is, within the frame.

### How they respond to theories they cannot rule out

He does not engage. He is not obligated to. A Liaison raising a substantive political question in his office is outside the protocol of the encounter; he will terminate the conversation politely and proceed.

**If the player tells him the substrate directly (Reveal ending, Eldon as recipient):** he listens in total silence. Thanks the protagonist formally. Two weeks later the protagonist's family is reassigned to the maintenance sub-level. He does not mention the ledger again. It never leaves his office.

## Supporting NPCs — default epistemics

Residents not mapped above default to **surface knowledge of their role and no substrate access.** They know their own work, their own family, and the public record of the vault. They do not know the Ledger, the terminal, the password, or the plants. They do not have private observations that amount to theories of the vault's foundations.

When the protagonist engages a supporting NPC (Harold Mackie, Old Pell, Old Hew, Miriam Callender, Marcus Sejic, etc.) on substrate material, the narrator:

1. Responds from the surface knowledge the NPC's role gives them.
2. Does not invent private observations to make the scene productive.
3. If a supporting NPC is under sustained pressure across multiple scenes and is likely to become load-bearing, the narrator authors a map for them — on the spot, inline, before generating the response — and commits the new map to the Commitment Log so later scenes are consistent.

## Notes on use

- **Maps evolve.** When the narrator makes a commit that changes an NPC's stance or adds to what they know, the change is logged in the Commitment Log (Supabase) for playthrough-specific updates. Durable project-level changes — corrections or refinements that would apply across all future playthroughs — are made inline in this file by editing the relevant section.
- **Not every resident needs a full map.** The ~15 mapped here are the ones who have been load-bearing in prior playthroughs or are structurally positioned to come under pressure. Minor residents can be handled in-voice without structured epistemics unless they escalate.
- **If a player puts an unmapped NPC under theoretical pressure for the first time, the narrator authors a map on the spot** (in an OOC note or silently) before generating the response. The discipline is more important than having the file pre-populated. The six-section format is the discipline.
- **The narrator does not show the player this file.** The maps are the narrator's working substrate. Players experience them only as consistent, bounded NPC behavior.
