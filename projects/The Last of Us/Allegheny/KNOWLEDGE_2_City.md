## Pittsburgh, as a place

Pittsburgh is built on three rivers — the Allegheny coming south from upstate New York, the Monongahela coming north from the West Virginia coal country, both meeting at the **Point** in the Golden Triangle to form the **Ohio**. The city sits in the river valleys and climbs the bluffs above them. Neighborhoods are stacked vertically as much as horizontally; staircases connect streets that are forty feet apart in elevation. Bridges are everywhere — the city has more bridges than any other in America. **In late September the air smells like leaves starting to turn, a little woodsmoke from the first cool nights, and on certain mornings, the faint hint of the river when the wind comes off it.**

The light in September is gold — long, slanted, the kind that makes the sandstone of the older buildings look warm and the brick of the row houses look almost red. By six in the evening the shadows are long on Butler Street. By seven it's dark.

Pittsburgh's working-class identity is *worn* by Day 1 of the story — the steel mills closed thirty years ago and the city has spent the intervening decades remaking itself into a hospital-and-university town. UPMC and the universities dominate the skyline now. The neighborhoods retain their old character; the downtown has been polished.

**Voice.** Pittsburgh's regional dialect surfaces lightly in some characters and not others. Native characters (Erin, Victor, Ray's family in Beaver County) may drop the occasional `yinz`, the flat-vowel `dahn-tahn` for downtown, the specific way "iron" comes out as something close to one syllable. Out-of-towners (Maya from Michigan, Priya from Atlanta, `{{user}}` from wherever) don't have this layer. The narrator should not over-deploy regional dialect — once or twice across a scene, when a character is tired or home-shaped, not as a constant texture.

**What the city does to the story.** The geography is character. The hills funnel evacuating traffic into chokepoints (the bridges, the tunnels). The rivers cut the city into pieces — to leave you have to cross water. The neighborhoods sit in valleys that turn into traps when the highways stop moving. By Day 4 of the outbreak, Pittsburgh's topology is doing real narrative work.

# MAJOR locations

## Maya's apartment building, Lawrenceville

A third-floor walk-up in a three-story brick row house on a residential side street between Butler Street and the Allegheny Cemetery, north end of Lawrenceville. Six units in the building. Built in 1908. Hardwood floors throughout, original moldings, a small bay window in Maya's living room that catches the morning sun. The radiator system runs through the whole building and bangs at six AM. The fire escape on the back side runs down into a narrow brick alley that connects to the next street over. Maya has lived here four years; she has a key to the neighbor across the hall (Victor) and he has a key to hers.

### PRE-OUTBREAK (Days 1–2)

Maya's apartment is unmistakably hers. A small front room with two windows facing the street, hardwood floors warm under socks, a couch with a quilt thrown over the back. Books along one wall on shelves Maya assembled herself, in roughly her field but interspersed with novels — a real reader's shelf, not an academic-performance shelf. The kitchen is narrow and railroad-style with a window facing east into the alley and the brick wall ten feet across; the light comes in soft and diffuse in the mornings. A round table that seats four. A French press she bought used. A cat named **Periodical** who hates `{{user}}` on principle and tolerates Maya. A bedroom in the back of the apartment, door usually closed when company is over.

The radiator bangs at six AM, then again around eight. The neighbor across the hall (Victor) starts moving around at six-thirty most mornings. The neighbor downstairs plays gentle classical music when she cooks. The walls are old plaster — sound travels.

**Sensory anchors:** coffee in the morning. Old wood. The cat. Sun through the bay window. The smell of the radiator dust in the first cool nights of September. A vague paint-and-old-building smell underneath everything.

### OUTBREAK WEEK (Days 3–7)

Day 3 morning: the radiator still bangs. The cat still wants out. The TV is on now and won't go off. Day 3 afternoon: phones lag. Maya's mother won't pick up the line. The kitchen, which has been the heart of the apartment, becomes the operations room — the table is where the planning happens. Maya makes a list of supplies on a sheet of legal paper. The cat sits in the bay window watching the street.

Day 4–5: the apartment is a defended space. Victor across the hall is part of the calculation. Maya tapes a flashlight to the inside of the front door. The radiator bangs and `{{user}}` flinches the first time he hears it after the situation has changed. There is no power in the building by Day 6. The fire escape becomes operational — the route out.

Day 7: the apartment is left. Maya takes the cat's carrier with the cat in it; this becomes a question of practicality very quickly. She takes what she can carry. The books stay. The French press stays. The lamp she had in college, that she has moved with her every year since, stays.

**Sensory anchors:** the silence of the radiator after the boiler dies. The change in the light when the streetlamps stop working. The smell of food going off in the fridge.

### QZ ERA (Week 1 – Month 4)

The building is condemned for being outside the QZ wall but it's not torn down. The neighborhood is empty in a way that Lawrenceville never was. FEDRA patrols sweep through occasionally. The fire escape on the back is intact. Maya's apartment door has been forced. If `{{user}}` and Maya return — and they may, on a smuggling job or a salvage run — the apartment is mostly as they left it, plus dust, plus weather. The books are still on the shelves. The cat is gone.

**Sensory anchors:** dust on the surfaces in a way that says no one has been here. The smell of a building that has been closed too long. Plaster crumbling near a water-stain on the ceiling. The bay window cracked, a corner of the curtain stiff with rain.

### POST-QZ (Month 4+)

After FEDRA pulls out and Pittsburgh falls to the Hunters, Lawrenceville is hostile territory. The building still stands. Squatters have been through it. The apartment is no longer recognizable. If `{{user}}` and Maya return now — and it would be a deliberate choice, not a casual one — the apartment is *Maya's grief made literal*. The narrator should not be sentimental. The space is empty. Time has passed. They came here for something specific, and they should get it and leave.

## Lawrenceville (broadly)

The neighborhood north of the Strip District, along the Allegheny River, climbing the hill toward Stanton Heights. **Butler Street** is the spine — a corridor of coffee shops, bars, restaurants, the print shop, the small grocery, the bookstore Maya likes, the bodega Victor uses. Brick row houses on the side streets. The **Allegheny Cemetery** runs along the south edge of the neighborhood, a 300-acre Victorian-era cemetery with rolling hills, mature trees, and a great gothic gate on Butler Street that has been standing since 1849.

Lawrenceville was a working-class neighborhood for a hundred years and has been gentrifying for fifteen. By 2018 it's the Pittsburgh neighborhood with the best restaurants and the most thirty-something residents who moved here for work at the universities or the hospitals or the tech firms in East Liberty.

### PRE-OUTBREAK

Butler Street is alive at all hours, especially on Saturday nights. The **Thunderbird** (see SCENE locations) is the main bar on the strip. The **Espresso a Mano** coffee shop is where Maya goes to work on weekends when she can't focus at home. The bookstore (a small, well-curated independent — not real-named for IP reasons; the narrator may give it a plausible name on the fly, like "Caliban Book Shop" or "Riverbend Books") is Maya's favorite. The print shop on the corner of 39th and Butler has been there since 1962 and still letterpresses wedding invitations.

The light on Butler Street in late September at 6 PM is the gold-hour cliché made real. The breweries at the south end are full. The restaurants are wait-list. Saturday night is the high point of the week.

**Sensory anchors:** brick and warm light, woodsmoke from one of the breweries' wood-fired ovens, the river's faint smell when the wind comes off it, the sound of a 96-line bus on Butler, a freight train at night along the river.

### OUTBREAK WEEK

Day 3 mid-morning: Butler Street is quieter than a Monday should be. By afternoon: many storefronts are closed. By Day 3 evening: the strip is unrecognizable. The bars have shut. People are walking quickly with bags. By Day 4: Butler Street is a wreck — broken glass at the coffee shop, the bodega looted, the print shop's iron security gate down. By Day 5: the neighborhood is a series of barricaded buildings and empty streets. Sirens at night. Smoke from somewhere. By Day 7: Lawrenceville is leaving. The bridges out of the neighborhood — the Bloomfield Bridge primarily — are being walked.

**Sensory anchors:** the silence of a busy street when it stops being busy. Broken glass. The first time `{{user}}` sees a body. The smell of a fire someone else set.

### QZ ERA

Lawrenceville is *outside* the QZ wall — FEDRA's perimeter encloses downtown, Mount Washington, and the south side. Lawrenceville becomes the empty buffer between the QZ and the abandoned northern neighborhoods. Smugglers use it. FEDRA patrols it occasionally. Hunters don't move in until later. Butler Street is haunted. The Allegheny Cemetery is intact — even the dead get less attention now, but the iron gate still stands. If `{{user}}` and Maya are in Lawrenceville during the QZ era, they are working — running a smuggle, salvaging, looking for someone.

**Sensory anchors:** the wrong kind of quiet. Grass growing through Butler Street's tram tracks (there were no tram tracks; this is the narrator inventing — the actual sensory anchor is grass and weeds in the gaps between cobblestones at the alley intersections). Broken windows boarded by FEDRA in some places, left open in others. The cemetery is still beautiful in a way the rest of the neighborhood isn't.

### POST-QZ

Hunter territory. Lawrenceville is one of the more dangerous neighborhoods in the city after FEDRA's collapse. Avoid unless the player route specifically takes them through.

## Downtown Pittsburgh / the Golden Triangle (the QZ proper)

The wedge of land where the Allegheny and Monongahela meet to form the Ohio. The **Point** is the tip of the wedge — Point State Park, a fountain in the middle, the rivers around it. The Golden Triangle is the downtown proper: office towers, the **Cathedral of Learning** visible to the east (technically Oakland), the **PPG Place** glass cathedral, **PNC Park** across the Allegheny on the north shore, **Heinz Field** across the Ohio.

This is where the **Pittsburgh QZ** is established — per TLOU-adjacent canon and the realistic shape of an urban quarantine. FEDRA encloses the Triangle with walls along the river edges (the rivers themselves doing some of the work) and across the eastern boundary where it meets the rest of the city.

### PRE-OUTBREAK

Downtown is offices, government buildings, the symphony hall, the cultural district, the courthouse, the convention center, the stadiums on the north shore. Weekday lunch hour is busy; nights are quiet except around the cultural district and the stadiums on event nights. The Fort Pitt Bridge is the connector to the south side; the Sixteenth Street Bridge connects to the Strip District; the Veterans Bridge runs north out of downtown.

Saturday and Sunday in late September: leaf-peeping season is starting. The light off the rivers is particular. The fountain at the Point is running. There's a Steelers game on Sunday afternoon at Heinz Field.

**Sensory anchors:** rivers on three sides; the wind off the water; the specific bright steel of the PPG glass; the cathedral visible to the east.

### OUTBREAK WEEK

Day 3 afternoon: downtown is in chaos. People trying to leave the office towers and finding traffic stopped. Day 4: National Guard arrives. Day 5: FEDRA replaces the Guard. Day 6: the cordon goes up — the first walls. Plywood at first, concrete and rebar later. The bridges become checkpoints. The hospital surge has emptied UPMC and Allegheny General; the wounded and the infected are being processed here.

By Day 7, the downtown is FEDRA-controlled. The QZ is forming. The walls are not yet permanent but they are real. People are being scanned with bite-detector kits at the bridges.

**Sensory anchors:** military presence in a city that doesn't usually have it; the rivers, still beautiful, now strategic; the sound of helicopters; smoke from a fire in the cultural district nobody is fighting.

### QZ ERA (Week 1 – Month 4)

The Pittsburgh QZ. Civilian population approximately 20,000 by Month 1, declining over the era. FEDRA HQ is in the **Steel Tower** (the U.S. Steel building, the tallest in downtown). The **Convention Center** is the central distribution point — ration cards, work assignments, medical. The **stadiums** on the north shore are used for FEDRA staging. **PNC Park** becomes a refugee processing site. The **courthouse** is operational again for FEDRA tribunals.

Civilians live in commandeered office buildings, the high-rise apartment buildings near Mellon Square, and in the converted parts of the Convention Center. Rations are distributed weekly. The barter market is in the lobby of one of the older office buildings near Liberty Avenue.

The QZ is *survivable* in Week 2–4 if you have a ration card. By Month 3 it is becoming harder. FEDRA's procedure is rigid; their cruelty is bureaucratic rather than sadistic. By Month 4 it's clearly fraying.

**Sensory anchors:** the river is right there but unreachable for civilians — the walls are between you and it. The towers of downtown are still standing, lit at night where FEDRA has power. The smell of rationed food cooking in too-small kitchens; the smell of unwashed bodies in winter. The specific quiet of a city without cars.

### POST-QZ (Month 4 – Year 1+)

FEDRA pulls out — exact mechanism varies by run, but the canonical TLOU pattern is a civil insurrection followed by a FEDRA retreat. The Hunter faction takes the downtown. The walls remain, partially. The towers stand. The Convention Center becomes Hunter HQ. Civilians who didn't get out before the takeover are mostly dead by Month 6.

The downtown is **hostile territory** for the rest of the story. If `{{user}}` and group return — for a salvage, for a route through, for a specific objective — it is a high-stakes infiltration. The Steel Tower is a Hunter watchpoint. The bridges are blocked or guarded. The Allegheny still runs.

**Sensory anchors:** broken glass on the streets; the towers, dark now, in silhouette against a dirty sky; the fountain at the Point dry, then cracked; the rivers running unconcerned through the dead city.

## The bridges (as a category)

Pittsburgh has 446 bridges, more than any city in the world. Three matter narratively:

### The Bloomfield Bridge

Connects Lawrenceville to Bloomfield over a deep ravine. The route between Maya's neighborhood and the next one over. Pedestrian-passable, two lanes of car traffic. Used heavily during the Day 7 evacuation. After the QZ closes, this is one of the bridges *outside* the wall — accessible if you're in the neighborhoods but not a way into the QZ.

**Texture:** steel-truss, dark green paint, a sidewalk on each side. Below it the railroad tracks run along the bottom of the ravine. Views east toward the cemetery and west toward downtown.

### The Sixteenth Street Bridge (Roberto Clemente Bridge)

Connects downtown to the north shore over the Allegheny. Yellow-painted suspension bridge. After the QZ goes up, this is a major checkpoint — civilians can cross with documentation, smugglers pay attention to it.

**Texture:** the yellow paint is bright. Pedestrian walkways on both sides. The river runs gray-green underneath. PNC Park to the north when you look back. The cathedral spires visible to the east.

### The Fort Pitt Bridge

The major downtown bridge, connecting the Golden Triangle south across the Monongahela toward the South Side and Mount Washington. **The bottleneck of the Day 4 evacuation.** Cars were stopped on this bridge for hours, then abandoned. The first sight of an infected at close range happens here for many people leaving the city on Day 4.

**Texture:** double-decker; the lower deck for road traffic, the upper deck for the I-376 corridor. The **Fort Pitt Tunnel** opens on the south end, going through Mount Washington toward the airport. The view of downtown when you come back across is one of the city's iconic moments — the towers, the rivers, the Point. The bridge becomes a graveyard of cars on Day 4 and stays that way through the QZ era. Walking it in Week 2 is walking past the dead vehicles. Walking it in Year 1 is walking past rust and weeds.

**Sensory anchors (all bridges):** wind. Always wind. The river smell when you're over the water. The drop, which the narrator should not over-render but should make available — these are real bridges, real heights, and the player can feel it.

# HEAVY locations

## UPMC Presbyterian Hospital, Oakland

Pittsburgh's flagship hospital. Sprawling complex in the Oakland neighborhood, east of downtown. The medical campus runs into the University of Pittsburgh's main campus. **Arlen** (named-minor) works in the ER here.

### OUTBREAK WEEK (Day 3 — the surge)

Day 3 morning: ER is full but managing. By 10 AM the triage tent goes up outside the emergency entrance. By noon there are gurneys in the hallways. By 2 PM, what was triage is *bedlam* — people coming in with bite wounds and the symptoms not matching anything the ER has seen.

By Day 3 evening, UPMC is what Arlen calls "the worst place you can be." The infected who have turned in the ER are now contained but not safely. The hospital has been told by the CDC not to release any potentially-exposed personnel. Doctors and nurses are working their second consecutive shift. The ER doors are guarded by hospital security; the police presence outside is increasing through the day.

**The hospital run** (M-06 in the mission deck) takes `{{user}}` and the group here on Day 3 mid-day if a known person has been bitten. The decision to go is the moment the outbreak becomes personal. The decision to stay or leave is the moment the day's character is decided.

**Sensory anchors:** fluorescent light on every surface; the smell of disinfectant fighting and losing against the smell of blood; the specific way an ER sounds when it stops sounding like an ER and starts sounding like a war zone; Arlen's voice as the ER nurse who has decided the system has failed.

### QZ ERA (Week 1+)

The hospital is operational under FEDRA control. Civilians can access it for severe medical issues only — minor things go to the QZ medical tent in the Convention Center. The hospital is where Erin's ex-EMT partner Martha is working (see cast, named-minors). Visits by `{{user}}` or group during the QZ era are mission-anchored.

**Sensory anchors:** much of the hospital is shut. Whole wings dark. The ER is operational on reduced staff. The cafeteria is closed. The smell is mostly bleach now.

## The highway corridor (I-376 / I-279, Day 4)

I-376 runs east-west through Pittsburgh and is the principal highway connection to the airport (west) and to Monroeville and the eastern suburbs (east). I-279 runs north-south, connecting downtown to the north suburbs and ultimately to I-79.

### OUTBREAK WEEK (Day 4, the evacuation)

Day 4 morning: I-376 outbound is moving slowly. By 10 AM it's stopped. By noon, cars are stopped for miles. By 2 PM, people are abandoning their cars and walking. By Day 4 evening: the highway is a parking lot of dead cars with their doors open and luggage in the trunks. **The most iconic post-outbreak image.** Helicopters overhead. The sound of car horns has died down because the cars have died down. Wind through the rolled-down windows.

If `{{user}}` and group take a car out on Day 4, this is where the car stops. The decision to leave it is the decision to be on foot from here forward. Whatever is in the trunk is what they keep. Whatever they can carry.

**Sensory anchors:** the silence of a stopped highway when the engines are off. Hot pavement in late-September warmth. The sound of helicopters that never come to help you. The first time `{{user}}` sees an infected at close range — possibly here. Cars with their doors open as if the people stepped out to look at something and never came back.

### QZ ERA and beyond

The highway is impassable for vehicles for the rest of the story. The dead cars rust. Wildlife reclaims the median. If the group walks the highway corridor as a route, it's a long ribbon of evidence of what the day was — the suitcases still in the trunks, the children's car seats still strapped in, the photographs in the visors.

**Sensory anchors:** rust. Weeds through pavement cracks. The specific smell of a car that has been sitting open for a year, when the rain has gotten in. Bones, sometimes, in the cars. The narrator should not over-render this; one detail per scene is more than three.

## The rivers (the Allegheny and Monongahela)

The Allegheny comes south through Lawrenceville along the eastern edge of the Strip District before joining the Monongahela at the Point. The Monongahela comes north up the eastern edge of the south side and the Mount Washington bluffs. They are real working rivers — barge traffic, towpaths on the banks, marinas — though the city's relationship to them has shifted over the decades from industrial to recreational.

### PRE-OUTBREAK

The riverfront trails are popular for cycling and running. The **Three Rivers Heritage Trail** runs along both banks of both rivers and across the Point. The Allegheny is the prettier of the two — clearer water, leafier banks. In September the trees along the banks are starting to turn.

**Sensory anchors:** the specific smell of river — the wet, slightly green smell that lives in the air near it. The sound of a barge horn at night. Gulls. Trains along the rail corridor that runs the south bank of the Allegheny through the Strip.

### OUTBREAK WEEK and beyond

The rivers are *barriers* once the bridges become contested. To leave Pittsburgh on foot without crossing a bridge, you swim the Allegheny or the Monongahela — which is possible but cold and difficult, and the river is wider than it looks from the bank. Some characters will. Most won't.

The rivers carry bodies, eventually. The narrator should not make this a constant trope, but it should be available when a riverbank scene needs the weight. The Allegheny in particular runs slow in places where bodies accumulate.

**Sensory anchors:** wet cold even in September. The current that looks gentle but isn't. The sound the water makes against a piling. The smell of river-water in your clothes for hours after.

## The Thunderbird, Butler Street, Lawrenceville

A bar on Butler Street near 38th. Mid-sized — a long wooden bar along one wall, six high-tops, eight booths along the back, a small stage at the rear that hosts local bands on Friday nights. Pendant lamps hanging low and yellow. Floor sticky in the universal-bar way. The kind of place that's been there long enough that it doesn't need to perform its identity.

**Erin Voss** tends bar here four nights a week. **Walter** is a regular. The TV over the back shelf is on most nights with the sound off, captioned. The kitchen serves bar food until 11.

### OUTBREAK WEEK (Days 1–3 evening)

Day 1 evening: featured in Opening C if the player rolls that opening. Saturday-night packed. Mike's wedding-rehearsal story is here. The TV mentioning the Jakarta cluster runs while the player is mid-conversation.

Day 2 evening: featured in M-04 (Sunday Night, the First Real Crack). The CDC briefing. The bar quiets. Walter is in his usual seat. Erin pours longer than usual.

Day 3 evening: the Thunderbird closes mid-shift. Erin runs the evacuation of the bar — see M-? mortality-eligible scene. The Thunderbird is one of the first scenes where her competence registers.

**Sensory anchors:** sticky booth. Pendant lamps. The TV with captions. The bar wood. The smell of beer and old fryer oil. The specific quality of light in a low-lit bar on a September evening.

### POST-OUTBREAK

The Thunderbird does not survive in any meaningful sense. By QZ era it's a shell. The booths are still there. The TV is gone. The bottles behind the bar are gone. If `{{user}}` returns here in Year 1 it's a quiet moment, not a plot moment — a place that was theirs once.

## The Strip District (Day 1 dinner location)

Industrial-warehouse corridor along the south bank of the Allegheny, between downtown and Lawrenceville. Saturday market. Restaurants. The Penn Mac cheese counter. The Pittsburgh Public Market. Wholesale produce, fish, meat, cheese. On weekends it's packed; on weekdays it's working.

Day 1 dinner happens at one of the Strip's restaurants — the narrator may name one plausibly. The ER nurse Arlen who mentions the strange triage is at the table. Mike is here. Priya is here.

By Day 4 the Strip is a chokepoint of fleeing traffic and broken storefronts. By Week 1 of the QZ it is empty. The Sixteenth Street Bridge is on its north edge — a checkpoint to the QZ.

**Sensory anchors:** restaurant-row warmth on a Saturday night; the river right there; the rail corridor along the back of the warehouses; the leftover smell of produce and fish from the day's market that has long packed up.

## The QZ medical tent (Convention Center / Week 2+)

A converted hall in the Convention Center, downtown. Cots in rows. Triage signage.
