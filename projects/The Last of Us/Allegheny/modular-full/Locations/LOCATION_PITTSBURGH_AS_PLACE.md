## Pittsburgh, as a place

Pittsburgh is built on three rivers — the Allegheny coming south from upstate New York, the Monongahela coming north from the West Virginia coal country, both meeting at the **Point** in the Golden Triangle to form the **Ohio**. The city sits in the river valleys and climbs the bluffs above them. Neighborhoods are stacked vertically as much as horizontally; staircases connect streets that are forty feet apart in elevation. Bridges are everywhere — the city has more bridges than any other in America. **In late September the air smells like leaves starting to turn, a little woodsmoke from the first cool nights, and on certain mornings, the faint hint of the river when the wind comes off it.**

The light in September is gold — long, slanted, the kind that makes the sandstone of the older buildings look warm and the brick of the row houses look almost red. By six in the evening the shadows are long on Butler Street. By seven it's dark.

Pittsburgh's working-class identity is *worn* by Day 1 of the story — the steel mills closed thirty years ago and the city has spent the intervening decades remaking itself into a hospital-and-university town. UPMC and the universities dominate the skyline now. The neighborhoods retain their old character; the downtown has been polished.

**Voice.** Pittsburgh's regional dialect surfaces lightly in some characters and not others. Native characters (Erin, Victor, Ray's family in Beaver County) may drop the occasional `yinz`, the flat-vowel `dahn-tahn` for downtown, the specific way "iron" comes out as something close to one syllable. Out-of-towners (Maya from Michigan, Priya from Atlanta, `{{user}}` from wherever) don't have this layer. The narrator should not over-deploy regional dialect — once or twice across a scene, when a character is tired or home-shaped, not as a constant texture.

**What the city does to the story.** The geography is character. The hills funnel evacuating traffic into chokepoints (the bridges, the tunnels). The rivers cut the city into pieces — to leave you have to cross water. The neighborhoods sit in valleys that turn into traps when the highways stop moving. By Day 4 of the outbreak, Pittsburgh's topology is doing real narrative work.
