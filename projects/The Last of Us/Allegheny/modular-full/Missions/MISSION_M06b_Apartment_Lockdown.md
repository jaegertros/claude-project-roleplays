# M-06b: The Apartment Lockdown

**ACT:** Day 3, Monday midday to afternoon
**TRIGGER:** Either (a) no one in the group is bitten yet AND the player chooses to stay home, or (b) the player chooses to barricade rather than go to the hospital despite having a bite victim. The alternate path to M-06.
**ANCHOR LOCATION:** Maya's apartment, Lawrenceville
**PRESENT NPCS:** Maya, Mike (if he made it to the apartment by midday — usually yes), Victor (across the hall, in and out throughout the afternoon), Periodical the cat
**MORTALITY-ELIGIBLE:** no (in default form — but see craft notes if path-b variant)

## PREMISE

The group has decided not to leave the apartment. The world is breaking on the TV; sirens move closer and farther across the afternoon; the radiator stops banging at one o'clock and doesn't start again. They are doing what they can do, which is wait, watch, and try to plan. By evening, the question of *what comes next* is the only question left.

## SPECTACLE

- The apartment, now a defended space — Maya at the kitchen table making a list on a sheet of legal paper, Mike pacing, Victor passing through the hallway with the cat
- The TV running continuously in the living room. Sound off, captions on, until something happens worth listening to
- Outside: sirens, occasional voices in the street, the sound of a helicopter that doesn't seem to be helping anyone
- The cat, calm and unbothered, in the bay window

## THE BEAT

- Triage of the situation. What do they have. What do they need. Maya makes the list.
- A check on Victor. Victor has been trying to reach his daughter Janice in Cleveland all day; he has not gotten through. He is calmer than Mike but not by much.
- The 4 PM news cycle. The CDC's first afternoon update. The phrase "stay indoors" enters federal recommendation. The Pittsburgh mayor's voice on a local broadcast — visibly out of his depth — endorsing it.
- Mike, by this point, has accepted that he is here for the duration. The wedding is canceled, his hotel is half-evacuated, and the apartment is the only place that makes sense. The garment bag with his wedding suit is on the back of the door.
- A small specific scene around dinner — they eat what's in the fridge before it spoils, because the power is starting to flicker. Maya cooks. She moves through the kitchen the same way she did Saturday morning, and that small recognizable rhythm is the most precious thing in the apartment.
- Late afternoon to evening: a neighbor (not Victor, someone from the building they don't know well) pounds on the door at one point looking for help. The player can choose to open the door or not. The narrator runs whatever happens.
- By dusk, the group has settled into a shape: this is where they are. The next question is what they do tomorrow.

## EXIT CONDITIONS

- Evening arrives, the group has consolidated, the next day's plan is forming (stay, leave, find others, gather supplies). The story rolls toward Day 4 — **M-07** (The Highway) becomes the next mission if leaving; the narrator improvises if staying.
- A specific external event forces a decision (the building's power finally fails; FEDRA loudspeaker trucks arrive in the neighborhood; the neighbor situation escalates)

## HIDDEN

- Maya's mother (in Detroit) is still fine on Day 3 afternoon. She and Maya exchange one short text exchange around 2 PM that gets through. Maya keeps the text but does not show `{{user}}` what it says. It says: *honey, can you come home. dad is scared. please come home.*
- Victor's daughter Janice in Cleveland is fine but cannot reach her father because Cleveland's networks are worse than Pittsburgh's. She is on the road trying to drive to him. She will not make it. Victor will learn this Thursday.
- The neighbor who pounds on the door is asking for help with her infant son. She had been at the hospital that morning. The infant has a fever. The narrator does not telegraph this; the player can ask, or can let the neighbor go. There is no right answer.

## CRAFT NOTES

M-06b is the **quieter** path to the same Day 3 evening that M-06 reaches loudly. The emotional center is *holding the apartment together as a working space* while the world breaks outside. The narrator should render the *texture of waiting* — the way Maya makes lists, the way Mike paces, the way Victor checks the windows.

The neighbor's knock is the moment of moral choice. If the player opens the door:
- The narrator runs the encounter at local scope. The mother is exhausted and afraid. The baby has a fever and a bite-mark she did not see at the hospital (because she did not look). The player can offer help, refuse, give what they can spare and send her on, or take the family in (which would be a campaign-defining choice and the narrator should treat it with weight — the failsafe may apply if this becomes a recurring family).
- The infant's bite means the infant will turn within hours. The mother may or may not survive what happens next. *This is mortality-eligible territory* — but for unnamed minors only. The narrator should not make the mother a recurring character.

If the player does not open the door, the narrator does not punish the choice. The knocking stops after a while. The neighbor's fate is not the player's to know. The narrator may render the not-knowing as a weight that lives in the apartment that night.

This mission is *paced for breath*. The player should leave it feeling like they have *done* something — made a plan, eaten a meal, made a decision about the neighbor, settled the group into something that can hold for tomorrow. The work is the point.
