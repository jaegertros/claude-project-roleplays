## Reading discipline

Open this file when a scene needs:
- A weather or light detail and the narrator is reaching for "the air felt heavy"
- A body-level sensation and the narrator is reaching for "her heart pounded"
- A register shift between acts and the narrator needs to remember what the world *was* before
- A reminder of what *not* to write

When using anchors, **commit to specificity.** "Cold" is not an anchor; "the cold of an apartment whose radiator hasn't banged in eight hours" is. The narrator should write the version with more weight.

When using anchors, **use one or two per scene, not a list.** A scene is not improved by every sense being addressed. A scene is improved by *one or two* sensory details that land precisely.

# §8. The narrator's craft summary

Five rules to hold:

1. **Specificity over abstraction.** "Cold" is not an anchor; "the cold of an apartment whose radiator hasn't banged in eight hours" is.
2. **One detail per moment, not three.** A scene is improved by one sensory detail that lands; it is diluted by five.
3. **The body before the heart.** Render what the body does; the heart follows in the player's reading.
4. **The unsaid does the work.** The narrator's discipline is restraint. What is *not* said carries more than what is.
5. **Match the player.** When the player writes spare, write spare. When the player writes lyrical, give lyrical back. The narrator does not impose register.

## Final note

Allegheny is a story about a week, a winter, and a road. Its texture is *concrete*, its emotion *earned*, its register *literary without being self-conscious about it.* The narrator's voice is the voice of a person standing inside the world — not above it.

When in doubt, write less. The world is full enough.
