# §4. The atmosphere of *outside* — the road era

After Pittsburgh. The world is open in a way it has not been. The cold is cleaner. Whatever happens next has not happened yet.

## Light

- *Pittsburgh on the horizon* on the first night out. A silhouette of buildings against a sky that is colder than the buildings. The fires are mostly out by now. The city is *dark in a way it was not before.*
- *Rural light* — the absence of light pollution. Stars visible in numbers `{{user}}` has not seen since childhood, if then. The narrator can use this once. Not three times.
- *Sun on a back road at midday* in November or December — *thin*. The sun is south. The shadows are long even at noon.
- *Snow light at night.* Snow doesn't get fully dark. The light reflects.

## Air and weather

- *Wind across an open field* without buildings to break it. Different from city wind.
- *Cold by Month 4-6* is sustained. The body lives in cold. Warmth is a *resource* — a fire, a thawed water bottle, two bodies under one blanket.
- *Spring rain* in March-April of Year 1 — the first warm rain. The narrator should let this be a moment if it lands in a scene. *Mud everywhere.*
- *Summer of Year 1* — green is back. The world has been at it for a year and is *growing* again in ways the player did not expect. The narrator should not over-render this; it should land *small* — one moment of green where the player notices.

## Sound

- *The road* — no traffic. The sound of the road is *footsteps and breath* and whatever the wind is doing.
- *Birds* return to a presence the player had not noticed they had stopped having. By Month 6, birds are *part of the soundscape* in a way they hadn't been in the QZ.
- *The campfire.* The narrator should commit to specific small fire sounds — the pop of a knot, the way wood settles.
- *Distant gunfire, occasionally.* The road is not safe; the player should hear evidence of that without seeing it. *One round, distant. Two rounds. Silence.*
- *Other people's voices, occasionally, from across a treeline.* The narrator should make these *brief and ambient*; the group does not always investigate.

## Smell

- *Pine forest in winter* — the resin smell that is sharper in cold.
- *Woodsmoke.* The first time the group has a fire is a moment. The woodsmoke smell *signals safety* in a way that is itself a kind of grief, because it didn't used to.
- *Bodies in fields, occasionally.* The narrator should be careful — one or two across the road era, with weather and time accounted for. A body in late March that has been outside since November smells different than one from yesterday.
- *Food from the group's own cooking.* Specific. The narrator should commit to what they eat — canned beans, scavenged crackers, a rabbit Victor caught (if Victor is alive), trout from a creek (if Maya remembered her father's gear from the apartment). The food should become *familiar* the way real cooking does.

## The body on the road

- *Cold becomes the constant.* Every scene is calibrated to whether the player is cold *now*, *too cold*, or *warm.*
- *Walking* — the specific tiredness of foot travel that doesn't have a destination. The narrator can render this through small specifics. *The boots have started to come apart at the seams of one of the boots. The fix is paracord and tape. It will need to be redone every two days.*
- *Sleep on the road* is in places: a barn, an abandoned house, a clearing if the weather permits. The narrator should render *where* the group sleeps differently each time — sleep in a barn is one texture, sleep in an abandoned house is another.
- *Wounds and the cold* — small injuries that wouldn't matter in normal life take longer to heal. A cut on a hand that would have been gone in three days takes ten. The narrator can render this through `{{user}}`'s body without making it a setpiece.

## What the narrator should hold

The road is *what comes after.* The narrator should resist staging the road as constant suffering — that's a different story. *Allegheny's road is a slow life under conditions that are difficult but survivable.* There are bad days; there are also evenings around a fire that are real. The narrator should let both happen.
