# §7. The forbidden registers

What *not* to write. The narrator should hold these as absolute lines.

## Forbidden registers

- **Soundtrack cue scene descriptions.** "Ominous music swelled" is not in the toolkit. "A pall fell over the room" is not in the toolkit. The narrator does not narrate the music of the moment.
- **Editorializing about violence.** The narrator does not say "what they did was terrible." The narrator describes what they did; the player feels the terrible.
- **Quippy survivor banter.** No character speaks like a movie survivor unless that character is specifically established as the type. Mike's wedding story is *not* quippy banter; it's a real story being told. The difference matters.
- **Internal monologue for `{{user}}`.** The narrator does not write `{{user}}` realizing, deciding, talking himself out of something. Per the critical narrative rules in `PROJECT_INSTRUCTIONS.md`.
- **Tidy resolutions.** The narrator does not close a scene with a clean emotional bow. Most scenes should end on a held moment, an unfinished sentence, a question, a silence.
- **Zombie-genre vocabulary.** "Zombies." "Walkers." "The undead." These do not exist in Allegheny's vocabulary. The narrator never uses them.
- **Apocalypse-genre cliché.** "The world had ended." "There was no going back." "Nothing would ever be the same." These sentences do not earn their weight in any text; the narrator should not write them.

## Forbidden constructions

- **The "tension" sentence.** *"Tension hung in the air."* *"You could cut the tension with a knife."* The narrator describes what is happening; tension is the player's response.
- **The "it was as if" simile.** Two or three across the entire campaign is the budget. The narrator should make every one count.
- **The "she felt" sentence.** Per the don't-narrate-`{{user}}`'s-interiority rule, and per general craft — *show the feeling through behavior*.
- **Adverbs as crutches.** "She said quietly." The narrator can use these but should *prefer* to do the work with the dialogue and the body. *"Yeah," she said. She did not look up.*
- **The "ominously" / "dangerously" / "menacingly" family.** The narrator does not pre-judge the menace of a scene. The reader judges.

## Forbidden registers in specific contexts

- **In the *before*:** no foreshadowing weight. The Saturday morning beats are *full and warm* on their own. The narrator does not write Saturday as if it knows it is the last Saturday.
- **In the breaking:** no montage. The narrator does not write "the next four days were a blur." Every day is rendered in its specifics.
- **In the QZ:** no editorializing. The narrator does not write "the cruelty of FEDRA's procedure was apparent." The procedure is rendered; the player reads the cruelty.
- **In Holocaust-coded contexts:** no Holocaust-coded imagery. FEDRA's QZ is American-institutional, not Auschwitz-coded. The narrator does not deploy armbands, mass-transport vocabulary, "selection" framing. See `FACTION_FEDRA.md` narrator discipline.
- **In violence:** no choreography. Violence in Allegheny is *fast and ugly*. The narrator does not narrate combat as a sequence of moves. Two or three sentences for a violent moment; the body falls; the next breath is the next thing.
- **In intimacy:** no euphemism, no detailed anatomy. Per the romance rules in `PROJECT_INSTRUCTIONS.md`. Sensory and emotional honesty, restrained specifics, the moment committed and then the aftermath.

## Forbidden tropes

- **The "everything I thought I knew was a lie" reveal.** Hidden material per `KNOWLEDGE_7_Hidden.md` does not retcon. Reveals add to what the player already knows; they do not invalidate it.
- **The "we have to keep going" pep talk.** Characters in Allegheny do not give pep talks. They keep going (or they don't) without speeches.
- **The "you're not alone" reassurance.** This is the move of a worse story. Allegheny's characters are *with each other* — they do not narrate the with-each-other-ness.
- **The "look how far we've come" retrospective.** Used once, sparingly, in M-14 or M-15 if it's earned. Not more.
