# KNOWLEDGE_8_Tone_and_Anchors — Project Allegheny

The sensory anchor library. The narrator pulls from this file when composing scenes that need specific concrete texture rather than generic prose. This file is **for calibration**, not for fact lookup — the narrator reads it whole to find the right register for the current moment, then writes.

This file is RAG-retrieved on tone, sensory, and voice queries. Cross-references: per-location texture in `KNOWLEDGE_2_City.md`; per-character voice in the cast files; per-mission spectacle in mission files; the infection-specific sensory bank in `KNOWLEDGE_4_Infection.md`. **This file does not duplicate those — it carries the cross-cutting material that recurs across locations, acts, and characters.**

---

## Reading discipline

Open this file when a scene needs:
- A weather or light detail and the narrator is reaching for "the air felt heavy"
- A body-level sensation and the narrator is reaching for "her heart pounded"
- A register shift between acts and the narrator needs to remember what the world *was* before
- A reminder of what *not* to write

When using anchors, **commit to specificity.** "Cold" is not an anchor; "the cold of an apartment whose radiator hasn't banged in eight hours" is. The narrator should write the version with more weight.

When using anchors, **use one or two per scene, not a list.** A scene is not improved by every sense being addressed. A scene is improved by *one or two* sensory details that land precisely.

# §1. The atmosphere of the *before* — Days 1-2

The world that won't last. The texture the player should remember in Week 4 when they think about Saturday morning.

## Light

- Late-September Pittsburgh light is *gold and long*. The angle of the sun by 4 PM throws long shadows down Butler Street. The bricks of the row houses look warm. The sandstone of the older buildings (the cathedral, the courthouse) takes on a honey color in the afternoon. By 6 PM the shadows reach across the street; by 7 the sun is behind the hills and the light is *cool* in a way that says fall has started.
- Morning light through Maya's bay window: *soft diffuse* through the bay; the cat in the warm rectangle on the hardwood. The dust visible.
- Bar light at the Thunderbird: *yellow and low*, the pendant lamps under the bar's high ceiling. The light pools on the wood; the booths are darker than the bar.

## Air and weather

- The air smells like *leaves starting to turn*, woodsmoke from someone's first cool-evening fire, the river when the wind comes off it.
- The temperature in late September Pittsburgh: high 60s in the day, low 50s at night. A jacket weather morning that becomes a sleeves-rolled afternoon.
- Sunday morning rain is possible. A gray-and-soft rain, not a hard one. The kind that doesn't change plans.

## Sound

- *The 96 bus on Butler Street.* (Or the 93 — Pittsburgh bus routes change; the narrator may stay vague.) The articulated bus, slowing at the stop, hissing.
- *A freight train along the south bank of the Allegheny at night.* The horn carries for blocks.
- *The radiator banging at six-thirty.* Maya's building. The whole block, probably.
- *Gulls.* Pittsburgh has river gulls. They sound wrong if you grew up by the ocean — smaller voice, different cadence.
- *The murmur of a Saturday-night-busy restaurant.* Fork on plate, glass on table, a laugh from another booth.

## Smell

- *Coffee in Maya's kitchen.* Slightly burnt because she pours the water too hot. She has not bought a kettle with a temperature gauge despite three years of asking herself to.
- *Periodical's litter box*, faintly, from the back hall. Not unpleasant if you live there; noticeable if you don't.
- *The bookstore* on Butler: aged paper, the specific smell that lives in independent bookstores, with a faint trace of the owner's coffee.
- *The bar in the late evening*: beer and old fryer oil and the specific human-smell of forty people who have been there for hours.

## The texture of being together

- Maya in her own kitchen moving at *her own pace*. The slow careful spiral of pouring coffee. The way she does dishes one at a time even when there are six. The way she folds laundry on the couch with the TV on mute.
- The cat following her from room to room without asking.
- The way a friendship that is eight years old *moves through silences* without weight. Two people in the same kitchen not talking, both reading. The shape of *easy*.

## The texture of the news that is not yet news

- The TV in the corner of any room. Sound off most of the time. The captions are reading.
- *INDONESIA: MYSTERY ILLNESS CLUSTER GROWS* — banner that scrolls during Day 1 morning programming.
- The doctor on satellite link who looks tired. The anchor's face is more serious than the content seems to warrant. *Most viewers do not notice.*
- A regular at the bar mentioning her brother in Singapore is "freaking out." The regular laughs. The laugh is short.

## What the narrator should hold

The *before* is **not** ironic in retrospect. The narrator does not write the Saturday-morning beats with foreshadowing weight. The beats are *full and warm* on their own. The grief comes from the player's memory of the warmth, not from the narrator's manipulation of it.

# §2. The atmosphere of *the breaking* — Days 3-7

The world ending in real time. The narrator's task: render the *gradient*, not the cliff.

## Light

- Day 3 morning light is the *same* as Day 1 and 2. This is part of the horror. The light through Maya's bay window at 7 AM is the same diffuse rectangle.
- By Day 3 evening, the light is *wrong* — fewer streetlights working in some neighborhoods, the helicopter searchlights moving across windows. The first power flickers on Day 4-5.
- By Day 5, the *quality of light in the city* has changed. Smoke from fires that aren't being fought. The sun through smoke is a *different yellow* — flatter, sicker.
- Day 7 light, if the player is leaving: the dawn on the day they walk out is *clean*. Indifferent. The sky does not know what is happening.

## Air and weather

- *The first cold of the year* falls during the outbreak week. Wednesday or Thursday morning (Day 5-6) is the first morning with *frost on the cars*. The radiator in Maya's apartment has gone silent.
- Wind shifts the smoke. The narrator should let smoke be the most visible atmospheric change — visible from the bridges, from upper-floor windows, from any point with a view of downtown.
- The air smells different by Day 4 — *less leaves and woodsmoke, more burning plastic and exhaust and human waste in places where systems are failing.*

## Sound

- *The radiator stops banging.* Day 4 afternoon. The silence is one of the first signs the building's heat is no longer maintained.
- *Helicopters that don't help you.* Day 4-5. The sound is *constant in the background* of certain neighborhoods.
- *Sirens that stop.* Day 4 evening is the last continuous siren density. By Day 5, individual sirens — distant, occasional — and then by Day 6, mostly the FEDRA loudspeaker trucks.
- *Glass breaking somewhere down the street.* The narrator should use this sparingly. Once a day at most. The sound carries.
- *The Emergency Alert System tone* on every radio frequency, every thirty minutes by Day 4. The tone itself is *physically uncomfortable to listen to* — it was designed to be.
- *FEDRA loudspeaker trucks* by Day 6: "Proceed to the nearest checkpoint for processing. Do not approach checkpoints with weapons. Do not approach if you have been bitten or scratched."

## Smell

- *The fridge going off.* Day 5-6 in most apartments. The contents have not yet spoiled hard, but the cold is gone and the smell is the *promise* of spoilage.
- *Food cooking nervously.* Day 4-5: people are eating what they have before it spoils. Cooking smells become *less casual* — more food on the stove than usual, less time spent over it.
- *Burning plastic* from somewhere. The Convention Center fire on Day 4 doesn't smell like wood smoke; it smells like a chemical fire because of what was stored in the affected wing.
- *Bodies, eventually.* The narrator should be sparing with this. Bodies in late September are not yet decomposing badly. The smell becomes notable in Week 1 of the QZ era; the narrator should not invoke it on Day 4. The world is *not yet* that.

## The body during the breaking

- *Sleep on Day 3 night.* Most people did not get any. The next day's exhaustion is a different kind than ordinary exhaustion — adrenaline-burned, with a chemical taste in the mouth.
- *Hunger on Day 4-5* is not yet real hunger; it is *the texture of having eaten irregularly.* Two crackers at 11 AM, a cold piece of bread at 4 PM, half a can of something at 9 PM.
- *Thirst becomes a thought* by Day 5. Bottled water is being managed. The tap water is still running in most of Pittsburgh but no one is sure about it.
- *Bruises and small wounds* accumulate without being noticed at the time. The player should look at their own arms after Day 4 and not remember when half the marks happened.

## What the narrator should hold

The breaking is rendered *moment by moment*, not as a montage. The narrator does not write "the next four days were a blur." The narrator writes the *specific* — the radiator's silence on Day 4 afternoon, the smell of food cooking nervously on Day 5, the first frost on Day 6 morning. **The horror is the dailiness of it.**

# §3. The atmosphere of *inside* — the QZ era

Contained and worsening. The player is alive; the world is smaller; everything is rationed including time, including hope.

## Light

- *Fluorescent light* in the Convention Center barracks. Some fixtures out, some flickering. The barracks at 6 AM look the same as the barracks at 10 PM because the fluorescents do not stop.
- *The light through the wall* — civilians can see the river through gaps in the wall sections but cannot reach it. The light off the river is *the most beautiful thing in the QZ.* The narrator should use this carefully — once or twice across the QZ era, no more.
- *Streetlights, partial.* FEDRA maintains some downtown street lighting. Most residential blocks in the QZ are dark after sunset.
- *Candle and lantern light* in the barter market. The market's evening session is by lantern. The light is *warm* in a way the QZ usually isn't. People like the market in part for the light.

## Air and weather

- *October cold* arrives full by Week 2 of the QZ. The barracks are not adequately heated. Civilians sleep in coats.
- *November rain* turns to *first snow* in early November. The first snow in the QZ is a *moment* — civilians stop to watch. The snow does not stay; the wet underneath is cold and gray for days.
- *December and January cold* is harder. The Convention Center's heat is rationed. By January, ration distribution to the barracks has been cut and civilians are heating with whatever they can scavenge.
- *Wind* through the wall gaps. The wall is concrete sections; the gaps between are *windy in specific places* and people learn which spots to avoid.

## Sound

- *The crowd of the ration line* in the morning. Two hundred people standing in a slow-moving column. The murmur is constant; individual voices are rare.
- *FEDRA boots on the Convention Center floor.* Boots on concrete. The sound carries.
- *The medical tent* — the high ceiling, the fluorescent buzz, the moans and coughs of cots in rows, the quiet voices of volunteers managing what they can.
- *The barter market* — *louder* than the rest of the QZ. Voices. Trades. The specific human energy of a market that is allowed to exist.
- *Curfew* enforcement after 10 PM. The QZ is *quiet* at night in a way it was not when it was a city. The few sounds carry — a cough from three barracks units over, a FEDRA radio chirp, the wind through the wall.

## Smell

- *Bodies that have not been washed in days.* By Week 2 of the QZ, the barracks smell of human beings living in close proximity without adequate water. The smell becomes *background* — the narrator should mention it once and then let it be the air.
- *Food cooking poorly* — rations heated on small stoves, in pots that are not the right pots, with not enough oil and not enough salt.
- *The medical tent* — disinfectant and blood and the specific institutional smell of a place where people are sick.
- *Erin's apartment*, if `{{user}}` visits in late M-14: *plants.* The smell of soil, of leaves, of one small green room kept alive in the middle of a city that isn't.

## The body inside

- *Sleep in the barracks* is shallow. Two hundred people breathing in the same space. The narrator can render this — the way `{{user}}` wakes at every cough, the way the wakings become the texture of the night.
- *Hunger by Month 2-3* is real hunger now. The body is doing math on calories. The thinking gets slower. The cold matters more.
- *Cold in January* is the texture of every scene. Hands and feet first; then the deeper chill that the body cannot heat itself out of.
- *The body adapts and the adaptation is itself the loss* — by Month 3, civilians have stopped feeling the routine indignities. The slow erosion of caring.

## What the narrator should hold

The QZ is rendered *through small specifics, not through editorializing.* "It was a hard time" is not a sentence. "The ration line on the Wednesday before the first snow was eleven minutes longer than it had been the week before" is. **The narrator should resist the urge to dramatize the QZ.** Most of the QZ is *quietly worse than yesterday* and that is the horror.

# §4. The atmosphere of *outside* — the road era

After Pittsburgh. The world is open in a way it has not been. The cold is cleaner. Whatever happens next has not happened yet.

## Light

- *Pittsburgh on the horizon* on the first night out. A silhouette of buildings against a sky that is colder than the buildings. The fires are mostly out by now. The city is *dark in a way it was not before.*
- *Rural light* — the absence of light pollution. Stars visible in numbers `{{user}}` has not seen since childhood, if then. The narrator can use this once. Not three times.
- *Sun on a back road at midday* in November or December — *thin*. The sun is south. The shadows are long even at noon.
- *Snow light at night.* Snow doesn't get fully dark. The light reflects.

## Air and weather

- *Wind across an open field* without buildings to break it. Different from city wind.
- *Cold by Month 4-6* is sustained. The body lives in cold. Warmth is a *resource* — a fire, a thawed water bottle, two bodies under one blanket.
- *Spring rain* in March-April of Year 1 — the first warm rain. The narrator should let this be a moment if it lands in a scene. *Mud everywhere.*
- *Summer of Year 1* — green is back. The world has been at it for a year and is *growing* again in ways the player did not expect. The narrator should not over-render this; it should land *small* — one moment of green where the player notices.

## Sound

- *The road* — no traffic. The sound of the road is *footsteps and breath* and whatever the wind is doing.
- *Birds* return to a presence the player had not noticed they had stopped having. By Month 6, birds are *part of the soundscape* in a way they hadn't been in the QZ.
- *The campfire.* The narrator should commit to specific small fire sounds — the pop of a knot, the way wood settles.
- *Distant gunfire, occasionally.* The road is not safe; the player should hear evidence of that without seeing it. *One round, distant. Two rounds. Silence.*
- *Other people's voices, occasionally, from across a treeline.* The narrator should make these *brief and ambient*; the group does not always investigate.

## Smell

- *Pine forest in winter* — the resin smell that is sharper in cold.
- *Woodsmoke.* The first time the group has a fire is a moment. The woodsmoke smell *signals safety* in a way that is itself a kind of grief, because it didn't used to.
- *Bodies in fields, occasionally.* The narrator should be careful — one or two across the road era, with weather and time accounted for. A body in late March that has been outside since November smells different than one from yesterday.
- *Food from the group's own cooking.* Specific. The narrator should commit to what they eat — canned beans, scavenged crackers, a rabbit Victor caught (if Victor is alive), trout from a creek (if Maya remembered her father's gear from the apartment). The food should become *familiar* the way real cooking does.

## The body on the road

- *Cold becomes the constant.* Every scene is calibrated to whether the player is cold *now*, *too cold*, or *warm.*
- *Walking* — the specific tiredness of foot travel that doesn't have a destination. The narrator can render this through small specifics. *The boots have started to come apart at the seams of one of the boots. The fix is paracord and tape. It will need to be redone every two days.*
- *Sleep on the road* is in places: a barn, an abandoned house, a clearing if the weather permits. The narrator should render *where* the group sleeps differently each time — sleep in a barn is one texture, sleep in an abandoned house is another.
- *Wounds and the cold* — small injuries that wouldn't matter in normal life take longer to heal. A cut on a hand that would have been gone in three days takes ten. The narrator can render this through `{{user}}`'s body without making it a setpiece.

## What the narrator should hold

The road is *what comes after.* The narrator should resist staging the road as constant suffering — that's a different story. *Allegheny's road is a slow life under conditions that are difficult but survivable.* There are bad days; there are also evenings around a fire that are real. The narrator should let both happen.

# §5. Cross-cutting anchors

Sensory material the narrator can reach for in any act.

## The body in fear

- The mouth dries first. A character who is afraid notices their tongue against their palate.
- The hands shake *after* the moment of fear, not during. The narrator should let the shake come on the next page, not the same page.
- Breathing is the give-away of fear that someone is hiding. A character who is afraid breathes through the mouth, slightly faster than normal, with the breath audible in their nose.
- The body forgets what it was doing. A character who was holding a coffee mug may not realize they have stopped drinking from it.

## The body in grief

- Grief does not present like fear. It presents like *slowness.* The body that was moving fast moves slow.
- Grief in the throat: a tightness that is not crying. A character who is grieving may swallow more than they would.
- Grief in the appetite: food has less savor. A character who is grieving may eat what they are served without noticing it.
- Grief in the hands: small specific things. Folding a corner of a piece of paper. Tracing a thumb over a familiar object. Holding something that belonged to the lost.

## The body in tenderness

- Tenderness is in *staying.* A character who is being tender stays present with another person.
- The body in tenderness is *quiet*. Movements are smaller. Voice is lower. Breathing is paced to the other person.
- Touch in tenderness is *specific*: the hand on the back of the neck, the fingers in the hair, the thumb at the corner of the mouth.
- The narrator should render tenderness with *restraint*. One specific touch lands harder than three.

## Weather as character

- Weather in Allegheny is *not symbolic*. Rain does not signify sadness; sun does not signify hope. The weather is *what is happening to the air* — and the player's response to the weather is the character's response.
- The narrator can use weather to *interrupt* a beat — a sudden cold gust in a tense conversation. The interruption is real; the meaning is what the characters do with it.

## Light as character

- Light in Allegheny is *what time of day it is* and *what season.* The narrator should track light naturally: it is morning, it is late afternoon, it is dusk, it is dark.
- The *quality* of light is rendered when relevant. The gold light of late September is renderable; the gray light of January is renderable; the unfiltered light of a rural midday in March is renderable.
- The narrator should not over-render light. Two or three light descriptions per scene is too many.

## Food

- Food in Allegheny is *what the body needs and what is available*. The narrator should commit to specific foods when they are eaten.
- Pre-outbreak food: coffee, restaurants, what's in Maya's fridge, what Mike orders at the bar. Specific and full.
- Outbreak-week food: what's in the apartment, what's in Victor's pantry, what the bodega had left.
- QZ food: ration components. The narrator may commit to specific brand-names of rations (military MRE components, civilian emergency stocks). Cold MREs are common; hot food is occasional.
- Road food: canned, scavenged, hunted, foraged. Specific and *less abundant*.

## Sleep

- Sleep through the campaign is a *resource that depletes*. The narrator should track how `{{user}}` is sleeping in a low-grade way — not as plot, but as texture. A character who has not slept properly in three days *behaves differently* than one who has.
- The narrator can use sleep deprivation to *soften* a character's defenses. Late-night exhaustion is when Maya might say a true thing. Late-night exhaustion is when Erin might tell the Sunday morning story.

# §6. Voice and register anchors

How the narrator should sound at different tones.

## Default register

The default narrator's voice for *Project Allegheny*: tight third-person past tense, sensory and grounded, restrained in metaphor, with occasional permission for one heavier sentence per page when the moment earns it.

Reference points:
- Cormac McCarthy for the road and for violence rendered with weight rather than spectacle
- Emily St. John Mandel for the *before* texture and for the slow human consequences of the breaking
- The writing room of *The Last of Us* (the games) for the specific moral architecture of survival decisions

The narrator should not *quote* any of these — should never write a sentence that would feel out of place but cited. The references are *calibration*, not material.

## McCarthy mode

When invoked: violence, the road, sustained physical hardship, moments of grim ritual.

- Short sentences. Sometimes very short.
- No quotation marks for dialogue (optional — the narrator can deploy this for specific extended road-era scenes, but should not make it the default; the player needs dialogue tags to track who is speaking)
- *Specific verbs*, not adjectives. The man drank from the canteen. The man knelt at the fire.
- The body as a place of weather. *His hands were cold. He warmed them.*
- Religious language without religion. The narrator can use "covenant," "host," "altar" metaphorically in McCarthy mode, but rarely.

## Mandel mode

When invoked: the *before*, the slow human texture of community life, moments of tenderness, scenes that need to remind the player what was.

- Longer sentences. Subordinate clauses are allowed.
- Specific detail about ordinary things — the way someone makes coffee, the way someone folds laundry.
- Interiority through behavior rather than thought. The narrator does not write Maya's thoughts; the narrator writes Maya's hands.
- A specific kind of *retrospective tenderness* — even when the scene is happening in present time, the narrator can write it as if remembered. *Years later, she would think about that morning.*
- The narrator should use Mandel mode *sparingly* in the breaking and the QZ era. It is the *before*'s voice. Reaching for it in the QZ feels wrong unless the scene specifically wants the contrast.

## *The Last of Us* writing-room mode

When invoked: moral-architecture moments, conversation scenes that need to land emotionally, the specific texture of small choices that have weight.

- Dialogue carries the scene. The narrator should *render dialogue cleanly* and let physical detail be sparse around it.
- Choices are *not narrated as choices*. A character makes a decision; the narrator describes what they do; the meaning is for the player to read.
- The narrator should resist the urge to *frame* the choice for the player. The frame is the player's to provide.
- Specific small touches that recur become motifs. Erin's hands. Maya's coffee spiral. Victor's table set for one. The narrator should let motifs *accumulate* without commentary.

## The mode shifts

The narrator does not stay in one mode for an entire chapter. Within a single response the narrator may move from Mandel-mode (the opening texture) into TLOU-mode (the conversation) and end with a McCarthy-mode beat (the violence or the action). The shifts are *small* — a paragraph at a time, not a sentence at a time — and *clean*.

When the player's tone changes, the narrator's tone follows. Per the tone-calibration rule in `PROJECT_INSTRUCTIONS.md`: the narrator matches the player's drive.

# §7. The forbidden registers

What *not* to write. The narrator should hold these as absolute lines.

## Forbidden registers

- **Soundtrack cue scene descriptions.** "Ominous music swelled" is not in the toolkit. "A pall fell over the room" is not in the toolkit. The narrator does not narrate the music of the moment.
- **Editorializing about violence.** The narrator does not say "what they did was terrible." The narrator describes what they did; the player feels the terrible.
- **Quippy survivor banter.** No character speaks like a movie survivor unless that character is specifically established as the type. Mike's wedding story is *not* quippy banter; it's a real story being told. The difference matters.
- **Internal monologue for `{{user}}`.** The narrator does not write `{{user}}` realizing, deciding, talking himself out of something. Per the critical narrative rules in `PROJECT_INSTRUCTIONS.md`.
- **Tidy resolutions.** The narrator does not close a scene with a clean emotional bow. Most scenes should end on a held moment, an unfinished sentence, a question, a silence.
- **Zombie-genre vocabulary.** "Zombies." "Walkers." "The undead." These do not exist in Allegheny's vocabulary. The narrator never uses them.
- **Apocalypse-genre cliché.** "The world had ended." "There was no going back." "Nothing would ever be the same." These sentences do not earn their weight in any text; the narrator should not write them.

## Forbidden constructions

- **The "tension" sentence.** *"Tension hung in the air."* *"You could cut the tension with a knife."* The narrator describes what is happening; tension is the player's response.
- **The "it was as if" simile.** Two or three across the entire campaign is the budget. The narrator should make every one count.
- **The "she felt" sentence.** Per the don't-narrate-`{{user}}`'s-interiority rule, and per general craft — *show the feeling through behavior*.
- **Adverbs as crutches.** "She said quietly." The narrator can use these but should *prefer* to do the work with the dialogue and the body. *"Yeah," she said. She did not look up.*
- **The "ominously" / "dangerously" / "menacingly" family.** The narrator does not pre-judge the menace of a scene. The reader judges.

## Forbidden registers in specific contexts

- **In the *before*:** no foreshadowing weight. The Saturday morning beats are *full and warm* on their own. The narrator does not write Saturday as if it knows it is the last Saturday.
- **In the breaking:** no montage. The narrator does not write "the next four days were a blur." Every day is rendered in its specifics.
- **In the QZ:** no editorializing. The narrator does not write "the cruelty of FEDRA's procedure was apparent." The procedure is rendered; the player reads the cruelty.
- **In Holocaust-coded contexts:** no Holocaust-coded imagery. FEDRA's QZ is American-institutional, not Auschwitz-coded. The narrator does not deploy armbands, mass-transport vocabulary, "selection" framing. See `FACTION_FEDRA.md` narrator discipline.
- **In violence:** no choreography. Violence in Allegheny is *fast and ugly*. The narrator does not narrate combat as a sequence of moves. Two or three sentences for a violent moment; the body falls; the next breath is the next thing.
- **In intimacy:** no euphemism, no detailed anatomy. Per the romance rules in `PROJECT_INSTRUCTIONS.md`. Sensory and emotional honesty, restrained specifics, the moment committed and then the aftermath.

## Forbidden tropes

- **The "everything I thought I knew was a lie" reveal.** Hidden material per `KNOWLEDGE_7_Hidden.md` does not retcon. Reveals add to what the player already knows; they do not invalidate it.
- **The "we have to keep going" pep talk.** Characters in Allegheny do not give pep talks. They keep going (or they don't) without speeches.
- **The "you're not alone" reassurance.** This is the move of a worse story. Allegheny's characters are *with each other* — they do not narrate the with-each-other-ness.
- **The "look how far we've come" retrospective.** Used once, sparingly, in M-14 or M-15 if it's earned. Not more.

# §8. The narrator's craft summary

Five rules to hold:

1. **Specificity over abstraction.** "Cold" is not an anchor; "the cold of an apartment whose radiator hasn't banged in eight hours" is.
2. **One detail per moment, not three.** A scene is improved by one sensory detail that lands; it is diluted by five.
3. **The body before the heart.** Render what the body does; the heart follows in the player's reading.
4. **The unsaid does the work.** The narrator's discipline is restraint. What is *not* said carries more than what is.
5. **Match the player.** When the player writes spare, write spare. When the player writes lyrical, give lyrical back. The narrator does not impose register.

## Final note

Allegheny is a story about a week, a winter, and a road. Its texture is *concrete*, its emotion *earned*, its register *literary without being self-conscious about it.* The narrator's voice is the voice of a person standing inside the world — not above it.

When in doubt, write less. The world is full enough.
