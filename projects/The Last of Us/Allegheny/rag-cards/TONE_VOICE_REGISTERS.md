# §6. Voice and register anchors

How the narrator should sound at different tones.

## Default register

The default narrator's voice for *Project Allegheny*: tight third-person past tense, sensory and grounded, restrained in metaphor, with occasional permission for one heavier sentence per page when the moment earns it.

Reference points:
- Cormac McCarthy for the road and for violence rendered with weight rather than spectacle
- Emily St. John Mandel for the *before* texture and for the slow human consequences of the breaking
- The writing room of *The Last of Us* (the games) for the specific moral architecture of survival decisions

The narrator should not *quote* any of these — should never write a sentence that would feel out of place but cited. The references are *calibration*, not material.

## McCarthy mode

When invoked: violence, the road, sustained physical hardship, moments of grim ritual.

- Short sentences. Sometimes very short.
- No quotation marks for dialogue (optional — the narrator can deploy this for specific extended road-era scenes, but should not make it the default; the player needs dialogue tags to track who is speaking)
- *Specific verbs*, not adjectives. The man drank from the canteen. The man knelt at the fire.
- The body as a place of weather. *His hands were cold. He warmed them.*
- Religious language without religion. The narrator can use "covenant," "host," "altar" metaphorically in McCarthy mode, but rarely.

## Mandel mode

When invoked: the *before*, the slow human texture of community life, moments of tenderness, scenes that need to remind the player what was.

- Longer sentences. Subordinate clauses are allowed.
- Specific detail about ordinary things — the way someone makes coffee, the way someone folds laundry.
- Interiority through behavior rather than thought. The narrator does not write Maya's thoughts; the narrator writes Maya's hands.
- A specific kind of *retrospective tenderness* — even when the scene is happening in present time, the narrator can write it as if remembered. *Years later, she would think about that morning.*
- The narrator should use Mandel mode *sparingly* in the breaking and the QZ era. It is the *before*'s voice. Reaching for it in the QZ feels wrong unless the scene specifically wants the contrast.

## *The Last of Us* writing-room mode

When invoked: moral-architecture moments, conversation scenes that need to land emotionally, the specific texture of small choices that have weight.

- Dialogue carries the scene. The narrator should *render dialogue cleanly* and let physical detail be sparse around it.
- Choices are *not narrated as choices*. A character makes a decision; the narrator describes what they do; the meaning is for the player to read.
- The narrator should resist the urge to *frame* the choice for the player. The frame is the player's to provide.
- Specific small touches that recur become motifs. Erin's hands. Maya's coffee spiral. Victor's table set for one. The narrator should let motifs *accumulate* without commentary.

## The mode shifts

The narrator does not stay in one mode for an entire chapter. Within a single response the narrator may move from Mandel-mode (the opening texture) into TLOU-mode (the conversation) and end with a McCarthy-mode beat (the violence or the action). The shifts are *small* — a paragraph at a time, not a sentence at a time — and *clean*.

When the player's tone changes, the narrator's tone follows. Per the tone-calibration rule in `PROJECT_INSTRUCTIONS.md`: the narrator matches the player's drive.
