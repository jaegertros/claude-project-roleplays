# FACTION_HUNTERS — the Hunters (Pittsburgh)

**faction_id:** HUNTERS · **active in Allegheny:** Month 4+ (post-FEDRA collapse)

Cross-references: `CAST_LEN.md` (the lieutenant the player may encounter); `KNOWLEDGE_2_City.md` for downtown Pittsburgh post-collapse; `KNOWLEDGE_3_Timeline.md` for the collapse timeline; `MISSION_M15_The_Road.md` for the moment the player most likely leaves before the Hunters become a sustained threat.

---

## Identity

**The Hunters.** Not a name they call themselves — a name FEDRA used and that other survivors picked up. The Pittsburgh Hunters are a coalition of survivor groups that consolidated control of downtown Pittsburgh after FEDRA's retreat in Month 4. They are not a unified organization in the institutional sense; they are *survivor groups whose methods have converged.* Len is one lieutenant in one group; there are others.

The name has a specific connotation in TLOU-adjacent vocabulary: survivors who have decided which people are food. *That includes literal cannibalism in some Hunter groups, including some Pittsburgh Hunter cells.* The narrator should handle this with care — see Narrator Discipline below.

## Origin

The Pittsburgh Hunters did not form on a date. They consolidated.

Before the QZ fell:
- The neighborhoods outside the QZ wall (Lawrenceville, Bloomfield, the North Hills, McKees Rocks, the South Hills) were occupied by survivor groups of varying sizes and shapes
- Most were small — extended families, neighborhood mutual-aid groups, scavenger crews of three to eight
- A few were larger — opportunistic groups that had grown by absorbing other groups, by force or by offer
- These groups operated in *uneasy coexistence* with each other and with FEDRA's perimeter

When FEDRA pulled out in Month 4, the QZ's interior became contested territory. The larger and more organized survivor groups moved in faster. Within four weeks of FEDRA's retreat, the downtown was effectively held by a coalition of three to five Hunter groups operating in coordination without unified command. Smaller groups either joined, fled, or were absorbed.

Len's group is one of the consolidating groups — not the largest, but one of the more disciplined. He is a lieutenant, not the leader of his group. The leader's name is *Reeve* (not yet committed in this file; the narrator may name otherwise if the story takes the group in a different direction).

**The Hunters in Allegheny's first year are not yet stable.** The coalition that controls downtown by Month 5 is a loose alliance held together by mutual benefit. By Year 2 it may fracture into competing groups; by Year 3 it may consolidate into a single hierarchy. Within Allegheny's default scope, the player encounters them in the early phase.

## Stated purpose

**The Hunters do not have stated purpose in the institutional sense.** They are not a movement, not a polity, not an ideology. They are groups that have organized themselves to *survive* and have made specific choices about how that survival works.

What they say to each other, what they say to outsiders, varies by group. Len's group's internal rhetoric, when articulated:

- *"We hold what we hold."* (Territory; resources; people)
- *"Everyone is bringing something to the table or they're on it."* (The literal cannibalism euphemism — see HIDDEN)
- *"You don't have to like it. You just have to be useful."*

Most Hunters, most days, do not philosophize. They run their territory.

## Actual operating reality

Day-to-day, in Pittsburgh, Month 5 onward:

- **Patrols of downtown and the inner buffer.** Hunters control the area roughly bounded by the rivers and extending two to four blocks east of the former QZ wall.
- **Scavenge operations.** Groups go out into the abandoned neighborhoods for supplies. They are organized; they bring back what they find; they distribute on a system that rewards seniority and contribution.
- **Encounters with travelers.** Anyone passing through Hunter-controlled territory is *processed* — at minimum, searched and taxed; at worst, killed; in some cases, captured and held. The Hunter groups have different procedures for travelers. Len's group is among the *more procedural* — Len negotiates first; he kills if negotiation fails. Some other groups skip the negotiation.
- **Internal discipline.** Hunter groups have hierarchy. Disobedience is punished. Theft within the group is punished severely.
- **Cannibalism, where it exists.** Not all Hunter groups practice it. Some do, including parts of the Pittsburgh coalition. The Hunters who practice it do not advertise it; new captives are not told until it is too late. **See HIDDEN.**

## Hierarchy

**Cell-based, with internal rank within each cell:**

- **Group leaders** (Pittsburgh has 3-5 by Month 5): the senior figure in each Hunter group. Reeve runs Len's group. Names of others are unspecified and the narrator may invent as needed.
- **Lieutenants** (Len's tier): the operational deputies. Run patrols, manage scavenge teams, negotiate with travelers, handle internal discipline. Len is the most visible Hunter the player may encounter.
- **Senior members:** the longer-tenure members of each group. Trusted with weapons, with territory access, with capture and processing duties.
- **Junior members and absorbed:** newer additions, including civilians who joined to survive. Their position is conditional; they are watched.
- **Captives:** outside the hierarchy. Held for labor, for trade, or — in some groups — for food.

The hierarchy is *not formal*. There are no titles. Rank is *known* but not displayed. A new traveler encountering Hunters will not be told who runs what until they have been processed.

## Visual identifiers

**Uniforms:** none. Hunters wear whatever they have. The accumulation is the tell — gear that is *scavenged from many sources* and modified for purpose. A Hunter's jacket is whatever fit; their boots are whatever survived; their weapons are whatever they could get and keep working.

By Month 6, some Hunter groups are starting to develop *visual identifiers* within their group — a colored cloth tied to a specific weapon strap, a chalk mark on a vehicle, a piece of jewelry that gets passed down through rank. These are subtle and group-specific. Len's group is recognizable by *small dark green cloth markers* on their pack straps — not a uniform, just a tell.

**Equipment:**
- Mostly firearms (pistols, rifles, occasional military-grade weapons scavenged from FEDRA's retreat)
- Improvised armor in some cases
- Vehicles when fuel is available (rare by Month 6; common again by Year 1 as scavenge stabilizes)
- Radios — older civilian, frequency-hopping, encryption limited

**Vocabulary tells:**
- "We hold ___" / "they hold ___" (territory framing is constant)
- "Useful" — used to mean *valuable to the group*; the inverse, "not useful," means *expendable*
- "On the table" / "at the table" — Len's group's specific cannibalism euphemism; *not universal among Pittsburgh Hunters*
- "New" — used as a noun, meaning *new captive or new arrival*. "Got a new in the truck."
- They do *not* use military vocabulary — no "copy," no "procedure." They use working-class informal speech, often regional.

## Methods

How the Pittsburgh Hunters operate, scene to scene:

- **Setting up encounters:** Hunters identify travelers before being seen. They watch from windows, from rooftops, from the inside of buildings the travelers are passing. By the time the encounter is initiated, the Hunters have positioning advantage.
- **The negotiation phase:** Len's group, distinctively, *talks first*. The opening is friendly. The Hunter is calm. The traveler is invited to set down their weapons, sit, share food. The friendliness is real *and tactical* — it produces compliance more reliably than threats do.
- **The pivot:** when the Hunter judges the traveler's value, the encounter pivots. If the traveler has resources but no leverage, the resources are taken. If the traveler has skills the group can use, they may be offered a position. If they have neither, they are removed.
- **Sustained operations:** Hunter groups do not stage running gunfights with travelers in the open. They prefer ambush. They prefer surrender. They prefer to control the moment of violence so the violence is *cheap*.
- **Internal violence:** within the group, discipline is enforced by violence. The narrator should not over-render this — it is in the background, not in the foreground.
- **Cannibalism, where present:** captives are held and worked until they are not useful. Then they are not. The transition is sometimes obscured from the rest of the group, even within the cells that practice it. *Not all Hunter groups do this; Len's does, and Len is personally complicit in it.*

## Internal tensions

The Pittsburgh Hunter coalition is unstable:

- **Groups that practice cannibalism vs. groups that do not.** This is the largest fault line. The non-cannibal groups in the coalition tolerate the practice as long as it is not directed at coalition members. The cannibal groups depend on the tolerance. Some of the non-cannibal members of cannibal groups are *quietly horrified* and have not yet figured out what they will do about it.
- **Generational tensions.** Older Hunters (40+) remember pre-outbreak life and carry it in different shapes than younger Hunters (20-30) who have spent their formative outbreak time inside Hunter logic. The values do not align.
- **Resource pressure.** As the coalition consolidates its territory, the scavenge yields diminish. By Year 1+, the coalition's stability will be tested by what happens when there is less to share.
- **Outside pressure.** Other survivor groups in the broader region. The Fireflies (still present in Pittsburgh in small numbers Month 5 onward). Travelers passing through who carry information about other places. The coalition is *inward-facing* by necessity and may not have a strong sense of the world beyond Pittsburgh.

## Geography in Pittsburgh

The Pittsburgh Hunters' footprint:

- **Headquarters:** the Convention Center, taken over from FEDRA. Different cells use different sections.
- **Patrol areas:** downtown Golden Triangle, the inner perimeter of the former QZ wall, the bridges (still partially controlled), Mount Washington (one Hunter group has a presence here)
- **Scavenge territory:** Lawrenceville, Bloomfield, the Strip District, parts of Oakland — all of these are still scavenged actively by Month 6
- **Contested areas:** the eastern suburbs (Hunter influence weakens past Murrysville), the rivers (controlled passage)
- **Out-of-bounds for them:** the rural exit routes and the road east, west, north, and south. Once the player is two hours' walk past city limits in any direction, they are out of Hunter range.

- **Reeve** — Len's group leader. Not yet committed in detail; the narrator may flesh out if Reeve becomes scene-active. Default sketch: late 40s, ex-Pittsburgh construction worker, took over a small Hunter group in Month 2 and grew it by absorbing two others; pragmatic; the cannibalism is *his* decision, made when food got tight in the first winter; he carries the weight of that decision visibly but not openly.
- Other lieutenants the narrator may invent as needed.

## The player's likely arc with the Hunters

The Hunters are *primarily a threat the player avoids.* Several trajectories:

- **No contact.** Player leaves Pittsburgh in M-15 (Month 4) before the Hunters consolidate. They hear about the Hunters as rumors on the road. They do not return to Pittsburgh.
- **One contact, survived.** Player encounters a Hunter group in or near Pittsburgh in Month 4-6, talks their way through or escapes, never returns. The encounter is *formative* — the player now knows what the city has become.
- **Sustained contact.** Player has reason to return to Pittsburgh (a salvage, an extraction, a search for someone). Hunter encounters scale up. The player learns the territory.
- **Captivity.** Rare and dangerous. If a player is captured by Hunters, the narrator runs the captivity arc at full weight — see Mortality-eligible territory in the missions deck. Some Hunter captivity scenes are unsurvivable; the narrator does not pretend otherwise.

**The player is unlikely to ally with the Hunters in Allegheny's default scope.** The opportunity structure is wrong — the Hunters do not recruit travelers the way the Fireflies do. They process them. A player can negotiate, can trade, can leave the territory alive, but the path to joining a Hunter group is narrow and the narrator should not present it as a clean choice.

## HIDDEN

**Cannibalism in Len's group.** Len's group has been practicing cannibalism since the second month of the FEDRA collapse, when their scavenge yields collapsed and the group faced starvation. The decision was Reeve's. The practice has stabilized: captives held for labor, then processed when no longer useful, then consumed by the group. The non-cannibal members of the group know — there is no hiding it — but most participate by silence rather than by direct act.

The player should *not* learn this on first contact. Len's friendly opening does not mention it. If the player is captured or sustained-engaged with Len's group, the discovery is the horror beat — Len's dialogue example in `CAST_LEN.md` for first contact is *the friendly version*; his actual character includes this practice.

If the player asks about cannibalism among Hunters in general, the narrator's response depends on AWARENESS and contact:
- AWARENESS 4 or below: an NPC may have heard the rumor; cannot confirm
- AWARENESS 5 with Firefly contact: Casey or a Firefly contact may confirm that some Pittsburgh Hunter groups practice it; cannot name specific groups
- AWARENESS 5 with sustained contact: the player may witness or be told directly

**The Pittsburgh coalition's instability.** By Year 1+, the coalition is likely to fracture. The narrator may surface this in road-era improvisation if the player is interested. The fracture is *not* a story the player is likely to be present for unless they specifically return to Pittsburgh in late Year 1 or Year 2.

## Narrator discipline — rendering the Hunters

How to write Hunter scenes well.

- **Friendliness is the horror.** Len's first impression is *warm*. The friendliness is real (he means it) and tactical (he uses it). The narrator should render his calm, his hand gestures, his even tone. The horror is the gap between the surface and what waits.
- **No cackling villains.** Hunters are *not* The Walking Dead's Negan. They are not having a good time. They are doing the work of being who they have become. The narrator should write them as exhausted, capable, and quiet — not theatrical.
- **The cannibalism is real but not constant.** Not every Hunter scene needs to invoke it. Most Hunter scenes the player encounters in Allegheny's default scope are *negotiations* and *evasions* — the cannibalism is the shadow that gives the negotiations weight, but it is not the visible content of most scenes.
- **No torture porn.** When Hunter violence happens, the narrator renders it tightly — quick, real, ugly. The narrator does not linger on suffering. The horror is in *what people have decided* to do, not in extended depictions of doing it.
- **Captivity arcs are dangerous and rare.** If the player ends up captured by Hunters, the narrator gives the arc weight but does not stretch it. Two or three scenes; an exit or an end. *Not a long-running survival-horror arc*.
- **Resist the cannibalism reveal as setpiece.** The discovery should land *small* — a moment of recognition, a piece of evidence, a thing the player puts together. The narrator does not stage a "you have been eating it the whole time" reveal; that is genre-clichéd and the narrator can do better.

The Pittsburgh Hunters are *what the world made cheap.* The narrator's job is to render them as people who have made choices the world made cheap, without softening the choices and without dehumanizing the chooser. Both are crafts. Both are required.
