# FACTION_FIREFLIES — the Fireflies

**faction_id:** FIREFLIES · **active in Allegheny:** Week 3 onward (QZ era and beyond)

Cross-references: `CAST_CASEY.md` (the cell organizer); `MISSION_M12_The_Pamphlet.md` (first contact); `KNOWLEDGE_7_Hidden.md` (the immunity project and the Fireflies' deeper goals, gated by player discovery); `KNOWLEDGE_2_City.md` for the Pittsburgh QZ where they operate.

---

## Identity

**The Fireflies.** A loose resistance organization that emerged across the United States in the first weeks of the outbreak, formalizing into a recognizable movement by Week 6-8 of the FEDRA era. Their symbol is a stylized firefly — simple, hand-drawable, recurring on leaflets, walls, and the inside covers of items they distribute. Their motto, when they speak one aloud, is *"Look for the light."*

In Allegheny's window, the Fireflies are a real but small political project. Their national strength is overstated by FEDRA propaganda and understated by their own rhetoric. The truth is between.

## Origin

The Fireflies grew from three overlapping currents in the first weeks of the outbreak:

- **Pre-outbreak political networks** — small groups of activists, journalists, and academics who had been documenting FEDRA's executive-order expansion in real time during the first week and who recognized, faster than most, that the emergency authority would not be rolled back.
- **Disaffected federal personnel** — federal employees, contractors, and military reservists who refused FEDRA's federalization, deserted, or were never called. Some carried documents.
- **QZ civilians** — survivors who had been in the early QZs long enough to see the procedures harden, and who began organizing inside the wire.

By Week 6 of the outbreak (early November 2018), these currents had begun communicating across QZs via courier, scavenged radio, and the surviving fragments of pre-outbreak infrastructure. By Week 8, the name "Fireflies" was in use nationally. By the end of 2018, a recognizable command structure was in place — though "command structure" overstates the centralization.

**The Fireflies are decentralized by design.** There are regional cells (Boston, Pittsburgh, Atlanta, the Pacific Northwest, possibly others) that coordinate but do not take orders from a single point. This is partly tactical (no single point of failure for FEDRA to attack) and partly necessity (communication infrastructure does not support tighter centralization). The Pittsburgh cell answers to a regional command in the Boston direction, but the relationship is *one of consultation*, not authority.

## Stated purpose

The Fireflies' stated purpose, in their leaflets and recruitment language: *to restore democratic government, end FEDRA's emergency authority, and rebuild civilian life on terms consented to by civilians.*

The leaflets are specific:
- FEDRA's emergency executive authority is unconstitutional
- The QZs are not temporary; they are becoming permanent prison camps
- The civilian population has the right to know what FEDRA is doing and to consent to it
- The Fireflies exist to organize that resistance

The language is *credible* and *honest as far as it goes*. It is also partial. There is a longer-game purpose that Casey and the Pittsburgh cell do not currently know — see HIDDEN below.

## Actual operating reality

Day-to-day, the Pittsburgh Firefly cell operates at small scale:

- **Recruitment.** Identifying potential members in the QZ. Working through intermediaries (a QZ medical volunteer who has been quietly screening sympathetic civilians; a barter-market regular who passes leaflets). Casey is the public face.
- **Information collection.** Documenting FEDRA's specific actions — who was removed when, what orders preceded which raids. Ray's notebook, if it reaches them, would be a substantial intelligence acquisition.
- **Underground transit.** Routes through and around the QZ wall. The Fireflies have at least two known crossing points by Week 4 of the QZ era.
- **Material support.** Distributing what they have — antibiotics, ammunition, occasional weapons — to QZ allies. Not at scale; in small quantities.
- **Strategic patience.** They are *building* in Pittsburgh. They are not yet planning an open action. Casey's pitch language ("we have a hypothesis... we are organizing") is honest about this — they are not promising imminent revolution.

## Hierarchy

**Cell-based, regional coordination.** No single "leader" of the Fireflies nationally, though regional figures exist:

- **Regional command (Boston):** the closest thing to senior leadership the Pittsburgh cell reports up to. A name circulates among Fireflies — the player will not learn it in Allegheny's default scope.
- **Cell organizers:** Casey's role in Pittsburgh. Casey is the senior Firefly in the Pittsburgh QZ. They report to Boston regional.
- **Cell members:** the active Fireflies in the QZ. Estimated 8-12 active in Pittsburgh by Week 4, with another 15-25 sympathetic civilians who would help in small ways but are not committed.
- **Sympathizers:** civilians who know about the Fireflies, would not turn them in, may pass information. A larger pool — perhaps 100-150 in the Pittsburgh QZ.
- **Couriers:** moving between QZs. A risky role; couriers are the Fireflies' most-exposed personnel.

**Internal note for the narrator:** the Fireflies in Allegheny's window are *smaller and less competent than Casey's pitch makes them sound*. Casey is a good organizer; Casey is not running an army. If the player asks specific questions about Firefly numbers, Firefly resources, Firefly long-term strategy, Casey's answers should be *honest but bounded* — they will not lie, and they will not exaggerate, but they will also not commit to specifics they do not have.

## Visual identifiers

**Uniforms:** none. Fireflies wear civilian clothing. The lack of uniform is itself the tell — when civilians see a person who is clearly *not* FEDRA but is clearly *organized*, they may be looking at a Firefly.

**Symbols:** the stylized firefly logo. Simple enough to hand-draw. Appears on:
- Leaflets (mimeographed or hand-lettered)
- Walls (chalk, paint, occasionally carved)
- The inside covers of distributed items (antibiotics, ammunition, occasional weapons — the Firefly logo on a pill bottle marks it as a Firefly distribution)
- Member tattoos in some cases — a small firefly on the inside of a wrist or behind an ear. Not universal; some cells discourage tattoos because they make identification easier if a member is captured.

**Vocabulary tells:**
- "The cause" — used by committed Fireflies; the narrator should use it sparingly, when a character is being earnest
- "Cell" — operational unit
- "Boston" — sometimes used as shorthand for regional command
- "Look for the light" — the motto; said sometimes as a greeting between Fireflies, sometimes as a sign-off
- "Comrade" is *not* used. The Fireflies' political idiom is American-democratic-revival, not socialist. They talk about *citizens* and *democracy* and *consent of the governed*.

## Methods

How the Pittsburgh cell operates in practice:

- **Leafleting.** Distribution of mimeographed material in QZ stairwells, ration-line waiting areas, the barter market. The leaflets are *short* and *specific* — they name a recent FEDRA action and frame it. They do not include meeting times or locations; those come through personal contact.
- **Recruitment meetings.** Small, low-visibility, in locations that change. A basement, a stairwell, a back room at the barter market. Casey runs most of these in the Pittsburgh QZ.
- **The Tomas-Tea code.** A specific phrase the Pittsburgh cell uses for in-person verification. *"How do you take your tea?"* asked at an unusual moment. The correct response is *"Tomas drinks it black."* (The phrase has no historical meaning; it is arbitrary and changes every six weeks. By Month 4 the phrase will have changed.)
- **The smuggle network.** Two crossings under the QZ wall — one through a service tunnel near the medical infrastructure, one through a Convention Center maintenance route. Both require timing past FEDRA patrols. Both are known to the cell core; neither is shared with new recruits.
- **Material redistribution.** Antibiotics from FEDRA-controlled medical stores rerouted to civilians who need them. Small quantities; consistent; deliberately not enough to be noticed.

**The Fireflies do not, in Pittsburgh, run open operations against FEDRA.** No bombings, no assassinations, no kidnappings. They are *building* and *documenting*. When FEDRA collapses in Month 4, the Fireflies will move; until then, they are patient.

## Internal tensions

The Fireflies are not unified in their long-term aims:

- **The Restorationists:** the wing whose stated purpose is rebuilding democratic government. They want to outlast FEDRA, document its abuses, and re-establish civilian rule when the emergency ends. Casey is closest to this wing.
- **The Revolutionaries:** a smaller wing who believe FEDRA is an inevitable consequence of the pre-outbreak American state and that what comes after should be different. They are willing to use methods the Restorationists are not. They are quieter in the Pittsburgh cell but present.
- **The Pragmatists:** members who are in it for survival reasons more than political ones. They were going to die in their QZ; the Fireflies offered a way to live longer. Their commitment is real but conditional.

The tensions are mostly latent in Allegheny's window. The Fireflies have not yet had to make the choices that surface them. They will, but not in 2018 or early 2019.

## Geography in Pittsburgh

The Pittsburgh Firefly cell's footprint:

- **Inside the QZ:**
  - The barter market lobby and adjacent stairwell (recruitment, leafleting)
  - The Convention Center medical tent (one Firefly contact embedded as a volunteer)
  - Multiple safehouses in the QZ residential buildings (locations rotate)
- **Outside the QZ:**
  - At least one safehouse in Lawrenceville (an apartment whose occupant is a sympathetic civilian who left before the cordon went up)
  - A drop point in Bloomfield (a specific intersection with a hollow utility box)
  - A meeting site in the buffer zone (location varies; smugglers use it too)
- **At the wall:**
  - Two known underground crossing points (see Methods)

After FEDRA's collapse in Month 4, the Pittsburgh cell's footprint changes — they no longer need to evade FEDRA, but they now face Hunters as an active threat. The cell's geography shifts toward survival rather than resistance.

## NPC anchors

- The cell includes 7-11 other named members the narrator can introduce as needed — the narrator generates them lightly using the threshold rule. One specific member to keep available: *an older woman who watches the door at Casey's meetings; does not speak much*. She has no name yet; the narrator may name her if she becomes recurring.
- The Boston regional contact whose name the player will not learn in Allegheny.

## The player's likely arc with the Fireflies

Several trajectories:

- **Refuse contact.** The leaflet shows up, the player ignores it; M-12 does not fire. The Fireflies remain a thread in the world but not in the player's story. Casey may try again later (Month 2-3) with a smaller approach. The player can refuse a second time.
- **Listen but not commit.** Player meets with Casey in M-12, hears the pitch, takes the code phrase, leaves without commitment. The Fireflies are now a *possible* ally. The player may or may not return.
- **Run errands.** Player accepts small jobs — a delivery, a piece of information, a meeting with someone Casey wants vouched. The relationship is *transactional*. The player gets paid in things FEDRA can't access (antibiotics, ammunition).
- **Full commitment.** Player joins the cell. They are now a Firefly. Casey introduces them to other members. They participate in cell operations. By Month 3, the player is *on a list FEDRA is building* — see the Hidden in `MISSION_M14_The_Long_Winter.md`.
- **Bring Ray.** If Ray defects and the player brings her to the Fireflies, the cell's interest in the player escalates. Ray's notebook is a substantial intelligence acquisition; the cell will *invest* in the player who delivered it.

The narrator does not push. Casey does not push. The player decides what they want to be.

## HIDDEN — the longer game

**The Fireflies have a long-term project the Pittsburgh cell does not yet operationally know about: developing a Cordyceps vaccine or treatment from immune subjects.**

In Allegheny's window:
- The vaccine project exists at the national level — primarily in Salt Lake City, where the Fireflies have a research facility under construction
- The Pittsburgh cell has been *told* that the long-term project is research-focused, without specifics
- The cell's instruction is to *identify potential immune individuals* if any surface in the QZ — through the medical tent, through observation, through luck
- No confirmed immune has been encountered in Pittsburgh in 2018. The instruction is *standing*, not *operational*.
- Casey personally believes the vaccine project is a possibility worth working for, but is not invested in it the way some of the regional command is

The player learns about this *only* if they reach AWARENESS 5 and have built deep Firefly trust — typically Month 3 or later. The narrator does not raise the topic unprompted. See `KNOWLEDGE_7_Hidden.md` for the full hidden material.

**The player's character is not immune in Allegheny's default scope.** If the player wants to build toward immunity as a story element, they must explicitly negotiate that with the narrator. The Fireflies are not, in default play, going to discover the player has an immunity they do not have.

## Narrator discipline — rendering the Fireflies

How to write Firefly scenes well.

- **Credible, not heroic.** The Fireflies have a real political project with real costs. The narrator should render them as people with reasons, not as Rebels-from-Star-Wars. Casey is *good at their work*; that is different from being *good*.
- **Honest about scale.** The Pittsburgh cell is small. If the player asks about Firefly numbers, Casey's answer is honest. The narrator should not have Casey *exaggerate* to motivate the player; the player's motivation is the player's to find.
- **Patient pace.** The Fireflies' pace is *slow*. The narrator should resist staging Firefly scenes as setpiece resistance moments. Most Firefly scenes are quiet — a leaflet found, a conversation in a stairwell, a meeting in a basement, a small package handed over. The drama is in the slowness.
- **No call-to-arms language.** Casey does not say *"join us."* Casey says *"I'm not asking for an answer tonight."* The narrator should hold this line; Firefly recruitment is *non-coercive by design and by craft.*
- **Vocabulary discipline.** The Fireflies talk about *citizens*, *democracy*, *consent*, *organizing*. They do not talk about *the revolution*, *comrades*, or *the struggle*. They are an American political project, not an imported one.
- **The longer game is a player discovery, not a narrator reveal.** The vaccine project should never come up in Casey's pitch. It surfaces only when the player has done the work — AWARENESS 5, deep trust, the right question asked.

The Fireflies are an *available trajectory.* Whether the player takes it, partially takes it, refuses it, or betrays it is the player's story.
