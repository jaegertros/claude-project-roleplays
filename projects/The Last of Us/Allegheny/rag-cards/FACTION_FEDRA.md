# FACTION_FEDRA — Federal Disaster Response Agency

**faction_id:** FEDRA · **active in Allegheny:** Day 5 onward; controls Pittsburgh QZ Week 1 – Month 4

Cross-references: `CAST_DRAKE.md` (the cordon officer); `CAST_RAY.md` (the defector); `MISSION_M09_The_Cordon.md` (first sustained encounter); `KNOWLEDGE_2_City.md` for the QZ proper; `KNOWLEDGE_3_Timeline.md` for the FEDRA collapse in Month 4.

---

## Identity

**Federal Disaster Response Agency.** A federal agency established by executive order during the outbreak's first 72 hours, drawing personnel from the National Guard, active military, FEMA, federal law enforcement, and emergency federalization of civilian medical and security workers. **In Allegheny's world, FEDRA is not a pre-outbreak entity** — it is invented in the third week of September 2018 and operational by Day 5.

The acronym is pronounced *FED-rah*. Civilians say it without much affection.

## Origin

The first 72 hours of the outbreak produced a federal response that was simultaneously massive and inadequate. The National Guard mobilized to Pittsburgh and other cities on Day 3 evening; their authority was unclear, their orders were contradictory, and within 48 hours their command structure was failing.

FEDRA emerged on Day 4 by executive order as the unified authority — absorbing the Guard's deployments, federalizing civilian medical and security personnel, and assuming control of "public health enforcement" with extraordinary emergency powers. The order was justified as temporary. By Day 7 the temporary architecture was hardening into a permanent institution.

**The personnel are not, mostly, FEDRA loyalists.** They are people who were doing other jobs three weeks ago and are now wearing a different patch. Ray's trajectory — civilian paramedic + Army Reserve sergeant → emergency federalization → FEDRA enlisted — is typical. This matters for how the narrator renders FEDRA characters: most of them are *new at this*, even though their procedures suggest decades of institutional weight.

## Stated purpose

Public health enforcement under emergency federal authority. Specifically:
- Containment of infected populations
- Establishment and management of Quarantine Zones (QZs)
- Distribution of rations and medical supplies to QZ civilians
- Maintenance of order within QZs
- Restoration of civilian governance "when conditions permit" (a phrase that quietly stops being said after Month 2)

## Actual operating reality

In Allegheny's window, FEDRA's actual day-to-day operation is:
- **Cordoning** — walls, checkpoints, scanner stations. Most FEDRA personnel time is spent on perimeter duty.
- **Scanning** — bite detection at all entry points. False positives result in civilians removed to "isolation," which by Week 2 is understood by FEDRA enlisted (and rumored among civilians) to mean execution.
- **Ration distribution** — weekly, points-based, opaque to civilians. The points system rewards compliance with work assignments.
- **Work assignment** — adults receive clearing, salvage, medical, or kitchen detail. Refusal results in ration reduction.
- **Suppression** — protests, hoarding, contraband. The procedures harden over time.
- **Sweeps** — periodic clearing of buildings outside the perimeter for holdouts, infected, and contraband.

By Month 3, the operating reality has visibly diverged from the stated purpose. By Month 4, the divergence has become catastrophic and the QZ collapses.

## Hierarchy

Loose adaptation of military structure with civilian-administrative overlay:

- **Director (Pittsburgh):** the senior FEDRA official in the city. Identity not specified — the player may or may not encounter them. They are operational by Day 7, replaced once during the era, retreating with FEDRA in Month 4.
- **Sector Captains:** mid-rank officers commanding QZ sectors (typically 4-6 sectors at peak). Drake is a Lieutenant subordinate to a Sector Captain.
- **Lieutenants:** the most visible FEDRA officers to civilians. Drake's tier. Command checkpoints, scanner stations, suppression units.
- **Sergeants and Corporals:** NCOs running the day-to-day. Ray's tier as enlisted, before her transfer.
- **Enlisted:** the rank-and-file. National Guard transfers, federalized civilians, military reservists. The largest population.
- **Medical Personnel:** civilian medical workers under emergency federalization. Erin would have been pulled into this tier had her recognition come earlier; she enters the QZ medical tent through Week-2 civilian volunteer routes instead, which is its own messy story.
- **Administrative:** ration card processing, work-assignment management, the bureaucratic infrastructure that makes the QZ run.

The hierarchy is *visibly held together by procedure*. When procedure fails, hierarchy fails. This is what happens in Month 4.

## Visual identifiers

**Uniforms:**
- Combat utilities in olive drab or coyote brown (transferred National Guard and military origin)
- FEDRA patch on the right shoulder — a stylized eagle over a chevron pattern, gold on black
- Sector identifier patch on the left shoulder (e.g., "PIT-4" for Pittsburgh Sector 4)
- Officer rank insignia on collars and chest tape
- By Week 4 of the QZ era, uniforms are increasingly rumpled — laundry is not a priority. Drake's pressed uniform in late October is conspicuous in a way that says something about him.

**Equipment:**
- Standard sidearms (M9 or M17 service pistols)
- M4 carbines (or M16 variants) for most enlisted
- Bite-scanner kits (handheld, finger-prick blood test, 30-second reaction)
- Body armor for sector teams and suppression units
- FEDRA-issued radios on closed frequencies

**Vocabulary tells:**
- "Category 1" — civilian who has tested bite-positive or is being treated as such
- "Sector" — administrative division of the QZ
- "Procedure" — used in place of "policy" or "rules"; FEDRA personnel say *"that's procedure"* the way other people say *"that's how it is"*
- "Civilian" — used as a category designation, sometimes neutrally, sometimes with weight depending on the speaker
- "Isolation" — euphemism for removal of bite-positive individuals
- "CBI" — Cordyceps Brain Infection (official FEDRA term; civilians say "infected")
- Acronym-heavy speech in general — FEDRA personnel adopt military communication norms even when they did not come from military backgrounds. Ray's "copy" instead of "okay" is the standard tell.

## Methods

How FEDRA actually operates, scene by scene:

- **At the cordon:** civilians are processed through scanner lanes. Procedure is rigid. Officers like Drake do not deviate. Failed scans are handled by sector teams; the failed civilian is moved to a holding truck and then to "isolation." The procedure is *visible*; the consequence is not. Most civilians understand the consequence anyway.
- **In the QZ:** patrols on rotation. Each patrol is two to four enlisted. They do not engage civilians unless procedure requires (suspected contraband, suspected infection, suspected illegal contact). When they do engage, the engagement is procedural.
- **In suppression:** when a protest or illegal gathering occurs, FEDRA's response escalates by a defined ladder. Verbal dispersal → physical dispersal with batons → tear gas → live rounds. The ladder is documented; the ladder is followed; the ladder by Month 3 reaches live rounds faster than it did in Week 2.
- **In sweeps:** sector teams enter buildings, secure them, scan inhabitants, remove infected and bite-positive, escort cleared civilians to the QZ. Resistance is met with lethal force. Most sweeps in October-November are routine; by December the sweeps are smaller because there are fewer civilians outside the wall.
- **In Firefly suppression:** by Week 3, FEDRA is aware of Firefly activity in the QZ. Their response is *intelligence-led* — they monitor, identify, and arrest. Public executions are not used; disappearances are. By January, three Fireflies have been arrested off-screen and processed.

## Internal tensions

FEDRA is not monolithic. The major tensions:

- **Federal command vs. local pragmatism.** Pittsburgh's FEDRA leadership receives orders from federal command that are based on national models that don't account for Pittsburgh's specifics. Local officers (the Sector Captains and below) make adjustments. By Month 2, the local adjustments are significant enough that some orders are quietly not carried out.
- **Federalized military vs. federalized civilians.** Personnel who came from military backgrounds (active duty, Reserve, National Guard) have a coherence with FEDRA's procedural culture. Federalized civilians (paramedics, EMTs, federal law enforcement, some local police) do not. The tension produces ongoing friction, particularly at the NCO and Lieutenant level.
- **Old hands vs. new arrivals.** Personnel who were on the ground Days 4-7 carry the trauma of those days and the unit cohesion that came with it. Personnel rotated in during Week 2-3 (replacements, reinforcements) have less of it and are sometimes treated as outsiders.
- **Belief vs. procedure.** Some FEDRA personnel believe in the mission as articulated. Most do not, in the sense that most do not believe FEDRA's stated purpose is being achieved by FEDRA's actual operations. They follow procedure because procedure is the structure that holds. When procedure begins to crack, so do the personnel — see the FEDRA collapse in Month 4.

Ray's notebook documents specifically *the gap between procedure and outcome* — orders given for one stated purpose that, when executed, produced a different real result. The notebook would matter to anyone within FEDRA who is keeping their belief operational by not looking. It matters even more to anyone outside FEDRA.

## Geography in Pittsburgh

FEDRA's footprint in the city, by phase:

**Days 5-7 (cordon formation):**
- Concentration at the bridges (Sixteenth Street, Fort Pitt, Veterans) and major intersections
- First perimeter walls — plywood and chain-link — going up around downtown
- Staging at the stadiums on the north shore (PNC Park, Heinz Field)

**Week 1 - Month 4 (QZ operation):**
- **Headquarters:** the Steel Tower (U.S. Steel building) downtown. Senior leadership is here.
- **Central distribution:** the Convention Center. Rations, work assignments, medical triage.
- **Staging and personnel quarters:** the stadiums on the north shore
- **Holding/processing for bite-positive removal:** an unspecified facility off the south shore (the player should not be told exactly; FEDRA does not publicize it; it is reachable by truck from the cordon checkpoints)
- **Sector checkpoints:** at every QZ entry point
- **Patrol presence:** throughout the QZ interior; varying density (heavier near distribution centers, lighter in the residential areas)

**Month 4 (retreat):**
- FEDRA pulls out under civilian pressure and command decision
- The Steel Tower is evacuated; records and hardware are loaded onto trucks
- The Sixteenth Street Bridge is the primary withdrawal route
- The retreat takes approximately 72 hours; the QZ collapses over the same period

After the retreat, no FEDRA presence remains in Pittsburgh. The buildings they used remain — increasingly occupied by Hunters in Week 5 of the collapse and beyond.

## NPC anchors

- **DRAKE** (Lieutenant Andrew Drake) — the recurring FEDRA officer face, commanding the cordon sector the group passes through. See `CAST_DRAKE.md`. He is by-the-book procedural and not personally cruel; the cruelty is the procedure.
- **RAY** (Rachael Cordero) — FEDRA enlisted who defects in Week 4 or Month 2. See `CAST_RAY.md`. Her notebook is the player's window into FEDRA's internal reality.
- Named-minor FEDRA personnel may appear in scenes; the narrator generates them lightly using the threshold rule (third appearance = LIGHT-tier entry).

## The player's likely arc with FEDRA

Several trajectories:

- **Compliant civilian.** Player enters the QZ, accepts work assignments, draws rations, keeps head down. The relationship is *managed by the system*; the personal encounters with FEDRA are procedural and bounded.
- **Quiet resistance.** Player participates in QZ life but bends rules — barter market, small contraband, occasional sympathies for Fireflies. FEDRA's response if caught is procedural; the player learns the difference between minor infractions (ration reduction) and major ones (isolation or worse).
- **Active resistance.** Player commits to Firefly work or independent action against FEDRA. The relationship is now adversarial. The player is, by Month 2, on a FEDRA list of some kind. Risk escalates.
- **FEDRA cooperation.** Less common but possible — player builds rapport with specific FEDRA personnel (Ray before her defection; possibly Drake at a distance). The relationship is *conditional* and complicated; the FEDRA collapse in Month 4 puts whatever was built into a different frame.
- **Refusal to enter the QZ.** Player stays in Lawrenceville or leaves the city early. FEDRA exists as a force at a distance — the cordons, the sweeps, the rumored realities inside. The relationship is *as the enemy of survivors outside the wall.*

The narrator does not push the player toward any of these. The player builds their own.

## Narrator discipline — rendering FEDRA

How to write FEDRA scenes well.

- **FEDRA is procedure, not cruelty.** The horror is bureaucratic. Drake reads the order; the order says scan the civilian; the scan returns positive; the procedure moves the civilian to the truck. No one in the sequence is sadistic. The result is still terrible.
- **FEDRA personnel are tired.** By Week 2, every FEDRA member has been on rotating shifts for three weeks. Their exhaustion is visible. The narrator should render this — uniforms not perfectly maintained, voices flat, attention drifting at edges. Drake's pressed uniform in October is a *character beat* that contrasts with the surrounding norm.
- **Most FEDRA personnel are not committed believers.** They are people doing a job that has stopped making sense to them. The narrator should resist the urge to write FEDRA characters who deliver speeches about order or duty. Ray's pre-defection version of herself rarely talks; that is the norm.
- **Vocabulary is the tell.** FEDRA personnel say "copy," "procedure," "Category 1," "civilian." When they slip into normal vocabulary — when a Sergeant says "okay" instead of "copy" or "this woman" instead of "this civilian" — that is a character beat. The narrator should let it happen.
- **The QZ is not Auschwitz.** The narrator should avoid Holocaust-coded imagery — armbands, mass-transport language, "selection" framing. FEDRA's procedures are *bureaucratic* in a specifically American institutional shape. Convention Centers, scanner kits, ration cards, work assignments. The horror is in the *recognizability* of the institutional shape, not in its evocation of historical genocide.
- **FEDRA is not the Empire.** No FEDRA character knows they are in the wrong genre. They are doing what they were told to do. They do not give monologues about order. They process the next civilian. They take the next shift. They go to sleep on a cot in the stadium and wake up and do it again.

The player is allowed to hate FEDRA. The narrator should not editorialize. The player's hatred is one valid response to what they see. So is the player's complicity if they choose it. So is the player's indifference. The institution generates all of these responses; the narrator renders the institution.

## Internal craft notes

When writing a FEDRA character whose name the narrator has not previously established, default to:
- A first name that does not suggest a specific cultural background unless the character is named
- A rank below Drake's (Sergeant or Corporal) for procedural interactions; at Drake's level only for officer-level moments
- A backstory that includes pre-outbreak civilian or near-civilian work, not career military, unless specifically established otherwise
- Vocabulary discipline: they say "copy" and "procedure"; they do not say "civilian" sneeringly; they do not philosophize

The first FEDRA character to speak in any scene establishes the *temperature* of that FEDRA presence. If they are warm-procedural (a Sergeant who is tired but polite), the scene's FEDRA is functional. If they are cold-procedural (a Lieutenant who does not look at the civilian's face while they speak), the scene's FEDRA is what FEDRA is becoming.

By Month 4, FEDRA's temperature has shifted noticeably colder. The narrator should render this through the personnel themselves — not through editorializing.
