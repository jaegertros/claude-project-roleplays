# KNOWLEDGE_5_Era.md
## Historical guardrails for the 1715 Caribbean

The narrator's job is to render the period without lecturing it. This file is the reference shelf — pull from it when a scene needs the right ship, the right coin, the right disease, the right superstition. Nothing here should ever be quoted directly into narration as exposition. The world is conveyed through prices, dialogue, smells, and consequence.

---

## THE MOMENT IN HISTORY

The action opens in **mid-to-late 1715**. The world Eriksohn wakes into is not a stable one.

- **The War of the Spanish Succession just ended.** Treaty of Utrecht signed 1713; Treaty of Rastatt 1714. Twelve years of European war over the Spanish throne are formally over. The Caribbean is now drowning in unemployed sailors — privateers, navy men, marines — who had spent a decade raiding under letters of marque and who no longer have a legal employer. Many of them are about to invent piracy as a profession for themselves.

- **The 1715 Spanish Treasure Fleet just wrecked.** On the night of 30–31 July 1715, a hurricane drove eleven of twelve Spanish galleons onto the reef off Florida's east coast (modern-day Sebastian / Vero Beach area). Roughly 1,000 sailors and soldiers died. An immense fortune in silver, gold, jewels, and contraband Chinese porcelain went to the bottom in shallow water. By the time the story opens, the Spanish are running a salvage camp on the Florida coast. Henry Jennings's privateer raid on that camp in late 1715 / early 1716 is the spark that lights the Republic of Pirates. Treasure-fleet silver is flooding ports across the Caribbean, much of it stolen, all of it untraceable.

- **Nassau is becoming a pirate haven.** The town has no functioning English government. Former privateers — Benjamin Hornigold, Henry Jennings, Charles Vane, and the still-young Edward Teach — are operating from New Providence with impunity. London hasn't yet decided what to do. By 1718 it will send Woodes Rogers and the noose. For now, Nassau is the unofficial capital of the Brotherhood.

- **Spain is broke and angry.** The treasure loss is catastrophic. Spanish patrols are everywhere they can afford to be. Anyone caught with treasure-fleet silver — minted at the Mexico City, Lima, or Bogotá mints, easy to identify — risks being hanged as a pirate regardless of citizenship.

- **The Royal Navy is thin.** England has ships in the Caribbean but not enough of them, and they're spread between Jamaica, Barbados, and the Leeward Islands station. The North American mainland (Carolinas) gets even less coverage.

- **Slavery is the engine of the entire region.** Sugar runs on enslaved labor. The Royal African Company's monopoly was broken in 1698, opening the trade to independent merchants. Every island economy is calibrated around the slave trade and the sugar trade. This is not a footnote. This is the world.

## THE POLITICAL MAP

- **Spain** — Cuba, Puerto Rico, Hispaniola (eastern half), Trinidad, the Spanish Main (mainland from Venezuela through Panama and Mexico). Bleeding from the treasure loss. Suspicious of every foreign sail.
- **England** — Jamaica, Bahamas (nominally), Barbados, the Leeward Islands, Bermuda. Newly united with Scotland (1707 Act of Union); ships now sail under the Union Jack. Government distracted by the Jacobite rising at home.
- **France** — Saint-Domingue (western Hispaniola), Martinique, Guadeloupe. Recovering from war debt; tolerant of buccaneers who hurt the Spanish.
- **Netherlands** — Curaçao, Sint Eustatius, smaller holdings. Officially neutral, practically the trans-shipment hub of Caribbean smuggling.
- **Denmark-Norway** — St. Thomas (Charlotte Amalie), St. John. Officially neutral. A practical free port. (And, in our world, **Sankt Hjalmar.**)
- **Indigenous** — Caribs in the Lesser Antilles (St. Vincent, Dominica). Largely independent. Mainland nations across the Spanish Main hold inland territory.
- **Maroon communities** — escaped enslaved people living free in inland Jamaica (the First Maroon War is brewing), Saint-Domingue, Hispaniola, and on smaller islands wherever the interior is unmapped enough.

## SHIPS AND THE SEA

The narrator does not need to be a marine archaeologist, but should know enough not to put a frigate where a sloop belongs.

**Common vessels in 1715:**

- **Sloop** — single mast, fast, fore-and-aft rigged, maybe 8–14 guns. Pirate favorite. Shallow draft means it can hide where larger ships can't follow.
- **Schooner** — two masts, fore-and-aft rigged, fast and maneuverable. Smaller crew than a sloop of comparable tonnage.
- **Brigantine** — two masts, mixed rig (square forward, fore-and-aft aft). Larger than a sloop. Common merchant and pirate vessel.
- **Snow** — similar to a brigantine, slightly larger. Workhorse merchant.
- **Frigate** — Royal Navy designation; three-masted, fully ship-rigged, 24–44 guns. Predator.
- **Galleon** — Spanish; large, square-rigged, treasure-fleet workhorse. Slow, heavily armed, hauls everything. The class the 1715 fleet was built around.
- **Periagua** / **piragua** — large dugout canoe with auxiliary sail, used for inter-island travel by indigenous, enslaved, free Black, and poor white sailors. Cheap, fast, common.

**Sailing realities to honor:**

- You cannot sail directly into the wind. Beating to windward is slow, miserable work.
- Tropical waters destroy hulls. Worms eat the planking, weed and barnacles foul the bottom. Every ship careens (beaches itself and tips over) every few months to clean the hull. A ship that has not careened recently is slow and rotting.
- Salt food, rancid water, no fresh fruit at sea = scurvy on long voyages. Caribbean voyages are short enough that this is rare but not unknown.
- Hurricane season runs roughly June through November. *June too early to be sure / Risk we must / July stand by / August, look high / September, remember / October, all over.*
- Lighthouses essentially do not exist. Navigation at night is by lead line, dead reckoning, and prayer.

**Crew sizes:**
- Merchant brigantine: ~15–25.
- Pirate sloop: 60–90 (pirates ran heavy crews to overwhelm targets and provide prize crews).
- Royal Navy frigate: 200–300.

## CURRENCY AND PRICES

(See KNOWLEDGE_6_Utility.md for the full price index. Brief reference here.)

- **1 real** = base silver unit.
- **1 piece of eight (peso, "dollar")** = 8 reales.
- **1 escudo** = 2 pieces of eight (gold).
- **1 doubloon** = 2 escudos = 32 reales (gold).

Spanish currency dominates the entire Caribbean because Spanish mints produced most of the actual coinage in circulation. English, French, and Dutch colonies use pieces of eight as commonly as their own currencies. A doubloon is a small disk worth roughly six months' wages for a common laborer.

**Treasure-fleet coinage** is identifiable by mint marks (Mexico, Lima, Bogotá). Carrying it in 1715–1716 is dangerous — Spanish authorities will hang you for it without trial. Fences in Nassau, St. Thomas, Curaçao, and Sankt Hjalmar take it at heavy discounts.

## WEAPONS

What people actually carry, and what they actually do.

- **Cutlass** — short, heavy, single-edged blade. The boarding weapon. Designed to be swung in tight spaces below deck. Will not parry like a rapier — meant to bludgeon and hack.
- **Boarding pike** — long spear for repelling boarders or clearing a deck. Cheap, lethal, common.
- **Flintlock pistol** — single shot, 30–60 second reload depending on practiced speed. Inaccurate beyond 10 paces. Misfires in wet weather. Pirates often carry **four to six** loaded pistols hung from a baldric for boarding actions, because reloading mid-fight is impossible.
- **Musket / musketoon** — long arm. Slower than a pistol to bring up. More accurate. The marine's weapon.
- **Blunderbuss** — short, flared-barrel scattergun. Devastating at boarding range, useless at distance.
- **Knife** — every sailor carries one. Most fights start with knives because pistols are reserved.
- **Belaying pin, marlinspike, capstan bar** — improvised weapons that are always to hand on a working ship.

**Powder problems:**
- Black powder gets wet and won't fire. Caribbean humidity is a constant enemy of firearms.
- Pistols misfire one shot in five or worse.
- A reloaded pistol is not as reliable as a freshly-charged one; powder fouling builds in the barrel.

**Armor:** essentially nonexistent. Officers may wear a thick leather coat for some protection. Steel breastplates are obsolete and impractical in the heat. A heavy waistcoat may turn a knife at glancing angles but won't stop a ball.

## DAILY LIFE — DISEASE, FOOD, WATER, RUM

- **Yellow fever, malaria, dysentery, typhus, smallpox** — the Caribbean kills more sailors than the Spanish do. Fresh arrivals from Europe ("the seasoning") have roughly a one-in-three chance of dying in their first year.
- **Water on ships** is stored in casks and goes foul within weeks. Sailors drink small beer, rum, or watered rum instead. Constant low-grade intoxication is the baseline state of ship life.
- **Food at sea** — salt pork, salt beef, ship's biscuit (hardtack, riddled with weevils), dried peas, hard cheese. Fresh provisions on shore: fruit, fish, turtle (heavily eaten in this period), goat, salt cod, plantain, cassava.
- **Citrus** prevents scurvy but the etiology isn't yet medically understood. Spanish ships do better than English on long voyages because of the abundance of citrus in their ports.
- **Rum** — sugar-byproduct distillate, cheap, ubiquitous. The Caribbean drink. Aged rum is for officers; raw "kill-devil" is for sailors. Often cut with water on Royal Navy ships (the term *grog* post-dates 1715 by about thirty years — use "watered rum").
- **Tobacco** — chewed, smoked in clay pipes, snuffed. Everywhere.
- **Coffee** is becoming common in the wealthier ports. Chocolate (drunk hot, unsweetened or bitter-sweet) is a Spanish luxury.

## SLAVERY

The non-negotiable reality of every Caribbean scene.

- **Sugar islands** (Barbados, Jamaica, Saint-Domingue, Martinique) run on enslaved labor in punishing conditions. Mortality rates on sugar plantations exceed birth rates. The system requires constant replacement from Africa.
- **Domestic slavery** in towns is more common than rural plantation work in smaller colonies. Enslaved women work as cooks, washerwomen, market traders (some with significant economic autonomy). Enslaved men work the docks, the workshops, the carriages.
- **Maritime slavery** — many enslaved men sail as crew, sometimes hired out by owners for ship's wages that go to the owner. Some are sailors of considerable skill.
- **Free Black population** exists in every port: manumitted, self-purchased, the children of free mothers under Spanish and French law, the offspring of mixed unions, escapees who passed as free elsewhere.
- **Maroon communities** — escaped enslaved people living in inland territory beyond colonial control. Treated as a constant threat by plantation societies.
- **Piracy and race** — pirate crews are unusually integrated by the standards of the era. Some pirates are free Black men who joined willingly; others are enslaved men taken from prize ships and given a choice (join or be sold). Pirate articles often (not always) include equal shares regardless of race. This is not a moral triumph — pirates also took enslaved people as cargo when convenient — but it is more racially flexible than the colonial norm.

The narrator does not editorialize on slavery. It is the world. Show it through prices, encounters, scenes. Let consequence emerge.

## RELIGION AND SUPERSTITION

- **Spanish colonies** — Catholic, with missions (Franciscan, Jesuit, Dominican) in active operation. Anti-Protestant suspicion runs deep.
- **English and Dutch colonies** — Protestant majorities. Anglicanism (England), Reformed Calvinism (Dutch). Dissenting sects (Quakers, Baptists, Moravians, Pietists) tolerated unevenly.
- **French colonies** — Catholic, though more Huguenot population than the Spanish would tolerate.
- **Danish colonies** — Lutheran (Church of Denmark-Norway), but Danish authorities are pragmatic about religious diversity in their free ports. Jews, Moravians, Catholics, dissenters all trade freely in Charlotte Amalie and Sankt Hjalmar.
- **Sephardic Jewish merchants** — small but commercially significant communities in Curaçao, Charlotte Amalie, Sankt Hjalmar, Newport. Descended from Iberian Jews expelled in 1492 and 1497, many having transited Amsterdam. Multilingual, well-networked, financially central.
- **Syncretic religion among the enslaved** — early forms of what would become Vodou, Santería, Obeah practiced quietly. Whites are mostly ignorant of it. Some enslaved people are formally Catholic, formally Protestant, or formally Muslim (West African Muslims appear in the trade) while also practicing inherited belief.
- **Sailor superstition** — strong. Bad luck to sail on a Friday, to whistle on deck (it calls wind, which may be more than asked for), to bring women aboard, to bring clergy aboard. Tattoos serve protective purposes (a swallow for returning home; a rooster on one foot and a pig on the other to prevent drowning, both creatures hating water).

## WHAT IS HOLLYWOOD PIRATE AND WHAT IS NOT

The narrator avoids the following clichés:

- **"Arrr, matey" pirate speech.** This is a 20th-century invention (Robert Newton's 1950 Long John Silver). Sailors of 1715 speak in the regional accents of their origin: London, Bristol, Cornish, Scots, Irish, Welsh, Dutch, French, various Spanish, West African, Creole. Cant and slang are real (a "lurcher" is a swindler; "Davy Jones's locker" is documented; "splice the mainbrace" means a rum ration) but the cartoon "arrr" is anachronistic.
- **Eyepatches as fashion.** Eyepatches existed; they covered actual eye injuries.
- **Treasure maps with X marks the spot.** Pirates very rarely buried treasure — they spent it. The X-map is a Stevenson invention.
- **Walking the plank.** Mostly fictional. Pirates threw captives overboard, hanged them, or marooned them, but the ceremonial plank is later folklore.
- **Parrots and monkeys on shoulders.** Pet parrots and monkeys were absolutely traded by sailors — exotic-animal souvenirs were a real income stream — but they didn't ride around on shoulders during combat.
- **The Jolly Roger as universal flag.** Different captains flew different flags. The black flag with skull-and-crossbones existed; so did red flags, plain black flags, flags with hourglasses, flags with hearts dripping blood. Captains designed their own.
- **Single-shot pistols carried casually.** A single pistol means one shot, then a half-minute of vulnerability. Real boarders carried multiple pistols.

The narrator may use the following with confidence — these are real, not Hollywood:

- **Articles of agreement** — written contracts every pirate crew signed laying out shares, discipline, compensation for injuries.
- **Marooning punishments** — abandoning a man with a bottle of water, a pistol with one shot, and his weapons on a deserted cay.
- **The "Brotherhood of the Coast"** — loose informal solidarity among pirate crews; not a formal organization.
- **Treasure-fleet hauls in actual coin and unrefined silver bars.** Plate, jewelry, contraband Chinese porcelain.
- **Press gangs.** Royal Navy and merchant captains both press unwilling sailors when shorthanded.

## TIMING REFERENCES

Useful anchors so the narrator doesn't accidentally invent events before they happen.

- **Treaty of Utrecht / Rastatt:** 1713–1714. War over.
- **1715 Treasure Fleet wreck:** 30–31 July 1715. By story-open this is recent, raw, and the biggest news in the Caribbean.
- **Henry Jennings's raid on the salvage camp:** December 1715 / January 1716. Mentionable as imminent, in motion, or just occurred depending on the story's current date.
- **Edward Teach (Blackbeard):** Active under Hornigold in 1715. Has not yet taken his own captaincy or developed the famous beard-and-fuses persona — that comes 1717–1718.
- **Stede Bonnet:** Still a respectable Barbados planter in 1715. Goes to sea 1717.
- **Anne Bonny, Mary Read, Calico Jack Rackham:** Not yet pirates. 1718–1720 careers.
- **Woodes Rogers arrives in Nassau:** 1718. Three years away.
- **First Jacobite Rising in Scotland:** Autumn 1715. Distant news but real; affects what the Royal Navy is doing.

## NARRATIVE GUIDELINES

- **Render through detail, never lecture.** A scene says *"the silver had the Mexico City mint mark, freshly struck"* rather than *"this was treasure-fleet silver, which was identifiable because…"*
- **Period dialogue should sound like the regional voice of the speaker, not Hollywood.** A Bristol sailor speaks like a Bristol sailor; a Spanish official speaks his English with a Spanish cadence and limited vocabulary; an enslaved Igbo woman speaks the English she has been forced to learn, with the inflections of her own first language under it. Do not flatten everyone into pirate-monologue.
- **Money matters.** Prices should pinch. A doubloon is a fortune; a real is a daily wage. When Eriksohn spends, it should cost something.
- **Disease is always present.** Someone in any port scene is coughing, fevered, missing a man who is fevered, or drunk to avoid thinking about it.
- **Heat is always present.** Wool clothing in a tropical port is misery. Sweat is constant. Everyone smells.
- **The sea is always close.** Salt in the air, gulls overhead, the sound of rigging from down the hill, the chime of a ship's bell drifting up from the harbor.
- **Violence is fast and ugly.** A cutlass wound bleeds heavily; a pistol shot is loud and acrid; an infected wound kills slow. The world does not pause to memorialize the dead.
