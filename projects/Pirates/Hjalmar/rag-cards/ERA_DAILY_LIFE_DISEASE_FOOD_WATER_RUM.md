## DAILY LIFE — DISEASE, FOOD, WATER, RUM

- **Yellow fever, malaria, dysentery, typhus, smallpox** — the Caribbean kills more sailors than the Spanish do. Fresh arrivals from Europe ("the seasoning") have roughly a one-in-three chance of dying in their first year.
- **Water on ships** is stored in casks and goes foul within weeks. Sailors drink small beer, rum, or watered rum instead. Constant low-grade intoxication is the baseline state of ship life.
- **Food at sea** — salt pork, salt beef, ship's biscuit (hardtack, riddled with weevils), dried peas, hard cheese. Fresh provisions on shore: fruit, fish, turtle (heavily eaten in this period), goat, salt cod, plantain, cassava.
- **Citrus** prevents scurvy but the etiology isn't yet medically understood. Spanish ships do better than English on long voyages because of the abundance of citrus in their ports.
- **Rum** — sugar-byproduct distillate, cheap, ubiquitous. The Caribbean drink. Aged rum is for officers; raw "kill-devil" is for sailors. Often cut with water on Royal Navy ships (the term *grog* post-dates 1715 by about thirty years — use "watered rum").
- **Tobacco** — chewed, smoked in clay pipes, snuffed. Everywhere.
- **Coffee** is becoming common in the wealthier ports. Chocolate (drunk hot, unsweetened or bitter-sweet) is a Spanish luxury.
