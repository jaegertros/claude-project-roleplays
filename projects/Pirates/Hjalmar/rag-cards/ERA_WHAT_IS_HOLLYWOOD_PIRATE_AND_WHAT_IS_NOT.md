## WHAT IS HOLLYWOOD PIRATE AND WHAT IS NOT

The narrator avoids the following clichés:

- **"Arrr, matey" pirate speech.** This is a 20th-century invention (Robert Newton's 1950 Long John Silver). Sailors of 1715 speak in the regional accents of their origin: London, Bristol, Cornish, Scots, Irish, Welsh, Dutch, French, various Spanish, West African, Creole. Cant and slang are real (a "lurcher" is a swindler; "Davy Jones's locker" is documented; "splice the mainbrace" means a rum ration) but the cartoon "arrr" is anachronistic.
- **Eyepatches as fashion.** Eyepatches existed; they covered actual eye injuries.
- **Treasure maps with X marks the spot.** Pirates very rarely buried treasure — they spent it. The X-map is a Stevenson invention.
- **Walking the plank.** Mostly fictional. Pirates threw captives overboard, hanged them, or marooned them, but the ceremonial plank is later folklore.
- **Parrots and monkeys on shoulders.** Pet parrots and monkeys were absolutely traded by sailors — exotic-animal souvenirs were a real income stream — but they didn't ride around on shoulders during combat.
- **The Jolly Roger as universal flag.** Different captains flew different flags. The black flag with skull-and-crossbones existed; so did red flags, plain black flags, flags with hourglasses, flags with hearts dripping blood. Captains designed their own.
- **Single-shot pistols carried casually.** A single pistol means one shot, then a half-minute of vulnerability. Real boarders carried multiple pistols.

The narrator may use the following with confidence — these are real, not Hollywood:

- **Articles of agreement** — written contracts every pirate crew signed laying out shares, discipline, compensation for injuries.
- **Marooning punishments** — abandoning a man with a bottle of water, a pistol with one shot, and his weapons on a deserted cay.
- **The "Brotherhood of the Coast"** — loose informal solidarity among pirate crews; not a formal organization.
- **Treasure-fleet hauls in actual coin and unrefined silver bars.** Plate, jewelry, contraband Chinese porcelain.
- **Press gangs.** Royal Navy and merchant captains both press unwilling sailors when shorthanded.
