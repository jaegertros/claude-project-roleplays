# KNOWLEDGE_4_Hidden.md
## World secrets — what the narrator knows that nobody in the town entirely knows

This file holds the *world's* hidden information — distinct from `pc_secrets`, which holds Cael's hidden personal history. These are facts about Sankt Hjalmar that no single NPC fully holds, that can be discovered through investigation, and that the narrator may surface when player action and trigger conditions align.

Reveals require work. The narrator does not volunteer these secrets in passing dialogue. They surface when the player asks the right person the right question in the right setting — and even then, mostly in fragments.

---

## I. THE PLANTATION COUNCIL'S INTERNAL SPLIT

The Council has fifteen seated members. The split is not 14-1 with Hans Müller as the lone dissenter; the split is closer to 9-6, with the Rasmussens and their allies in the majority but the dissent organized and growing.

**The Rasmussen bloc (9):** Klaus Rasmussen, Erik Rasmussen (proxy-voting Klaus's seat when needed), three Rasmussen-related cousins on their own seats, two long-allied sugar families (the Krüger and the Storgaard households), and two minor planters who owe the Rasmussens money.

**The dissenting bloc (6):** Hans Müller and his Holstein-Danish faction (three seats), one English-descended planter family (the Pratts, a single seat), and two coffee-hills planters who do not want the proposed expansion of the slave market because they cannot match the sugar capital that would flow in.

**The contested issues:**

- **The slave-market expansion.** The Rasmussens want a larger pen and a regularized auction schedule that would bring more slavers through Sankt Hjalmar's harbor. The Müller bloc opposes for mixed reasons — practical (more sugar capital means less coffee capital, hurting them) and partly moral (Müller's late wife was a Hernhutter and made him uncomfortable on this question for thirty years; he has not entirely shed the discomfort).
- **The petition to London.** A faction within the Rasmussen bloc — *not* led by Klaus or Erik but by the Storgaards — quietly favors petitioning the British Crown to take Sankt Hjalmar formally under English protection in exchange for naval security against Spanish and Brotherhood incursion. This is treason against the Danish charter. Klaus does not know the Storgaards have written a letter. Erik does know; he has been keeping the knowledge in reserve.
- **The garrison expansion.** Klaus has been quietly pressing Magnus Bremer for a larger garrison. Bremer has declined, citing Selskab budget. Klaus suspects the Selskab is hiding profits to avoid the request; Bremer is in fact hiding profits, though for different reasons (see Section VII).

**The Tuesday meeting (Day 2):** Hans Müller will press the slave-market expansion vote and lose 9-6. The vote is publicly contested. The Storgaards will not show their hand on the petition question — they are waiting for a crisis that justifies the discussion.

**What the player can learn and how:**

- The basic 9-6 split is widely known in the upper town. Anyone in the boarding house network (Madame Jensen, her residents) can supply the headcount.
- The Storgaards' petition letter is known to: Erik, the Storgaards themselves, Hosea Penn (who printed a single copy as a fair-copy draft and was paid handsomely for discretion), and a Copenhagen lawyer named Vibe who has the original. Hosea Penn will not volunteer this knowledge. He will confirm it under specific pressure.
- The garrison/Selskab tension is visible to anyone who attends a Council meeting. Bremer's reasons for declining the garrison expansion are in Section VII.

## II. DON ESTEBAN'S SPANISH NETWORK

Don Esteban de Ribera is more than a wine merchant. He runs a paid network of seven informants in Sankt Hjalmar, reporting back to a viceregal intelligence office in San Juan through a courier system using the small Spanish coastal vessels that put in monthly.

**The informants (in order of value to Esteban):**

1. **Lauritz Knudsen** — Customs House. Monthly retainer of 1 escudo. Provides ship-clearance advance notice and manifest details on cargoes Esteban flags. Knudsen does not know who Esteban reports to; assumes him a private merchant with competitive interests.
2. **A waiter at Jensen's boarding house** — a young Spanish-speaking Catholic named Marco, paid 4 reales per useful overheard conversation. Reports on conversations at the upper-town tables.
3. **A market vendor named Inéz** (not to be confused with Doña Inés of El Yelmo) — runs a vegetable stall, paid 2 reales per significant item of gossip. Reliable on lower-town rumor.
4. **A garrison private named Lukas Schmidt** — 1 piece of eight per month. Reports on garrison readiness and any prisoner taken. Of limited value most months.
5. **A dockworker named Pieter** — 4 reales per useful item. Reports specifically on Brotherhood captain movements. Pieter occasionally also accepts work from Erik Rasmussen, who pays better but asks for different intelligence.
6. **A laundress at the Rasmussen estate's town house** — 4 reales per useful item. Reports on visitors and overheard family conversation.
7. **A boy at the print shop** — 2 reales per page-image Esteban specifically asks about. The boy is twelve, runs errands, can read enough to identify whose name appears on a manifest or a letter.

**What Esteban reports back to San Juan:**

- Brotherhood captain movements, prize cargo sales, vessel conditions.
- Council politics — Esteban has correctly identified the 9-6 split and the Storgaard petition. (The petition would be of interest to Madrid because it would deny Spain a future negotiating position over Sankt Hjalmar.)
- Specific bounty targets, of which Cael is currently the highest-priority active case.

**What Esteban does *not* know:**

- That his Pieter the dockworker is double-employed by Erik Rasmussen.
- That Father Joaquín Vega has heard, in confession, of his existence as more than a wine merchant.
- That Isaac de Lima's Curaçao correspondents have flagged him.
- That the Sephardim of the port consider him a danger.

**What the player can learn and how:**

- Marco the waiter can be caught listening. He will deny it to anyone but will eventually break under pressure.
- Inéz the vegetable seller is widely thought a gossip but not specifically identified as paid. Karin Holm at the next stall knows but has no reason to say.
- Pieter the dockworker can be caught by the right Brotherhood captain.
- The big reveal — that "Don Esteban the wine merchant" is a Spanish intelligence officer — requires either Father Joaquín being moved to speak obliquely, or de Lima being willing to share what his Curaçao office has flagged, or a Brotherhood captain (Maartens) having heard the same through his own channels.

## III. THE SEPHARDIC LEDGER

Isaac de Lima's counting house holds outstanding paper on twenty-three captains active in the eastern Caribbean. The portfolio is the densest informational asset in the port.

**The basic structure:** A captain borrows from de Lima at moderate rates — typically 8% to 12% per annum, depending on the security and the relationship — to outfit a voyage or purchase a vessel. The loan is repaid from prize money. de Lima keeps the paper.

**The implication:** de Lima knows, more precisely than anyone, which captains are solvent, which are skating, which are quietly fencing through Curaçao, and which are about to default. He shares none of this with the Selskab.

**Captains currently on de Lima's books with active loans:**

- **Cael Eriksohn** — roughly 600 pieces of eight outstanding from the past two cruises; the *Whisht* came back in poor condition in August and has not generated repayment income since.
- **Pieter Maartens** — fully paid up after a successful cruise last winter.
- **Three Brotherhood captains** Cael does not know personally, two of them currently at sea, one wintering in Nassau.
- **Six merchant captains** running Curaçao-Sankt Hjalmar-Charlotte Amalie circuits.
- **Eleven smaller debts** from one-time outfitters, individual sailors, two Council families.

**The internal information advantage:**

- de Lima knows the Storgaards have a Copenhagen lawyer named Vibe. He has not connected this to the Crown petition; he assumes a private commercial matter. The connection would tell him a great deal.
- de Lima knows Don Esteban's name has been flagged by his Curaçao office but he does not know precisely why.
- de Lima knows Cael's *Whisht* is on the wharf in poor condition and that Cael paid off his crew in August. de Lima has not pressed because Cael's prior reliability earned grace. The grace runs out around the end of October.

**What the player can learn and how:**

- A direct conversation with de Lima about the *Whisht*'s paper produces a frank summary of the debt.
- A direct conversation about other captains produces nothing — de Lima does not gossip about clients.
- A serious commercial proposition (a joint venture to retire the debt, the sale of useful intelligence, a sponsorship of a particular voyage) will produce attention but not internal information.
- A relationship of confidence over multiple visits will produce information by degrees.

## IV. THE MISSION'S CONFESSIONAL

Father Joaquín Vega has heard seventeen years of confessions from the Spanish remnant, the Catholic free Black community, and various Catholic transient sailors. He cannot violate the seal. He can, however, ask oblique questions and speak in generalities that point a careful listener toward what he knows.

**Specific things Father Vega has heard:**

- Agnes Rasmussen was pregnant in 1714. (From the servant Else, who confessed her own suspicion of her mistress's morning sickness.)
- "Don Esteban" is more than a wine merchant. (From a Spanish-speaking informant of Esteban's, who confessed unease at the work.)
- The Rasmussen brig was raided in the Mona Passage in December 1714 and Frederik was killed by what at least one survivor believed was a deliberate pistol-ball from the captain of the raiding sloop. (From a survivor who became a Sankt Hjalmar resident and converted to Catholicism in penance.)
- The Storgaards' Copenhagen lawyer has been in correspondence regarding a petition. (From a Storgaard household servant who is Catholic.)
- A man was attacked behind the Helm three nights before the campaign opens. (From a Catholic dockhand who witnessed Stickjaw and his fellows dragging the body. The dockhand did not intervene and confessed his cowardice the next morning.)

**What Father Vega will do:**

- He will *not* repeat any of these specifics aloud.
- He will engage Cael in a conversation about, for example, "the danger of secrets concealed for love rather than gain" — and let Cael decide whether the conversation is about Cael's own situation.
- He will pray with anyone who asks. He will refuse to pray with Don Esteban.
- He will, in an emergency, send a sealed letter to the bishop in Puerto Rico. He has not done so in two years.

**What the player can learn and how:**

- Father Vega can be sought out for confession (Cael is not Catholic but anyone can speak privately with the priest). The conversation will produce no specifics; it may produce questions Cael did not know to ask.
- A *Catholic* NPC the player has cultivated may pass between the player and the priest a question that the priest can frame an answer to without violating the seal.
- The priest will speak more directly about the political situation (the Storgaard letter, Esteban's nature) than about personal matters (Agnes, the brig raid) if pressed — political knowledge is not strictly confessional.

## V. LEFÈVRE'S CUSTOMER LIST

The apothecary keeps no formal records — he is professional about that — but his memory is excellent and his categorization is precise.

**Recurring customers and their staple purchases:**

- **Agnes Rasmussen** — laudanum, in modest quantities, the past six months. She is not addicted; she sleeps poorly. (Lefèvre suspects what happened to her at the convent. He does not say.)
- **Klaus Rasmussen** — cinchona bark for an intermittent fever he has had since 1711, and a tincture for the trembling of his left hand which he now collects monthly.
- **Erik Rasmussen** — nothing personally. Sends a servant for the household supplies.
- **Don Esteban de Ribera** — sleeping draughts (occasional), a specific Andalusian-style preparation for joint stiffness, and — twice in the past two years — a quantity of arsenic for "rat control at the warehouse." Lefèvre believes the rat-control story exactly as much as one believes it ought to be believed.
- **Margriet (the Helm)** — herbal preparations for hangovers, for cuts, for the occasional wound she does not want a surgeon's name attached to.
- **Søren Lind's wife Hilde** — a recurrent stomach complaint that has worsened in the past three months. (Lefèvre suspects the stress of her husband's secret. He does not say.)
- **Father Joaquín Vega** — basic herbal preparations for the mission's poor, paid for in small donations.
- **Captain Maartens** — preparations for shipboard infections, gum disease, and one quiet purchase of mercurial preparation for an officer's case of pox.
- **The Madsen family wet-nurse on St. Thomas** — through a chain of intermediaries; small herbal preparations for an infant's colic. (Lefèvre does not know whose child. The order goes through a Sankt Hjalmar agent who pays in advance.)
- **An unknown buyer at the Three Anchors** — paid for laudanum and a small lock-picking set together in February 1715. Lefèvre remembers the face and the transaction; he does not know the name.

**What the player can learn and how:**

- Lefèvre does not volunteer customer information. He will, however, answer specific questions if the asker pays for the information (typical price: 1 piece of eight per question about a specific customer's habits).
- Lefèvre will refuse to answer questions about customers who are paying him for discretion (Agnes, Don Esteban, Hilde Lind).
- Lefèvre will answer questions about Klaus Rasmussen's health to anyone who pays — Klaus is not paying for discretion and Lefèvre treats him as a routine professional matter.

## VI. THE MAROON NETWORK

Madre Joana's settlement of seventy people survives because (a) the Selskab does not pay for punitive expeditions over difficult ground, and (b) certain townspeople trade with the Maroons for things the town does not officially permit.

**Active trading relationships (town to settlement):**

- **Eliza Free** — herbal preparations going both directions; she is the most regular bridge.
- **Joseph Tide** — quietly sells damaged barrel stock to Madre Joana's people for storage of dried meat and water.
- **Big Anna** — repairs blades brought down from the settlement; sometimes sends up scrap iron she does not need.
- **Petter Halvorsen** — when his catch is good, gives a portion to a Maroon contact through Eliza.
- **A free Black sailor named Caleb Mason** (no relation to the protagonist) — runs occasional smuggled gunpowder up the highland trail. Last delivery was August.
- **Ezekiel Boatswain** — provides a small annual donation in coin from the Methodist meeting's collections.

**What Madre Joana's settlement has:**

- A working community with kitchen gardens, goat herding, smoke-curing, weaving.
- Roughly thirty men and women capable of fighting if they had to. Six of them are former Brotherhood sailors who escaped both slavery and the sea.
- A cache of about thirty muskets, twelve pistols, and a few barrels of powder — supplied over the past four years through various channels including, in 1714, a single purchase from the *Whisht*'s prior cargo (Cael arranged this through an intermediary; Madre Joana never met him directly).
- Three hidden paths from the settlement to the leeward beach, one to the eastern cove, one over the central ridge to the windward side.
- A lookout system at three approaches to the main valley.

**What the player can learn and how:**

- The settlement's existence is open knowledge in the town. Its location is rough knowledge.
- The trading network is hidden but discoverable through any of the named intermediaries.
- The 1714 *Whisht* rifle deal is locked behind a specific reveal: Cael speaking the phrase "the rifles in 1714" in Madre Joana's hearing.
- The lookout system is invisible until the player actually approaches the settlement.

## VII. THE SELSKAB'S BOOKS

The chartered company's official quarterly figures, audited annually in Copenhagen, are not the figures Governor Magnus Bremer actually keeps.

**The hidden numbers:**

- Sankt Hjalmar's harbor and customs revenue for the past three quarters has exceeded the Selskab's reported income by approximately 18%. The difference is held in a separate ledger by Bremer personally.
- The unreported revenue is being used by Bremer for two purposes, both of which he considers honorable:
  - A discretionary fund for emergencies that the Copenhagen Selskab would refuse — yellow fever outbreaks, hurricane damage to the lower town, replacement of Customs House equipment when the Selskab refuses requisitions.
  - A quiet pension fund for the four longest-serving Danish residents of Sankt Hjalmar — including Petter Halvorsen, who Bremer respects — paid in small quarterly amounts they receive without knowing the source.
- Bremer also draws a small personal allowance for his own household above his stipend. Roughly 2 doubloons per quarter. He considers this proportionate to thirty-eight years of colonial service. The Selskab, if it knew, would consider it embezzlement.

**What the player can learn and how:**

- Magnus Bremer's personal ledger is in a locked drawer in his study at Government House. A skilled house-breaker could find it. The implications would be politically substantial — the Selskab would replace Bremer immediately.
- A conversation with Bremer over Madeira, after he has been governor for thirty-eight years and is tired, might surface the existence of the discretionary fund without details.
- One of the four pensioners (Petter Halvorsen most likely) could be persuaded to confirm receiving quiet money from a source he does not name. Petter would not betray the source if asked directly.

## VIII. THE SLAVE-PEN BOOK

The Customs House holds a clothbound register noting every enslaved person who has passed through Sankt Hjalmar's pen since 1709. Knudsen maintains it. The Selskab requires it.

**What the book records:**

- Date of arrival, name of vessel, port of origin, number of persons, sex breakdown, ages (estimates).
- Date of departure, name of vessel, port of destination, number sold locally vs through-passed.
- Buyer names for local sales (these are the records the Plantation Council would prefer not to scrutinize).
- Death records for those who did not survive the pen.

**What the book does *not* record:**

- The names of the enslaved people themselves. (They are entered as numbers.)
- Acts of violence within the pen. (Knudsen does not record what he does not see, and he arranges not to see.)
- Names of African origin. (Some enslaved people were entered with European-imposed Christian names; many were entered with no name at all.)

**The book includes one specific record relevant to the campaign:**

- **August 1696, vessel *La Esperanza* out of Bonny, 47 persons taken aboard, 31 surviving the passage:** a six-year-old girl listed as #14, sold locally to "H. Müller, Tre Kilder." This is the entry for the woman who would later, under a different name, become Madre Joana.

If Madre Joana ever learned that the book exists and that her own purchase is recorded in it, she would want the book burned. Hans Müller does not know that the woman who escaped his estate in 1701 is currently leading the Maroon settlement that the Council periodically discusses destroying.

**What the player can learn and how:**

- Knudsen will produce the book to anyone with official authority or to anyone who pays well. (He considers it routine business; he does not connect any individual entry to a current person.)
- The connection between entry #14 and Madre Joana exists only in the head of one person — Mette Madsen on St. Thomas, who briefly knew the young woman before her escape and would recognize her if they met again.

## IX. THE PRINT SHOP ARCHIVE

Hosea Penn keeps every page he has ever printed, in order, in a locked chest in his back room. The archive is the most complete record of paid printing in the eastern Caribbean.

**Contents include:**

- All issues of the *Sankt Hjalmar Avis* since 1710.
- Bills of lading printed for captains who paid for clean copies.
- Auction broadsides for cargo sales, including the *Whisht*'s December 1714 sale at Curaçao (printed locally to Hosea's order through a Curaçao correspondent, copies stored here).
- Three handbills printed for the Selskab in 1713 announcing harbor-fee changes.
- One fair copy of the Storgaard petition to the Crown (printed July 1715, paid for at a substantial rate including a discretion fee). The original Hosea returned to the Storgaards; he kept the proof.
- Various private commissions — wedding programs, funeral cards, an Anglican prayer book Hosea was paid to print for a Lutheran patron who collected unusual editions.

**What this archive can prove:**

- The Storgaard petition exists in physical form within the town. This is treason against the Danish charter.
- The *Whisht*'s December 1714 raid was followed by a cargo sale through Curaçao — the broadside identifies the cargo, the date, and the broker.
- Various official harbor-fee figures over the years, against which the Selskab's current figures can be checked. (See Section VII.)

**Hosea Penn's discipline:**

- He does not volunteer the archive's contents.
- He will not lie about what is in it.
- He has, exactly twice in five years, refused to produce a specific item to a paying inquirer — once for the Storgaards, once for the bishop's office in Puerto Rico. He cited Quaker conscience both times.

**What the player can learn and how:**

- The existence of the archive is public knowledge.
- Access requires either Hosea's cooperation (he will produce what is not under his discretion-refusal lock) or a break-in (the chest is locked; Lefèvre has the right tools for sale).
- Hosea's discretion-locked items are: the Storgaard petition proof and one private letter from the bishop's office to a Catholic Council family.

## NARRATOR USAGE

The narrator does not unfurl these secrets unprompted. Each one has a discovery path; each path requires player action. The narrator may sprinkle small consistencies — Hilde Lind's stomach complaint visible when the player visits the chandlery and she steps out, Marco the waiter glancing too long at an upper-town dinner conversation — that hint at the deeper structure without exposing it.

When a player commits to a theory about any of these secrets, the narrator updates the Theory Ledger. Confirmed-in-world status is reached when player action surfaces evidence the narrator considers conclusive — a found document, an NPC speaking on the record, a physical object whose origin admits one explanation.
