# KNOWLEDGE_2_Locations.md
## Sankt Hjalmar — the island, the town, and everything beyond the harbor wall

This is the geography reference. Every place named here has been thought through twice: once for sensory texture, once for what kind of trouble lives there. The narrator pulls from this file when the player goes somewhere — to find the right smell, the right sounds, the right proprietor, the right tension waiting under the floorboards.

---

## THE ISLAND IN OUTLINE

**Sankt Hjalmar** is a volcanic island roughly eighteen square miles in area, oriented northeast-to-southwest, in the eastern Caribbean east of Puerto Rico and northwest of the Lesser Antilles chain. It is officially a possession of Denmark-Norway, administered under charter by the Sankt Hjalmar Selskab — a small chartered company answering to Copenhagen but in practice answering to the harbor fees that keep it solvent.

- **Shape:** A rough teardrop, the broad end to the northeast, the tail to the southwest. A central ridge of forested volcanic hills runs the length of the island, climbing to roughly 2,400 feet at the peak (Hjalmars Top — uninhabited, cloud-wreathed half the year).
- **The harbor:** A deep, southwest-facing crescent bay on the leeward side, sheltered by the bulk of the island from the prevailing trade winds. The mouth of the harbor is flanked by two stone headlands — the **Spanish Headland** (south, taller, capped by ruined Fort Concepción) and the **Danish Headland** (north, lower, capped by the working Christiansborg Blockhouse).
- **The reefs:** Coral reefs surround roughly half the island's perimeter — concentrated on the windward (eastern) and southern coasts. The harbor approach is reef-free; the wreck-strewn coasts elsewhere have killed more captains than the garrison has. Local pilots know three back-channels through the reefs that strangers do not.
- **Climate:** Tropical maritime. Steady northeasterly trade winds. Hurricane season June through November. The leeward (southwest) side is drier; the windward (northeast) side catches the rain and feeds the plantation valleys.
- **Population:** Roughly 2,500 in the town itself; roughly 6,000 on the island total. Of the island population, a clear majority are enslaved Africans working the windward plantations. Free residents include Danish-Norwegian administrators and shopkeepers, Sephardic merchants, mixed-European trade families, a small Spanish remnant, free Black townspeople, and a transient sailor population that swells and shrinks with the tide of ships in port.

## THE TOWN — DISTRICTS

Sankt Hjalmar Town climbs the hillside in three rough terraces north of the harbor wall. Older Spanish stucco-and-tile buildings cluster at the lower terraces; Danish half-timber and brick dominate the upper. The streets are unpaved cobblestone where they are anything; the rains turn the lower lanes to slurry in October.

### Wharf Side (the Lower Town)

The harbor edge and the first terrace above it. Tar, rope, fish, urine, rum, sweat, blood, gunpowder, hot metal from the smithy, woodsmoke. Loud at all hours. The town's transient population — sailors on shore leave, dockworkers, prostitutes, gamblers, runners, fixers, fences — lives here and conducts its business here.

- **The Helm Tavern.** The biggest of three wharf-side public houses, named for the slurred Anglo pronunciation of the island. Long low room, packed earth floor, a single trestle bar against the back wall, four tables, a fireplace that is rarely used in this climate. The keeper is a heavy Frisian woman called Margriet who pretends to less English than she has. The Helm's clientele is mixed — Danish administrators come for the imported beer, Brotherhood sailors come for the rum, the merchants' clerks come for both. The alley behind the Helm runs forty yards back to the wharf edge between two warehouses; it is unlit at night and ends at deep water. Things happen in this alley that the Helm pretends not to know about.

- **The Three Anchors.** Smaller, rougher, Brotherhood-aligned. Wooden bar, sawdust floor, a side door onto the back lots. The keeper is a one-eyed Cornishman called Tregenza who lost the eye and his ship in the same action in 1709 and now watches the door of his tavern like he is still expecting boarders. The Three Anchors will harbor a man in trouble for one night without asking questions; on the second night the questions begin.

- **El Yelmo.** The smallest of the three, on the south side near the old Spanish fountain. Spanish remnant and Free Quarter clientele. The keeper, an old woman called Doña Inés, makes a peppery rum punch with sour orange that the rest of the town considers a curiosity and her own people consider a sacrament. Conversation here is in Spanish or in the Spanish-inflected English of the Free Quarter. Strangers are noticed.

- **Lind's Chandlery.** Three doors down from the Helm. Coiled rope, kegs of pitch, ironwork, lanterns, sailcloth, twine, blocks and tackle, slow matches in oilcloth, powder horns, the smell of tar and linseed oil. The proprietor — **Søren Lind** — is a lean Danish man in his late thirties with weathered hands and a tattoo just visible above his shirt collar. He is unfailingly polite and remembers every face. He is more than a chandler; he is also a man with a past at sea who has chosen to deny it on dry land. (See KNOWLEDGE_1_NPCs.md.)

- **The Careenage.** A shallow tongue of beach at the south end of the wharf where ships are hauled out, tipped on their sides, and scraped clean of weed and worms. Always at least one vessel out of the water, exposed, vulnerable. The smell here is of rotted seaweed, hot tar, and stripped barnacle.

- **The Fish Market.** A long open-sided shed at the north end of the wharf. Operates from before dawn until midmorning. Mostly women run the stalls — Danish, Spanish, free Black, Sephardic, a handful of Cherokee-Carib mixed traders down from the Lesser Antilles. The information traffic in this shed is the densest in the town. Karin Holm — Stickjaw Holm's sister — runs the eel and conch stall on the seaward end.

- **The Slave Pen.** A walled compound at the back of the wharf, behind the Customs House. Used when a slaver puts in, otherwise empty and quiet. The walls are stained. The narrator does not mention it casually but does not pretend it is not there.

- **The Customs House.** A stone-faced two-story building at the head of the wharf. The harbormaster's office is on the second floor; the ground floor is a goods-inspection hall with long tables and oil-lamps. The harbormaster is **Lauritz Knudsen**, Danish, mid-fifties, polite and corruptible in measured doses. Above his desk hangs a framed engraving of King Frederik IV that he has never looked at in seven years of service.

### Merchant Row (the Middle Town)

The second terrace, climbing the hill. Brick countinghouses, shopfronts with painted signs, the Sephardic quarter, the better lodging houses. Cleaner streets, fewer drunks, more business done in writing.

- **Christianstorv.** The market square at the head of Merchant Row — paved with imported cobblestone, ringed with arcades. A stone fountain in the center, of obscure Spanish-era origin, the saint atop it weathered featureless. Market days are Tuesday and Friday; on those mornings the square fills with planters' produce, livestock, imported cloth, ironwork, fish, fruit, and rumor. The pillory still stands at the south edge of the square; it is used roughly four times a year.

- **De Lima & Sons, Counting House.** A narrow brick building on the east side of Merchant Row, two stories, stone steps, a brass plate beside the door. Inside: dark wood, lamp-light, the smell of beeswax and ink and the faint salt of marine documents. **Isaac de Lima** is the senior partner — Sephardic, fluent in five languages, the most carefully informed man in the town. De Lima & Sons holds paper on more captains than the Company does. Loans go out, ships come back; sometimes ships do not come back, and the paper stays out. Isaac does not press; he waits.

- **The Mahamad House.** The Sephardic prayer house, on a side street off Merchant Row — a converted Spanish merchant's house with whitewashed walls, a small inner courtyard, a wooden ark at the eastern wall holding two Torah scrolls brought from Amsterdam in 1692. Twenty-three Sephardic households in town; the Mahamad House sees most of them on Friday evenings. The hazzan is a soft-voiced man called Ribera (no relation to the Spanish Ribera — the surname is common). The community is small, multilingual, financially central, and very careful.

- **Jensen's Boarding House.** A clean three-story building above a tailor's shop. Rooms by the night, the week, the month. The proprietor — Madame Jensen, Danish, fifty, widowed — does not ask questions but does enforce a strict no-firearms-discharged-on-the-premises rule, learned the hard way in 1711.

- **The Apothecary.** Two doors down from De Lima's. Run by a half-Dutch, half-French apothecary called Lefèvre, who keeps an unusually large stock of laudanum, mercurial preparations, ground cinchona bark (quinine), and certain stronger powders that he sells only to known customers. He speaks of his stock in a low precise voice and writes down nothing.

- **The Print Shop.** The town's single press, run by an English Quaker called Hosea Penn, prints the weekly broadsheet (the *Sankt Hjalmar Avis*) in Danish and English, plus broadsides, bills of lading, and the occasional handbill. Hosea is the closest thing the town has to a newspaperman; he prints what he is paid to print and remembers the rest.

### Government Hill (the Upper Town)

The top terrace, climbing to the crest of the hill above the town. Wider streets, larger lots, walled gardens. The Danish administration lives and works here.

- **Government House.** The Selskab's headquarters and the governor's residence — a two-story Danish colonial building of yellow stucco over brick, with deep wraparound verandas on both floors and a red tile roof. The governor is **Magnus Bremer**, Danish-Norwegian, mid-sixties, a former naval officer who lost the use of his left arm at the Battle of Køge Bay in 1677 and has been gently administering colonies ever since. He is fair, slow, and absolutely uninterested in disrupting the arrangement that keeps Sankt Hjalmar profitable.

- **Christianskirke.** The Lutheran church — small, wooden, painted white, with a single bell-tower visible from the harbor. Pastor Søren Bagger preaches in Danish on Sunday mornings to a congregation of perhaps eighty. The church holds no jurisdiction over anyone who does not choose to be in it.

- **Fort Christiansborg / The Blockhouse.** The working Danish fort on the north headland — small, square, walls of cut volcanic stone, four guns facing the harbor mouth and four facing the sea approach. Garrisoned by twenty-eight Danish-Norwegian soldiers under Captain Holger Vinter. The garrison includes one corporal who will become important — **Tomás O'Brien**, Charleston-born Irish-Spanish mixed-heritage, transferred to the Danish service four years ago. He drinks at the Helm on his off-evenings and watches faces.

- **The Garrison Barracks.** Adjacent to the fort, half-timber on stone, sleeping quarters for the soldiers, a small parade ground, an armory with the bulk of the Danish powder stores. The garrison is undermanned for any serious crisis; everyone in the upper town knows it.

- **Government Square.** A small paved square between Government House and the church, with a single mahogany tree at the center. Public proclamations are read here. So is the occasional sentence of court.

### The Spanish Quarter (the Old Town)

A smaller district below Merchant Row, west of the wharf — the remnant of the original Spanish settlement that predated the Danish charter. Stucco-and-tile architecture, narrow stepped streets, deep shaded courtyards, the smell of incense and chickens and old stone.

- **La Misión de San Yelmo.** The old Spanish mission — a small stone church with a beaten-copper cross, a walled garden, a single bell. Father **Joaquín Vega**, Franciscan, Mexican-born, in his early sixties, has held the mission for seventeen years. He says Mass on Sunday mornings in Latin to a congregation of perhaps forty — Spanish remnant families, the Catholic free Black families, occasional Catholic sailors in port. He is older than he looks, more political than he sounds, and the Spanish remnant's quiet eyes and ears.

- **The Spanish Fountain.** A working stone fountain in a small plaza behind the mission, the only freshwater source in the lower town that is not piped from the upper springs. Women carry water from here at dawn and at dusk. Conversation in Spanish, free Black creole, and broken Danish flows around the fountain in equal measure.

- **The Old Tavern (El Yelmo).** Listed above with the wharf taverns. Spiritually it belongs to the Spanish Quarter.

### The Free Quarter

Strictly speaking not a separate district but a loose constellation of households at the western edge of the town, where free and emancipated Black families have settled over the past two decades. Brick and wood houses, kitchen gardens, smithies, a cooperage, a small Methodist meeting house (no proper pastor; the elder runs it) tolerated unevenly by the Lutheran establishment.

- **The Cooperage.** Run by **Joseph Tide**, a free Black master cooper, formerly enslaved on a Barbados plantation, self-purchased in 1703, in Sankt Hjalmar since 1709. Builds and repairs barrels for the rum trade, the salt-fish trade, and the water-cask trade. Joseph knows every captain who buys his barrels and every captain whose barrels leak.

- **The Smithy.** Run by **Big Anna**, a free Black woman, formerly enslaved on a Saint-Domingue sugar plantation, escaped 1701, in Sankt Hjalmar since 1705. Iron and steelwork — horseshoes, knives, fittings, repairs. Strong, quiet, watchful. Will repair a knife without asking how it came to be notched.

- **The Methodist Meeting House.** A single-room wooden building with a packed earth floor and benches. The elder is **Ezekiel Boatswain**, a free Black former sailor, in his fifties, who took the call after surviving a wreck off the Mona Passage in 1707. Holds services Wednesday evenings and Sunday afternoons. Reads the Bible in English with a Carolina cadence.

## THE PLANTATION BELT (Windward Valleys)

North and east of the central ridge, where the trade winds drop the rain. Sugar in the broader valleys, tobacco on the slopes, coffee on the higher ground. Twelve major plantations, three minor ones. The plantation belt is connected to the town by a single road that runs from Christianstorv north over the ridge and then forks east and west to serve the valleys.

The plantations are worked by roughly 4,200 enslaved Africans. Conditions vary by family — some are managed with the cold calculating cruelty of pure profit; others (a small minority) are slightly less brutal because the family considers itself enlightened. Either way the mortality rate is high. The Plantation Council meets monthly at Government House.

- **The Rasmussen Estate (Trefoldighed — "Trinity").** The largest of the sugar plantations, in the broadest of the windward valleys, four miles from town by the eastern fork. The estate house is a two-story Danish colonial structure of white stucco with green shutters, surrounded by gardens of frangipani and bougainvillea. The Rasmussens — **Klaus Rasmussen** (patriarch, mid-sixties, increasingly fragile), **Erik Rasmussen** (eldest son, thirty-four, ruthless and effective), **Agnes Rasmussen** (daughter, twenty-one, quiet and watching), and several younger cousins — dominate the Plantation Council. The estate runs on the labor of roughly 380 enslaved people. The internal politics of the family are complicated and not unanimous.

- **The Müller Estate (Tre Kilder — "Three Springs").** Second-largest, two valleys over. Coffee and sugar. The Müllers are old Holstein-Danish, less politically active than the Rasmussens, considered slightly more humane in the management of their people (a faint distinction). Patriarch is **Hans Müller**, sixty, who broke with the Rasmussens on the Council last year over the proposed expansion of the slave markets.

- **The Smaller Plantations.** Ten others, each smaller, several owned by absentee planters who live in Copenhagen and have never seen the island. Their overseers form their own loose informal community of cruelty and resentment.

- **The Coffee Hills.** The upper ground of the windward side — smaller holdings, mostly worked by enslaved and free labor mixed, owned by middling families who do not sit on the Council and who would benefit politically from the Rasmussens' decline.

- **The Eastern Cove (Skæringsbugten).** A small windward bay on the far side of the island, reef-flanked, accessible only by experienced pilots. The Brotherhood sometimes uses this cove for quiet careenings and for landing goods that have not been declared at the Customs House. The road to the cove is a foot-track only.

## THE HIGHLANDS (The Bush)

The forested volcanic interior of the island — uncultivated, roadless, considered worthless by the Selskab and therefore the de facto territory of others. Steep ravines, dense second-growth forest, freshwater streams, wild fruit, feral hogs descended from Spanish stock, dangerous footing in the rains.

- **The Maroon Settlement (no Danish name; the residents call it Lugar Quieto — "Quiet Place").** A community of roughly seventy escaped enslaved people, living in a high valley on the northern slope of the central ridge. Wooden houses, kitchen gardens, goats, a freshwater spring, lookouts at three approaches. The community has existed since 1697 and survives because (a) the Selskab does not want to spend money on a punitive expedition over difficult ground, and (b) certain residents of the town quietly trade with them — for hides, herbs, smoke-cured meat, and information. The leader is a woman called **Madre Joana**, Kongo-born, formerly enslaved at the Müller estate, escaped 1701.

- **Eliza Free's Cottage.** A single-room house on the upper edge of the Maroon settlement, set apart from the main cluster. Eliza Free is part of the community but lives slightly apart by preference. She is the community's healer and its connection to certain quiet visitors from the town. Her cottage is where Cael spent the two days he does not remember.

- **The Hjalmars Top Trail.** A goat-track up the central ridge to the cloud-wreathed peak. Used by Maroons, by occasional planters' hunting parties (who do not climb to the top), and by no one else. Springs along the way.

- **The Old Spanish Stones.** Halfway up the southern slope, three carved stones of obscure origin — pre-Spanish, possibly Taíno. Marked with spiraling figures and one upright stone twice as tall as a man. The Spanish remnant considers them haunted; the Maroons do not.

## THE SEA APPROACHES

- **The Harbor Mouth.** Half a mile across between the two headlands, three to four fathoms over a sand-and-coral bottom in the channel proper. Two stone navigation marks (whitewashed cairns) on the headlands. The Blockhouse's guns can range the channel; Fort Concepción's guns are spiked, fallen, or rusted into uselessness.

- **The Reef Belt.** Surrounds the windward and southern coasts. Three known channels through it — **Petter's Cut** (north, named for the Halvorsen family of fishermen who have used it for two generations), **The Spanish Channel** (east, marked on Spanish charts that the Selskab does not own copies of), and **The Whisht Channel** (south, named after a sloop that ran through it in heavy weather in 1714 and is remembered for it). All three require local knowledge.

- **The Leeward Beach (Strand Vest).** A two-mile crescent of white sand on the southwestern shore, west of the harbor headland, accessible from the town by a footpath over the headland. Used by fishermen, by women collecting shellfish at low tide, and by people who wish to come or go from Sankt Hjalmar without being recorded by the Customs House. **Petter Halvorsen** keeps his boat here.

- **Fort Concepción (Spanish Ruin).** The half-ruined Spanish fortification on the southern headland — built 1581, abandoned during a hurricane in 1672, never reoccupied. Stone walls partly collapsed, the gun emplacements empty, the cisterns broken. A favored spot for trysts, for duels, and for meetings that should not be witnessed. The view from the parapet covers the harbor mouth and the western sea horizon.

## OFF-MAP LOCATIONS THE NARRATOR SHOULD KNOW

Locations the player may travel to during the campaign or that exert pressure from a distance:

- **Charlotte Amalie, St. Thomas.** The other Danish-Norwegian colony, four days' sail to the northwest. A larger, busier free port. Contains a convent (the Convent of Our Lady of Sorrow) and, in a farming valley behind the town, a particular family who care for a particular child.

- **Nassau, New Providence.** The Republic of Pirates, ten days' sail north-northwest. Several captains and crews known to Cael have wintered there in the past two years.

- **San Juan, Puerto Rico.** The nearest major Spanish port, two days' sail to the west. Don Esteban's correspondents and pay-masters operate from there.

- **Charleston, Carolina.** Three to four weeks' sail north. Cael's birthplace. People there still remember the killing of Constable O'Brien in 1711.

- **The 1715 Treasure Fleet Wreck (Florida coast).** Roughly seven days' sail north-northwest. A Spanish salvage camp is on the beach; treasure is being recovered; raiders are already organizing.

- **The Mosquito Coast.** Ten to twelve days' sail to the west. Jens Eriksohn died there. Cael has not returned since.

## NARRATIVE GUIDELINES FOR LOCATIONS

- **Each location is at least three senses deep.** A scene set in the Helm should smell like the Helm, sound like the Helm, look like the Helm. No location is rendered in plain prose.
- **Population is present.** Even in quiet locations, the narrator names at least one specific person who is there — a fish-seller, a drunk in the corner, a child running an errand. The town is not empty.
- **Distances matter.** The walk from the wharf to Government Hill is twenty minutes uphill. The ride to the Rasmussen estate is an hour and a half. The trek to the Maroon settlement is half a day. Time spent traveling is time the narrator advances.
- **Geography enables and constrains.** The narrator uses location: the alley behind the Helm is a place where you can be jumped; the leeward beach is a place where you can vanish from the town; the Maroon settlement is a place from which the town cannot reach you quickly.
- **Hidden hooks belong to specific places.** When a narrator query against `pc_secrets` returns a fact tied to a person, that person lives somewhere on this map. The two files (Locations and pc_secrets) are designed to braid: pull the secret, find the place, find the person, write the scene.
