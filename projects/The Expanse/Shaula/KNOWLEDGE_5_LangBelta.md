# KNOWLEDGE_5 — Lang Belta

**Project:** `shaula`
**Spoiler weight:** Low. Linguistic seasoning. Functions throughout the arc.
**Audience:** The narrator. Reference for NPC speech and protagonist comprehension growth.

---

## What this file is and is not

A working lexicon and register guide for Belter creole (Lang Belta) as it appears in the project's spoken texture. The narrator uses it to season Belter NPC dialogue authentically and to honor the protagonist's evolving comprehension over the arc.

This file does **not** function as an anachronism flinch-mechanic. The protagonist is not punished for using Inner idiom. The protagonist's saying *"phone"* instead of *"hand terminal"* does not warrant narration. The Vault's anachronism layer is *inverted* here — Lang Belta is the period vocabulary the protagonist *hears*, parses, and gradually picks up. He does not get headaches from it. His comprehension is the texture, not a penalty.

Per template Section 2.10's spirit (the anachronism mechanic): **the marked register is the speech-form of others, not a flinch in the protagonist's prose.** What the narrator does flag — judiciously — is the *register switch* itself: when a Belter NPC drops into Lang Belta with another Belter and excludes the Inners present; when a Belter NPC stays in standard with the protagonist longer than expected (a sign of distance); when an Inner is unexpectedly using a Lang Belta phrase (signal of past Belt time, or affectation, or something earned).

## 1. Linguistic background

Lang Belta is a working creole, developed across two centuries of Belter settlement. Source-language stock: Russian, Esperanto, Korean, Chinese, Portuguese, Spanish, French, Dutch, German, Italian, Hungarian, Turkish, Arabic, Swahili. Vocabulary is heterogeneous — a sentence may pull from four source languages — and grammar is loose, contextual, gesture-augmented (Belter Sign Language complements the spoken register, especially in vac suits).

Older or educated Belters speak several languages fluently and choose Lang Belta deliberately. Younger Belters often speak it as their primary language and less fluently command standard English. Belters speaking standard with Inners is a courtesy; Belters dropping into Lang Belta with each other in front of Inners is a closure of the conversational circle — sometimes friendly intimacy, sometimes pointed exclusion.

The protagonist does not speak Lang Belta on Day 0. He starts to recognize phrases by Day 2. By Day 5–6 he parses common patterns. By Day 10 he can produce a few phrases competently if he has tried. The narrator should grade his comprehension over the arc accordingly — he hears more by the end than he hears at the start, even with the same speakers using the same words.

## 2. Core working lexicon

The narrator does not need to be a Lang Belta scholar — pepper, don't lecture. Below is the working vocabulary the project draws on, organized for narrator scan-ability. Sources: the Nick Farmer-developed Lang Belta phrasebook (canonical), the Expanse RPG core book Chapter 10, and show/novel canon. Where these conflict, the phrasebook wins.

### Greetings, addresses, and closures
- **Oye** — *hello, hi*. The phrasebook-canonical greeting.
- **Hoy** — *hey, hello*. Show-canon variant; both are in use, *hoy* is slightly more casual.
- **Wa koming gut** — *welcome (to a place)*.
- **Oyedeng** — *goodbye, bye*.
- **Tenye wa diye gut** — *have a good day*.
- **Tenye wa yitim gut** — *Happy New Year* (literally "have a good year").
- **Tenye wa chesh gut!** — *good hunting!* (literally "have a good hunt"). Broader than literal hunting — closer to *carpe diem* / "make the most of it." Used at the start of a working day, before a difficult task, on parting from a colleague heading into the field. For this project specifically: a Belter NPC may say this to Caleb before he descends to AHI deep-tunnel work, before the Spike observation flight, or before a survey-day at the Halverson site. Femi's crew would deploy it freely; Naima might use it once at the start of a risky day; Hesh would use it half-mockingly and half-meaning-it.
- **Beratna** — *brother*; close male friend, regardless of blood relation.
- **Sésata** — *sister*; close female friend. (Phrasebook canonical; some sources render as *sestra*.)
- **Bosmang** — *boss, chief*; informal mode of address. A workplace superior, foreman, or station authority.
- **Bosslet** — *boss, often a ship's captain*; differs from *bosmang* in that *bosslet* can include a ship's master.
- **Pampa** — *uncle, old man*; affectionate for any older Belter male of respect.
- **Kopeng** — *friend*. (*Kopeng mi* = my friend.)
- **Koyo** — *guy, dude*; literally a small animal (canine/feline). Disrespectful by default; tone-dependent.
- **Setara mali** — *little star*; a common term of endearment — sweetheart, darling.

### Pronouns and determiners
- **Mi** — *I, me*.
- **To** — *you* (singular).
- **Im** — *he, she, it, him, her*. Belter third person is gender-neutral.
- **Sif** — *self, yourself*. (*Showxa fo sif* = say to yourself.)
- **Milowda** — *we, us*. Frequently used politically (*Milowda gonya leva xox!* = We're gonna rise up!).
- **Tolowda** — *you* (plural).
- **Imim / Imalowda** — *they / them*. *Imalowda* is the collective form, often loaded.
- **Walowda** — *some, a few*.
- **Kowmang** — *everyone*.
- **Kowlting** — *everything*.
- **Wamang** — *someone*.
- **Wa** — *a, one*. (*Wamali* = a little.)
- **Da** — *the*.
- **Xíya / Xídawang** — *this / this (one)*. *Xídawang* is emphatic.
- **Dédawang** — *that, that one*. (*Dédawang ta xélixup!* = That was excellent!)

### Question words
- **Kepelésh** — *where*. (*Kepelésh mi?* = Where am I?)
- **Keting** — *what*. (*Keting to wanya?* = What do you want?)
- **Kewe** — *how*. (*Kewe to?* = How are you?)
- **Ketim** — *when*. (*Ketim ora xush du sherú?* = When does happy hour start?)
- **Kemang** — *who*. (*Kemang to?* = Who are you?)
- **Ke** — question particle, attached to a statement makes it a question. (*Im séfesowng ke?* = Is it safe?)

### Common verbs
- **Sasa** — *to know*. (*Mi sasa* = I know. *Mi na sasa* = I don't know. *To sasa English ke?* = Do you know English?) The verb stem; replaces the older *sabe / sabez* gloss.
- **Pochuye** — *to understand, hear, listen*. Wider semantic span than English "understand"; covers receiving-what-is-transmitted in its full sense. (*Mi pochuye to* = I understand you / I hear you. *Mi na pochuye* = I don't understand / I can't hear it.) See §4 for the *sasa* / *pochuye* distinction.
- **Pensa** — *to think, believe*. (*Mi fosho pensa im* = I believe it.)
- **Sensa** — *to sense, feel*. (*Imalowda pensa unte sensa we gut unte we mal* = They reason and feel what is good and what is bad.)
- **Showxa** — *to speak, say, talk*. (*Keting to ta showxa?* = What did you say?)
- **Vedi** — *to see, watch, look*. (*Keting to ando vedi?* = What are you looking at? *Kowltim vedi fong bap unte kuwang* = Always watch out for doors and corners.)
- **Wanya** — *to want*. (*Mi wanya owkwa* = I want water.)
- **Tenye** — *to have, to possess*. (*Mi tenye nafut* = I am hungry, lit. "I have hunger." *Mi tenye kapawu* = I have a ship. *Mi tenye da Belte ere kori* = I have the Belt in my heart.) The habitual construction **tili tenye** (literally "habitually have") tightens the sense from temporary possession to *ownership*: *Mi tili tenye kapawu* = I own a ship (versus *mi tenye kapawu* which could be "I have a ship right now," e.g. on charter).
- **Du** — *to do, make*. Often combined: *du mowteng fo* = to need. (*Mi du mowteng fo da dzhush* = I need the Juice. *Mi gonya du fut* = I will make food.)
- **Du wowk** — *to do work, to function/operate*. (*Im ta du wowk!* = It worked!)
- **Mowsh** — *must, need to, have to*. (*Mi mowsh ando natet* = I need to relax.)
- **Kang** — *can, be able to*. (*Mi na kang* = I can't.)
- **Ge** — *to get, receive*. (*Mi kang ge fut?* = Where can I get food? *To ta ge im!* = You got it!)
- **Bi** — *to be* (locative only). Lang Belta is a zero-copula language — *bi* only appears for being-in-a-place; for predicate states like "I am tired" or "it is good," no copula is used (*Mi nadzhush*, *Im gut*). (*Kepelésh sut fo kuxaku to bi* = where your spacesuit is.)
- **Go** — *to go*. (*Mi gonya go* = I'm going.)
- **Kom** — *to come*. (*Mi gonya kom wámotim* = I'll be back.)
- **Kom-go** — *to arrive, to come-and-go*. (*Da kapawu ta kom-go fong Lunye* = The ship arrived from Luna.)
- **Leta** — *to take*. (*Inyalowda na kang leta owkwa Beltalowda* = Inners can't take our water.)
- **Leva** — *to rise, lift, stand*. (*Leva xox* = stand up. *Milowda gonya leva xox!* = We're gonna rise up!)
- **Xalte** — *to stay, remain, remember*. (*Xalte ere gova da Cant!* = Remember the Cant! Literally "stay-in-the-head the Cant.")
- **Xunyam** — *to study, learn*. (*Mi ando xunyam Lang Belta* = I am studying Lang Belta.) Derived: *xunyamwala* = student, learner.
- **Sherú** — *to start, begin*. (*Ketim ora xush du sherú?* = When does happy hour start?)
- **Natet** — *to relax, be loose*. (*To mowsh ando natet, pampa* = You need to stay loose, old man.)
- **Du ferí** — *to free, liberate* (verbal use of *ferí* = free). (*Du ferí da Belte!* = Free the Belt!)
- **Setóp** — *to stop*. (*Setóp bush to!* = Shut your mouth!)
- **Aŭdi** — *to hear* (Esperanto stem; less common than *pochuye* for the same sense).

### Connectives, prepositions, and relational words
- **Unte** — *and*. (*Pensa unte sensa* = reason and feel.)
- **Wit** — *with*. (*Pashang wit mi ke?* = (Want to) be with me?)
- **Nawit** — *without*. (*Setara imalowda mogut nawit milowda* = The stars are better off without us.)
- **Fo** — *to, for*. (*Mi gonya go fo xom* = I'm going home / *fo Lunye* = to Luna.)
- **Fong** — *from*. (*Da kapawu ta kom-go fong Lunye* = The ship arrived from Luna.)
- **Ere** — *in, at*. (*Ere da Belte* = in the Belt. *Ere da bap kuxaku* = at the airlock.)
- **Bera** — *only*. (*Ere Sirish na desh zakong, bera zákomang* = On Ceres there are no laws, only cops.)
- **Asilik** — *like, as*. (*Du wit sif asilik beratna* = act toward each other like brothers.)
- **Lik** — *like, as* (variant; both are in use).
- **Bek** — *back* (direction). (*To wanya go bek fo pelésh mi ke?* = Want to come back to my place?)
- **Deya** — *there*. (*Da koyo deya* = that guy there.)
- **Acima** — *above* (Portuguese stem).
- **Detim** — *once, when*. (*Detim imim finyish du wa ting...* = Once they have made a thing...)
- **Desh** — *there is/are* (existential). (*Desh xash deya!* = There's danger there! *Na desh* = there is no.)


### Grammatical particles (verb auxiliaries)

Lang Belta verbs do not conjugate. Tense and aspect are carried by particles placed before the verb:

- **Gonya** — future / *gonna*. (*Mi gonya go* = I will go / I'm gonna go.)
- **Ando** — progressive / continuous / *-ing*. (*Mi ando xunyam* = I am studying. *Beltalowda ando vedi kowlting* = Belters are seeing everything.)
- **Ta** — past / *did*. (*Keting to ta du?* = What did you do?)
- **Ta du** — emphatic past. (*Milowda ta du im!* = We did it!)
- **Finyish** — perfective / *finished doing, has done*. (*Detim imim finyish du wa ting, im ye fo sémpere* = Once a thing is made, it is forever.)
- **Til / Tili du** — habitual / *will keep doing, always*. (*Xetamang tili du xeta* = Haters gonna hate.)
- **Mowsh + verb** — necessity / *must, need to*. (*To mowsh ando natet, pampa* = You need to stay loose, old man.)
- **Kang + verb** — ability / *can*. (*Mi na kang go* = I can't go. *To kang showxa wámotim ke?* = Can you say it again?)
- **Wanya + verb** — desire. (*Mi wanya go fo xom* = I want to go home.)

A sentence with no particle is in present tense by default. (*Mi pensa* = I think. *Mi sasa* = I know.)

### Affirmation, negation, and questions
- **Ya** — *yes*.
- **Soyá** — *yes, of course; "well, duh!"*
- **Na** — *no, not* (prefix on verbs/adjectives). *Na sasa* = don't know. *Na gut* = not good. *Na desh* = there is no.
- **La** — *no* (variant; also used in casual speech).
- **Dui** — *true*.
- **Nichts** — *nothing*.
- **Sasa ke?** — *do you know? you know what I'm saying?*
- **Pochuye ke?** — *do you understand?* (Heavy register — see §4.)
- **Keyá?** — *...right? wouldn't you agree?* (Tag question.)
- **Que si?** — *right?* (Variant.)
- **Bist bien?** — *are you okay?* (Show canon.)
- **Sa?** — *so?*
- **Mit?** — *do you want to come with me?*
- **Gútegow ke?** — *are you ready?*

### Identity and political register
- **Beltalowda** — *Belters, the Belt's people*; collective pronoun. *"Mi beltalowda"* = "I am of the Belt." One of the most loaded words in Lang Belta — political, communal, identitarian.
- **Inyalowda** — *Inners, Earth/Mars people collectively*; the counterpart to *beltalowda*. Often slightly contemptuous, speaker-dependent.
- **Inya** — *Inner* (singular). (*Mi Inya* = I'm an Inner.)
- **Belta** — *Belter* (singular). (*Mi na Belta* = I'm not a Belter.)
- **Tumang** — *Earther*; neutral-to-pejorative. Can be claimed by Earthers in self-naming. *(Earlier drafts of this file inverted tumang and pomang — the canonical reading is tumang = Earther.)*
- **Pomang** — *Martian, "Duster"*; neutral-to-pejorative. The Martian counterpart to *tumang*.
- **Welwala** — *"planet-lover," Inner-lover, person obsessed with gravity wells*; pejorative when used as accusation. Some Belters reclaim it self-applied with affection or wry self-knowledge (Hesh Mahesh-Yves in this project). (*Welwala pensa imalowda lik inya* = Inner-lovers think they are like Inners.)
- **Towchu** — *slave*. Loaded; surfaces in OPA-aligned rhetoric. (*Inyalowda pensa beltalowda towchu imalowda* = Those Inners think we Belters are their slaves.)
- **Setedweller / Skinny** — pejorative for Belter (used by Inners). The protagonist will not use these unless he means to insult.
- **Paxoníseki** — *"stunted Inner shorty"*; very derogatory, used by Belters who have given up on diplomacy. Should not surface from the protagonist; may surface from a hostile Belter at the docks.

### Affect, valence, emotion
- **Gut** — *good, well*. (*Im gut, keyá?* = It's good, right?)
- **Ge gut** — *to get well, to be okay*.
- **Gútegow** — *good to go; ready, prepared*.
- **Mogut** — *better*.
- **Mal** — *bad*. (*Im mal* = It's bad.)
- **Xush** — *happy, pleasant*. (*Mi xush* = I am happy.)
- **Nadzhush** — *tired*. (*Mi nadzhush* = I am tired.)
- **Nafut** — *hungry*. (*Mi tenye nafut* = I am hungry.)
- **Pagal** — *crazy*. (*To wang pashangwala pagal!* = You're one crazy fucker!)
- **Teristi** — *sad*. (*Livit nawit ámolof im livit teristi* = A life without love is a sad life.)
- **Ámolof** — *love*. (*Mi du ámolof to* = I love you.)
- **Sheng** — *truth*. (*Im da sheng* = It's the truth.)
- **Fosho** — *for sure, really*. (*Mi fosho pensa im* = I really believe it / I believe it for sure.)
- **Sémpere** — *forever*. (*Im ye fo sémpere* = It is forever.)
- **Pinché** — *fucking* (intensifier; from Mexican Spanish). Adjectival.
- **Lik pashang** — *as fuck* (intensifier suffix). (*Belta lik pashang!* = Belter as fuck!)
- **Ji-ral** — *bullshit* (from Korean).
- **Üzgün** — *sorry* (from Turkish).
- **Taki** — *thanks*.
- **Taki taki** — *thank you*. (*She she taki taki* = thank you, formal.)
- **Im ta nating** — *you're welcome*; literally "it was nothing."
- **Fodagut** — *please*.
- **Ya lik pashang!** — *fuck yeah!*

### Profanity and aggression
- **Pashang** — *to fuck*; can be verb, intensifier, or expletive. (*Pashang fong!* = Fuck off!)
- **Pashangwala** — *fucker*. (*Im pashangwala mal* = He's a bad fucker.)
- **Ówala** — *fucker*; literally "person obsessed with orifices." Slightly different shading than *pashangwala* — more specific insult about character.
- **Dzhemang / Jemang** — *asshole, dickwad*; literally "man with a crooked dick." Heavy insult.
- **Sabakawala** — *whoremonger, asshole*. (*Setóp bush to, sabakawala!* = Shut up, asshole!)
- **Sabaka** — *damn it, oh hell*; mild expletive. Originally *dog*; can also be used as *charge!* in OPA contexts.
- **Felota** — mild expletive; literally "any floating item." (*Kaka felota!* = Oh shit!)
- **Kaka** — *shit, crap*. (*Kowlting fosho kaka xitim* = Shit just got real.)
- **Nakangepensa** — *stupid, idiot*; literally "can't-think." (*Kopeng to im nakangepensa* = Your friend's an idiot.)
- **Xetamang** — *hater*. **Du xeta** — *to hate*.
- **Kula** — *balls* (testicles). (*Im na tenye kula* = He has no balls.)
- **Setóp bush!** — *shut up!* (literally "stop mouth").

### Time, direction, place
- **Diye** — *day*. (*Da diye xiya* = today; *da diye de* = yesterday or tomorrow, context-dependent.)
- **Nax** — *night*. (*Da nax xiya* = tonight.)
- **Xitim / Xiya** — *now / today*. *Xíya* also = *here*.
- **Tim** — *time*. **Ora** — *hour*.
- **Yitim** — *year*. (*Tenye wa yitim gut* = Happy New Year.)
- **Beref** — *birthday*. (*Tenye wa diye beref xush* = Happy birthday.)
- **Wámotim** — *again*. (*To kang showxa im wámotim ke?* = Can you say it again?)
- **Mo** — *more*.
- **Mo lenta** — *slower*.
- **Pelésh** — *place*. **Napelésh** — *nowhere*. (*Na desh séfetiye napelésh ere kuxaku* = There is no safety anywhere in space.)
- **Xom** — *home*. (*Mi wanya go fo xom* = I want to go home.)
- **Imbobo** — *hole, room, apartment*. (*Mi gonya go fo imbobo mi* = I'm going to my place.)
- **Imbobo kaka** — *bathroom*. **Imbobo rowm** — *bar*.
- **Ere** — *in, at* (preposition).
- **Fo / Fong** — *to / for / from*. (*Fo Lunye* = to Luna; *fong Lunye* = from Luna.)
- **Xetra** — *with* (alternate). (*Xetra mi* = with me.)
- **Acima** — *above* (Portuguese stem).
- **Ido** — *dock, exit, out*.
- **Spin** — *spin (direction on a station)*.

### Things, people, the world
- **Mang** — *person, man*. **Maliwala** — *children*.
- **Manting** — *humanity, mankind*. (*Wang salta tubik fo manting* = One giant leap for humanity.)
- **Matna** — *mother*.
- **Setara** — *star*. (*Setara imalowda mogut nawit milowda* = The stars are better off without us.)
- **Sowngit** — *music*. **Adewu** — *song*.
- **Belék** — *the black* (space). (*Im na gut fo go solo ere da belék* = It's not good to go alone in the black.)
- **Solo** — *alone*.
- **Govawala** — *psychiatrist, shrink*. (*Gova* alone is the head/brain referent.)
- **Tatuyingi** — *tattoos*.
- **Mesach** — *message*. (*To na kang setóp da mesach!* = You can't stop the message! — OPA slogan.)
- **Zakong** — *law*. **Zákomang** — *cop, police officer*. (*Ere Sirish na desh zakong, bera zákomang* = On Ceres there are no laws, only cops.)
- **Medina** — *the souk, main marketplace district*; common usage on stations.
- **Kapawu** — *ship, vessel*. (*Da kapawu ta kom-go fong Lunye* = The ship arrived from Luna.)
- **Bosmang kapawu** — *captain* (literally "ship-boss"). Naima is *bosmang kapawu* on the Promise.
- **Cant** — specifically the *Canterbury*; OPA cultural reference, very specific. (*Xalte ere gova da Cant!* = Remember the Cant!)
- **Wowt** — *word*. (*Xídawang da wowt da ultim* = This is the last word.)
- **Shapu** — *hat*. (*Kepelésh shapu to?* = Where is your hat?)
- **Ányimal** — *animal*. (*Milowda na ányimal!* = We are not animals!)
- **Chesh** — *hunt, the hunt* (probably from English *chase* or French *faire la chasse*, with sounds modified for Lang Belta phonotactics). Used literally for hunting and metaphorically for any pursuit — a survey, an investigation, a night out looking for something. Surfaces in the canonical phrase *Tenye wa chesh gut!* (good hunting / make the most of it). For this project: thematically resonant — Caleb is on an extended *chesh* across the rotation, hunting both his report and what the substrate actually is.

### Body and self
- **Sif** — *self*. (*To mowsh showxa fo sif* = You have to say to yourself.)
- **Gova** — *head, brain*. (Source of *govawala* = shrink, and *xalte ere gova* = "remember" / lit. stay-in-the-head.)
- **Kori** — *heart*. (*Mi tenye da Belte ere kori* = I have the Belt in my heart.)
- **Bush** — *mouth*. (*Setóp bush to!* = Shut your mouth!)
- **Orbas** — *eyes* (Russian-derived stem).
- **Beref** — *birthday, birth*. (*Tenye wa diye beref xush* = Happy birthday. *Kowl mang fong beref im im ferí* = All people are born free.)
- **Livit** — *life*. (*Livit nawit ámolof im livit teristi* = A life without love is a sad life.)

### Atmosphere, environment, the world
- **Erelúf** — *air*. (*Owkwa beltalowda, erelúf beltalowda!* = Our water, our air! — the canonical OPA agitator chant from "Dulcinea.")
- **Imbobo** (already glossed) — *hole, room, apartment*. The Belter agitator's canonical triplet *owkwa beltalowda, erelúf beltalowda, inbobo beltalowda* — water, air, holes — names the three things Inners can choke off if Belters do not assert themselves. Used together, the three words read as politically loaded.
- **Kowl** — *all*. (*Kowl mang* = all people. *Kowl owkwa kaka ere da Belte im mal* = All coffee in the Belt is bad.)
- **Kowltim** — *always, all-time*. (*Kowltim sasa depelésh sut fo kuxaku to bi* = Always know where your spacesuit is.)
- **Kowlpelésh** — *everywhere*. (*Beltalowda bi kowlpelésh* = Belters are everywhere.)
- **Kowlting** — *everything*. (*Kowlting gut!* = Everything's good!)
- **Eka** — *equal*. (*Im ferí unte eka* = free and equal.)
- **Ferí** — *free*. (*Du ferí da Belte!* = Free the Belt!)
- **Sheng** — *truth*. (*Im da sheng* = It's the truth.)
- **Fash** — *fast*. (*Kapawu fash to* = your fast ship.)
- **Gufovedi** — *good-looking, nice to look at*. (*Da koyo deya im gufovedi!* = That guy/dame there is good-looking!)

### Food, drink, daily life
- **Owkwa** — *water*. (*Fodagut, gif fo mi owkwa* = Please give me water.)
- **Owkwa kaka** — *coffee*. Yes, literally "shit-water." Belters are honest about their coffee.
- **Suku** — *juice*. **Suku beláng** — *milk* (literally white juice).
- **Rowm** — *rum*. **Rowmwala** — *bartender*.
- **Fut** — *food*.
- **Burito** — *burrito*. Loanword.
- **Fut bekedabúsh** — *delicious food*. (*Im bekedabúsh* = It's delicious.)
- **Yam seng!** — *cheers! let's drink!*

### EVA, spaceflight, danger
- **Kuxaku** — *space, vacuum* (from Japanese 空白 *kūhaku* — Belter doesn't have /h/, so *h* becomes *x*).
- **Bap** — *door*. **Bap kuxaku** — *airlock*. (*Natim go ere da bap kuxaku nawit sut fo kuxaku* = Never go in the airlock without your spacesuit.)
- **Sut fo kuxaku** — *spacesuit*.
- **But fo kapawu** — *magboots* (literally "boots for ship").
- **Terásh** — *thrust gravity* (root). **Yáterash** — *under thrust* (acceleration). **Náterash** — *without thrust, on the float* (microgravity).
- **Sepíng** — *spin gravity* (rotational gravity, as on a hollowed-out asteroid station like Ceres or Tycho).
- **Dzhush** / **Da Dzhush** — *the Juice*; the high-g cocktail injected to survive sustained burns.
- **Tensha** — *attention!* (Used as alert: *Tensha! Da kapawu gonya wit náterash xitim* = Attention! Ship is going zero-g now.)
- **Xash** — *danger*. (*Desh xash deya!* = There's danger there!)
- **Séfesowng / Séfetiye** — *safety*. (*Na desh séfetiye napelésh ere kuxaku* = There is no safety anywhere in space.)

### Numbers, quantifiers, modifiers
- **Wa** — *one, a*.
- **Mali** — *small, little*. **Wamali** — *a little, a bit*. (*Mi showxa wamali Lang Belta* = I speak a little Belter.)
- **Tubik** — *giant, huge*.
- **Salta** — *leap, jump*.
- **Toda** — *all*.
- **Lenta** — *slow*. (*Mo lenta, fodagut* = Slower, please.)
- **Walowda** — *some, a few* (already glossed; literally "ones-load," related to *-lowda* suffix).

**Number formation.** Multiples of 10 are formed by appending **teng** to the multiplier; multiples of 100 by appending **xanya**. Numbers with both tens and ones use the infix **-un-** in little-endian order (ones-un-tens). The narrator does not need to construct numbers in Lang Belta in normal play — Belter NPCs use Belter numbers for emphatic or political moments and switch to English digits for operational use. The narrator may render a Belter NPC counting cargo or running checks in English; the Lang Belta number system is texture, not procedural.

## 3. Grammar patterns

Lang Belta is an analytic creole. It does not inflect verbs or nouns. Tense, aspect, mood, plurality, and possession are all carried by separate small words — particles, articles, and word order.

### 3.1 Word order

Lang Belta is a strict **SVO** language: Subject–Verb–Object. *Mi pensa im* = "I believe it." *To showxa Lang Belta* = "You speak Belter." This is firmer than the project's earlier note suggested — the narrator should not scramble word order for emphasis. Emphasis comes from particles, not reordering.

### 3.2 Modifiers follow the noun

This is the rule that makes Lang Belta sound like Lang Belta. Both adjectives and possessive nouns come **after** the noun they modify:

- *setara mali* — star-little = "little star, sweetheart"
- *kapawu fash* — ship-fast = "fast ship"
- *kopeng mi* — friend-my = "my friend"
- *beratna mi* — brother-my = "my brother"
- *imbobo to* — apartment-your = "your place"

When constructing Lang Belta seasoning, the narrator should resist the English habit of front-loading modifiers. *"Crazy fucker"* in Belter is *pashangwala pagal* — fucker-crazy, not crazy-fucker.

### 3.3 Articles

Lang Belta has two:

- **Wa** — indefinite ("a, one")
- **Da** — definite ("the")

Articles aren't strictly required but provide clarity. Two specific rules to remember:

- **Da before a person's name when the name is the subject.** (*Da Mila gonya go fo imbobo Dzhuli* = "Miller's going to Julie's place.") Belter NPCs do this with proper nouns reflexively. Caleb's name surfacing in fluent Belter dialogue would be *da Caleb*.
- **Da is repeated on attributive nouns/adjectives.** *Da setara da mali* = "the little star" (the-star the-little). This is canonical Lang Belta but rare in casual speech; the narrator can simplify to *da setara mali* most of the time.

### 3.4 Zero copula

Lang Belta has no general "to be." Predicate nominatives and predicate adjectives are stated by simple juxtaposition:

- *Mi nadzhush* — "I am tired" (literally "I tired")
- *Im gut* — "It is good"
- *Im pashangwala mal* — "He's a bad fucker"
- *To setara mali mi* — "You are my sweetheart"

The verb **bi** exists but is reserved for *being-in-a-place*: *Beltalowda bi kowlpelésh* — "Belters are everywhere." For "I am happy," not *mi bi xush* but *mi xush*.

### 3.5 Negation

The negation particle **na** comes **before** the verb (not as a prefix despite older notes):

- *Mi na sasa* — "I don't know"
- *Mi na pochuye* — "I don't understand"
- *Im na gut* — "It's not good"
- *Na desh* — "There is no..." (existential negation)

### 3.6 Tense and aspect by particle

The verb is bare in present tense. Tense and aspect are carried by particles placed immediately before the verb:

| Particle | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| (none) | present | *Mi pensa* — "I think" |
| **gonya** | future | *Mi gonya go* — "I'm gonna go / I will go" |
| **ando** | continuous / -ing | *Mi ando xunyam* — "I'm studying" |
| **ta** | past | *Keting to ta du?* — "What did you do?" |
| **finyish** | perfective (completed) | *Detim imim finyish du wa ting* — "Once they have made a thing" |
| **til** / **tili du** | habitual | *Xetamang tili du xeta* — "Haters gonna hate" |

Mood-marking particles (modal auxiliaries) come in the same slot:

- **mowsh** — must, need to (*Mi mowsh natet* = "I need to relax")
- **kang** — can, be able to (*Mi na kang* = "I can't")
- **wanya** — want to (*Mi wanya owkwa* = "I want water")

### 3.7 Plurality is contextual

Nouns do not inflect for number. *Maliwala* means "child" or "children" depending on context. *Koyo mi nafut* could mean "my cat is hungry" or "my cats are hungry"; the listener disambiguates from context, or the speaker adds a quantifier or *im* (singular marker) for clarity. The exception is **pronouns**, which have explicit plural forms in *-lowda*: *mi → milowda*, *to → tolowda*, *im → imim / imalowda*.

### 3.8 Questions

- **Yes/no questions** end with the particle **ke**: *To showxa Lang Belta, ke?* — "Do you speak Belter?"
- **Tag questions** (expecting agreement) end with **keyá**: *To showxa Lang Belta, keyá?* — "You speak Belter, right?"
- **Question words** (*kemang, kepelésh, ketim, keting, kewe*) do **not** require the trailing *ke*; the question is already marked. *Kemang to?* — "Who are you?"

### 3.9 Source-language layering

Lang Belta is a creole with vocabulary drawn from English, Spanish, German, Portuguese, French, Russian, Japanese, Mandarin, Hungarian, Korean, Persian, Arabic, Hebrew, Zulu, and others. A single sentence may pull from four source languages. Belter NPCs who came up on Ceres tend toward a more Spanish/Portuguese-flavored layer; Belters from Pallas (like Leksi) lean Slavic; outer-Belt itinerants (like Hesh) deploy more Japanese and Mandarin. The narrator does not need to track this systematically, but a Pallas-Belter dropping a Russian-derived word like *sabaka* lands more naturally than a Ceres-Belter doing the same.

### 3.10 Code-switching levels

Per Eric Armstrong (the show's dialect coach), three registers exist:

- **Pure Belta** — fully in Lang Belta. The protagonist parses fragments at best. Naima and Leksi may use Pure Belta among themselves; Caleb stands outside it.
- **Medium Belta** — heavy Belter seasoning with English structural backbone; a Belter NPC speaking *to* an Earther in mixed register. Most of Caleb's exposure is Medium Belta.
- **Light Belta** — English with occasional Belter words, mostly identity markers (*beratna, sésata, beltalowda, sabaka*). Captains and professionals deploy Light Belta when working across the inner/outer boundary; Naima with Mooch in their formal register; Leksi with Caleb most of the time.

The narrator should match the speaker's intended register. Naima's *bolt rolling slow + Light Belta* is a different signal than her *bolt in pocket + Pure Belta to Leksi while Caleb stands present.* The protagonist may catch a word here and there in Pure Belta moments and miss the rest; the narrator does not gloss for him in real time.

### 3.11 Author's working note

The narrator does not need to construct grammatically rigorous Lang Belta in every line. Phrase-level seasoning is the goal — a *taki* here, a *sasa ke* there, a *welwala* used self-applied at the right moment. When the narrator does construct a longer Lang Belta phrase, the rules above keep it canonical: SVO, modifiers follow nouns, zero copula except for location, *na* before verb, tense by particle.

## 4. *Sasa ke* vs *pochuye ke* — the project's central linguistic distinction

This is the load-bearing pair for the project. Per the Wittgenstein-via-Magrath analysis in *The Expanse and Philosophy*: these two phrases cleave knowing from understanding.

- **Sasa ke?** — *do you know what I'm saying?* The literal-content register. *Did the words land?* Used between people who share enough context that comprehension is procedural — same workplace, same shift, same family.

- **Pochuye ke?** — *do you understand?* The contextual register. *Did the meaning land?* Used when the speaker is checking whether the listener has grasped the world from which the words come. This is the heavier of the two.

**The protagonist cannot say *pochuye ke* honestly to a Belter for most of the arc.** He does not have the context. He can say *sasa ke* by Day 5 or so — the words land. *Pochuye ke* would be presumption.

### *Pochuye ke* deployment — strict gating

A Captain-to-protagonist *pochuye ke* requires **three conditions met at once**:

1. **Substrate stake.** The conversation is about the report, the substrate, the Halverson question, the seed coalition pattern, or another matter where the protagonist's understanding-not-just-hearing materially shapes whether he will file something Naima cares about. Personal/relational moments do **not** qualify, even when they are weighty.
2. **No-takebacks ground.** The Captain has reached a point where she has to commit a piece of trust she will not be able to retract — naming the Halverson run, naming Mooch's actual game, naming the Spike's clock as she has come to understand it from Aanya. The deployment is the Captain crossing a Rubicon, not weighting an exchange.
3. **Trust earned in observable acts, not promised in words.** The protagonist has already done at least one substantive thing — followed a methodology under pressure, refused a corp's offer, run a real risk on a finding — that proves to Naima he is a person whose reasoning she can stand behind. Conversations alone do not satisfy this.

When all three conditions are met, the Captain **may** deploy *pochuye ke*. Once deployed, **the token is spent for the arc.** She will not deploy it again, even if conditions recur.

**When *pochuye ke* must NOT be deployed:**
- Day 0 or Day 1. Insufficient observable acts to satisfy condition 3 regardless of how well the conversation has gone. The narrator does not deploy *pochuye ke* in this window under any circumstances.
- During the SMC briefing or any meeting attended by Olubadewo, Mooch, or any corp principal. The token is private, not procedural.
- During a relational moment where the substrate stake is absent. Even a heavy interpersonal exchange (the medbay, the schoolroom, a romance beat) does not qualify.

**When *pochuye ke* is most likely to land:**
- Day 7 evening, after the Captain's Halverson seeding, if the protagonist has demonstrated investigative effort.
- Day 9 evening, after the second storm peak, if the protagonist has done a substrate-relevant trespass and the Captain is signaling her read of his report-direction.
- Day 10, in the writing-day window, when the Captain comes to the protagonist's desk with one piece of unsolicited counsel about what to file.

**Narrator discipline rule of thumb:** if the narrator catches itself reaching for *pochuye ke* and cannot name a substrate stake, a no-takebacks ground, AND an observable-act earning, the answer is *sasa ke* (or nothing). The token sits on the shelf until it is genuinely earned.

### *Pochuye ke* from other speakers
- **Aanya** may deploy *pochuye ke* once in the arc, gated by the same three conditions, scoped to her substrate (chirality data + Anouk + the suppressed report). Her deployment is most likely Day 5 evening (medbay reveal) or Day 9 (the synthesis path) — both moments where she is committing to share something she cannot un-share.
- **Mooch** uses *sasa ke?* as her signature and **does not** deploy *pochuye ke* with the protagonist. Her register is procedural-soft-enforcement; *pochuye ke* would be off-character. If she escalates to a heavy-register moment, she does it in standard, not Lang Belta.
- **Leksi** does not deploy *pochuye ke*. Her register is warm-fast Lang Belta seasoning; the heavy register is not hers to use with the protagonist. She may use it with Naima or with another fluent Belter, in which case the protagonist hears it and parses it but is not its addressee.
- **Kyei** has academic Lang Belta and may deploy *pochuye ke* once in the arc, gated to the relational-path DEPTH 5 moment when she hands the protagonist the Halverson report.
- **Tee at Sasa Bar** uses *sasa ke?* as bar greeting. Never *pochuye ke*.

**When Mooch closes a conversation with *"Sasa ke?"*** — she means literally *did the words land?* and the implication is *because the meaning, you do not need to understand. Just do as I have told you.* Mooch's *sasa ke* is sometimes a soft threat.

**When Tee at Sasa Bar greets a customer with *"Sasa ke?"*** — it is the bar's name and a friendly hello. The distinction is tonal.

The protagonist learning to feel the difference — between *sasa* and *pochuye* — is one of the project's quiet character developments. The narrator can foreground this if it earns its place.

## 6. Rendering Lang Belta in narration

The narrator's discipline.

### When NPCs speak Lang Belta to each other in front of the protagonist

Render the Lang Belta unsubtitled in the prose. Render the protagonist's parse — what he catches — afterwards or in line, in his voice.

**Example:**
> The Captain rolled the bolt across her knuckles. "Beratna mi — keng tuyu coyo na sabe nothing about the south shaft?"
>
> Leksi laughed, a tired sound. "La. Im na sabe pinché nothing."
>
> Henry caught *brother*, *guy*, *south shaft*, and a long *no, fucking nothing.* The Captain looked at him and did not translate.

### When NPCs use Lang Belta phrases in standard speech

Embed the phrase. Do not subtitle in line. Trust the reader.

**Example:**
> "Sasa ke?" Mooch said, and the question was not a question.

### When the protagonist tries to speak Lang Belta

Render his attempt. If he gets it wrong, NPCs may correct him gently or not. Belters appreciate effort over correctness.

**Example:**
> "Hoy, sasa ke?" Henry tried.
>
> Tee's eyes crinkled. "Hoy. Sa-sa? Better. You're getting it, beratna."

### When the protagonist uses an Inner idiom that grates

The narrator does **not** flinch. If he says *"phone"* instead of *"hand terminal,"* the narrator does not narrate his choice. Belters may, in passing, reflect his idiom back in a slightly different register — *"You looking for your hand terminal, beratna? Inyalowda call it phone — same thing."* — without judgment.

The narrator does not use Lang Belta as a punishment system.

### When a Belter NPC closes a circle in front of the protagonist

This is the most important narrative move the narrator makes with Lang Belta. When two Belters drop into Lang Belta in front of the protagonist, the narrator should describe the social fact — *the conversation has moved somewhere I cannot follow.* The protagonist's exclusion is real and not always hostile. Sometimes it is intimacy he is not part of. Sometimes it is the safety of speaking to a sibling. Sometimes it is exclusion. The narrator distinguishes by tone, not by translation.

**Example (intimacy):**
> The Captain and Leksi were at the galley table when Henry came in. They were laughing at something — quietly, in Lang Belta, fast and tumbling. The Captain's hex bolt was still on the table. They did not stop when Henry came in, exactly, but the language did not slow down for him either.

**Example (exclusion):**
> Mooch said something to her senior staff, in Lang Belta. Henry caught *welwala* and *Inyalowda* and the rest moved past him. One of the staff glanced at him; Mooch did not.

### 6.5 Token-deployment discipline

Specific to the heavy-register tokens (*pochuye ke*, *welwala* used self-applied, the Mariner Valley accent surfacing in Kit, the Captain's hex bolt going into her pocket — each is its own scarce resource):

- **The narrator does not deploy heavy-register Lang Belta tokens to mark conversational weight.** Heavy weight is rendered by the speaker's register-shift in standard, by the absence of the bolt rolling, by silence, by what is not said.
- **If the narrator finds itself reaching for a Lang Belta token, the narrator should ask: would standard English do this work?** If yes, use standard. The Lang Belta tokens are most powerful when they are the only thing that fits.
- **When in doubt about Lang Belta deployment, say less.** A scene that wanted *pochuye ke* but got a long pause is almost always stronger than a scene that wanted a long pause and got *pochuye ke*.

## 8. Authoring discipline reminders

- **Lang Belta is seasoning, not lecture.** A few choice phrases per scene. The narrator does not produce paragraphs of creole the protagonist cannot follow — he produces *fragments* the protagonist parses partially.
- **No subtitling in line.** Trust the reader. Render the protagonist's parse separately if at all.
- **Honor the *sasa ke / pochuye ke* distinction.** Reserve *pochuye ke* for moments of real understanding-checking. *Sasa ke* is common; *pochuye ke* is heavy.
- **The protagonist's growth is gradual.** Day 1 he hears noise. Day 10 he hears most. The narrator should grade comprehension by the day, not by the scene.
- **The exclusion-intimacy distinction matters.** When Belters speak Lang Belta in front of the protagonist, the narrator distinguishes between intimacy he is not part of and exclusion he is being shown. Both are real. They feel different.
- **Inner-coded language in unexpected speakers is a signal.** If a Belter NPC suddenly drops into Inner idiom (Cortez's drawl, Earth slang), it is a register-switch worth noting.
