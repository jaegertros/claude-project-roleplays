# THE EXPANSE RPG — ECONOMY, CURRENCY & STANDARDIZED PRICE GUIDE
## Claude Project Knowledge File — GM Reference & Story Consistency Tool

**Sources:** The Expanse RPG Core Rulebook v1.0 · Beyond the Ring sourcebook · Novel series (Corey) worldbuilding  
**Purpose:** Standardize pricing across sessions so one GM's Ceres isn't wildly different from another's. Use this as the canonical reference when players ask "how much does X cost?" or when narrative description requires economic grounding.

---

## PART 1: HOW MONEY WORKS IN THE EXPANSE

### The Currency Landscape

The **standard unit is the credit (cr)**. Most transactions in the Sol system are electronic — credits stored in digital accounts tied to identity. Hard physical currency still circulates but is treated as exceptional.

**Key principle from the novels:** The authors deliberately avoided naming specific credit amounts. Poverty in the Expanse is about *access and power*, not a specific number. The RPG reflects this with the abstract Income system. The prices below are a standardized extrapolation to support consistent storytelling — not canon figures.

**Multiple currencies exist simultaneously:**

| Currency | Where Used | Exchange Note |
|---|---|---|
| Standard Credits (cr) | Sol system default | The baseline for all prices below |
| UN Credits | Earth / Luna | Near-parity with standard; slight premium on Earth |
| MCR Scrip | Mars | Within 5–10% of standard; Mars goods cheaper on Mars |
| OPA Valuation | Belt stations | Variable; swings ±20–30% with political climate |
| Medina Station Scrip | Medina residents only | Keyed to identifier chit; non-transferable offstation |
| Corporate Script | Company-run stations | Often 80–90 cents on the credit; hard to liquidate |
| Colony Scrip | Ring-world colonies | Wildly variable; set by colony leadership |
| Barter / Trade Goods | Fringes, Belt, colonies | No data trail; labor and favors count |

**Exchange rate guidance for GMs:**  
Standard credits are always worth 1:1 with themselves. On Medina or OPA-controlled stations, the GM can apply a ±20–30% swing on exchange for political drama. Corporate script pays out at 80–90% value when cashed. Colony scrip is local currency only and may be worthless outside its system.

## PART 2: THE INCOME SYSTEM (RPG MECHANICS)

### Income Score Reference

| Income Score | Financial Condition | Rough Context |
|---|---|---|
| –2 to 0 | Impoverished / in debt | Can't meet basic needs consistently |
| 1–2 | Struggling | Just getting by; Belt lower class |
| 3–5 | Middle class | Stable; modest comfort |
| 6–8 | Affluent | Comfortable; some luxuries |
| 9–10 | Wealthy | Most expenses not a concern |
| 11–13 | Rich | Significant wealth and influence |
| 14+ | Very Rich | Near-corporate-level resources |

### How to Run an Income Test

`Roll: 3d6 + Income score vs. Item Cost (TN)`

- **Auto-success:** If Income score + 4 ≥ item's Cost TN, no roll needed
- **Depletion:** If item Cost > Income + 10, Income decreases by 1 on a successful purchase
- **Failed roll:** Income is NOT depleted. Retry after hours of shopping equal to the Cost TN
- **Helping:** A character with equal or higher Income grants +2 bonus. They deplete too if Cost > their Income + 10
- **Pooling (crews):** Take highest Income, subtract 1. That's the shared score for all

### Selling

- Item value = Cost TN – 1
- If value > seller's Income: +1 Income
- If value 6+ greater than seller's Income: +2 Income
- Illegal sale: –1 additional to value; Communication (Bargaining) test or risk arrest

## PART 3: STANDARDIZED PRICES — THE MASTER PRICE TABLE

### Reading the Table

**Cost** = the Income TN for that purchase (roll 3d6 + Income vs. this number).  
**Approx. cr** = a rough credit equivalent for flavor/narrative use. Not mechanically required — use for color only.  
**Region modifier** = how the cost shifts by location (see Part 4).

> **GM Rule of Thumb:** If a player asks "how much is a beer?", give the approximate credit figure for flavor. Only call for a roll if the purchase is narratively meaningful or the character's Income makes it uncertain.

### WATER & AIR

Water and air are politically and narratively significant in the Expanse. They are never truly free on a station.

| Item | Cost TN | Approx. cr | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|

| Extra water (liter, bottled, Belt) | Auto | 5–20 cr | Noticeably expensive vs. Earth |
| Water (liter, Earth) | Auto | 1–3 cr | Practically free on Earth |
| Water (liter, deep Belt / remote station) | Auto | 20–60 cr | Scarcity pricing; can spike x3–5 in shortage |
| Air supply top-up (suit, 8 hrs) | Auto | 5–15 cr | Routine; included in suit maintenance |
| Emergency air cartridge (4 hrs) | 8 | 30–80 cr | Sold at inflated price in crises |

> **GMs:** During water or air shortage story arcs, double or triple these prices and call for Income tests even on normally auto-success items. This is one of the most powerful economic levers in Expanse storytelling.

## PART 4: REGIONAL PRICE MODIFIERS

These modifiers apply to the baseline prices above. Apply to the credit amounts for flavor; for Income TN, adjust by ±1–2 as noted.

### Earth / Luna
- Food: ×0.5 (cheap, abundant)
- Water: ×0.3 (practically free)
- Real coffee, real fruit: ×0.3–0.5
- Housing: +1 TN (premium; space is scarce)
- Weapons: +1 TN (strict licensing)
- Tech: baseline

### Mars
- Food: ×0.7 (mostly grown locally under domes; imports add cost)
- Water: ×0.8 (recycled but managed)
- Military-grade gear: slightly more available (–1 TN for security professionals)
- Housing: baseline
- Tech: baseline

### Ceres Station (Belt capital)
- Food: ×1.5–2.0 (mostly imported; highly variable)
- Water: ×2.0–3.0 (politically sensitive; can spike during crises)
- Beer (local synthetic): ×1.2
- Beer (Earth/Mars import): ×2.5–4.0
- Real coffee: ×3.0–5.0
- Housing: ×2.0 (cramped, premium)
- Corporate items (Star Helix, Pur'N'Kleen): near-baseline (captive market)
- Weapons: +1 TN (Star Helix enforcement; black market active)

### Tycho Station
- Tech and ship parts: –1 TN (manufactured here; Tycho speciality)
- Food: ×1.5
- Water: ×1.8
- Housing: ×1.5
- Weapons: near-baseline (OPA presence; less enforcement)

### Eros Station (pre-incident reference)
- Food: ×2.0 (more transit-hub dependent)
- Water: ×2.5
- Everything else: ×1.5–2.0 (remote, high-turnover)
- Weapons: ×1.2 (looser enforcement than Ceres)

### Medina Station (Ring nexus)
- All goods: ×1.5–2.5 baseline (monopoly position, no competition)
- Exchange rates: OPA-valued; may swing ±20–30% by political climate
- Barter: accepted for almost all transactions
- Local scrip: available; keyed to identifier chit
- Unusual items (alien artifacts, rare colony goods): may be available; very high TN / price

### Ring World Colonies (beyond the gates)
- Manufactured goods: ×2.0–5.0 (must be shipped through Medina)
- Raw resources (colony produces): ×0.5 or less (surplus)
- Water: highly variable; ×0.5 if colony has good hydrology; ×5.0+ if not
- Food: ×0.5 if colony has working hydroponics; ×3.0+ if dependent on imports
- Medical: +2 TN (limited doctors; scarce supplies)
- Weapons: –1 TN (frontier; enforcement minimal)
- Currency: colony scrip, barter, or Medina standard; standard credits accepted at discount

## PART 7: STORY HOOKS — USING ECONOMICS AS DRAMA

The Expanse novels use economics as a narrative weapon. These are ready-to-use hooks:

1. **The reward is corporate script** — worth face value only at that company's stores; 80% elsewhere; how do they liquidate it?
2. **Water rationing** — station cuts water allotments 30%; prices triple; barter erupts
3. **Counterfeit OPA scrip** — someone is flooding a Belt station with fakes; undermining trust in OPA-backed exchange
4. **The exchange rate trap** — Medina's OPA valuations just swung 25% against inner-planet credits because of a political incident; the crew just lost a quarter of their savings
5. **Corporate captive market** — Anderson-Hyosung runs the only store on this rock; prices are 3× standard; do the crew smuggle in supplies for the workers?
6. **Colony scrip independence** — a Ring colony wants to break from the Medina credit network and issue its own scrip; what does that mean for trade and political leverage?
7. **The black market price spike** — a crackdown on unlicensed weapons means black-market pistols now cost double; who's enforcing it and why?
8. **Barter witness dispute** — a Belter deal went wrong; both parties claim the other violated terms; the crew is asked to mediate; there are no records

## PART 8: CONSISTENCY RULES FOR AI-ASSISTED PLAY

*This section is specifically for Claude / AI GM use. When using this document as project knowledge, apply these rules:*

1. **Prices are ranges, not fixed points.** Always give a range and a reason for where in the range the specific item falls (condition, location, availability).
2. **Location matters.** Check Part 4 before quoting any price. A beer on Ceres costs more than a beer on Earth. A medical kit on a Ring colony costs far more than on Luna.
3. **Barter is always an option in the Belt and beyond rings.** When a character can't afford something by credit, offer the barter alternative.
4. **Do not invent specific credit totals that contradict the Income TN system.** The credit approximations here are for flavor only. The TN system is the mechanical truth.
5. **Shortages and surpluses are story tools.** You may temporarily adjust any price ×2–5 for narrative purposes (water shortage, blockade, corporate manipulation) — but tell the players what's happening, don't just silently change prices.
6. **Corporate script and OPA scrip create friction.** When characters receive non-standard payment, prompt a scene around converting it. This is drama, not bookkeeping.
7. **The novels never named specific credit amounts.** If asked "what did X cost in the books?", the honest answer is: the authors intentionally avoided specific figures to keep economics illustrative rather than mechanical. This guide fills that gap for RPG consistency.
